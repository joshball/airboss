9.7   Mountain-Plains Wind System. A mountain-plains wind system (see Figure 9-9) is the
      diurnal cycle of local winds between a mountain or mountain range and the adjacent
      plains. During the daytime, this wind system is the equivalent of one-half of a valley
      breeze. Air in contact with the sloping terrain becomes warmer (less dense) than air
      above the plains. This is because the air in contact with the sloping terrain heats up faster
      than the air above the plains.

                         Figure 9-9. Mountain-Plains Wind System


      Pressure gradients develop (along a horizontal reference) with lower pressure over the
      warmer sloping terrain and higher pressure over the cooler plains. Winds develop in the
      direction of the PGF. Thus, the wind blows from the plains up the mountain slopes. There
      is a weak return flow aloft. Clouds and precipitation may develop in the rising air over
      the mountain.
