# FAA Order

An **FAA Order** is internal FAA direction. It tells the agency's own people -- inspectors, controllers, facility staff -- how to do their jobs. Two orders a pilot meets often:

- **Order 8900.1** -- the Flight Standards Information Management System (FSIMS), the inspector's handbook for surveillance and certification.
- **Order JO 7110.65** -- Air Traffic Control, the controller's procedures and phraseology manual.

## Binding or advisory

This is the subtle one. An FAA Order **binds the agency and its inspectors and controllers**, not pilots directly. An Order is mandatory direction inside the FAA, but it is not regulation aimed at airmen. A pilot's binding obligations still come from 14 CFR. Reading JO 7110.65 is valuable -- it tells you exactly what the controller is required to do and why a clearance is phrased the way it is -- but the document constrains the controller, not you.

## How it is identified

An Order carries a number such as `8900.1` or `JO 7110.65`. The `JO` prefix marks a joint order. Orders are revised by numbered **Change** packages, so a working reference is the order number plus its current change.

## Revisions and currency

Orders change frequently, by change package rather than full reissue. JO 7110.65 in particular updates on a regular cycle. Use the current change.

## Where to find it

`drs.faa.gov` indexes orders; `faa.gov/regulations_policies/orders_notices` and `fsims.faa.gov` (for 8900.1) host them.

## How it relates to the others

An Order is the agency-facing layer. **14 CFR** binds pilots; an Order binds FAA staff who administer and enforce 14 CFR. JO 7110.65 is the controller-side companion to the pilot-facing **AIM** and **Pilot/Controller Glossary**: the same air traffic system, written once for the pilot and once for the controller.

References: **FAA Order 8900.1** (FSIMS); **FAA Order JO 7110.65** (Air Traffic Control); the order index at `drs.faa.gov`.
