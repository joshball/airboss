# AIM

The **AIM (Aeronautical Information Manual)** is the FAA's operational how-to. It covers the procedures, phraseology, and hazards a pilot uses in day-to-day flying: how the airspace works, how to read approach lighting, how to talk to ATC, what wake turbulence and icing do. Where 14 CFR says *what* the rule is, the AIM tells you *how* to operate inside the system.

## Binding or advisory

Advisory. The AIM is guidance, not regulation. It explains how the system is meant to work and how to operate within it, but the legal obligations live in 14 CFR. In practice the AIM and the rules track each other closely, and the AIM often paraphrases a regulation, but if the two ever appear to disagree, 14 CFR governs.

## How it is identified

An AIM citation such as `7-1-6` has three parts:

- `7` -- the chapter (here, Safety of Flight).
- `1` -- the section within the chapter.
- `6` -- the paragraph.

So "AIM 7-1-6" is chapter 7, section 1, paragraph 6. The chapter-section-paragraph shape lets you cite a precise piece of operational guidance.

## Revisions and currency

The AIM is revised on a regular publication cycle and reissued with change bars marking what moved. Use the current edition: an out-of-date AIM can describe a procedure or a piece of phraseology that has since changed.

## Where to find it

`faa.gov/air_traffic/publications` hosts the current AIM. It is also published in the combined **FAR/AIM** book alongside 14 CFR.

## How it relates to the others

The AIM is the operational layer. **14 CFR** is the binding rule it operates within. The **Pilot/Controller Glossary** is the AIM's companion: the shared vocabulary that the AIM and ATC phraseology both draw on. An **Advisory Circular** explains an acceptable means of complying with a specific rule; the AIM gives the broader operational picture.

References: the **AIM** at `faa.gov/air_traffic/publications`; the **Pilot/Controller Glossary** that ships with it.
