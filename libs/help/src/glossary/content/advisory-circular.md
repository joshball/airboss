# AC

An **AC (Advisory Circular)** is FAA guidance. It explains, in plain language, **one acceptable means** of complying with a regulation in 14 CFR. It is the FAA showing its work: here is a way to satisfy the rule that we will accept.

## Binding or advisory

Advisory. This is the single most misunderstood point in the documentation ecosystem. An AC is **not itself binding**. It describes *an* acceptable method, not *the only* method -- a pilot or operator is free to comply with the underlying regulation a different way. The regulation in 14 CFR is what binds you; the AC just hands you a known-good route to satisfying it. Treating the AC as law over-constrains you; ignoring the regulation it explains under-constrains you.

## How it is identified

An AC identifier such as `AC 00-45H` has three parts:

- `AC` -- the document-type prefix.
- `00-45` -- the AC number. The first segment is the **subject series** and mirrors the 14 CFR subchapter structure: `00` general, `20` aircraft, `60` airmen, `61` certification and ratings, `90` air traffic and general operating rules, `150` airports.
- `H` -- the revision letter. Each full revision advances the letter.

The identifier locates the document; the **title** ("Aviation Weather Services") describes it. `AC 61-65` is guidance *about* 14 CFR Part 61.

## Revisions and currency

A new revision letter replaces the prior letter entirely. A "Change 1 / CHG 2" is a partial amendment layered on the current revision. "Cancelled" means the AC is withdrawn -- sometimes with a successor (AC 00-6A cancelled, AC 00-6B exists), sometimes without (AC 00-2 cancelled, replaced by the Dynamic Regulatory System, not a document). Always confirm the current edition before relying on an AC.

## Where to find it

`drs.faa.gov` (the Dynamic Regulatory System) is the modern master search. `faa.gov/regulations_policies/advisory_circulars` also lists them. No single FAA office owns all ACs: the Airports office owns the 150-series, Flight Standards owns others.

## How it relates to the others

An AC sits one layer out from **14 CFR**: the regulation binds, the AC explains an acceptable means. **FAA handbooks** go deeper still, giving training-depth treatment of the same material. The **AIM** gives operational procedures.
