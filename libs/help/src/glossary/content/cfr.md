# 14 CFR

**14 CFR** is **Title 14 of the Code of Federal Regulations** -- the body of federal aviation law. It is the binding part of the documentation ecosystem: when 14 CFR says you must do something, you must, and a violation is an enforceable matter.

Pilots almost always call these rules the **FAR (Federal Aviation Regulations)**. "FAR" and "14 CFR" name the same thing. "FAR" is the older colloquial term; "14 CFR" is what the document is actually titled. You will see both, and the citation **FAR/AIM** on the cover of the common combined book refers to 14 CFR paired with the (non-regulatory) AIM.

## Binding or advisory

Binding. 14 CFR is regulation. This is the load-bearing distinction in the whole FAA documentation system: a 14 CFR section is law, while an Advisory Circular about that same section is only guidance. A pilot who treats an AC as binding over-constrains; a pilot who treats a 14 CFR section as optional under-constrains. The rule is the rule.

## How it is identified

A 14 CFR citation reads `14 CFR 91.137(a)(2)`:

- `14` -- the title (aviation).
- `91` -- the part (general operating and flight rules).
- `137` -- the section within the part.
- `(a)(2)` -- the paragraph and subparagraph.

Common parts: **Part 1** (definitions), **Part 61** (pilot certification), **Part 91** (general operating rules), **Part 121 / 135** (air carrier and commuter operations), **Part 141** (pilot schools).

## Revisions and currency

14 CFR changes through formal rulemaking. The authoritative, always-current text is the **electronic CFR (eCFR)** at `ecfr.gov`. Printed FAR/AIM books are a snapshot and go stale; for a currency-sensitive question, read eCFR.

## Where to find it

`ecfr.gov` (Title 14) is the canonical, continuously updated source. `faa.gov/regulations_policies` links into it.

## How it relates to the others

14 CFR is the spine. **Advisory Circulars** explain one acceptable means of complying with a regulation. **Airworthiness Directives** are themselves regulatory and are issued under 14 CFR Part 39. The **AIM** gives the operational how-to that supports compliance but is not itself law.

References: **14 CFR** (Title 14, Code of Federal Regulations); the **eCFR** at `ecfr.gov`.
