# Review of ADR 019 -- Reference Identifier System

## Summary

**Verdict: approve with substantial revisions.** The instinct is right -- identifier as contract, registry as resolver, body as source of truth, four-tier storage. Those four moves are good and worth keeping. But the *shape* of the identifier as written paints us into corners in three load-bearing places: (1) the dotted-slug grammar collides with the natural addressing of several corpora the user has already named (POHs, NTSB reports, sectional/approach plates, time-series weather, ASRS, podcasts, and anything ICAO/EASA), (2) the edition model conflates "the identifier" and "the version" in ways that will hurt during the very annual rollover the ADR claims to handle, and (3) the multi-reference / range / exclusion microsyntax is over-clever and will not survive contact with copy-paste, RAG, or non-airboss Markdown viewers.

The phasing is also too optimistic about the gap between Phase 1 (validator) and Phase 3 (renderer). Lessons authored between those phases either fail validation against an empty registry, or land with broken substitution tokens at render. That gap needs an explicit answer in the ADR, not a footnote.

Recommendation: rework §1 (identifier shape), §1.4 (multi-reference), §3.1 (substitution tokens), and §8 (phasing). §2, §4, and the overall registry-as-contract direction can stand with smaller changes.

## Critical concerns

### C1. The dotted-slug grammar collapses on real corpora

The ADR shows clean examples for CFR / AIM / AC / interp / orders / handbooks / POHs. The corpora the user named in the reviewer charter (and that STORAGE.md already lists) include:

- POHs and POH supplements (per-airframe, per-revision, with manufacturer-specific section names like "Section 2 Limitations" vs. Cessna's vs. Cirrus's vs. Piper's)
- NTSB accident/incident reports (event-keyed, not document-keyed: NTSB ID, docket number, factual report, probable cause)
- Manufacturer service bulletins / service letters / SAIBs / ADs (registered against an airframe + serial range, not against a section)
- ICAO Annexes (numbered Annexes 1-19 with chapters/sections, in multiple ICAO languages)
- EASA / Transport Canada / CASA / foreign reg sets (parallel structures to FAA, often translated)
- Sectional charts, TACs, IFR enroute charts (geographic, by chart name and edition cycle every 56/28 days)
- Approach plates / SIDs / STARs (airport ID + procedure name + revision date, FAA changes them every 28 days)
- FAA forms (8710-1, 8060-4, 8500-8 etc., with revision dates)
- InFOs and SAFOs (numbered like `InFO 18006` with year-prefix collisions)
- Maintenance manuals, IPCs, wiring diagrams (per airframe + ATA chapter + revision)
- Type Certificate Data Sheets (TCDS A-numbers, revision letters)
- METAR/TAF historical archives (station + timestamp, time-series, not document)
- ACS / PTS (named area of operation, task, element)
- 8083 family (already covered, but check the rest -- 8083-2, -3C, -6, -9, -15, -16, -25, -27, -28, -30, -32, -34, plus FAA-S-ACS-* publications)
- Pilot/Controller Glossary (alphabetic, terms with cross-refs, revised with AIM)
- NASA ASRS reports (ACN number, redacted, 200K+ records, queryable by callback ID)
- Flying Magazine / AOPA Pilot articles (issue date + page, or stable URL)
- Podcast episodes (show + episode number + timestamp range for citing a moment)
- Court opinions outside NTSB Board orders (FAA enforcement appeals to the federal circuit -- Pittsburgh Press v. FAA etc.)

For most of these, the dotted-slug "outside-in" hierarchy works, but the ADR doesn't say *who decides* the slug shape per corpus. A POH section like "Section 2 - Limitations" is presented as `pohs.c172s.skyhawk.section-2.vne` -- but POH "Section 2" is a manufacturer convention; Cirrus uses different section names; older POHs predate the GAMA standard format and have only chapters. The NTSB report numbering scheme isn't section-shaped at all -- it's `WPR23LA123` (region + year + class + sequence). Forcing it into `ntsb.reports.WPR23LA123` is fine, but then "the location within the document" segment becomes meaningless because an NTSB report is monolithic.

**Why it matters:** if the slug shape is allowed to vary per corpus (which it has to), then the dotted shape is just a convention, not a grammar. The ADR currently presents it as a grammar (with rules about hyphens, dots, outside-in), and that grammar is going to be broken by every third corpus we add.

**What to do:**

- Either explicitly demote the dotted shape to "convention -- each corpus's WP defines its own slug rules in the registry" and stop treating dot/hyphen/outside-in as universal, or
- Adopt an explicit per-corpus structured form: `corpus:doc:locator` where `locator` is opaque to the parser and only the registry resolves it. Treat it like URN structure (`urn:nbn:de:101-X`) where nothing past the second segment is parseable without the resolver.

The current "dotted segments outside-in" grammar will produce a code-rot conversation every time we add a corpus.

### C2. The identifier *is* the version pin, but the ADR wants it both ways

§1.3 says edition is "optional" and unpinned identifiers resolve to "current." This is the line that causes pain:

> Lessons MAY use unpinned identifiers when referring to "the rule" abstractly without quoting text.

A 2026 lesson without a pin, rendered in 2030 against the 2030 registry, silently changes meaning. The validator's `version-may-be-stale` warning fires only on *pinned* identifiers (per §1.3). Unpinned identifiers don't trip the warning because by definition they always resolve to current. So the lesson author who didn't pin gets no warning that they should re-verify; the lesson just drifts.

The `references_pinned_to: 2026` lesson-level frontmatter shortcut (§3.3) helps, but it's optional and lessons that omit it have unpinned identifiers that drift forever.

**Why it matters:** the entire annual-rollover workflow in §5 depends on lessons being pinned. If pinning is optional, the rollover machinery only works for the disciplined subset of lessons. The ADR should either:

- Make pinning **mandatory at author time** -- every identifier has an `@edition` or the validator rejects, OR
- Make pinning **automatic at first-write time** -- the validator stamps `@<current>` on any unpinned identifier the first time `bun run check` sees it, and the lesson then carries the pin permanently in the body, OR
- Make `references_pinned_to` mandatory frontmatter on every lesson, with the validator filling it in if missing.

"Lessons MAY pin" is a shortcut to "most lessons won't pin" because the path of least resistance is no pin. Then we are right back where we started: a 2030 reader sees a 2026 lesson that points at amended text and quietly says wrong things.

### C3. The `..` range and `!` exclusion microsyntax will not survive

§1.4 invents a microsyntax inside the URL portion of a Markdown link:

```text
regs.cfr-14.91.103..91.107,!91.105
regs.cfr-14.91.103,91.105..91.107
regs.cfr-14.91.103..91.107@2026
```

Problems:

- **Markdown link URLs are URL-shaped.** Most Markdown renderers (GitHub, GitLab, eCFR's own viewer if you ever paste a lesson there, anything that uses CommonMark + autolink) will refuse `regs.cfr-14.91.103..91.107` as a URL (no scheme), or worse, will URL-encode the comma and bang in a way that breaks our parser.
- **Copy-paste between lessons.** An author copies a paragraph with `[The trio @list](regs.cfr-14.91.167..91.171)` from one lesson into another. Looks fine. But the *next* author thinks `..` is a typo and edits it to `-`, or to a single dot, or to a comma, and now it's silently three different things in three places.
- **RAG / search snippets.** When a snippet of lesson body shows up in a RAG citation footnote, the URL portion is what gets shown to a downstream system. `regs.cfr-14.91.167..91.171,!91.169` is illegible to a human reader; comparing two snippets with different exclusions is illegible to a diff tool.
- **Render-mode brittleness.** Audio TTS, screen readers, and voice-only assistants will read "regs dot cfr dash fourteen dot ninety one dot one zero three dot dot ninety one dot one zero seven comma bang ninety one dot one zero five" -- which is not a citation a listener can follow. The `@list` substitution token saves the *output*, but the *input* still has to be parseable.

**Why it matters:** the multi-reference syntax is an ergonomic nice-to-have. Pushing it into the URL portion of a Markdown link makes the URL non-URL, and that contradicts the ADR's own claim that "the scheme must work in plain Markdown."

**What to do:**

- Move multi-reference grouping out of the URL and into the renderer. Authors write multiple separate links, possibly inside a single span, and a renderer pass coalesces adjacent same-corpus citations:

  ```markdown
  The IFR fuel and alternate trio at [§91.167](regs.cfr-14.91.167), [§91.169](regs.cfr-14.91.169), [§91.171](regs.cfr-14.91.171).
  ```

  The renderer can detect adjacency and emit "§91.167-91.171" automatically. No microsyntax in the URL.

- Or, if grouping at the URL level is non-negotiable, use a real URL scheme (`airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/103-107?exclude=105&edition=2026`). At least it's URL-shaped, parseable, and survives copy-paste.

The current `..` and `!` design fails the "plain Markdown" goal it sets for itself.

### C4. The four-tier storage model needs a fifth tier (or the indexed/generated split is wrong)

The ADR splits "indexed" (DB rows from derivatives, owned by corpus) and "generated" (platform computations, continuously rebuilt). This is right as far as it goes, but it misses a tier:

- **Embeddings, full-text indexes, RAG vectors, knowledge-graph edges.** These are derived from the *indexed* tier (the structured derivative DB rows) but they are themselves indexed artifacts that the platform queries at render time, not "generated platform artifacts" in the same sense as a leaderboard.

Concretely: a vector index over the CFR derivative text is owned by which tier? It's not the corpus's responsibility (corpus owns its derivative + the row-shaped indexed mirror). It's not "generated" in the user-interaction sense. It sits between.

Also: the ADR describes "generated" as "continuously rebuilt as users interact." That's true for mastery scores and recommendations. It's *not* true for embeddings, which are computed once per derivative version and persist until re-ingestion. Same physical class of artifact (Postgres rows / pgvector), different lifecycle.

**Why it matters:** if we ship four tiers and then in 2027 add semantic search, we'll either jam embeddings into "indexed" (and break that tier's "owned by the corpus" rule) or into "generated" (and break that tier's "continuously rebuilt" rule). Either way the model is wrong.

**What to do:** rename the tiers around lifecycle, not source:

| Tier        | Lifecycle                                    | Owner           |
| ----------- | -------------------------------------------- | --------------- |
| Source      | External; we don't write                     | Outside         |
| Derivative  | Rebuilt when source changes                  | Corpus pipeline |
| Indexed     | Rebuilt when derivative changes              | Corpus pipeline |
| Computed    | Rebuilt on schema/algorithm change           | Platform        |
| Generated   | Rebuilt continuously from user interaction   | Platform        |

That's five tiers. Or fold "computed" and "generated" into one if you prefer four, but say "computed/generated" and admit there are two sub-lifecycles inside it.

### C5. The validator-before-registry phasing has no answer for lessons authored in the gap

§8 has Phase 1 (validator) before Phase 2 (initial registry) before Phase 3 (renderer). The ADR says Phase 1 "rejects malformed identifiers before any registry exists" -- but §1.5 says "every identifier must resolve (the registry has an entry)." If Phase 1 lands before Phase 2, the validator either:

- Rejects everything (registry is empty -> no identifier resolves -> all lessons fail validation), or
- Allows everything (no registry -> nothing to check against -> validator is a no-op until Phase 2), or
- Has a bypass mode that allows unresolved identifiers with a warning.

The ADR doesn't say which. And the same problem hits Phase 2 -> Phase 3: lessons can pass validation against the partial registry but the renderer doesn't exist yet, so what do existing lesson pages render? Plain Markdown with raw `regs.cfr-14.91.103` URLs, which the existing lesson tooling will render as broken links.

**Why it matters:** the user has authored Week 1 capstones already, today, with eCFR URLs in plain text. Migration of those is "Done in a single mechanical PR after Phase 1 lands" (§9). But Phase 1 is just the validator. There's no renderer until Phase 3. So between Phase 1 and Phase 3 the lessons are migrated to identifier syntax that doesn't render anywhere yet -- the lessons appear to readers (the user, agents, anyone reading the repo) as broken Markdown.

**What to do:**

- Either reverse the phase order (renderer before validator before mass-migration), or
- Make Phase 1's validator emit warnings, not errors, until Phase 2 has the registry populated, or
- Add an explicit "fallback-to-eCFR-URL" rule: any identifier whose resolution fails falls through to a hardcoded mapping (`regs.cfr-14.91.103` -> `https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-91/subpart-B/section-91.103`). This makes the identifier syntax work *before* the registry exists, because the URL shape is computable from the slug for the regs corpus.

The phasing as written has a 2-3 phase gap where lessons are in a broken state. That should not survive review.

## Significant concerns

### S1. The "current" semantics for ACs that change continuously is hand-waved

§9 says "ACs that are continuously-revised through Change documents (handled per-corpus in registry edition entries; ACs use the change-N convention from ADR 019 §1.2)." That's a forward-reference to a half-defined convention. AC 61-65 is a real example: it has Revision J, and Revision J has had Change 1, Change 2 issued against it. The ADR shows `ac.61-65.j.change-2` but doesn't say:

- What does unpinned `ac.61-65` resolve to? Latest revision? Latest revision-plus-changes? The current text as eCFR/FAA shows it?
- If a lesson cites `ac.61-65.j` and Change 3 is issued, does the lesson now drift? Change 3 modifies the J text; is the J pin still meaningful?
- When the FAA issues Revision K, does `ac.61-65` (unpinned) jump to K, breaking lessons that referenced J-specific paragraphs?

This is the *same* problem as C2 but for ACs specifically, and it's worse because AC change history is more granular than CFR amendment cycles.

### S2. Cross-corpus cross-references have no first-class representation

§7 says lessons cite multiple corpora and "these are independent identifiers; the lesson body weaves them with prose." That's fine for prose flow. But a real workflow that the platform will want to support:

- "Show me every lesson that cites *both* §91.103 *and* the Walker letter." -- this needs a query that knows about cross-corpus reference clusters, not just individual references.
- "When the Walker letter is superseded by a newer Chief Counsel interpretation, find every lesson whose §91.103 + Walker pairing is now stale." -- this is a multi-edge graph query.
- "Render the bibliography grouped by corpus." -- requires the bibliography parser to know corpus, not just identifier.

The ADR has no schema for cross-corpus relationships and no query model. It treats references as a flat list. That works for v1 but the user's reviewer charter explicitly asked about "cross-reference graph queries 'what lessons cite both 91.103 and 91.169?'" which the current model doesn't address.

### S3. The substitution token vocabulary is closed and small

§3.1 lists six tokens (`@short`, `@formal`, `@title`, `@cite`, `@list`, `@as-of`). That's it. But the user already authored a capstone oral that quotes regulations inline ("the IFR fuel and alternate trio") and might plausibly want:

- `@text` -- inline the current full text of the section (for short sections)
- `@text-paragraph` -- inline just paragraph (b)
- `@quote` -- block-quoted full text with citation
- `@summary` -- the canonical_short summary (one sentence) the registry can carry
- `@plain-reading` -- a registry-stored "plain reading" gloss
- `@last-amended` -- the date the section last changed
- `@deeplink` -- the URL only, for "see also" formatting
- `@chapter`, `@subpart`, `@part` -- containing-element titles, for breadcrumb rendering
- `@all` -- everything (for a debug or audit view)

The ADR lists six tokens and says nothing about how the vocabulary grows. Token addition is going to be a constant pressure as authors discover they want one more thing. Either the ADR commits to "the token set is open and registered in `libs/constants/src/sources/tokens.ts`" with an extension procedure, or the renderer has to ship every author request as code.

### S4. Internationalization is mentioned and dismissed

The reviewer charter explicitly asks about ICAO, EASA, foreign reg sets, translations. The ADR does not address them. The corpus prefix model `regs.cfr-14.91.103` is FAA-implicit. For ICAO the natural shape is `icao.annex-6.part-i.4.3.1` which fits the dotted shape, but:

- Same regulation in two languages: `icao.annex-6.part-i.4.3.1@en-2024` vs `icao.annex-6.part-i.4.3.1@fr-2024`. Is language part of edition? Of identifier? Of registry metadata? The ADR doesn't say.
- Mixed-corpus lesson that cites both 14 CFR 91.103 and ICAO Annex 2, 3.1.7 -- both about preflight responsibility, different agencies. Currently both expressible as `regs.cfr-14.91.103` and `icao.annex-2.3.1.7`, but does the renderer know they're the "same kind of thing" for "see also" purposes? Not from the identifier shape alone.
- Translated FAA documents (the FAA publishes some material in Spanish): `regs.cfr-14.91.103@2026-es`? Or a different corpus? Not addressed.

### S5. Lesson-body extraction for bibliography assumes one canonical parser

§3.3 says "the parser walks the body, extracts every identifier-shaped URL, and builds the bibliography." This assumes:

- One parser exists and runs.
- The parser sees the lesson in a parseable state.
- Identifier-shaped URLs are the only references.

What about:

- References inside `<details>` blocks, inside HTML embeds, inside MDX expressions, inside Svelte components in `apps/study/`? Different rendering paths, different parsers, different extraction rules.
- References in a quiz answer explanation (`covers_regulations` style frontmatter on a card vs. inline in the explanation body)?
- References in a scenario fixture file (TypeScript, not Markdown)?
- References in a code comment or test fixture?
- References pasted into a PR description, into an issue, into a Slack message?

The "body is the source of truth, parser builds the bibliography" rule works only inside Markdown lessons. Anywhere else, the registry needs a programmatic API. The ADR doesn't expose one.

### S6. Aliases are tracked but the lesson-side warning is weak

§6 says when a section is renumbered the validator emits an info notice; when renumbered across sections, a warning. But the alias is attached to the *Edition* not to the identifier. So a lesson written against `regs.cfr-14.91.103.b.2` in 2026 still resolves in 2030 even though (b)(2) became (c)(2) -- via the alias chain.

Several issues:

- Alias chains compound. (b)(2) -> (c)(2) in 2027 -> (d)(2) in 2029. The 2026 lesson resolves through two hops. The notice is "an alias was applied" but doesn't show the chain.
- Alias chains break if any link is missing. If we ingest 2026, skip 2027 (gap), then 2028 -- the chain has a hole. The lesson doesn't resolve. Or it does but the resolution is wrong.
- The author of the 2026 lesson never knows their reference was aliased unless they re-verify (and that workflow lives in §5 which only triggers for human-flagged hash changes, not for alias changes).
- Aliases are one-to-one in the schema. Real renumbering can be many-to-one (paragraphs (b) and (c) merge into new (b)) or one-to-many (paragraph (a) splits into new (a) and (b)). One-to-many is invertible at write time but ambiguous at read time; many-to-one is the opposite.

### S7. The registry-as-resolver assumption breaks when the registry is unavailable

§2 makes the registry a code module (`libs/constants/src/sources/`) and says it's queried at render time. Good for build-time validation. But the ADR also says (§1.1, §3.3) that the resolver looks up `live_url`, `derivative_path`, `indexed_table` -- these point to runtime state (a DB row, a file path), not constants. So the registry is *also* a runtime entity.

The user asked about: "registry is unavailable at render time, registry has stale data, registry diverges from indexed DB, registry is queried by external tools." None of these are addressed. Examples:

- Build of `apps/study/` succeeds because validation against the constants-registry passes. At runtime the indexed DB is a different shape (schema migration, partial backfill). The renderer queries indexed and gets `null` -- what's the fallback?
- An external tool (a script that exports a study guide as PDF) wants the registry. Does it import the TypeScript constants? Query a JSON dump? Hit a /api/registry endpoint? None specified.
- Two deployment environments (dev / prod) have different indexed-DB states. The constants are the same but the runtime data isn't. A lesson rendered in dev shows current text; in prod it shows last-week's text. Reproducible? Discoverable?

### S8. Print/export modes for substitution tokens are not specified

The renderer is the substitution engine. So:

- Print export: does `[@cite as of @as-of](regs.cfr-14.91.103@2026)` render as "§91.103 Preflight action as of 2026" with a footnote citation? Or as a hyperlink (which is meaningless on paper)? Or as a footnote with the URL?
- Audio TTS: `[@cite](regs.cfr-14.91.103)` -- does the TTS read "section 91.103, Preflight action" or "regs dot cfr dash 14 dot 91 dot 103"? The ADR doesn't say.
- Plain-text export: does the URL portion get stripped, kept, footnoted?
- RSS / email digest: same question.
- Slack unfurl: when a lesson URL is shared in Slack, the unfurl preview parses what?
- Embedded in another lesson via include: do tokens re-substitute in the host lesson's context, or are they pre-substituted at include time?

The ADR specifies the render-time model for one rendering surface (`apps/study/`). Every other render surface has unspecified behavior, and "we'll figure it out per-surface" is the corner the user is asking us to avoid.

## Minor concerns / nits

### N1. `8083-25c` (lowercase) vs `8083-25C` (uppercase) inconsistency

The ADR uses `handbooks.phak.8083-25c.12.3` (lowercase c) in §1.2 but ADR 018 / STORAGE.md use `8083-25C` (uppercase C) for the same thing. Pick one. FAA documents capitalize the revision letter; the slug should match for grep-ability.

### N2. The `interp.ntsb.administrator-v-merrell.ea-5310` form mixes case-name and order-number

`interp.ntsb.administrator-v-lobeiko` for one, `interp.ntsb.administrator-v-merrell.ea-5310` for the other (with disambiguation suffix). What's the rule? When does the disambiguation get added? Author judgment? Always? Only on collision?

### N3. `aim.glossary.pic` -- is `pic` the entry's slug or the entry's term?

The Pilot/Controller Glossary contains "Pilot in Command" not "PIC." If the slug is `pic`, that's an ad-hoc shortening. If the slug is `pilot-in-command`, the ADR's example is wrong. Either way, glossary entries deserve their own corpus prefix because the AIM glossary is shared with the Pilot/Controller Glossary which is published separately.

### N4. `pohs.c172s.skyhawk` has redundancy

`c172s` and `skyhawk` are the same airplane. Either drop "skyhawk" (the model code is enough) or drop "c172s" (the marketing name is the human-readable form). Both creates two ways to say the same thing -> aliases needed -> ambiguity at search time.

### N5. The `@list` token is the only one whose output depends on multi-reference parsing

`@short` substitutes one field. `@cite` substitutes two. `@list` requires the renderer to know it's iterating a multi-reference identifier, normalize the list, and emit comma-and-dash output. This is a different category of token. Either rename it (`@enum`, `@multi`) or document explicitly that multi-reference identifiers behave differently in token substitution.

### N6. The `last_verified` lesson frontmatter and `last_verified` registry field have the same name with different meanings

Lesson `last_verified: 2026-04-27` means "an author audited this lesson against the registry at this date." Registry `last_verified: <date>` means "an engineer audited this registry entry's metadata at this date." Same name, different audit trails. Rename one.

### N7. Validator output format is not specified

§1.5 lists the rules but not the output. Is it Biome diagnostic format? A JSON report? A CLI table? Inline annotations? This affects which CI integrations are possible.

### N8. "Author rarely has to type the canonical title" -- but they have to type the canonical slug

Substituting `@cite` rather than typing `§91.103 Preflight action` saves typing the *title*. But the author still types `regs.cfr-14.91.103` -- which is harder to remember than `§91.103`. The ergonomic win for human authors is small. Make sure the authoring tooling (IDE plugin? hangar UI?) lets authors paste a CFR section number and have the slug filled in, otherwise typing the slug is the bottleneck.

### N9. Acceptance criteria are missing

ADR 018 has a checklist of acceptance criteria. ADR 019 doesn't. Add one. (LFS line in `.gitattributes`-equivalent: `libs/constants/src/sources/registry.ts` exists; STORAGE.md updated to four tiers; etc.)

## Cases the ADR doesn't address

A list of plausible-but-unaddressed scenarios. Some are duplicates of concerns above; called out here for completeness so the user can scan and triage.

1. **A regulation is renumbered between Parts** (rule moved from 91.103 to 91.105). §6 says this gets a warning, but doesn't specify whether the alias resolves silently or breaks the lesson.
2. **A lesson cites a paragraph that splits in a future edition** -- (b)(2) becomes (b)(2)(i) and (b)(2)(ii). One-to-many alias. What does the lesson resolve to?
3. **A lesson cites paragraph (b)(2) and (b)(3) and they merge** into a new (b)(2). Many-to-one alias. The lesson now has two references that resolve to the same target -- de-dupe? Keep both pointing to the same thing? Flag?
4. **A regulation is *deleted*** (sunset clause, redesignated to nothing). The lesson references something that no longer exists. Resolver returns what?
5. **A regulation is *reserved***. (Reserved sections are common -- 91.149, 91.151 reserved, etc.) Does a reference to a reserved section pass validation? Should it?
6. **A pinned reference's edition doesn't exist yet in the registry** -- lesson is authored ahead of ingestion. `@2027` validates against an empty 2027 registry. Build fails or warns?
7. **Two ingestions race** -- an annual and a mid-year correction publish in the same week. Registry has both partial. Validator state is non-deterministic.
8. **Partial ingestion** -- ingestion stops halfway. Registry has Part 91 entries but not Part 61. Lessons that cite 61.x fail validation; lessons that cite 91.x succeed. Build is half-broken.
9. **Multi-reference range crossing pinned editions** -- `regs.cfr-14.91.103..91.107@2026` -- but in 2026 §91.106 was reserved, so the range has a gap. Resolver returns 4 entries instead of 5? Errors?
10. **Lesson moved between repos** (FIRC migration into airboss) -- identifiers stay the same, but the registry import path differs. Are identifiers globally unique across all consumer repos?
11. **A user-generated card cites a regulation** (Abby creates a personal flashcard). Does the user-card system use identifier syntax? If so, who validates? If not, why does only platform content get the validation benefit?
12. **A scenario fixture file (`*.fixture.ts`) needs to reference a regulation** for a test. TypeScript, not Markdown. Same identifier? Different syntax? Imported from `@ab/constants`?
13. **An audio episode transcript references a regulation by spoken name** ("ninety-one one-oh-three"). The transcript indexer can't match that to `regs.cfr-14.91.103` without an alias rule. Spoken-form aliases are not in the registry schema.
14. **A search query "which lessons mention fuel reserves"** -- "fuel reserves" is not a regulation; it's a topic. The identifier system addresses references, not topics. Topic-based search is unaddressed.
15. **An embedding-based "lessons related to this concept"** -- requires vector indexes that the four-tier model doesn't quite have a tier for (see C4).
16. **Mass re-compute when an AC is revised** -- `ac.61-65.j` -> `ac.61-65.k` is a corpus event. How are dependent lessons identified? Reverse index from registry to lessons. Not specified.
17. **Exporting a complete academic-style bibliography** -- IEEE / Chicago / APA citation format from `canonical_formal`. Needs a fielded export, not just a flat list.
18. **Regulation-diff alerts to subscribers** -- "tell me when 91.103 changes" -- pub/sub on registry changes. Not specified.
19. **Change-history visualizations across editions** -- reading multiple `Edition` records and rendering a diff. Schema supports it; UX is undefined.
20. **A cited regulation doesn't have a stable URL** (some Chief Counsel letters live only on PDF mirrors that come and go). What's `live_url` set to? `null`? An archive.org URL?
21. **A POH from a manufacturer that ships in a non-standard format** (Cessna 1960s-era POHs are a few stapled pages). The dotted-slug `pohs.c172.1965.section-???` doesn't fit. Per-airframe convention drift.
22. **NTSB redaction or release-with-redactions** -- a lesson cites an NTSB report that gets re-released with new redactions. Is that a new edition or an update to the existing one?
23. **A lesson rendered as a share-card preview (1200x630 social image)** -- substitution tokens render to plaintext for OpenGraph metadata, but `@cite` could overflow. Truncation rule unspecified.
24. **A lesson rendered as a tooltip (hover preview, 200 char max)** -- which substitution token? Truncate where?
25. **An RSS feed of new lesson content** -- references render to what? Plain text? Hyperlinked HTML? Both?
26. **A Slack unfurl of a lesson URL** -- the unfurl bot pulls the lesson, sees identifier-shaped URLs, doesn't understand them, shows them raw. Author embarrassment.
27. **A lesson included via transclusion in another lesson** -- the included lesson's references are now in two contexts. Substitute once at transclusion time, or at host-render time?
28. **Plain-text export to a clipboard** -- when a user copies a lesson body, do identifier URLs survive (broken in any external paste target) or get rewritten to live URLs (lossy on round-trip back into the repo)?
29. **A lesson rendered to PDF for offline study** -- hyperlinks survive in PDF; identifier-URLs don't resolve when the PDF is opened outside our renderer. Auto-rewrite to live URLs at PDF-export time?
30. **A reviewer opens a lesson in GitHub's Markdown viewer** (not our renderer). Identifier-URLs render as broken links. Does the lesson still read as a draft? At what point in the workflow does this matter?
31. **The registry diverges between two branches** -- a feature branch adds a registry entry; main doesn't have it yet. Lessons on the feature branch validate; on main they don't. Cross-branch PR review breaks.
32. **A registry entry is deleted** (mistake, or de-precation) -- lessons that referenced it are now broken. No safety check on registry deletion.
33. **A lesson is deleted** but the registry still has the entry -- orphan. No GC story.
34. **`references_pinned_to: 2026` is set** but a body identifier is *also* pinned `@2027`. Conflict. Lesson-level says 2026; reference-level says 2027. Which wins? §3.3 doesn't say.
35. **Authoring two lessons that both cite `regs.cfr-14.91.103@2026`** in the same week, and one of them is wrong about what it says. The validator can't catch wrongness; only resolution. Cross-lesson consistency check is undefined.

## Recommendations

In priority order. Each should be resolved before the ADR moves to "accepted."

**Priority 1 -- before approval:**

1. **Resolve C2 (mandatory pinning).** Pick one of the three options and commit. The annual rollover machinery is broken without this.
2. **Resolve C3 (multi-reference syntax).** Move grouping out of the URL or adopt a real URL scheme. The current `..` and `!` will not survive contact with reality.
3. **Resolve C5 (phasing gap).** Reverse phase order, or specify validator's grace mode, or specify URL fallback. Lessons cannot live in a "broken until Phase 3" state.
4. **Resolve C1 (slug grammar vs convention).** Demote the dotted-slug grammar to a per-corpus convention defined in the registry, and stop describing it as a universal grammar.

**Priority 2 -- before Phase 1 builds:**

5. **Address C4 (storage tier model).** Either five tiers or admit two sub-lifecycles inside "generated."
6. **Resolve S2 (cross-corpus relationships) and S5 (parser scope).** Add a programmatic registry API (`@ab/sources` lib) with a typed query model.
7. **Address S4 (i18n).** At minimum, document the scheme for ICAO and translated documents even if implementation is deferred.
8. **Resolve S6 (alias chains).** Specify chain depth, gap handling, many-to-one and one-to-many alias semantics.
9. **Resolve S8 (render-mode coverage).** Specify behavior for at least: print, audio TTS, screen reader, plain-text export, RSS, share-card. "Per-surface TBD" is not acceptable.

**Priority 3 -- before Phase 3 builds:**

10. **Resolve S1 (AC change/revision semantics).** Concrete rules for `ac.<doc>` unpinned, `ac.<doc>.<rev>` pinned, change-N treatment.
11. **Resolve S3 (token vocabulary extension).** Open the token set and document the registration path.
12. **Resolve S7 (registry availability).** Specify dev-vs-prod consistency, external-tool API, fallback behavior.
13. **Walk every item under "Cases the ADR doesn't address"** and either resolve in the ADR, plan in a follow-on WP with a trigger, defer with an explicit trigger, or drop. Per CLAUDE.md "no undecided considerations for future work," none of these can survive as TBD.

**Priority 4 -- nice-to-have:**

14. Address all minor concerns / nits.
15. Add acceptance criteria checklist matching ADR 018's pattern.
16. Add a worked example end-to-end: lesson body -> validator -> registry -> renderer -> three render modes (web, print, audio). Forces every implicit assumption to surface.

---

The bones of the ADR are good. The four moves -- identifier as contract, registry as resolver, body as source of truth, four-tier storage -- are correct. But the *details* of the identifier shape, the version model, the multi-reference syntax, and the phasing have enough corner-painting risk that shipping as written would force a v2 within 18 months of the first ingestion landing. That's exactly the outcome the user asked us to prevent.

Reworking §1, §1.4, §3.1, §8 against this review's recommendations should get the ADR to a place where the v2 risk is measured in the small, not the large.
