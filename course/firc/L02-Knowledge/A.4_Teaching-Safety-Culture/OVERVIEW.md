## FAA Description (verbatim from AC 61-83K Appendix A)

### A.4 Main Section

Flight instructors are highly trained individuals. They hold vast amounts of knowledge gleaned during their own training and through their own experiences. Having a well-founded basis of knowledge is critical to being an effective instructor. However, if that highly trained and knowledgeable instructor is unable to convey their knowledge to a student, then the instructor is ineffective as a flight instructor, regardless of knowledge and expertise. Teaching is an art. It requires not just an understanding of the topic, but an understanding of how people think and learn. It requires a certain amount of psychological understanding. Virtually all instructors took coursework in the fundamentals of instruction, which offers a basic theoretical framework for the teaching and learning processes. As presented in the FIRC setting, this topic is expected to build upon and expand those skills necessary to effectively transfer knowledge to pilots. Incumbent upon this is building the instructor's ability to instill a culture of safety in the minds of their students and to spread and support the safety mindset throughout their aviation workplace on an everyday basis.

---

## What the FAA Wants Taught

The AC is explicit that knowledge alone does not make an effective flight instructor. A highly knowledgeable instructor who cannot transfer knowledge effectively is still ineffective.

The non-negotiable teaching points are:

- teaching is more than content mastery;
- instructors need an understanding of how people think and learn;
- instruction requires psychological understanding, not just technical expertise;
- the FIRC should build on FOI knowledge rather than simply repeat it;
- instructors must be able to instill a culture of safety in students;
- that safety mindset should also extend into the instructor's everyday aviation workplace.

## What This Topic Is Really About

This topic is about turning expertise into learning and turning learning into safety behavior.

The FAA is pushing CFIs beyond:

- "I know the material";
- toward:
- "I can diagnose what this student is actually understanding, adapt how I teach it, and build a durable safety mindset rather than a superficial performance."

## Teaching Emphasis for CFIs

CFIs should be able to:

- distinguish student repetition from real understanding;
- adapt their teaching to different student personalities, fears, and overconfidence patterns;
- explain not just what to do, but why it matters for safe decision-making;
- reinforce safety mindset as part of normal instruction, not as a separate sermon;
- contribute to a workplace culture where safety habits are modeled consistently.

The safety-culture side of this topic matters just as much as the teaching-method side:

- a CFI teaches safety through daily norms, language, standards, and choices;
- not only through formal risk-management lessons.
