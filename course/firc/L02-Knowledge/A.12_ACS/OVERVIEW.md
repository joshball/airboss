## FAA Description (verbatim from AC 61-83K Appendix A)

### A.12 Main Section

Since September 2011, the FAA has worked with aviation community stakeholders to improve standards, guidance, and test development practices for airman certification. The ACS framework integrates and aligns standards, guidance, and testing for airman certification. The ACS is built on today's PTS, which establishes the skill performance metrics for each flight proficiency element listed in the regulations for an airman certificate or rating. The ACS replaces the PTS when it is published. The ACS does not change or lengthen the check ride (practical test). Rather, its integrated approach will provide better guidance to applicants, instructors, and evaluators during each phase of the airman certification process. The ACS also enables the FAA to keep airman testing fully aligned with regulations, performance standards for knowledge and skill, and guidance materials such as handbooks. Additionally, the FAA is working with industry to refine ACS and its implementation plan. The rollout date for releasing the initial ACSs for Private Pilot-Airplane (PAR) and Instrument Rating Airplane (IRA) was June 15, 2016. On May 31, 2024, the FAA adopted a final rule to amend 14 CFR part 61 by incorporating by reference (IBR) the ACS and PTS. This final rule incorporates thirty pilot and flight instructor ACS and PTS in part 61 by reference through a centralized IBR section in new SS 61.14. To ensure that the implementation of the ACS is successful, stakeholders need to understand how to utilize the ACS for training and testing. Flight instructors play a leading role in this regard. For this reason, FIRC providers also need to support the introduction of ACS and the 2024 ACS IBR by providing ACS training to their attendees. Therefore, FIRC providers should develop a comprehensive lesson that facilitates the understanding, implementation, and utilization of ACS. You can find more information concerning ACS at https://www.faa.gov/training_testing/testing/acs/.

## What the FAA Wants Taught

The AC treats ACS as the framework instructors should understand and actively use, not just recognize by name.

The non-negotiable teaching points are:

- ACS integrates standards, guidance, and testing for airman certification;
- ACS replaces the PTS when an ACS is published for that certificate or rating;
- ACS does not change or lengthen the practical test;
- ACS is intended to keep training, evaluation, regulations, and guidance aligned;
- the 2024 incorporation-by-reference change in `§ 61.14` matters because current ACS and PTS documents now sit inside the regulatory structure differently;
- CFIs play a leading role in successful ACS implementation;
- FIRC providers should offer comprehensive ACS training.

The AC's 2011 and 2016 implementation history is useful context, but the durable lesson is how instructors use the ACS today.

## What This Topic Is Really About

This topic is about teaching and evaluating from an integrated standard instead of from habit, folklore, or isolated maneuvers.

The FAA wants instructors to use the ACS as a working tool for:

- lesson design;
- training emphasis;
- risk-management coaching;
- debriefing;
- and remediation.

## Teaching Emphasis for CFIs

CFIs should be able to explain and demonstrate:

- what changed when the FAA moved from PTS-only thinking to the ACS framework;
- how to identify which current ACS or PTS applies to a given certificate or rating;
- how to use the ACS for more than checkride preparation;
- how ACS-based training can connect knowledge, risk management, and skill instead of treating them as separate silos;
- why the 2024 `§ 61.14` incorporation-by-reference structure makes source currency especially important.

This topic works best when it feels operational:

- not "here is a standards document";
- but "here is how a good instructor uses the standards document before, during, and after training."
