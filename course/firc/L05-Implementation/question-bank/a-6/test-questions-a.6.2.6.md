# A.6.2.6 -- Aircraft Lights

---

### Q-A6-86 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-26)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, AC-1

AC 61-83K identifies proper use of aircraft lights as a teaching strategy for runway incursion avoidance. Why are aircraft lights relevant to preventing runway incursions?

A) Lights make the aircraft look more professional
B) Lights signal the aircraft's intentions and presence to other pilots, ground vehicles, and ATC, increasing the aircraft's conspicuity on the airport surface where visual detection is the primary means of conflict avoidance
C) Lights are only required for night operations
D) The FAA requires all lights to be on at all times

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K includes aircraft lights as a teaching strategy because lights communicate presence and intent on the airport surface. A pilot with the landing light on during taxi is more visible to other aircraft, ground vehicles, and tower controllers. Lights also signal intentions -- for example, turning on the landing light signals imminent runway entry or takeoff. This visibility reduces the chance that another aircraft or vehicle will conflict with the pilot's position, directly preventing incursions.

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, OD-1

Your student always turns on the landing light only after receiving takeoff clearance and entering the runway. Per AC 61-83K's guidance on aircraft lights, what should you teach?

A) This is correct -- the landing light should only be on during takeoff and landing
B) The landing light should be turned on before entering the runway to increase conspicuity during taxi to position, signaling to other aircraft and vehicles that the pilot intends to use the runway
C) Landing lights should never be used on the ground to avoid blinding other pilots
D) Light usage is a personal preference with no standard practice

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K emphasizes proper use of aircraft lights to signal intentions. Turning on the landing light before entering the runway increases the aircraft's visibility to others on the surface and signals the pilot's intention to enter the runway environment. Waiting until after takeoff clearance means the aircraft is less visible during the critical transition from taxiway to runway. Option C overstates the blinding concern. Option D ignores the AC's guidance.

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, RM-1

Which of the following are proper uses of aircraft lights during ground operations per AC 61-83K's teaching strategy? (Select all that apply)

A) Taxi light on whenever the aircraft is moving on the ground
B) Landing light on when crossing or entering any runway
C) Strobe lights on when entering an active runway for takeoff

**Correct:** A, B, C, E

Navigation lights from start to shutdown (E) ensure the aircraft is always visible. Turning all lights off (D) directly contradicts the AC's guidance on using lights to prevent incursions.

### Q-A6-91 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-28)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, OD-2

A student asks: "If I turn on my landing light while taxiing, won't it blind oncoming pilots?" How should the CFI address this concern?

A) Agree and recommend keeping the landing light off during taxi
B) Explain that the taxi light is the appropriate light during normal taxi, but the landing light should be turned on when approaching or crossing a runway -- the brief increase in visibility at a high-risk location outweighs the momentary brightness, and pilots are trained to expect landing lights near runways
C) Tell the student that blinding other pilots is never a concern
D) Suggest the student only use lights at night

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This is a legitimate concern that deserves a nuanced answer. The taxi light (lower intensity, appropriate beam angle) is designed for normal taxi movement. The landing light (higher intensity) is appropriate when entering the runway environment because that's when maximum conspicuity is most critical. AC 61-83K's guidance on proper use of aircraft lights implies using the right light for the right phase. The CFI should teach the distinction between taxi and runway light configurations.

A) Fly the flight -- the taxi light isn't required by regulation for day VFR
B) Assess the risk: while the taxi light may not be legally required for the flight, its absence reduces the aircraft's conspicuity during taxi at a busy airport, and the CFI should discuss this tradeoff with the student -- can the landing light substitute during taxi? Should the flight be relocated to a less busy airport? Is this an acceptable risk given the traffic environment?

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This scenario teaches risk assessment through the lens of AC 61-83K's lights guidance. The taxi light may not be a legal requirement for the flight, but AC 61-83K identifies lights as a safety tool for incursion prevention. The CFI should use this as a teaching moment: what is the risk of reduced conspicuity at a busy towered airport? What mitigations are available? This decision-making process is more valuable than a simple go/no-go answer. Option D directly contradicts the AC's guidance on daytime light usage.
