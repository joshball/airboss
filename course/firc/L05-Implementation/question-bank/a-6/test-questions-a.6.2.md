### Q-A6-26 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-11)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, ES-1

AC 61-83K Section A.6.2 identifies six teaching strategy categories for runway incursion avoidance. A CFI wants to restructure their curriculum to cover all six. Which list correctly represents these categories?

A) Weather briefing, flight planning, radio procedures, taxiing, takeoff, landing
B) Proficiency, planning, procedures, sterile cockpit, situational awareness, aircraft lights
C) Knowledge testing, simulator training, flight review, checkride prep, solo endorsement, cross-country planning
D) ATC communication, airport signage, runway markings, NOTAMs, TFRs, airspace classification

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K Section A.6.2 identifies six teaching strategy areas: proficiency (A.6.2.1), planning (A.6.2.2), procedures (A.6.2.3), sterile cockpit (A.6.2.4), situational awareness (A.6.2.5), and aircraft lights (A.6.2.6). These represent a comprehensive framework that moves from foundational skills to specific operational practices. The other options mix valid topics but don't match the AC's structure.

### Q-A6-27 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-11)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, CJ-2

A CFI notices a private pilot student struggles with taxi operations despite good knowledge test scores. Which teaching strategy category from AC 61-83K should the CFI prioritize first?

A) Aircraft lights -- the student probably isn't using lights correctly
B) Sterile cockpit -- the student is likely distracted
C) Proficiency -- the student needs more practice in the actual taxi environment to build skill, not just knowledge
D) Planning -- the student needs to study airport diagrams more carefully

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** When a student demonstrates knowledge but not skill, the gap is proficiency -- the ability to apply knowledge in real-time. AC 61-83K lists proficiency (A.6.2.1) as the first teaching strategy because training and continuing education are the foundation. A student who knows the right answers but can't execute during taxi needs more supervised practice, not more study. The other strategies may also help, but proficiency addresses the core knowledge-to-skill gap.

### Q-A6-28 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-12)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, RM-1

A CFI is conducting a biennial flight review with a pilot who hasn't flown into a towered airport in two years. Which teaching strategy categories from AC 61-83K are most critical to address during this review? (Select all that apply)

A) Proficiency -- recurrent training at towered airports
B) Planning -- preflight preparation for towered airport operations
C) Procedures -- progressive taxi and standard operating procedures
D) Aircraft lights -- reviewing proper light usage
E) All six categories equally, regardless of the pilot's specific gaps

**Correct:** A, B, C, D
**Explanation:** For a pilot who hasn't operated at towered airports in two years, all four listed strategies are directly relevant: proficiency is degraded from disuse, planning skills for towered operations need refreshing, procedures may have been forgotten, and light usage protocols may not be current. While all six categories matter, these four address the specific gap. Option E ignores the principle of targeted instruction based on identified deficiencies.

### Q-A6-29 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-12)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-2, OD-1

During a FIRC presentation, a CFI asks: "How do the six teaching strategies relate to the three major runway incursion causes?" Which answer best describes the relationship?

A) Each teaching strategy maps to exactly one incursion cause
B) The teaching strategies are preventive -- they address the underlying pilot behaviors (proficiency, planning, procedures, attention, awareness, visibility) that lead to the three incursion causes (ATC non-compliance, airport unfamiliarity, SOP failure)
C) The teaching strategies only address surface deviations, not airborne deviations
D) The three causes and six strategies are unrelated sections of the AC

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The three major incursion causes (Section A.6.1) identify what goes wrong. The six teaching strategies (Section A.6.2) address how to prevent those failures through training. Proficiency and planning prevent airport unfamiliarity. Procedures and sterile cockpit prevent SOP failures. SA and planning prevent ATC non-compliance. The strategies are the CFI's toolkit for addressing the root causes. They are interconnected, not one-to-one mapped.

### Q-A6-30 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-13)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, AC-1

A newly certificated CFI asks you how to incorporate runway incursion avoidance into their primary training curriculum. Which approach best reflects the AC 61-83K teaching strategy framework?

A) Dedicate a single ground lesson to runway incursions before the first solo
B) Integrate all six strategy areas progressively throughout training -- introduce proficiency and planning early, add procedures and sterile cockpit as taxi operations begin, and reinforce SA and lights as the student advances
C) Only address runway incursions when training at towered airports
D) Provide a handout on runway incursion statistics and let the student self-study

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The six teaching strategies in AC 61-83K are not a single lesson -- they represent an integrated approach that should be woven throughout training. Proficiency builds over time, planning is taught from the first flight, procedures are reinforced at every taxi, sterile cockpit applies to every critical phase, SA is an ongoing skill, and lights are part of every preflight. Option A is too compressed. Option C ignores non-towered incursion risks. Option D is passive.

### Q-A6-31 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-13)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, OD-2

A CFI conducting a flight review identifies that a pilot routinely taxis with the landing light off, doesn't brief the taxi route before moving, and engages in casual conversation during taxi. How many of the six AC 61-83K teaching strategy areas does this pilot's behavior potentially violate?

A) Two -- planning and aircraft lights
B) Three -- planning, sterile cockpit, and aircraft lights
C) Four -- planning, procedures, sterile cockpit, and aircraft lights
D) One -- sterile cockpit covers all of these behaviors

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** The pilot's behaviors map to four teaching strategy areas: (1) no taxi route briefing = planning failure (A.6.2.2), (2) casual conversation during taxi = sterile cockpit violation (A.6.2.4), (3) landing light off during taxi = improper aircraft light use (A.6.2.6), and (4) the combination suggests a lack of standard operating procedures for ground operations = procedures gap (A.6.2.3). This analysis helps the CFI structure corrective training across multiple areas rather than treating symptoms in isolation.

### Q-A6-32 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-14)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, ES-1

Which teaching strategy from AC 61-83K serves as the foundation that enables all the others?

A) Sterile cockpit -- without focus, nothing else works
B) Aircraft lights -- visibility prevents all incursions
C) Proficiency -- training and continuing education build the skills that make planning, procedures, SA, sterile cockpit, and proper light use possible
D) Procedures -- following checklists eliminates all errors

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K lists proficiency first (A.6.2.1) because training and continuing education are the foundation. A pilot who lacks proficiency cannot effectively plan, follow procedures, maintain SA, or apply sterile cockpit discipline. Proficiency is the enabling condition -- without it, the other strategies remain abstract knowledge rather than practiced skills. The other options each identify important strategies but none serve as the foundation for all others.

### Q-A6-33 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-14)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, CJ-1

A CFI wants to design a single training flight that exercises as many AC 61-83K teaching strategies as possible. Which flight plan elements would accomplish this? (Select all that apply)

A) Fly to an unfamiliar towered airport (proficiency + planning)
B) Require the student to brief the taxi route from the airport diagram before calling ground (planning)
C) Have the student request progressive taxi instructions (procedures)
D) Enforce no non-essential conversation below 1,000 feet AGL and during taxi (sterile cockpit)
E) Verify proper light configuration at each phase of flight (aircraft lights)

**Correct:** A, B, C, D, E
**Explanation:** All five elements map to specific AC 61-83K teaching strategies, and together they exercise all six areas (SA is inherently exercised through the combination of unfamiliar environment and active cockpit management). This integrated approach is more effective than addressing each strategy in isolation. A well-designed single flight can reinforce multiple strategies simultaneously.

### Q-A6-35 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-15)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, AC-2

A CFI notices that their flight school teaches planning, procedures, and sterile cockpit well, but rarely addresses proficiency, SA, or aircraft lights in a structured way. What is the risk of this incomplete coverage?

A) No significant risk -- the three strategies they teach well are sufficient
B) Students develop procedural habits without the foundational proficiency to adapt when procedures don't fit the situation, the SA to recognize when something is wrong, or the visibility practices to help others see them
C) The missing strategies are only relevant to commercial pilots
D) Aircraft lights are optional, so only proficiency and SA matter

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The six strategies are complementary, not redundant. Procedures without proficiency become rote. Procedures without SA mean pilots can't recognize when a procedure isn't working. Procedures without proper light use reduce the pilot's visibility to others on the surface. A training program that covers only half the strategies leaves systematic gaps that increase incursion risk. All six areas from AC 61-83K must be addressed for comprehensive prevention.
