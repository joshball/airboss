### Q-A6-56 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-10)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, RM-1

AC 61-83K identifies progressive taxi instructions as an excellent mitigation strategy for runway incursions. A student asks: "What exactly are progressive taxi instructions?" Which answer is most accurate?

A) Step-by-step taxi directions given by ground control, one segment at a time, so the pilot only needs to process the next turn rather than the entire route at once
B) A written taxi route provided by the FBO before engine start
C) An automated GPS guidance system for airport taxiways
D) Instructions to taxi progressively faster to clear the movement area quickly

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** Progressive taxi instructions are ATC-provided step-by-step directions where the controller guides the pilot one segment at a time. This reduces memory load and the chance of confusion, especially at unfamiliar or complex airports. AC 61-83K highlights progressive taxi as an excellent mitigation strategy because it directly addresses the "airport familiarity" and "ATC compliance" incursion causes. The pilot can focus on the immediate next action rather than memorizing an entire complex route.

### Q-A6-57 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-10)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, OD-1

Your student receives a complex taxi clearance at an unfamiliar Class C airport: "Taxi to runway 28L via Alpha, Bravo, cross runway 10, hold short runway 28R at Charlie." The student reads it back correctly but looks uncertain. What procedure should the CFI recommend?

A) Proceed -- the readback was correct, so the student understands
B) Request progressive taxi instructions from ground control, explaining that the pilot is unfamiliar with the airport
C) Ask another pilot on frequency for directions
D) Taxi slowly and look for signs

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A correct readback doesn't guarantee the pilot can execute the route. AC 61-83K identifies progressive taxi instructions as an excellent strategy precisely for this situation -- an unfamiliar airport with a complex taxi route. Requesting progressive taxi is not a sign of weakness; it's a proper procedure that reduces incursion risk. Ground controllers expect and accommodate these requests. Option A equates verbal recall with navigational competence. Options C and D are unreliable.

### Q-A6-58 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-11)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, AC-1

AC 61-83K states GA pilots should use proper procedures during taxi operations. Which of the following are proper taxi procedures a CFI should teach? (Select all that apply)

A) Complete readback of all taxi instructions, especially hold short instructions
B) Having the airport diagram visible and following along during taxi
C) Confirming position at each taxiway intersection
D) Taxiing at high speed to minimize time on active taxiways
E) Stopping and querying ATC if uncertain about position or clearance

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Proper taxi procedures include full readbacks (A), active use of the airport diagram (B), position confirmation at intersections (C), and stopping to verify when uncertain (E). These directly address the incursion causes of ATC non-compliance and airport unfamiliarity. Taxiing at high speed (D) is dangerous -- it reduces reaction time and makes it harder to identify intersections, signs, and markings. Speed does not reduce risk; deliberate, systematic procedures do.

### Q-A6-59 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-11)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, OD-1

A student asks: "When should I request progressive taxi instructions instead of accepting a standard taxi clearance?" Which CFI guidance best reflects AC 61-83K?

A) Only when the controller offers them
B) Whenever the pilot is at an unfamiliar airport, receives a complex clearance they're not confident they can follow, or feels uncertain about any portion of the taxi route
C) Never -- it slows down ATC operations and other pilots will judge you
D) Only during the practical test to show the examiner you know the procedure exists

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K describes progressive taxi as an "excellent mitigation strategy." The CFI should teach students that requesting progressive taxi is appropriate whenever the pilot's confidence in executing the taxi route is low -- unfamiliar airports, complex routes, low visibility, or any uncertainty. There is no stigma attached to the request. ATC would rather provide progressive instructions than deal with a runway incursion. Options C and D discourage a valuable safety tool.

### Q-A6-60 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-12)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, CJ-1

A CFI observes that a student consistently fails to read back hold short instructions, instead responding with "Roger" or "Wilco." The student's other ATC communications are fine. What procedural gap does this indicate?

A) The student has adequate radio skills -- "Roger" is an acceptable acknowledgment
B) The student has a specific procedural gap: hold short instructions require a full readback including the runway and the pilot's callsign, and "Roger" is not an acceptable response to a hold short instruction
C) The student should practice radio communication more but it's not a safety issue
D) This is an ATC problem -- the controller should correct the readback

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Hold short instructions are safety-critical and require a specific full readback per regulatory requirements. Responding with "Roger" to a hold short instruction is a procedural failure that ATC should catch, but the CFI must not rely on ATC as a safety net. This gap directly increases runway incursion risk because without a proper readback, neither the pilot nor ATC can confirm the instruction was received and understood correctly. AC 61-83K emphasizes proper procedures as a teaching strategy.

**Competencies:** ES-2, OD-2

You are mentoring a new CFI who says: "I don't bother teaching progressive taxi because my students should learn to handle complex taxi clearances on their own." How should you respond?

C) Suggest the new CFI only teach progressive taxi for the checkride
D) Tell the new CFI to let students figure out taxi procedures through experience

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The new CFI's attitude treats progressive taxi as a weakness rather than a tool. AC 61-83K explicitly identifies progressive taxi as an "excellent mitigation strategy." A well-trained pilot knows when standard procedures are sufficient and when additional support is needed. Teaching students to request progressive taxi when appropriate builds judgment and procedural competence, not dependence. Options A and D risk normalizing operating without adequate support.

### Q-A6-62 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-13)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, RM-1

During taxi at a towered airport, your student approaches a runway hold short line. ATC has not issued a hold short instruction -- the taxi clearance was "taxi to runway 27 via Alpha." Runway 9/27 intersects taxiway Alpha. What is the correct procedure?

A) Cross the runway -- there was no hold short instruction
B) Stop at the hold short line and confirm with ATC whether to cross or hold short, because the taxi clearance to "runway 27 via Alpha" does not authorize crossing any runways
C) Cross the runway quickly since it's the runway they're assigned to
D) Wait for other aircraft to cross first, then follow them

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A taxi clearance to a runway does not authorize crossing any runway unless ATC specifically states "cross runway [number]." The student must stop at every hold short line and either have prior crossing clearance or request it. This is a fundamental procedure that AC 61-83K emphasizes. Option A incorrectly interprets the clearance. Option C makes a dangerous assumption. Option D relies on other pilots' judgment rather than proper procedure.

Proper readbacks (B) address ATC compliance. Active diagram use (C) supports position awareness. Standard call-outs (D) create procedural barriers against inadvertent incursions. Option E is not just irrelevant but promotes exactly the wrong behavior -- rushing through taxi operations increases incursion risk.

### Q-A6-64 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-14)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, OD-2

A student taxiing at their home airport receives "taxi to runway 14 via Echo, Delta." The student has been to this airport hundreds of times and begins taxiing from memory without referencing the airport diagram. The taxi route is correct. Should the CFI intervene?

A) No -- the student knows the airport and the route is correct
B) Yes -- the CFI should reinforce that referencing the airport diagram is a standard procedure regardless of familiarity, because the habit of using the diagram is what protects the pilot at unfamiliar airports
C) No -- requiring the diagram at the home airport is excessive
D) Only if the student makes a wrong turn

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K emphasizes proper procedures for all taxi operations. The purpose of referencing the airport diagram is not to navigate an unfamiliar airport -- it's to build a habit that becomes automatic. A pilot who skips the diagram at familiar airports will also skip it at unfamiliar ones when they're under higher workload. The CFI should reinforce consistent procedural discipline. The fact that the route was correct this time doesn't validate skipping the procedure.

### Q-A6-65 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-14)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, OD-1

A flight school is experiencing an uptick in taxi incidents. The chief instructor reviews training records and finds that procedural training (readbacks, progressive taxi, diagram use) is taught during primary training but not reinforced during advanced training or flight reviews. What is the likely cause of the incidents?

A) The students are not paying attention during primary training
B) Procedural skills degrade without reinforcement -- pilots who learned procedures early but never practiced them in advanced contexts revert to informal habits, increasing incursion risk
C) The airport has changed its layout, confusing the pilots
D) The incidents are random and unrelated to training

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K links procedures to regulatory compliance and ongoing practice. Procedures taught once and never reinforced become abstract knowledge rather than active habits. The flight school's gap is in continuing education -- procedures must be practiced and assessed at every training level, not just during primary instruction. This directly mirrors AC 61-83K's proficiency guidance: training alone isn't enough; continuing education maintains the skill.
