# A.6.1 -- Lesson Development (Pilot Deviations)

---

### Q-A6-16 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-06)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, AC-1

A CFI is developing a lesson on runway incursion prevention. According to AC 61-83K, which three major areas lead to runway incursions?

A) Poor weather, mechanical failure, and inadequate fuel planning
B) Failure to comply with ATC instructions, lack of airport familiarity, and failure to adhere to SOPs
C) Student inexperience, complex airport layouts, and nighttime operations
D) Radio equipment failures, controller errors, and poor runway markings

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K Section A.6.1 identifies three major areas leading to runway incursions: (1) failure to comply with ATC instructions, (2) lack of airport familiarity, and (3) failure to adhere to SOPs. These are all pilot-side factors that CFIs can directly address through training. The other options list contributing factors but do not reflect the AC's three primary categories.

### Q-A6-17 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-06)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, OD-1

A student taxiing at an unfamiliar towered airport misses a turn onto taxiway Delta and continues straight on taxiway Alpha toward an active runway. Which of the three major runway incursion areas from AC 61-83K does this most directly represent?

A) Failure to comply with ATC instructions
B) Lack of airport familiarity
C) Failure to adhere to SOPs
D) Loss of situational awareness caused by radio congestion

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Missing a turn at an unfamiliar airport is a textbook example of "lack of airport familiarity" -- one of the three major areas identified in AC 61-83K. The student didn't disobey ATC; they simply didn't recognize the taxiway intersection. This is exactly the type of error that preflight planning with airport diagrams and progressive taxi instructions are designed to prevent. While SA loss (D) is involved, the root cause is airport unfamiliarity.

C) Poor taxi procedures
D) Equipment malfunctions
E) Distractions in the cockpit
F) Loss of situational awareness
G) Improper use of aircraft lights

**Correct:** A, B, C, E, F, G
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K Section A.6.1 lists six additional challenges: lack of proficiency, inadequate preflight planning, poor taxi procedures, distractions, loss of situational awareness, and improper use of aircraft lights. ### Q-A6-19 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-07)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, OD-2

A CFI reviews incident reports and finds that a local pilot received a runway incursion deviation after crossing runway 14 without clearance at a towered airport they fly out of daily. Which major incursion area from AC 61-83K is this least likely to represent?

A) Failure to comply with ATC instructions
B) Lack of airport familiarity
C) Failure to adhere to SOPs
D) Any of the three could apply equally

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The pilot flies out of this airport daily, so lack of airport familiarity is the least likely root cause. This is more likely a failure to comply with ATC instructions (missed or misunderstood a hold short instruction) or failure to adhere to SOPs (skipped the checklist step of confirming clearance before crossing). This distinction matters for CFIs: the same event at the same airport can have different root causes, and the corrective training depends on identifying the right one.

### Q-A6-20 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-08)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, ES-2

You are teaching a private pilot who dismisses runway incursion training: "I only fly out of non-towered airports, so ATC compliance doesn't apply to me." How should you address this?

A) Agree that runway incursions are primarily a towered airport concern
B) Explain that runway incursions can occur at non-towered airports too, and that two of the three major areas -- airport familiarity and SOP adherence -- apply regardless of ATC presence
C) Skip the runway incursion content and focus on airborne deviations instead
D) Suggest the pilot avoid towered airports entirely

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** While "failure to comply with ATC instructions" requires ATC, the other two major areas -- lack of airport familiarity and failure to adhere to SOPs -- are relevant at any airport. Runway incursions at non-towered airports occur when pilots fail to visually clear the runway, misidentify runways, or don't follow standard traffic procedures. The CFI must correct this misconception rather than reinforce it.

### Q-A6-21 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-08)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, CJ-2

A student reads back an ATC clearance: "Hold short of runway 27, taxiway Bravo." ATC does not correct the readback. Later, ATC amends: "Cross runway 27 at Bravo, contact ground on the other side." The student, confused, stops short and asks what to do. What lesson development point does this scenario best illustrate?

A) ATC is unreliable and students should rely on their own judgment
B) The importance of teaching students to listen for amended clearances and to confirm any change that conflicts with a prior instruction before acting
C) Students should never hold short unless they see the hold short markings
D) This scenario is too unusual to warrant dedicated lesson time

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This scenario illustrates a real-world challenge: clearances change. The student correctly held short per the original clearance, but the amended instruction to cross created confusion. CFIs should develop lessons that teach students to expect and handle clearance amendments -- confirming the new instruction, acknowledging the change, and acting on the most recent clearance. This directly addresses "failure to comply with ATC instructions" as a runway incursion cause.

### Q-A6-22 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-09)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, OD-1

When developing a lesson on runway incursion prevention, which sequencing of the three major incursion areas provides the best instructional flow?

A) Start with airport familiarity (prevention through planning), then SOPs (prevention through procedures), then ATC compliance (prevention through communication)
B) Address all three simultaneously since they are equally important
C) Only cover ATC compliance since it's the most common cause
D) Start with SOPs, skip airport familiarity for experienced pilots, and end with ATC compliance

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** A logical lesson flow builds from preparation to execution to communication. Airport familiarity comes first because preflight planning is the foundation for preventing incursions. SOPs provide the procedural framework for safe taxi operations. ATC compliance builds on both -- a pilot who knows the airport and follows SOPs is better equipped to correctly process and comply with ATC instructions. Option B lacks structure. Options C and D omit critical content.

### Q-A6-23 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-09)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, AC-2

A CFI is building a scenario-based lesson for runway incursion prevention. Which scenario best addresses all three major incursion areas identified in AC 61-83K simultaneously?

A) A cross-country to an unfamiliar towered airport where the student must plan the arrival, brief the taxi route from the airport diagram, follow ATC taxi instructions, and use standard operating procedures during taxi
B) A local flight where the student practices radio communications
C) A ground lesson reviewing airport signage and markings from a textbook
D) A simulator session focused exclusively on ATC communication failures

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** Option A integrates all three major areas: unfamiliar airport (airport familiarity), ATC taxi instructions (ATC compliance), and standard procedures during taxi (SOP adherence). Scenario-based instruction that combines all three areas is more effective than addressing each in isolation. Options B, C, and D each address only one factor and miss the integrated nature of real-world runway incursion prevention.

### Q-A6-24 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-10)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-1, CJ-1

A CFI is analyzing why a student crossed a hold short line without clearance. Which questions best help determine which of the three major incursion areas was the root cause? (Select all that apply)

A) "Did you hear ATC issue a hold short instruction?" (ATC compliance)
B) "Had you reviewed the airport diagram before taxiing?" (airport familiarity)
C) "What is your normal procedure when approaching a hold short line?" (SOP adherence)
D) "What type of aircraft were you flying?" (aircraft factor)
E) "Were you aware of which runway you were approaching?" (airport familiarity)

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Questions A and B/E map directly to the first two major incursion areas (ATC compliance and airport familiarity). Question C addresses the third area (SOP adherence). These diagnostic questions help the CFI determine whether the root cause was a communication failure, a navigation/awareness failure, or a procedural failure -- which determines the corrective training. Aircraft type (D) is not one of the three major areas.

### Q-A6-25 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-10)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-1, OD-2

A CFI discovers that multiple students consistently fail to hold short when instructed by ATC, despite understanding the phraseology. Review of training records shows they all received the same ground instruction. What does this pattern suggest about the CFI's lesson development?

A) The students are not paying attention -- this is a motivation problem, not a training problem
B) The ground instruction likely addresses knowledge (phraseology) but not skill application (recognizing and responding to hold short instructions in real-time under workload)
C) The CFI should stop teaching at towered airports
D) This is an ATC problem -- the controller's delivery must be unclear

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A pattern across multiple students points to a systemic instructional gap, not individual student failure. If students understand the phraseology but fail to comply in practice, the lesson likely emphasizes knowledge without building the skill of real-time compliance under cockpit workload. The CFI should revise the lesson to include scenario-based practice, progressive complexity, and real-time decision-making. This is exactly the kind of lesson development insight AC 61-83K encourages.
