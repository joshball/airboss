### Q-A5-27 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-11)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-2, OD-1

What is the primary purpose of the General Aviation Joint Steering Committee (GAJSC)?

A) To enforce FAA regulations on GA operators
B) To partner with industry to identify risk, pinpoint trends through root cause analysis, and develop safety strategies
C) To investigate GA accidents on behalf of the NTSB
D) To certify flight training curricula

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K describes the GAJSC as a partnership between the FAA and industry that uses data to identify risk, pinpoint trends through root cause analysis (RCA), and develop safety strategies. The GAJSC is not a regulatory enforcement body (A), does not investigate accidents (C -- that is the NTSB), and does not certify curricula (D). CFIs should understand the GAJSC because its findings directly inform what safety topics should receive emphasis in training.

### Q-A5-28 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-11)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, PS-1

What analytical method does the GAJSC use to move beyond identifying that accidents happen to understanding why they happen?

A) Statistical trend analysis only
B) Root Cause Analysis (RCA) -- a systematic process that examines accident data to identify the underlying causes, not just the proximate events
C) Pilot survey data collection
D) Aircraft type certification review

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K specifically identifies Root Cause Analysis (RCA) as the method the FAA and industry use to pinpoint trends. RCA goes beyond surface-level analysis (e.g., "the pilot lost control") to identify the underlying factors (e.g., inadequate stall training, failure to recognize compounding risks, lack of proficiency in crosswind operations). This deeper analysis produces actionable safety strategies rather than generic recommendations. CFIs benefit from understanding RCA because the resulting safety interventions often translate directly into training priorities.

### Q-A5-29 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-12)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-2

A GAJSC safety enhancement identifies that a significant number of LOC accidents occur during the transition from base to final in the traffic pattern. As a CFI, how should you apply this data-driven finding to your training?

A) Brief the finding to students during ground school and move on
B) Design specific training exercises that expose students to the base-to-final turn at various speeds, bank angles, and wind conditions, emphasizing the aerodynamic factors that make this turn particularly hazardous when performed with excessive bank or inadequate airspeed
C) Avoid teaching base-to-final turns and use only straight-in approaches
D) Post the GAJSC finding on the flight school bulletin board

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The value of GAJSC root cause analysis is that it identifies specific scenarios where accidents cluster. When data shows LOC during base-to-final turns, the CFI's response should be targeted training that addresses the specific aerodynamic hazards: the tendency to steepen the bank to avoid overshooting, the reduced stall speed margin in a banked turn, and the uncoordinated flight that can develop during the correction. Simply briefing (A) or posting (D) the finding does not change pilot behavior. Avoiding the maneuver (C) creates the exact skill gap the data is trying to close.

### Q-A5-30 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-12)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** PS-2, RM-1

What are the key elements of the GAJSC's approach to GA safety as described in AC 61-83K? (Select all that apply)

A) Using data to identify risk
B) Mandating specific training requirements for all pilots
C) Pinpointing trends through Root Cause Analysis
D) Developing safety strategies through industry partnership
E) Conducting ramp inspections of GA aircraft

**Correct:** A, C, D
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K describes three key elements of the GAJSC approach: using data to identify risk, pinpointing trends through Root Cause Analysis, and developing safety strategies through partnership with industry groups. The GAJSC does not mandate training requirements (B) -- that is a regulatory function -- and does not conduct ramp inspections (E) -- that is an FAA inspector function. The partnership model is significant because it brings industry expertise and buy-in to safety strategies, making them more likely to be adopted and effective.

Why is the partnership model between the FAA and industry (through the GAJSC) significant for flight instructors?

C) CFIs are required to implement all GAJSC recommendations
D) The GAJSC certifies flight instructor renewal courses

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The GAJSC's data-driven approach produces specific, actionable safety enhancements that often identify exactly the training gaps contributing to accidents. When root cause analysis reveals that a particular phase of flight or decision-making failure is driving accidents, the resulting safety strategies frequently include training-based interventions. CFIs who understand and apply these findings are directly contributing to the FAA's safety goals. The GAJSC does not provide legal protection (A), its recommendations are not mandatory (C), and it does not certify courses (D).

### Q-A5-32 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-13)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, RM-3

A GAJSC root cause analysis of recent stall/spin accidents reveals that a common factor is the pilot's failure to recognize the onset of an aerodynamic stall in a distraction-heavy environment (such as dealing with an abnormal situation while in the pattern). How should a CFI apply this finding?

A) Teach stall recovery only in dedicated stall practice lessons at altitude
B) Incorporate stall recognition training in realistic, distraction-rich scenarios -- such as simulated equipment failures in the pattern -- so students learn to recognize stall cues even when their attention is divided
C) Increase the number of stall practice hours in the syllabus
D) Teach students to always maintain extra airspeed in the pattern to prevent stalls

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Root cause analysis goes beyond "pilots stall" to identify why -- in this case, distraction degrades stall recognition. The training response must address the root cause, not just the symptom. Practicing stalls only at altitude in clean configurations (A) doesn't prepare pilots for stalls that occur during divided attention in the pattern. More practice hours (C) of the same type of training won't close the gap. Extra airspeed (D) is a band-aid that doesn't build recognition skill. The GAJSC finding calls for training that replicates the conditions under which the failure occurs.

### Q-A5-33 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-14)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1, OD-1

What distinguishes the GAJSC's approach from traditional accident investigation?

A) The GAJSC investigates accidents while the NTSB handles incidents
B) The GAJSC focuses on identifying systemic risk patterns across many accidents through data analysis, while traditional investigation focuses on determining the cause of individual events
C) The GAJSC has enforcement authority that the NTSB lacks
D) There is no meaningful difference

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Traditional accident investigation (NTSB) determines what caused a specific accident. The GAJSC takes a broader view, using data from many accidents to identify patterns, systemic risk factors, and trends through root cause analysis. This systemic approach reveals that individual accidents are often symptoms of the same underlying causes -- training gaps, decision-making failures, or procedural deficiencies. For CFIs, the systemic view is more useful than individual accident reports because it identifies what to emphasize across all students, not just what went wrong in one case.

### Q-A5-34 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-14)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-1, CJ-2

A GAJSC safety analysis of GA fatal accidents identifies that a disproportionate number involve pilots who had not completed a flight review in the previous 36 months (exceeding the 24-month requirement). As a CFI, which conclusions are supported by this data? (Select all that apply)

A) Flight reviews are an important safety intervention point and skill degradation between reviews contributes to accidents
B) The 24-month flight review interval should be shortened to 12 months by regulation
C) CFIs conducting flight reviews should thoroughly assess proficiency, not just check a box
D) Voluntary proficiency programs like the Wings program may provide additional safety benefit between required flight reviews
E) Pilots who miss flight reviews should have their certificates suspended

**Correct:** A, C, D
**Explanation:** The data supports that flight reviews serve as safety intervention points (A) -- when pilots skip them, skill degradation goes undetected. This reinforces that CFIs must conduct meaningful, thorough reviews (C) rather than treating them as administrative events. The data also supports the value of voluntary proficiency programs (D) that provide additional touchpoints between required reviews. The data does not support a specific interval change (B) -- that is a regulatory decision requiring broader analysis. Certificate suspension (E) is an enforcement matter beyond the scope of this finding.

### Q-A5-35 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-15)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, PS-2

You attend a safety seminar where GAJSC data is presented showing that a significant percentage of fatal approach-and-landing accidents involve unstabilized approaches where the pilot continued to landing instead of executing a go-around. As a CFI, what is the most effective way to apply this data?

A) Add a briefing about stabilized approaches to the ground school curriculum
B) Establish firm stabilized approach criteria with every student and practice go-arounds initiated from unstabilized approaches at various points in the pattern, building the decision-making habit and motor skills to go around without hesitation
C) Instruct students to always fly wider patterns to give themselves more time to stabilize
D) Share the statistics with students to motivate them to fly better approaches

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** GAJSC data shows the failure point is not lack of knowledge about stabilized approaches -- it is the decision and execution of the go-around when the approach is not stabilized. The training intervention must target both the decision (recognizing "this approach is not working") and the execution (positive power, pitch, configuration, climb). Briefings (A) and statistics (D) inform but do not build the habit. Wider patterns (C) change the geometry without addressing the core failure. The CFI must create the training conditions where students practice the actual decision and motor sequence the data shows pilots are failing to execute.

### Q-A5-36 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-15)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, OD-2

What does "data-driven" mean in the context of the GAJSC's approach to GA safety, and why should CFIs care?

A) The GAJSC collects data but lets individual instructors decide what to teach
B) Safety strategies are developed based on analysis of actual accident and incident data rather than assumptions, which means CFIs can prioritize training on the specific scenarios and skill gaps that are actually causing accidents
C) Data-driven means the GAJSC uses computer models to predict future accidents
D) It means the FAA publishes an annual accident statistics report

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** "Data-driven" means that safety strategies are based on what the data shows, not on assumptions about what might be dangerous. For CFIs, this is directly actionable: instead of guessing which skills to emphasize, they can align their training with the specific accident causes the data identifies. When GAJSC data shows that LOC during low-altitude maneuvering is the dominant fatal accident cause, a data-driven CFI allocates proportional training time to LOC prevention, stall awareness, and go-around execution. This targeted approach is more effective than distributing training time equally across all topics.
