# A.7.5 -- CFI WINGS Benefits

Questions covering FIRC WINGS credit, recent experience via WINGS instruction, client pool growth, and AC 61-91.

---

### Q-A7-57 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-29)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-2, RC-1

A CFI completing a FIRC asks whether the course has any connection to the WINGS program. According to AC 61-83K, what is the relationship?

A) FIRCs and WINGS are completely separate programs with no overlap
B) CFIs earn WINGS credit for completing a FIRC
C) FIRC completion automatically advances the CFI to WINGS Master level
D) WINGS credit is only available if the FIRC is conducted in person, not online

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.5, "CFIs earn WINGS credit for FIRC completion." This means the FIRC contributes to the CFI's own WINGS progression, creating a direct link between instructor renewal and the proficiency program. Option A is incorrect -- there is a defined connection. Option C overstates the benefit. Option D adds a delivery-method restriction not found in the AC.

**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.5, the connection between FIRC completion and WINGS credit reflects the FAA's integrated approach to safety promotion. Option C dismisses a deliberate policy choice. Option D adds a restriction not found in the AC.

### Q-A7-59 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-30)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-2, RC-2

A CFI is approaching their renewal deadline and needs to establish recent experience. They haven't given a flight review or practical test recommendation in the past 24 months. According to AC 61-83K A.7.5, how can WINGS instruction help?

A) WINGS instruction cannot be used to establish CFI recent experience
B) CFIs who instruct in the WINGS program can establish recent experience through their WINGS instructional activities
C) WINGS instruction only counts toward recent experience if the CFI also completes a FIRC in the same calendar year
D) Recent experience through WINGS requires at least 50 hours of WINGS instruction per year

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.5, "Those who instruct in WINGS can establish recent experience." This provides an additional pathway for CFIs to maintain currency, particularly valuable for instructors who may not regularly recommend students for practical tests. Option A directly contradicts the AC. Options C and D add restrictions not found in the source material.

### Q-A7-60 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-30)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** PS-2, ES-3

According to AC 61-83K A.7.5, which of the following are benefits that the WINGS program provides specifically to flight instructors? (Select all that apply)

A) CFIs earn WINGS credit for completing a FIRC
B) WINGS instruction can be used to establish CFI recent experience
C) CFIs can increase their client pool through WINGS post-certification proficiency training
D) WINGS participation automatically extends a CFI certificate for an additional 12 months
E) The AC references AC 61-91 for additional guidance on CFI and WINGS integration

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.5, CFIs earn WINGS credit for FIRC completion (A), can establish recent experience through WINGS instruction (B), can grow their client pool via WINGS proficiency training (C), and are directed to AC 61-91 for further guidance (E). Option D is fabricated -- WINGS does not extend CFI certificates; renewal is governed by 14 CFR 61.197.

### Q-A7-61 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-31)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, PS-2

A CFI with a small client base asks how to attract more certificated pilots for ongoing instruction. Based on AC 61-83K A.7.5, what strategy does the AC suggest?

A) Advertise lower prices than competing flight schools
B) Offer WINGS post-certification proficiency training, which gives certificated pilots a structured reason to continue flying with an instructor and can increase the CFI's client pool
C) Only market to student pilots, since certificated pilots don't need instruction
D) Wait for the FAA to assign pilots to local CFIs through the WINGS program

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.5, "CFIs can increase client pool through WINGS post-cert proficiency training." This positions WINGS as a business development tool -- certificated pilots seeking proficiency training become ongoing clients rather than one-time flight review customers. Option A is a generic business tactic unrelated to the AC. Option C ignores the post-certification market. Option D describes a system that does not exist.

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, RC-1

A CFI argues that instructing certificated pilots in WINGS is "not real instruction" because the pilots already have their certificates. Based on AC 61-83K, why is this perspective incorrect?

A) The FAA considers WINGS instruction to be equivalent to primary flight training
B) WINGS post-certification proficiency training is explicitly recognized in AC 61-83K as a legitimate and valuable instructional activity that can grow a CFI's client pool and establish recent experience
C) WINGS instruction is only recognized if the CFI holds a Gold Seal certificate
D) The AC agrees that post-certification instruction is less important than primary training

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.5, WINGS instruction is recognized as a means to establish recent experience and increase client pool, demonstrating that the FAA considers post-certification proficiency training a legitimate and valuable CFI activity. It is not equivalent to primary training (A), does not require Gold Seal status (C), and the AC does not diminish its importance (D).

### Q-A7-65 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-33)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, OD-1

A CFI who primarily instructs student pilots toward private certificates has never considered WINGS instruction. After reading AC 61-83K A.7.5, which insight should change their perspective?

A) WINGS is only relevant to CFIs who work at Part 141 flight schools
B) WINGS post-certification proficiency training represents a second revenue stream -- an opportunity to continue instructing pilots after they earn their certificates, growing the CFI's client base beyond primary students
C) WINGS instruction requires a separate FAA authorization beyond the CFI certificate
D) The CFI should stop primary instruction entirely and focus only on WINGS

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.5, CFIs "can increase client pool through WINGS post-cert proficiency training." For a CFI focused on primary students, this reveals an untapped market of certificated pilots who want structured proficiency training. It doesn't require special authorization (C), isn't limited to Part 141 (A), and shouldn't replace primary instruction (D) -- it supplements it.

### Q-A7-66 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-33)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-2, ES-1

A newly certificated CFI asks how WINGS fits into their long-term career as an instructor. Based on AC 61-83K A.7.5, which answer best captures the career benefits?

A) WINGS has no career relevance -- it's a program for pilots, not instructors
B) WINGS provides multiple career benefits: FIRC completion earns WINGS credit, WINGS instruction establishes recent experience for renewal, and delivering WINGS proficiency training expands the CFI's client base
C) WINGS only benefits CFIs during their first two years of instructing
D) The only career benefit is that WINGS instruction counts toward ATP flight time requirements

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.5, WINGS offers CFIs three distinct career benefits: FIRC-to-WINGS credit, recent experience through instruction, and client pool growth through proficiency training. These benefits are ongoing (not time-limited per option C), apply to instructors specifically (contradicting A), and relate to proficiency and business development (not ATP requirements per D). Together they create a compelling case for active WINGS engagement throughout a CFI's career.
