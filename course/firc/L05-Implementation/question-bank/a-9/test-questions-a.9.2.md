# A.9.2 -- The Flight Review

Questions covering flight review requirements per SS 61.56, proficiency-based evaluation (not a test), 24-month requirement, tailoring to individual pilots, minimum 1 hour flight, Part 91 review, and AELS.

---

### Q-A9-27 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-14)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-2, ES-1

A private pilot tells you they believe a flight review requires "1 hour of ground and 1 hour of flight." What should you clarify about the actual regulatory requirement under 14 CFR 61.56?

A) They are correct -- 1 hour ground and 1 hour flight is the standard
B) The regulation requires a review of the current general operating and flight rules of Part 91 and at least 1 hour of flight training -- there is no minimum ground time specified, though the Part 91 review is required
C) The flight review only requires 1 hour of flight with no ground component
D) The ground and flight portions must each be at least 2 hours

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.2 notes that "many pilots may not remember that SS 61.56(a)(2) also requires that the review must include 'a review of those maneuvers and procedures that, at the discretion of the person giving the review, are necessary for the pilot to demonstrate the safe exercise of the privileges of the pilot certificate.'" The regulation specifies a Part 91 review and minimum 1 hour of flight, but no minimum ground time. Option A is the common misconception the AC specifically addresses. Options C and D are incorrect.

### Q-A9-28 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-14)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-2, CJ-1

According to AC 61-83K, what is the fundamental nature of a flight review?

A) It is a practical test where the pilot must demonstrate proficiency to a pass/fail standard
B) It is a training event in which proficiency is evaluated -- not a test -- and there is no pass or fail
C) It is an FAA checkride administered by a designated CFI
D) It is a standardized evaluation identical for every pilot

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.2 states explicitly: "A flight review is a training event in which proficiency is evaluated. Thus, it is a proficiency-based exercise." It further states that "flight instructors should also understand that the flight review is not to be construed or conducted as a test, that there is no pass or fail, and that it is intended to be a learning experience." Option A contradicts the AC. Option C mischaracterizes the flight review. Option D ignores the requirement to tailor each review to the individual.

### Q-A9-29 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-15)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, ES-1

A pilot comes to you for a flight review. Their logbook shows they completed their last flight review 22 months ago. They ask if they can wait another 2 months since "it's not due yet." What should you advise?

A) They are correct -- they have until the 24th calendar month and should wait
B) While the regulation requires a flight review at least every 24 calendar months, the FAA recommends pilots complete flight reviews as needed rather than only once every 24 months -- waiting until the last moment treats it as a chore rather than a proficiency opportunity
C) They should complete it now because the 24-month period has already started counting down
D) They must complete it within 12 months of the previous review

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.2 states that "the FAA recommends that pilots complete a flight review as needed, not just once every 24 calendar months." Many pilots treat the flight review as a 24-month obligation rather than a proficiency tool. The CFI should encourage a proficiency mindset. Option A reinforces the minimal-compliance attitude the AC warns against. Option C mischaracterizes how the regulatory period works. Option D states an incorrect requirement.

### Q-A9-30 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-15)

**Type:** multiple-select

According to AC 61-83K, why do many flight instructors view the flight review negatively? (Select all that apply)

A) Some flight instructors consider the flight review a chore that they would rather not do
B) Many instructors are unclear on how to properly conduct a flight review
C) Flight instructors rarely encounter many pilots outside of the 24-month flight review requirement
D) The FAA requires too much paperwork for flight reviews
E) Flight reviews don't generate enough revenue for CFIs

Options D and E are not mentioned in the AC and do not reflect the stated concerns.

### Q-A9-31 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-16)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-1, CJ-2

You are conducting a flight review for a commercial pilot with multi-engine and instrument ratings who primarily flies charter operations in turboprops. How should you tailor the review compared to one for a VFR-only private pilot who flies a Cessna 172 locally?

A) Conduct the same standardized review for both pilots since the regulatory requirement is identical
B) Tailor each review to the individual -- the commercial pilot's review should reflect their operating environment, equipment, and the higher-order skills their certificate demands, while the private pilot's review should focus on their typical VFR operations and Part 91 knowledge
C) The commercial pilot doesn't need a flight review since they have recurrent training through their employer
D) Focus the commercial pilot's review entirely on instrument procedures since that is the highest risk

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.2 emphasizes that "each flight review is unique to the skills and experience level of the pilot receiving the review" and that "the instructor should be well versed in how to quickly assess a pilot's skills and experience and to fine-tune the review." A standardized approach fails both pilots. Option A ignores the individualization requirement. Option C confuses employer recurrent training with the Part 61 flight review requirement. Option D narrows the review inappropriately.

### Q-A9-32 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-16)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-2, RM-2

During a flight review, you determine that a pilot lacks proficiency in several areas and would need significantly more than 1 hour of flight training to reach an acceptable level. The pilot says "the regulation only requires 1 hour -- we're done, just sign me off." What should you do?

A) Sign off the flight review since the minimum 1 hour has been met
B) Explain that the 1 hour is a minimum, not a ceiling -- the flight review is a proficiency-based evaluation, and you cannot endorse it until the pilot demonstrates they can safely exercise their PIC privileges
C) Sign off the review but add a note recommending additional training
D) Refuse the signoff and require the pilot to complete a full course of dual instruction before trying again

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.2 states that "each pilot has individual needs that may easily require more time to resolve to satisfactorily complete a flight review." The 1 hour is a regulatory minimum, not the standard for completion. The pilot "should come away knowing something they did not know before, possess a degree of skill they did not have before, and meet proficiency standards required to conduct a safe flight." Option A treats the minimum as the standard. Option C is dishonest. Option D is unnecessarily rigid -- additional training can occur within the flight review itself.

### Q-A9-33 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-17)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-2, ES-1

During a flight review, you discover that a pilot does not meet FAA Aviation English Language Standards (AELS). According to AC 61-83K, what should you do?

A) Continue the flight review normally since AELS is not part of the flight review
B) Identify the AELS deficiency, provide a logbook entry for training received, advise the pilot of your finding, and notify the appropriate FSDO for further action
C) Fail the flight review based on the AELS deficiency
D) Refer the pilot to an English language class and reschedule the review

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.3 states that "when a flight instructor conducts a flight evaluation and/or flight training event, the instructor should identify any pilot that does not demonstrate FAA AELS. In cases like this, the instructor should provide a logbook entry for training received, advise the pilot of the instructor's finding, and notify their appropriate Flight Standards District Office (FSDO) for further action." There is no "fail" in a flight review (Option C), and the response requires FSDO notification, not just a referral (Option D).

### Q-A9-34 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-17)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** ES-1, RC-2

According to AC 61-83K, what should a pilot gain from a properly conducted flight review? (Select all that apply)

A) Knowledge they did not have before the review
B) A degree of skill they did not possess before the review
C) Proficiency standards required to conduct safe flight
D) A guarantee that they will not be involved in an accident for the next 24 months
E) Confidence that they have met the minimum regulatory requirement

**Correct:** A, B, C
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.2 states "the pilot under review/evaluation should come away knowing something they did not know before, possess a degree of skill they did not have before, and meet proficiency standards required to conduct a safe flight." Option D is impossible to guarantee. Option E reflects the minimum-compliance mindset the AC warns against -- the flight review should be about proficiency, not just paperwork.

### Q-A9-35 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-18)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, ES-2

A pilot who holds only a private pilot certificate with an airplane single-engine land rating comes to you for a flight review. They want you to conduct the entire review in their Piper Cub (no electrical system, no radio). How does this affect the Part 91 review portion?

A) The Part 91 review is the same regardless of aircraft since all pilots must know all of Part 91
B) Tailor the Part 91 review to the pilot's operating environment -- focus on the rules relevant to non-radio, non-electric operations while ensuring they understand the broader regulatory framework, such as right-of-way rules, VFR minimums, and airspace entry requirements without a transponder
C) You cannot conduct a flight review in an aircraft without a radio
D) Skip the Part 91 review since it only applies to instrument-rated pilots

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.2 emphasizes that "each flight review is unique to the individual pilot's skills and experience." The Part 91 review should be relevant to how the pilot actually operates while ensuring they understand the rules that affect their safety. A Piper Cub pilot operating from grass strips has different operational considerations than a pilot flying from towered airports. Option A ignores tailoring. Option C is incorrect -- no regulation prohibits flight reviews in non-radio aircraft. Option D is wrong -- Part 91 applies to all pilots.

### Q-A9-36 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-18)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-2, CJ-2

Before conducting a flight review, you learn that the pilot expects a 1-hour flight where you sit quietly while they fly the pattern and do some air work. They say a previous CFI conducted their reviews this way. What is the problem with this expectation?

A) There is no problem as long as the pilot demonstrates safe flight
B) The flight review requires active assessment and instruction by the CFI -- passively riding along while the pilot flies a routine flight does not constitute evaluation of proficiency or review of maneuvers and procedures necessary to demonstrate safe exercise of PIC privileges
C) The problem is only that pattern work is not sufficient -- they should fly a cross-country
D) The CFI should fly from the left seat during the review to maintain authority

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.2 describes the flight review as requiring the instructor to "quickly assess a pilot's skills and experience and to fine-tune the review 'on the fly' to effectively confer new knowledge or to refine skills already possessed." A passive ride-along does not accomplish any of this. The CFI must actively evaluate proficiency, identify weaknesses, and provide instruction. Option A accepts inadequate conduct. Option C focuses on routing rather than the fundamental problem. Option D mischaracterizes the CFI's role.
