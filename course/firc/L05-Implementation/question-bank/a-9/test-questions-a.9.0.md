### Q-A9-01 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-1, CJ-1

A private pilot comes to you for a flight review. They haven't flown in 18 months. During the ground portion, the pilot answers all your questions confidently and correctly. They can cite regulations, describe procedures, and discuss aerodynamics fluently. Their logbook shows 400 hours. Should you proceed to the flight portion with reduced scrutiny?

A) Yes, their strong ground performance indicates they are likely proficient
B) No -- ground knowledge and flight proficiency are separate dimensions. An 18-month gap requires careful flight evaluation regardless of ground performance, because motor skills and situational awareness degrade differently than declarative knowledge
C) It depends on whether they have simulator time during the 18-month gap
D) Flight reviews don't have a ground and flight portion -- the CFI decides the format

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This is a core AC 61-83K teaching point. Knowledge and skill degrade at different rates. A pilot can retain declarative knowledge (regulations, procedures) while losing procedural proficiency (scan, coordination, situational awareness). The CFI who reduces flight scrutiny based on strong ground performance is making a diagnostic error. The flight review must evaluate both dimensions independently. Option A conflates knowledge with proficiency. Option C is a factor but doesn't change the fundamental point. Option D is incorrect -- 14 CFR 61.56 requires at minimum a review of general operating rules and a flight.

### Q-A9-02 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-1

When conducting a flight review, what is the minimum required by 14 CFR 61.56?

A) At least 1 hour of ground instruction and 1 hour of flight instruction
B) A review of the current general operating and flight rules of 14 CFR Part 91 and a minimum of 1 hour of flight training including maneuvers that demonstrate safe PIC operations
C) Completion of a written test and a practical flight test to PTS/ACS standards
D) Whatever the CFI determines is appropriate based on the pilot's experience level

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** 14 CFR 61.56(a) requires a review of Part 91 general operating and flight rules, plus at least 1 hour of flight training that includes maneuvers and procedures the CFI considers necessary to demonstrate the pilot can safely exercise PIC privileges. There is no written test requirement and no minimum ground time specified (though ground review of Part 91 is required). The 1 hour of flight is a minimum -- the CFI should extend it if needed. Option A incorrectly specifies 1 hour of ground. Option C confuses flight reviews with checkrides. Option D omits the regulatory minimums.

### Q-A9-03 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-2

You are conducting an IPC for a pilot who flies regularly but exclusively uses the autopilot for all instrument approaches. They are proficient with automation but struggle when you ask them to hand-fly a precision approach. What should the IPC address?

A) The pilot is proficient since modern IFR flying uses automation extensively
B) Focus the IPC on hand-flying skills that are degraded, while acknowledging the pilot's automation proficiency -- the goal is to ensure the pilot can maintain aircraft control if automation fails
C) Fail the IPC since the pilot cannot hand-fly approaches
D) Recommend the pilot complete instrument training from scratch

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** An effective IPC diagnoses specific weaknesses and addresses them. This pilot has automation proficiency but degraded hand-flying skills -- a common and real-world important gap. The IPC should acknowledge what works, then focus on what doesn't. Automation failure in IMC is a life-threatening scenario if the pilot can't hand-fly. Option A ignores a critical proficiency gap. Option C is premature -- the IPC is training, not just evaluation. Option D is disproportionate to the specific weakness.

### Q-A9-04 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-1, ES-2

A friend who is a certificated pilot asks you to "just sign off a quick flight review" since they fly regularly and just need the paperwork. What should you do?

A) Accommodate the request since you know them and they fly regularly
B) Explain that a flight review is a proficiency evaluation, not paperwork, and conduct it to the full standard regardless of your personal relationship
C) Do a shorter review since you've flown with them before and know their skills
D) Suggest they complete a WINGS phase instead since that's easier

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Professional integrity requires conducting every flight review to the same standard regardless of personal relationships. "Quick signoffs" undermine the purpose of the flight review system and expose both the CFI and the pilot to liability. Even pilots who fly regularly may have blind spots that only a structured review reveals. Option A is the exact ethical failure AC 61-83K warns against. Option C reduces scrutiny based on relationship rather than demonstrated proficiency. Option D uses WINGS as an avoidance tool rather than a proficiency program.

### Q-A9-05 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-03)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-2

During an IPC, the pilot performs all approaches to standard but you observe they consistently fail to properly brief each approach before beginning. What action should you take?

A) Pass the IPC since the approaches were flown to standard
B) Address the approach briefing deficiency as part of the IPC, explaining that consistent briefing is a risk management practice that prevents errors and supports CRM
C) Note it for next time but don't hold up the IPC signoff for a briefing habit
D) Require the pilot to redo all approaches with proper briefings

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** An IPC evaluates more than just stick-and-rudder approach flying. Approach briefings are a critical component of instrument proficiency -- they set expectations, identify threats, and establish go-around criteria. A pilot who skips briefings is operating without a structured framework, even if the outcome was acceptable this time. The CFI should address this as part of the IPC and ensure the pilot understands why briefings matter. Option A overlooks a real deficiency. Option C defers an easy fix. Option D is disproportionate.

### Q-A9-06 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-03)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** ES-1, ES-3

When tailoring a flight review to an individual pilot, which of the following factors should a CFI consider? (Select all that apply)

A) The pilot's certificate level and ratings
B) The type of flying the pilot typically does (local VFR, cross-country IFR, etc.)
C) How long since the pilot's last flight
D) The pilot's age
E) Areas where the pilot self-identifies weakness or concern
F) Recent accident trends relevant to the pilot's type of flying

**Correct:** A, B, C, E, F
**Explanation:** An effective flight review is tailored to the individual. The pilot's certificate and ratings determine what privileges are being reviewed (A). Their typical flying determines relevant scenarios (B). Recency affects expected proficiency (C). Self-identified concerns reveal honest assessment (E). Relevant accident trends inform priority areas (F). Option D -- age -- is not an appropriate factor for tailoring a review. Age-related physiological changes are addressed through the medical certification system, not flight reviews.

### Q-A9-07 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-04)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, ES-1

A newly certificated CFI asks you what the most important thing they can do to improve GA safety is. You know they are eager to start teaching but have limited experience beyond their initial training. What is the best advice?

A) Focus on building flight hours as quickly as possible to gain experience
B) Recognize that as a flight instructor you directly influence the safety culture of every pilot you train -- invest in continuing education so the training you provide is current, thorough, and safety-centered
C) Specialize in instrument instruction since that is the highest-risk area
D) Avoid conducting flight reviews until you have at least 500 hours of instruction given

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K Appendix A.9 states that "flight instructors perform one of the most vital and influential roles in aviation" and that "continuing training provided by FIRCs enables the flight instructor to conduct meaningful IPCs and flight reviews." The CFI's influence on safety culture is the central theme. Option A prioritizes quantity over quality. Option C arbitrarily narrows scope. Option D sets an artificial experience barrier not found in regulations.

**Competencies:** ES-1, RC-2

According to AC 61-83K, which of the following describe the flight instructor's role in GA safety? (Select all that apply)

Option C is too narrow -- the influence extends beyond direct students. Option E ignores the instructor's role in IPCs, flight reviews, and continuing education.

A CFI completes a FIRC and immediately returns to conducting flight reviews using the same procedures and techniques they used before the course. They tell you, "I passed the tests, so I'm good." What concern does this raise?

Option A confuses test performance with competence. Option C arbitrarily limits the scope. Option D is an overreaction -- the CFI may have learned the material but needs encouragement to apply it.

### Q-A9-16 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-08)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-2, CJ-2

A FIRC provider's A.9 module devotes 40 minutes to IPC procedures and 5 minutes to flight reviews. A colleague suggests this is fine because "IPCs are more complex." What is the problem with this allocation?

A) There is no problem -- IPCs are indeed more complex and deserve more time
B) The flight review is the evaluation most GA pilots encounter, and instructors need substantial training on how to conduct effective, individualized reviews -- underweighting this topic leaves instructors unprepared for one of their most common and impactful responsibilities
C) The problem is only that the total time is under 45 minutes
D) Flight reviews should get more time than IPCs because they are required of all pilots

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.2 devotes extensive text to the flight review, emphasizing that "many pilots never encounter a flight instructor except for that every 24 calendar month requirement" and that each review must be tailored to the individual. A 5-minute treatment leaves instructors without the skills to conduct this critical evaluation effectively. Option A ignores the flight review's practical importance. Option C focuses on a total-time technicality rather than content balance. Option D swings too far the other direction without addressing the real issue of adequate coverage for both.
