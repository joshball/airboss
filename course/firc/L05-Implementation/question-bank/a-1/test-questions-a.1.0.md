### Q-A1-01 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-3, RC-4

Your student is planning a cross-country flight using an iPad with ForeFlight. The GPS database expired two weeks ago. The student says "It's fine, the map still shows everything." As the CFI, what is your best response?

A) Allow the flight since ForeFlight is for situational awareness only and doesn't require a current database
B) Explain that expired databases may contain outdated waypoint and procedure data, and require an update before using for navigation
C) Cancel the flight until the database is updated
D) Tell the student to use paper charts instead of the iPad

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** An expired GPS database may contain outdated waypoint coordinates, amended procedures, or removed fixes. While EFB apps like ForeFlight are advisory, teaching students to rely on expired data builds dangerous habits. The CFI should use this as a teaching moment about database currency requirements under 14 CFR 91.503 and AIM guidance -- not simply cancel or dismiss the tool entirely. Option A normalizes bad practice. Option C is overly restrictive when updating takes minutes. Option D abandons the tool rather than teaching proper use.

### Q-A1-02 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-2

During a training flight, your student's G1000 autopilot captures a glideslope but then begins an unexpected climb. The student says "I think it's doing the missed approach." The altitude bug is set at 3,000 feet (the missed approach altitude) and the autopilot is in ALT mode. What is the most important thing to teach from this event?

A) The autopilot malfunctioned and should be written up for maintenance
B) The altitude bug setting overrode the glideslope capture because the system prioritized the altitude constraint
C) The student should always hand-fly approaches to avoid automation issues
D) The G1000 autopilot cannot track glideslope and altitude simultaneously

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This is a classic mode confusion scenario. The autopilot correctly followed its logic: when the aircraft reached the altitude bug setting, it transitioned from GS to ALT mode. The teaching point is that the automation did exactly what it was told -- the pilot's setup was wrong. CFIs must teach students to understand mode priorities and altitude bug management. Option A misidentifies a pilot setup error as a mechanical failure. Option C avoids the problem rather than teaching automation management. Option D is factually incorrect.

### Q-A1-03 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-1, AV-3

You are teaching a private pilot student in a TAA (Technically Advanced Aircraft) equipped with a Garmin GTN 750 and GFC 700 autopilot. The student navigates exclusively by following the magenta line and has never used VOR or pilotage skills. What is your primary concern?

A) The student is not getting enough stick-and-rudder practice
B) The student is developing automation dependency that will become dangerous when the technology fails or degrades
C) The student will fail the checkride because VOR navigation is tested
D) TAA aircraft are too complex for private pilot students

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K specifically addresses the hazard of automation dependency. GPS and glass cockpit technology can fail, lose signal, or present misleading information. A pilot who can only follow the magenta line is a pilot who cannot navigate when the system degrades -- a real safety risk, not just a test deficiency. Option A addresses a symptom. Option C focuses on the test rather than the safety issue. Option D is incorrect -- TAA is appropriate for training when taught properly.

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-2, AV-1

During an IFR training flight, ATC issues a reroute. Your student reprograms the GPS but inadvertently activates a direct-to the wrong waypoint 50 nm off course. The student doesn't notice because the moving map looks "about right." What does this event best illustrate?

A) The student needs more practice with the GPS interface
B) ATC should provide vectors instead of reroutes during training flights
C) Over-reliance on automation without cross-checking against basic situational awareness and other navigation references
D) The GPS interface is poorly designed

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** This scenario illustrates the core AC 61-83K teaching point about automation dependency: pilots who trust the screen without cross-referencing heading, time, distance, or other nav aids lose situational awareness. The student's inability to detect a 50 nm course error from the moving map alone shows a failure of basic SA, not just a button press error. Option A addresses a symptom. Options B and D deflect responsibility from the pilot.

### Q-A1-05 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-03)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AV-3

Which of the following are limitations of GPS navigation that a CFI should ensure students understand? (Select all that apply)

A) GPS signals can be degraded by ionospheric interference, intentional jamming, or solar activity
B) GPS provides obstacle clearance on all routes, not just published procedures
C) A RAIM prediction failure means the GPS integrity cannot be assured for the planned approach
D) ADS-B weather data displayed on the moving map may be 15-20 minutes old
E) GPS-derived altitude is accurate enough to replace the barometric altimeter

**Correct:** A, C, D
**Explanation:** CFIs must teach GPS limitations honestly. GPS signals are vulnerable to multiple degradation sources (A). RAIM is the integrity monitoring system -- a prediction failure means the receiver cannot guarantee required accuracy (C). ADS-B weather (FIS-B) has significant latency and should not be used for immediate tactical decisions (D). Option B is false -- GPS provides obstacle clearance only on published IFR procedures with appropriate TERPS criteria. Option E is false -- GPS altitude is unreliable and never substitutes for barometric altitude.

### Q-A1-06 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-03)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-1, AV-2

A student transitioning from steam gauges to a glass cockpit spends most of the traffic pattern staring at the PFD. On downwind, the student fails to see a Cessna on a close base leg. As the CFI, what is the root instructional issue?

A) Glass cockpit aircraft are inherently less safe in the traffic pattern
B) The student has not developed an effective scan that integrates the glass panel with outside visual references
C) The student should turn off the PFD in the traffic pattern to force outside scanning
D) The student needs more time in the glass cockpit to get comfortable before flying in the pattern

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The fundamental instructional challenge in glass cockpit transitions is teaching pilots to integrate the new information source into their scan without becoming fixated on it. The PFD provides excellent data but it doesn't replace looking outside, especially in the VFR traffic pattern where see-and-avoid is the primary collision avoidance method. Option A blames the equipment. Option C is dangerous and impractical. Option D assumes time alone will fix a scan discipline problem -- it won't without deliberate instruction.

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-2

During a night IFR flight, your student's primary GPS fails. The student becomes visibly anxious and says, "I don't know how to navigate without it." As the CFI, what is your immediate priority?

A) Declare an emergency and request vectors to the nearest airport
B) Take the controls, tune a VOR, and demonstrate traditional navigation while coaching the student through the workload
C) Tell the student to use the backup GPS on their iPad
D) Have the student attempt to troubleshoot and restart the GPS unit

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K emphasizes that pilots must remain proficient in manual aircraft control and traditional navigation methods. When a student reveals total dependence on GPS, the CFI must first stabilize the situation (take controls if needed), then demonstrate the alternative skills the student should have been developing. Option A is premature -- loss of GPS is not an emergency when other nav aids are available. Option C shifts dependence to another device rather than addressing the skill gap. Option D wastes time troubleshooting when the immediate need is safe navigation. This scenario illustrates exactly the automation dependency the FAA warns about.

### Q-A1-08 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-04)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, AV-2

You observe a student using the autopilot's vertical speed (VS) mode to descend during an approach. The airspeed increases to 15 knots above Vfe. The student does not notice because they are programming the next waypoint on the GPS. What automation hazard does this scenario primarily illustrate?

A) Automation complacency -- the student trusts the autopilot to manage all flight parameters
B) Database error -- the approach was programmed incorrectly
C) Autopilot malfunction -- VS mode should limit airspeed automatically
D) Workload mismanagement -- the student is tasked beyond their capacity

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K warns about automation complacency and fixation. The autopilot in VS mode will hold a descent rate regardless of airspeed -- it does not manage speed. The student assumed the autopilot was "handling it" while focusing on the GPS, which is textbook automation complacency. Option B is unrelated to the speed exceedance. Option C mischaracterizes normal autopilot behavior as a malfunction -- VS mode is not designed to limit airspeed. Option D is partially true but misses the root cause: the student's trust in the automation, not their capacity, allowed the exceedance.

### Q-A1-09 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-05)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AV-3, PS-2

Which of the following are appropriate strategies for a CFI to help students maintain manual flying proficiency while training in glass cockpit aircraft? (Select all that apply)

A) Dedicate portions of each flight to hand-flying with the autopilot off, including partial panel exercises
B) Require students to demonstrate VOR tracking and pilotage on every cross-country flight
C) Prohibit autopilot use until the student has passed the instrument checkride
D) Teach students to consciously select the appropriate level of automation for each phase of flight
E) Have students practice manual reversions by simulating autopilot failures at various flight phases

**Correct:** A, B, D, E
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K states the FAA "strongly recommends that pilots and flight instructors strive for proficiency in every aspect of the aircraft flown, which includes maintaining the pilot's proficiency in manual aircraft control." Strategies A, B, D, and E all build manual proficiency and appropriate automation management. The advisory circular also emphasizes that "no one level of automation is appropriate for all flight situations" (D) and that pilots should be able to "address the situation" if automation fails (E). Option C is counterproductive -- the goal is teaching proper automation management, not avoidance. Banning the autopilot prevents the student from learning when and how to use it appropriately.

### Q-A1-10 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-05)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-2, AV-2

A student is preparing for an RNAV (GPS) approach and loads the procedure into the navigator. The NOTAM for that approach indicates the LNAV MDA has been raised by 200 feet due to a new obstruction. The student's GPS database does not reflect this change. What should you teach the student?

A) The GPS database always reflects current NOTAMs, so the NOTAM must be outdated
B) Use the higher MDA from the NOTAM, because NOTAMs supersede database information and the pilot is responsible for applying current NOTAM restrictions
C) Cancel the GPS approach and request an ILS approach instead
D) Fly the approach using the database MDA since the GPS is the primary navigation source

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** GPS databases are updated on a regular cycle but do not dynamically incorporate NOTAMs. The pilot is always responsible for checking NOTAMs and applying any restrictions that differ from the database. AC 61-83K addresses database currency and limits as a key automation hazard. Option A is factually incorrect -- databases and NOTAMs operate on different timelines. Option C is unnecessarily restrictive when the approach can be flown safely with the corrected MDA. Option D is dangerous -- the database MDA does not account for the new obstruction.

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-3, ES-2

Your student is flying a coupled ILS approach in IMC when the autopilot suddenly disconnects at 800 feet AGL. The student freezes and does not take control of the aircraft. As the CFI, beyond taking immediate control, what is the most important teaching point from this event?

A) The student should have been monitoring the autopilot disconnect altitude and been prepared to hand-fly
B) The autopilot should not have been used for the approach in IMC
C) The student needs more simulator time before flying in actual IMC
D) A different autopilot system would be more reliable

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K states that "if an automation system failure occurs, in whole or in part, the pilot should possess the knowledge and skill to address the situation." The student's freeze response reveals a critical gap: they were not mentally prepared for the automation to fail. CFIs must teach students to always be ready to take over manual control, especially during critical phases of flight. Option B contradicts proper use of automation as a workload management tool. Option C defers a fundamental airmanship issue that must be addressed in context. Option D blames equipment rather than pilot preparation.

During a VFR cross-country, your student is navigating solely by GPS direct-to. You cover the GPS screen and ask the student to identify their position. Option A normalizes the gap. Option C adds redundancy without addressing the skill deficit. Option D does not apply to VFR operations and avoids the underlying problem.

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AV-3, OD-2

A pilot transitioning from a traditional six-pack panel to a glass cockpit Cirrus SR22 asks you for transition training. According to AC 61-83K guidance, which elements should your transition training program include? (Select all that apply)

A) Instruction on the specific avionics suite installed in the aircraft, including FMS and autopilot modes
B) Practice recognizing and recovering from automation failures and partial system degradation
C) A minimum of 25 hours in the new aircraft type before solo flight

**Correct:** A, B, D, E

These align with the advisory circular's emphasis on learning "the airplane's limitation, systems limitations, and all manufacturer's recommended procedures." Option C is fabricated -- AC 61-83K does not specify a minimum hour requirement for transition training.

A newly certificated CFI asks you for advice on teaching in glass cockpit aircraft. They are comfortable flying the aircraft but have never taught in one. What is your most important recommendation?

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-2, RC-2

Your student is flying an RNAV approach and you notice the GPS annunciates "LNAV" but the student believes they are flying an "LPV" approach with lower minimums. They begin descending below the LNAV MDA. What does this scenario illustrate?

C) LPV and LNAV minimums are interchangeable when the approach is GPS-based

Descending below the LNAV MDA without the required vertical guidance is a dangerous deviation. CFIs must teach students to verify -- not assume -- the level of service their equipment is providing. Option A incorrectly blames the equipment. Option C is factually wrong and dangerous. Option D deflects from the monitoring discipline issue.

### Q-A1-16 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-08)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, AV-1

You are teaching an instrument student who has been trained exclusively in a glass cockpit aircraft. During a flight review in a rental Cessna 172 with traditional instruments, the pilot struggles to maintain altitude and heading because they are "looking for the flight path marker and trend vectors." What lesson does this highlight for CFIs?

A) Pilots trained in glass cockpits should only fly glass cockpit aircraft
B) Flight schools should standardize their fleets so pilots don't encounter unfamiliar panels
C) Training exclusively in one panel type can create skill gaps that become dangerous when the pilot flies different equipment, reinforcing the need for diverse training exposure
D) Traditional instruments are obsolete and should not be used for training

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K emphasizes that pilots must "remain proficient in manual aircraft control" and be able to fly safely without advanced automation. A pilot who cannot maintain basic attitude instrument flying without synthetic vision cues has a dangerous skill gap. This reinforces the AC's guidance that instructors should ensure students develop proficiency across all aspects of the aircraft, not just the technology they train on. Option A creates a dangerous limitation. Option B is impractical and avoids the training issue. Option D ignores that many GA aircraft still use traditional panels and that the underlying skills are fundamental.
