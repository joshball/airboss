# A.1.2 -- Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B)

Questions covering ADS-B replacing radar, foundational NextGen technology, ADS-B In/Out, traffic/weather/flight information services, and 14 CFR 91.225/91.227.

---

### Q-A1-27 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-14)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-3, RC-2

A student asks why ADS-B is considered an improvement over radar for air traffic surveillance. As a CFI, which answer best reflects the AC 61-83K description of ADS-B?

A) ADS-B uses satellites instead of radar to track aircraft movement, providing major advantages including real-time surveillance that is now the preferred method for ATC
B) ADS-B is identical to radar but uses newer equipment
C) ADS-B only works in terminal airspace and supplements radar everywhere else
D) ADS-B is primarily a weather service that also provides some surveillance capability

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K states that "ADS-B replaces radar technology with satellites to track aircraft movement, which in turn provides major advantages" and that "real-time ADS-B is now the preferred method of surveillance for air traffic control." Option B incorrectly equates ADS-B with radar. Option C fabricates a terminal-only limitation. Option D mischaracterizes ADS-B's primary function -- it is fundamentally a surveillance technology, though it also enables services like weather.

### Q-A1-28 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-14)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-3, AV-2

During a flight review, your client's aircraft is equipped with ADS-B Out but not ADS-B In. The pilot says, "I have ADS-B, so I can see all the traffic around me on my display." What should you correct?

A) The pilot is correct -- ADS-B Out provides traffic display capability
B) ADS-B Out only transmits the aircraft's position to ATC and other aircraft; ADS-B In is required to receive traffic, weather, and flight information services on a cockpit display
C) ADS-B Out and ADS-B In are the same thing
D) ADS-B traffic display requires a separate TIS-B subscription from the FAA

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** ADS-B Out transmits the aircraft's position and identification data. ADS-B In receives data from ground stations and other aircraft, enabling cockpit displays of traffic (TIS-B), weather (FIS-B), and flight information. AC 61-83K references "ADS-B traffic, weather, and flight-information services" as benefits for GA safety. A pilot with only ADS-B Out is visible to ATC and other equipped aircraft but cannot see them. Option A confuses Out with In. Option C is incorrect -- they are distinct capabilities. Option D is fabricated -- TIS-B is a free service provided through ADS-B In.

### Q-A1-29 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-15)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AV-3, OD-2

According to AC 61-83K, what benefits does ADS-B provide for general aviation? (Select all that apply)

A) Traffic information services that improve see-and-avoid capability
B) Weather information services that enhance situational awareness
C) Flight information services
D) Guaranteed separation from all other aircraft without ATC involvement
E) Improved safety and efficiency through advanced surveillance capability

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K states that "GA is safer with ADS-B traffic, weather, and flight-information services" and that "safety and efficiency improve with advanced ADS-B." These directly support options A, B, C, and E. Option D is incorrect and dangerous -- ADS-B enhances awareness but does not provide guaranteed separation. ATC still provides separation services, and see-and-avoid remains the pilot's responsibility in VFR conditions.

### Q-A1-30 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-15)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-2, AV-2

A student planning a VFR flight into Class C airspace asks whether their aircraft needs ADS-B equipment. What is the correct regulatory guidance?

A) ADS-B is only required in Class A and Class B airspace
B) 14 CFR 91.225 establishes ADS-B Out equipment requirements for specified airspace, including Class C airspace -- the student must verify their aircraft complies before entering
C) ADS-B is optional for VFR flights regardless of airspace class
D) ADS-B is only required for IFR flights

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** 14 CFR 91.225 specifies the airspace where ADS-B Out equipment is required, which includes Class A, B, and C airspace, as well as other specified areas. AC 61-83K identifies ADS-B as a foundational NextGen technology that CFIs must understand. A CFI should ensure students know the regulatory requirements before flight planning. Option A omits Class C and other required airspace. Option C is incorrect -- the requirement is airspace-based, not flight-rules-based. Option D is also incorrect for the same reason.

### Q-A1-31 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-16)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, OD-2

Your student is using ADS-B In traffic information on their cockpit display during a VFR flight. They see no traffic targets near their position and say, "We're clear of traffic -- no need to look outside." How should you respond?

A) Agree -- ADS-B traffic display shows all nearby aircraft
B) Explain that ADS-B traffic display may not show all aircraft, particularly those without ADS-B Out equipment or transponders, and that visual scanning remains the primary collision avoidance method for VFR flight
C) Tell the student to turn off the traffic display since it creates a false sense of security
D) Explain that ADS-B only shows IFR traffic, not VFR

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** ADS-B traffic display depends on other aircraft transmitting ADS-B Out or being detected by radar (TIS-B). Aircraft without transponders, aircraft outside radar coverage, and aircraft with malfunctioning equipment will not appear. This is analogous to the automation dependency warnings in AC 61-83K -- pilots must not become overdependent on any single system. See-and-avoid remains the pilot's primary defense. Option A is dangerously incorrect. Option C abandons a useful tool rather than teaching proper use. Option D is an oversimplification.

### Q-A1-32 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-16)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-2, RC-2

An aircraft owner asks you about equipping their 1975 Cessna 182 for ADS-B compliance. They currently have a Mode C transponder. What should you explain about the equipment requirements?

A) Their Mode C transponder already satisfies the ADS-B Out requirement
B) 14 CFR 91.227 specifies the performance requirements for ADS-B Out equipment, and they will need a compliant ADS-B Out transmitter -- their Mode C transponder alone does not satisfy the requirement
C) ADS-B compliance is waived for aircraft manufactured before 1980
D) They only need an ADS-B In receiver to be compliant

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** 14 CFR 91.227 defines the ADS-B Out equipment performance requirements, including position source accuracy and transmitter specifications. A Mode C transponder does not meet these requirements -- a compliant ADS-B Out transmitter (typically a UAT on 978 MHz or a 1090 MHz ES transponder) is required. AC 61-83K identifies ADS-B understanding as essential CFI knowledge. Option A is incorrect -- Mode C is not ADS-B. Option C is fabricated -- there is no age-based waiver. Option D confuses In and Out; the mandate requires Out, not In.

### Q-A1-33 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-17)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-2

During an instrument training flight, your student is using ADS-B In weather (FIS-B) on their MFD to navigate around a line of thunderstorms. The display shows a gap in the line that appears wide enough to fly through. What is the most important limitation you should teach about this weather data?

A) FIS-B weather data is real-time and can be trusted for tactical maneuvering around convective weather
B) FIS-B weather data has significant latency -- typically 15-20 minutes old -- and gaps that appear on the display may have already closed, making it unsuitable for tactical penetration decisions near convective weather
C) FIS-B weather is only available inside ADS-B coverage areas and stops working over remote terrain
D) FIS-B weather is only useful for planning the next day's flight, not for in-flight decisions

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** FIS-B weather data delivered through ADS-B In has significant latency. Thunderstorms can move, intensify, and fill gaps within the 15-20 minute delay. Using this data for tactical decisions near convective weather is dangerous. This connects to AC 61-83K's broader warning about automation dependency and understanding system limitations. Option A is factually wrong and dangerous. Option C is partially true (coverage varies) but not the primary limitation. Option D overstates the limitation -- FIS-B is useful for strategic in-flight weather awareness, just not tactical maneuvering near convection.

### Q-A1-34 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-17)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AV-3, OD-2

As a CFI, which of the following should you teach students about ADS-B limitations? (Select all that apply)

A) ADS-B traffic targets may not include all aircraft in the area, particularly those without ADS-B Out or transponders
B) ADS-B weather (FIS-B) data has latency and should not be used for immediate tactical decisions near convective weather
C) ADS-B performance can be degraded in mountainous terrain or areas with limited ground station coverage
D) ADS-B replaces the need for a transponder in all airspace
E) ADS-B In displays should supplement, not replace, pilot judgment and other information sources

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** CFIs must teach that ADS-B, like all automation, has limitations. Not all aircraft appear on traffic displays (A). Weather data has latency (B). Coverage varies by terrain and ground station proximity (C). ADS-B displays are tools that supplement pilot judgment, not replace it (E) -- consistent with AC 61-83K's warnings about automation dependency. Option D is incorrect -- 14 CFR 91.215 transponder requirements still apply, and ADS-B Out is an additional requirement per 91.225, not a replacement for the transponder.

### Q-A1-35 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-18)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-2, AV-2

A student pilot asks you the difference between the two ADS-B Out frequencies: 1090 MHz Extended Squitter (1090ES) and 978 MHz Universal Access Transceiver (UAT). Which statement is correct?

A) There is no operational difference -- both frequencies work identically in all airspace
B) 1090ES is required for operations above FL180, while UAT on 978 MHz is available for operations below FL180 -- and each has different equipment and capability implications
C) UAT is being phased out and all aircraft will eventually need 1090ES
D) UAT only works in the continental United States and 1090ES works worldwide, so 1090ES is the only practical choice

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The FAA's ADS-B implementation uses a dual-frequency approach. 1090ES is required above FL180 (Class A airspace) and is the international standard. UAT on 978 MHz is available for operations below FL180 and provides additional services like FIS-B weather. CFIs should understand both options per AC 61-83K's guidance that instructors must understand ADS-B. Option A ignores meaningful differences. Option C is not current FAA policy. Option D overstates the 1090ES advantage -- UAT works throughout the U.S. where most GA pilots operate, and 1090ES international compatibility only matters for flights outside the U.S.

### Q-A1-36 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-18)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-2, RC-2

As a FIRC provider, why does AC 61-83K require that CFIs receive training on ADS-B?

A) Because ADS-B is replacing all other navigation systems in the NAS
B) Because ADS-B is a foundational NextGen technology that has changed how surveillance, traffic awareness, and weather information work in the NAS -- and CFIs must understand these changes to teach their students effectively
C) Because CFIs are required to install ADS-B in their personal aircraft
D) Because ADS-B knowledge is tested on all FAA written exams

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K states that "flight instructors also need to fully understand" ADS-B and directs FIRC providers to "include training on... an overview of ADS-B." ADS-B is described as "a foundational NextGen technology" that has fundamentally changed NAS surveillance. CFIs who don't understand ADS-B cannot properly teach students about its capabilities, limitations, and regulatory requirements. Option A is incorrect -- ADS-B is a surveillance system, not a navigation replacement. Option C fabricates a personal equipment requirement. Option D overstates where the knowledge is tested.
