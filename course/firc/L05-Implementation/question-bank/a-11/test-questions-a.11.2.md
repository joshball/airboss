# A.11.2 -- Traffic Pattern Operations

One area where pilots have experienced LOC accidents is in the traffic pattern.

---

### Q-A11-27 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-14)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-2, CJ-1

A pilot on downwind notices they are being extended by ATC for traffic sequencing. They are now 2 miles from the runway on base. To make the runway, they steepen their turn to final and increase back pressure to prevent altitude loss. What LOC hazard has the pilot created?

A) The pilot is too far from the runway for a normal approach and should request a closer pattern
B) The combination of steep bank, increased back pressure, and low altitude creates the classic base-to-final stall/spin profile -- the pilot should fly a wider final or go around rather than steepen the turn
C) The pilot should add power to maintain altitude in the turn
D) Extended downwinds are safe as long as the pilot maintains visual contact with the runway

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.2, traffic pattern LOC accidents frequently involve pilots who overshoot or extend the pattern and then try to "save" the approach by steepening the turn to final. The combination of increased bank angle plus increased back pressure at low altitude and low airspeed is the base-to-final stall/spin chain. The correct action is to fly a wider final with a shallow turn, or execute a go-around. Option C addresses one symptom but not the root hazard. Option D ignores the aerodynamic risk entirely.

### Q-A11-28 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-14)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-2, AC-1

Which of the following are criteria for a stabilized approach that a CFI should teach as mandatory for traffic pattern operations? (Select all that apply)

A) On the correct glidepath by 500 feet AGL for a visual approach
B) Airspeed within +10/-5 knots of the target approach speed
C) Configured for landing (gear, flaps as appropriate)
D) Constant bank angle throughout the entire pattern
E) In a position to land on the intended runway without excessive maneuvering

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** A stabilized approach requires being on glidepath (A), on speed (B), properly configured (C), and in position to land without aggressive corrections (E). These criteria establish the go/no-go gate that prevents pilots from pressing into unstable approaches that lead to LOC in the pattern. Option D is incorrect -- bank angle varies throughout the pattern as the pilot makes turns and corrections. The key is that bank and corrections remain moderate, not constant.

### Q-A11-29 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-15)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-1

A student is flying the traffic pattern and consistently overshoots the turn from base to final. The CFI has corrected this three times. On the fourth pattern, the student overshoots again and this time rolls into a 45-degree bank to correct. What should the CFI do?

A) Let the student complete the turn -- they need to learn to correct their own overshoots
B) Take the controls, level the wings, and go around. Then on the ground, address why the student is consistently overshooting and why the steep bank correction is a lethal response
C) Coach the student to shallow the bank and accept a wider final
D) Have the student practice base-to-final turns at altitude before returning to the pattern

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A 45-degree bank correction at pattern altitude in a training aircraft is an immediate LOC precursor. This is no longer a coaching moment -- the CFI must intervene. But the pattern of consistent overshoots indicates a systemic problem (late base turn, not compensating for wind, poor planning) that must be addressed on the ground. Option C attempts to coach during a dangerous maneuver. Option A accepts an unacceptable risk. Option D is a valid training technique but doesn't address the immediate safety issue.

### Q-A11-30 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-15)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, AC-3

A CFI is teaching go-around procedures. The student asks: "When exactly should I go around? I don't want to waste time going around if I can still land." What is the best CFI response?

A) "If you have to ask whether you should go around, go around. The approach should feel stabilized and under control. Any doubt means go around."
B) "You should go around if you're more than two dots off the glideslope."
C) "Go around any time ATC tells you to."
D) "You should never have to go around if you fly the pattern correctly."

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** Go-around discipline is central to traffic pattern LOC prevention per AC 61-83K A.11.2. The student's framing -- "waste time" -- reveals the psychological barrier to go-arounds that CFIs must actively counter. The correct principle is that any doubt about the approach's stability means go around. A go-around is always safer than pressing a questionable approach. Option B is too specific and only addresses one parameter. Option C limits go-arounds to ATC directives. Option D implies go-arounds indicate failure, which is the opposite of the mindset CFIs should instill.

### Q-A11-31 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-16)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, RM-2

A pilot in the traffic pattern at an uncontrolled airport notices a faster aircraft on a long straight-in final. The pilot feels pressure to tighten their pattern to land before the faster aircraft. What LOC risk is the pilot accepting?

A) No additional risk -- tightening the pattern is standard procedure at uncontrolled airports
B) Psychologically-driven risk -- the pressure to "get in before" the other aircraft motivates steeper turns, faster descent, and reduced safety margins, creating the conditions for a traffic pattern LOC accident
C) The risk of a midair collision only
D) The pilot should extend upwind and wait

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Social and psychological pressure is a significant factor in traffic pattern LOC accidents. The desire to avoid inconveniencing another pilot or to avoid the "embarrassment" of extending or going around motivates pilots to steepen turns, increase descent rates, and accept approaches they would normally reject. This is exactly the scenario chain that AC 61-83K A.11.2 identifies in traffic pattern LOC. CFIs should teach students to recognize this pressure and have a pre-planned response: extend, go around, or sequence behind -- never compromise the approach.

### Q-A11-32 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-16)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AC-2, CJ-2

A CFI is developing a training module on traffic pattern LOC prevention. Which of the following should be included as key teaching points? (Select all that apply)

A) The go-around is the default response to any unstabilized approach
B) Wind correction in the pattern may require adjusting where to turn base, not how steeply to turn
C) Traffic pattern altitude should be maintained until turning base, then a gradual descent established
D) If the base-to-final turn is going to overshoot, steepen the bank to correct
E) A stabilized approach means constant airspeed, descent rate, and configuration by 500 feet AGL

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Each of these is a core LOC prevention concept for traffic pattern operations. Go-around as default (A) establishes the escape route. Wind-adjusted base turn point (B) prevents the overshoot that leads to steep corrections. Gradual descent from pattern altitude (C) maintains energy management. Stabilized approach criteria (E) establish the go/no-go gate. Option D is the exact opposite of correct -- steepening the bank to correct an overshoot is the entry into the base-to-final stall/spin chain per AC 61-83K A.11.2.

### Q-A11-33 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-17)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, AC-2

On a calm day, a pilot flies a tight, consistent traffic pattern. The next day, there is a 15-knot direct crosswind. The pilot flies the same ground track as the calm day, resulting in an overshoot on the base-to-final turn (the wind pushed them through final). What fundamental skill gap does this reveal?

A) The pilot needs more crosswind landing practice
B) The pilot has learned to fly a ground track pattern rather than a wind-corrected pattern -- they don't adjust their base turn point, crab angle, or rollout anticipation for wind, which leads to overshoots and the temptation to steepen the turn
C) The pilot should have used a steeper approach to compensate for the wind
D) Crosswind patterns should be avoided until the pilot has more experience

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Many pilots learn the traffic pattern as a fixed ground track rather than as a wind-corrected flight path. In wind, failing to adjust the base turn point (turn earlier in a tailwind on base, later in a headwind) leads to overshoots on final. The overshoot then tempts the pilot into the steep bank correction that is the entry to the base-to-final LOC chain. CFIs should teach wind-corrected patterns from the beginning, not as an advanced skill. Option A addresses landing technique but not the pattern management issue. Option C doesn't address the root cause.

### Q-A11-34 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-17)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, AC-3

A student on short final realizes they are too high and too fast. The student pushes the nose down aggressively to "dive" at the runway. What LOC risk does this create, and what should the CFI teach as the alternative?

A) No risk -- the student is maintaining visual contact with the runway and will flare normally
B) The dive increases airspeed and descent rate, creating risk of a hard landing or loss of control during the flare. The alternative is a go-around -- an unstable approach at short final is not correctable, it is go-around territory
C) The student should slip to lose altitude instead
D) The student should add full flaps to increase drag

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Diving at the runway from short final compounds the instability -- excessive airspeed leads to float, ballooning, or a hard landing, any of which can result in a bounced landing, ground loop, or runway departure. At short final, the approach is either stable or it's a go-around. There is no third option. CFIs must teach that the go-around decision becomes mandatory when the approach is unstable at short final, not optional. Option C is a valid technique but only for moderate excess altitude on a longer final. Option D at short final with excessive speed risks exceeding VFE.

### Q-A11-35 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-18)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, RM-2

A CFI is debriefing a student after a pattern session. The student made consistently good approaches except for one where they were high and fast and chose to force the landing rather than go around. The student says, "I didn't want to go around because it would add time to the lesson." How should the CFI address this?

A) Acknowledge the student's time concern and move on -- the landing was successful
B) Explain that the go-around is not a failure or a waste of time -- it is the correct response to an unstable approach, and a CFI who normalizes go-arounds produces pilots who survive traffic pattern LOC situations
C) Add an extra pattern to make up for the missed go-around practice
D) Suggest the student practice approaches at home in a simulator

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The student's reluctance to go around reveals a mindset the CFI must actively counter. Viewing go-arounds as failures or time-wasters leads to pressing unstable approaches, which is the direct path to traffic pattern LOC per AC 61-83K A.11.2. The CFI's job is to reframe the go-around as a demonstration of good judgment, not a penalty. This cultural shift in the student's thinking is more important than any single maneuver. Option A reinforces the dangerous mindset. Options C and D address mechanics without addressing the attitude.

### Q-A11-36 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-18)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** CJ-1, RM-1

Which of the following environmental factors should a CFI teach students to account for when planning their traffic pattern to avoid LOC? (Select all that apply)

A) Wind direction and speed, which affect ground track, groundspeed on each leg, and turn anticipation
B) Density altitude, which increases true airspeed and widens the ground track at pattern altitude
C) Terrain or obstacles that may require modifications to the standard pattern
D) The color of the runway markings
E) Other traffic in the pattern that may require spacing adjustments

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** All four factors directly affect how the pilot should plan and fly the traffic pattern to maintain safe margins. Wind (A) affects every turn and the glidepath. Density altitude (B) increases true airspeed and approach speed, widening the pattern. Terrain (C) may prevent standard patterns. Other traffic (E) creates sequencing pressure. CFIs should teach students to brief these factors before entering the pattern, not discover them during the pattern. Option D has no relevance to pattern planning or LOC risk.
