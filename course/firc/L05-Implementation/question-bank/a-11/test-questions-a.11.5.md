# A.11.5 -- LOC / Base to Landing

Specialized flight training emphasizing establishing and maintaining stabilized approaches.

---

### Q-A11-57 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-29)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-2, CJ-1

A pilot on base leg realizes they will overshoot the turn to final due to a stronger-than-expected tailwind on base. The pilot's instinct is to steepen the bank to 50 degrees and pull back on the yoke to maintain altitude through the turn. What is the aerodynamic chain of events this creates?

A) The aircraft will complete the turn safely because the increased bank compensates for the overshoot
B) The steep bank increases load factor to approximately 1.6G, raising the stall speed by about 26%. The added back pressure increases angle of attack toward the critical angle. The combination of high AOA, increased stall speed, and low altitude creates the classic base-to-final stall/spin entry -- often with insufficient altitude to recover
C) The aircraft will simply descend faster, which helps with the approach
D) The tailwind on base has no effect on the turn to final

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.5, base-to-final LOC is a leading accident profile. The physics are unforgiving: at 50 degrees of bank, load factor is ~1.6G, which raises the stall speed significantly. Adding back pressure to "hold altitude" pushes the wing toward the critical angle of attack. If the turn is also uncoordinated (bottom rudder to tighten it), one wing may stall before the other, producing a spin entry at an altitude where recovery is impossible. The correct response to an overshoot is to fly a wider final or go around.

### Q-A11-58 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-29)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-2, AC-1

Which of the following are elements of a stabilized approach that a CFI should establish as non-negotiable criteria for continuing to land? (Select all that apply)

A) On the correct glidepath -- not excessively high or low
B) On speed -- within the target approach speed window for the aircraft and conditions
C) Wings level on final -- no significant bank angle corrections needed
D) Aligned with the runway centerline without requiring aggressive maneuvering
E) Configured with gear and flaps as appropriate for the phase of approach

**Correct:** A, B, D, E
**Explanation:** Stabilized approach criteria per AC 61-83K A.11.5 include glidepath (A), airspeed (B), alignment without aggressive correction (D), and proper configuration (E). Any of these being unsatisfied is a go-around trigger. Option C is too restrictive -- minor wing corrections for crosswind are normal and expected on final approach. The key distinction is between normal corrections and aggressive maneuvering to salvage an unstable approach.

### Q-A11-59 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-30)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-1

A student overshoots the base-to-final turn. Rather than steepening the bank, the student correctly accepts the wider final and begins to align with the runway. However, the student is now left of centerline and begins applying right rudder without aileron to "kick" the nose toward the runway. What LOC risk has the student created?

A) None -- rudder-only corrections are standard technique on final
B) The student is now in a cross-controlled condition: right rudder with wings level creates a skid, and if the student then adds left aileron to prevent the right roll, they are fully cross-controlled. A cross-controlled stall at pattern altitude produces a spin with no altitude for recovery
C) The rudder correction will align the aircraft with the centerline safely
D) The student should add more rudder to complete the correction faster

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Cross-controlled flight near the runway is one of the most dangerous LOC setups. Using rudder alone to change the aircraft's heading without corresponding bank creates a skid. If the student then fights the resulting roll tendency with opposite aileron, the aircraft is cross-controlled -- exactly the condition that produces a spin entry at the stall. CFIs must teach that heading changes on final use coordinated bank-and-rudder turns, not rudder kicks. Option D would worsen the cross-control condition.

### Q-A11-60 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-30)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, AC-3

A CFI is teaching a student about go-around decision-making on final approach. The student asks: "If I'm at 200 feet and the approach is unstable, is it too late to go around?" What is the correct answer?

A) "Yes, at 200 feet you're committed to landing."
B) "It is never too late to go around as long as the aircraft has flying speed and you can apply power. At 200 feet, a go-around is absolutely executable -- add full power, pitch for climb, retract flaps incrementally, and fly the go-around. The danger is not the go-around at 200 feet; the danger is forcing an unstable approach to a landing from 200 feet."
C) "You should have gone around at 500 feet."
D) "At 200 feet, just try to land as best you can."

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.5, stabilized approach discipline requires that the go-around remain an option throughout the approach. A go-around from 200 feet is a normal maneuver that every pilot should be trained and comfortable executing. The real danger is the belief that you're "too low to go around" -- this belief traps pilots into forcing unstable approaches, which is the direct path to base-to-final LOC accidents. Option A creates the exact mental trap that kills pilots. Option C is hindsight, not helpful. Option D accepts the unstable approach.

### Q-A11-61 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-31)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, RM-2

During a landing approach, a pilot notices they are high and fast on short final. The pilot reduces power to idle, pushes the nose down to steepen the descent, and adds full flaps simultaneously. What LOC risk has the pilot created?

A) The pilot is correctly managing energy to reach the touchdown zone
B) The sudden configuration change (full flaps at high speed) may exceed VFE, and the aggressive nose-down pitch combined with rapid deceleration when below VFE creates a risk of a sudden pitch change, ballooning, or loss of control during the flare. The correct action for a high-and-fast approach on short final is a go-around
C) Adding full flaps at any point is always safe
D) The pilot should also add a forward slip to maximize descent rate

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Stacking corrections on short final -- power to idle, steep descent, full flaps -- is an attempt to salvage an unstable approach. Each change creates transient aerodynamic effects (pitch changes, trim changes, speed changes) that the pilot must manage simultaneously at the most critical phase of flight. If the flaps are added above VFE, structural damage is possible. The professional response is recognizing that a high-and-fast approach on short final is not correctable -- it's a go-around. Option D adds yet another destabilizing input to an already overloaded situation.

### Q-A11-62 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-31)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AC-2, CJ-2

A CFI observes the following behaviors in a student during pattern work. Which of these are direct precursors to a base-to-final stall/spin and require immediate correction? (Select all that apply)

A) The student consistently turns base with a tailwind, arriving at the base-to-final turn with excess groundspeed
B) The student uses bottom rudder (inside rudder) in the base-to-final turn to "help" the turn rate
C) The student maintains coordinated flight throughout the base-to-final turn
D) The student increases back pressure during the base-to-final turn to prevent altitude loss
E) The student allows bank angle to exceed 30 degrees in the base-to-final turn at pattern altitude

**Correct:** A, B, D, E
**Explanation:** Each of A, B, D, and E is a link in the base-to-final stall/spin chain per AC 61-83K A.11.5. Tailwind on base (A) causes overshoots. Bottom rudder (B) creates uncoordinated flight. Increasing back pressure (D) raises AOA toward critical. Excessive bank (E) increases load factor and stall speed. Together or individually, these behaviors set up the conditions for a low-altitude stall/spin. Option C -- coordinated flight -- is the protective behavior the CFI should be teaching and reinforcing.

### Q-A11-63 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-32)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, AC-2

A pilot is on final approach at an unfamiliar airport. The approach is stable, but the pilot realizes the runway is shorter than expected. At 300 feet AGL, the pilot begins to doubt whether they can stop on the runway. What is the correct decision?

A) Continue the approach and plan to use maximum braking
B) Go around, climb to pattern altitude, and reassess. If the runway length is genuinely insufficient, divert to an airport with a longer runway. A stable approach to a runway you can't stop on is still an unstable situation
C) Slip to lose altitude and land shorter
D) Land on the first third of the runway and hope for the best

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A stable approach doesn't guarantee a safe landing if the runway length is insufficient. The go-around is the correct response to any doubt about the ability to stop safely. After going around, the pilot can verify runway length, calculate landing distance, and either re-attempt with confidence or divert. This is the stabilized approach discipline extended to its logical conclusion -- the approach must be stable AND the landing must be achievable. Options A and D accept unnecessary risk. Option C may help but doesn't address the fundamental runway length concern.

### Q-A11-64 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-32)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-3, CJ-1

A student is practicing power-off 180-degree accuracy approaches. On the base-to-final turn, the student realizes they will be short of the runway. The student pulls back on the yoke to "stretch" the glide. What does the CFI need to teach about this instinct?

A) Pulling back is the correct response to being low -- it raises the nose and gains altitude
B) Pulling back increases angle of attack and temporarily raises the nose, but it also decreases airspeed and increases drag. The aircraft actually descends faster, not slower. At low speed in a turn, the increased AOA can exceed the critical angle, producing a stall/spin. The correct response to being low on a power-off approach is to accept the undershoot and land safely short, or add power if available
C) The student should push the nose down to gain speed and then pull up
D) Power-off approaches should not be practiced at pattern altitude

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The instinct to "stretch the glide" by pulling back is one of the most dangerous reflexes in aviation. It feels right -- raise the nose, gain altitude. But the physics work against the pilot: increased AOA at constant power (zero, in this case) trades airspeed for a momentary pitch change, but the increased drag actually steepens the descent. Worse, in a base-to-final turn, the increased AOA combined with bank angle and possible uncoordinated flight is the stall/spin entry per AC 61-83K A.11.5. CFIs must actively train students to resist this instinct.

### Q-A11-65 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-33)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-2

A CFI is observing a student's approach and notices the following sequence: the student turns base slightly late, overshoots final by 10 degrees, adds 5 degrees of bank to correct, and is now stable on final with adequate airspeed. Should the CFI intervene?

A) Yes -- any overshoot requires a go-around
B) No immediate intervention needed, but the CFI should debrief the overshoot and discuss how a 10-degree overshoot with a 5-degree correction is manageable, while a 30-degree overshoot tempts a 40-degree bank correction that enters LOC territory. The teaching point is recognizing the threshold where correction becomes dangerous
C) No -- the student self-corrected, so no discussion is needed
D) Yes -- the CFI should have taken the controls during the overshoot

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Not every overshoot is a crisis. A small overshoot with a small, coordinated correction is normal pattern flying. The CFI's job is to use this as a teaching moment: help the student understand the spectrum from "minor overshoot, gentle correction" to "major overshoot, aggressive correction that kills." The student needs to learn where the line is so they can recognize when an overshoot has crossed from correctable to go-around territory. Option A is overly rigid. Option C misses the teaching opportunity. Option D is disproportionate.

### Q-A11-66 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-33)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AC-1, RM-1

A CFI is designing a lesson plan focused on preventing base-to-final LOC. Which of the following exercises should be included? (Select all that apply)

A) Practicing overshoot recognition and go-around execution at safe altitude
B) Flying the traffic pattern with deliberate emphasis on adjusting the base turn point for wind
C) Demonstrating the effect of bank angle on stall speed using clearing turns at altitude
D) Practicing steep turns at 60 degrees of bank in the traffic pattern
E) Discussing NTSB accident reports involving base-to-final stall/spin accidents

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Each of these builds a component of base-to-final LOC prevention. Overshoot recognition and go-around practice (A) builds the correct response. Wind-adjusted base turns (B) prevent the overshoot in the first place. Bank angle and stall speed demonstrations at altitude (C) build understanding of the aerodynamics. Accident case studies (E) provide real-world context. Option D is dangerous -- steep turns at 60 degrees in the pattern is practicing the exact behavior that causes the accidents.
