# A.11.1 -- Training Accidents

Training accidents have occurred because the flight instructor and student both failed to recognize developing situations.

---

### Q-A11-17 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-09)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-2, CJ-2

During a dual instruction flight, a student begins a steep turn at 3,000 feet AGL. The student allows the nose to drop, airspeed increases to VNO, and bank steepens past 60 degrees. The CFI has been looking at the sectional chart and doesn't notice until the VSI shows 1,500 fpm descent. What does this scenario illustrate about training accidents?

A) The student needs more steep turn practice before attempting this maneuver
B) Both the CFI and the student failed to recognize the developing situation -- the CFI's attention lapse removed the safety backup that dual instruction is supposed to provide
C) The instruments were difficult to read, causing the delay
D) Steep turns should only be practiced above 5,000 feet AGL

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.1, training accidents occur because both the instructor and student fail to recognize developing situations. The CFI's inattention eliminated the dual-instruction safety margin. A student in training is expected to make errors -- that's why the CFI is present. When the CFI's attention is diverted, neither party is monitoring the flight path, and a developing LOC goes unrecognized until it becomes critical. Option A blames the student for a systemic failure. Options C and D are deflections.

### Q-A11-18 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-09)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-2, AC-1

Which of the following CFI behaviors increase the risk of a training accident where both instructor and student fail to recognize a developing LOC situation? (Select all that apply)

A) Programming avionics or reviewing charts during student maneuvers
B) Maintaining a continuous scan of flight instruments while coaching the student
C) Conducting lengthy verbal explanations during critical phases of flight
D) Allowing fatigue to degrade the CFI's own situational awareness over a long training day
E) Establishing clear intervention criteria before each maneuver

**Correct:** A, C, D
**Explanation:** Training accidents happen when the CFI's attention is diverted from the developing situation. Programming avionics during maneuvers (A), lengthy talking during critical phases (C), and CFI fatigue degrading awareness (D) all create windows where neither the student nor the instructor is effectively monitoring the flight. Option B -- continuous instrument scan -- is protective, not risky. Option E -- pre-briefed intervention criteria -- reduces risk by establishing clear triggers for CFI action.

### Q-A11-19 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-10)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, AC-3

A CFI is training a student for their instrument rating. During a practice ILS approach under the hood, the student allows the aircraft to descend below glidepath and airspeed to decay. The CFI is heads-down adjusting the ATIS frequency. By the time either of them notices, the aircraft is in a nose-high, decelerating attitude 200 feet below the glideslope. What is the primary lesson for CFIs from this scenario?

A) Students should not practice ILS approaches until they can maintain glidepath independently
B) The CFI must never allow their primary attention to leave the student's flight path management, especially during approach phases where the margin for LOC is smallest
C) The ATIS should have been obtained earlier in the flight
D) The student should have spoken up when they felt the aircraft decelerating

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The CFI's primary job during dual instruction is monitoring the student's performance and maintaining the safety margin. Administrative tasks like obtaining ATIS must be secondary and timed for low-workload phases. During an approach, the CFI's attention should be on the student's flight path, airspeed, and configuration. AC 61-83K A.11.1 specifically identifies the dual failure to recognize developing situations as the cause of training accidents. Option C identifies a contributing factor but not the root cause. Option D expects the student to have awareness the training is supposed to develop.

### Q-A11-20 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-10)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-2, RM-1

A CFI has been flying four training flights today, each about 1.5 hours. It's now late afternoon and they're starting their fifth flight with a pre-solo student practicing power-off stalls. The CFI feels sluggish and catches themselves zoning out briefly. What should the CFI do?

A) Push through the lesson -- the student has already been charged for the flight
B) Switch to less demanding maneuvers like straight-and-level flight for the rest of the lesson
C) Recognize their own fatigue as a direct threat to the safety margin in dual instruction, discontinue the lesson, and debrief the student on why
D) Have the student practice ground reference maneuvers instead, since those are lower risk

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** A fatigued CFI who is zoning out during stall practice is exactly the scenario AC 61-83K A.11.1 describes: the instructor failing to recognize a developing situation. Stall practice with a pre-solo student has minimal margin for error, and the CFI's fatigue-degraded awareness removes the safety backup. The professional response is to acknowledge the limitation, stop the lesson, and model the same go/no-go decision-making we teach students about flying. Options B and D reduce risk but don't eliminate the core problem. Option A prioritizes economics over safety.

### Q-A11-21 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-11)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, AC-1

An NTSB report describes a training accident where the CFI and student were practicing slow flight. The aircraft entered an uncoordinated stall at 2,500 feet AGL. The CFI told investigators they "didn't realize how slow we had gotten." What systemic failure does this represent?

A) The aircraft's stall warning system malfunctioned
B) A breakdown in the instructor's scan discipline -- the CFI was not actively monitoring the airspeed and coordination that define the boundary between slow flight and a stall
C) The student deliberately stalled the aircraft
D) Slow flight should not be practiced below 3,000 feet AGL

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** "Didn't realize how slow we had gotten" is the signature of a CFI whose scan has broken down. During slow flight training, airspeed and coordination are the critical parameters. The CFI's role is to maintain continuous awareness of exactly how close the aircraft is to a stall, because the student -- by definition -- is still learning to do so. The statement reveals that neither the CFI nor the student was effectively monitoring the developing situation, which is the exact pattern AC 61-83K A.11.1 identifies. Option A is speculation. Option D addresses altitude but not the root cause.

### Q-A11-22 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-11)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-2, CJ-1

A flight school wants to reduce training accidents caused by mutual failure to recognize developing LOC situations. Which of the following policies would directly address this risk? (Select all that apply)

A) Limiting CFI duty days to no more than 6 hours of flight instruction
B) Requiring CFIs to verbalize their scan -- calling out airspeed, altitude, and configuration at regular intervals during student maneuvers
C) Installing angle-of-attack indicators in all training aircraft
D) Requiring a pre-maneuver briefing that includes specific parameters where the CFI will intervene
E) Painting the stall speed on the dashboard

**Correct:** A, B, D
**Explanation:** Each of these directly addresses the dual-failure problem. Limiting duty time (A) reduces CFI fatigue, which is a primary cause of attention lapses. Verbalizing the scan (B) forces the CFI to actively monitor parameters rather than passively watching. Pre-briefed intervention criteria (D) give the CFI specific triggers to act on, rather than relying on subjective judgment when fatigued. Option C adds a tool but doesn't change the scan behavior that caused the failure. Option E is too simplistic to affect scan discipline.

### Q-A11-23 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-12)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-3, CJ-2

During a training flight, a student practicing power-on stalls adds full power but fails to apply right rudder. The aircraft yaws left and the left wing drops sharply at the stall break. The CFI, who was coaching the student verbally, is surprised by the wing drop. What does this reveal about the CFI's performance?

A) The CFI correctly allowed the student to experience the full stall characteristics
B) The CFI failed to monitor coordination during the stall entry -- the absent right rudder was a predictable precursor to the asymmetric wing drop, and the CFI should have prompted for rudder before the stall break
C) Power-on stalls are inherently unpredictable and the wing drop was unavoidable
D) The student should have been taught rudder inputs during ground school

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** In a power-on stall, the absence of right rudder to counter P-factor and torque is a well-known setup for an asymmetric stall break and potential incipient spin. The CFI should have been monitoring the student's rudder input as part of the stall entry, not just coaching verbally about the overall maneuver. The "surprise" indicates the CFI was not scanning the specific parameters that predict the outcome. This is the same dual-failure pattern from AC 61-83K A.11.1 -- both missed the developing situation. Option A confuses allowing stall experience with allowing a preventable hazard. Option C mischaracterizes a predictable outcome as unpredictable.

### Q-A11-24 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-12)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, RM-2

A newly certificated CFI asks a senior instructor: "How do I balance teaching the student with keeping the airplane safe? Sometimes I get so focused on explaining something that I lose track of where we are." What is the best advice?

A) Always maintain your instrument scan as the primary task -- teaching is secondary to safety monitoring during flight
B) Keep all instruction to ground sessions and fly silently
C) Let the student handle everything and only intervene if they ask for help
D) Focus on teaching during flight -- the student will handle the flying

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** This new CFI has identified the exact vulnerability AC 61-83K A.11.1 describes: attention diverted from flight monitoring to teaching. The correct hierarchy is aviate, navigate, communicate -- and for a CFI, "aviate" means monitoring the student's aviating. Teaching discussions can happen during straight-and-level cruise or on the ground, but during maneuvers and critical phases, the CFI's scan must be primary. Option B eliminates valuable in-flight teaching. Option C removes the safety margin. Option D inverts the priority.

### Q-A11-25 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-13)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, AC-2

A CFI is teaching a student to recover from unusual attitudes. During one recovery, the student correctly identifies a nose-low, wings-level attitude but applies excessive aft elevator, causing the aircraft to pitch up rapidly through level flight into a nose-high attitude. The CFI does not intervene because the aircraft is now "right side up." Why is this a training accident risk?

A) The aircraft is now safe and no intervention is needed
B) The student has traded one unusual attitude for another -- the rapid pitch-up could lead to an accelerated stall, and the CFI's failure to recognize this as a developing LOC situation is the same dual-failure pattern that causes training accidents
C) The student should have applied aileron instead of elevator
D) Unusual attitude recovery should only be practiced in simulators

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** An aggressive pitch-up from a dive recovery can impose excessive G-loading and lead directly to an accelerated stall -- trading one LOC condition for another. The CFI who sees "nose above horizon" and relaxes has stopped monitoring the developing situation. The student over-corrected, which is normal in training, but the CFI must recognize that the over-correction itself is now the hazard. This is precisely the AC 61-83K A.11.1 pattern: both failed to recognize the developing situation.

### Q-A11-26 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-13)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-1, AC-3

What steps should a CFI take before beginning any maneuver with elevated LOC risk during a training flight? (Select all that apply)

A) Brief the student on the specific parameters (airspeed, bank, pitch, altitude) that define the maneuver's safe envelope
B) State the specific conditions under which the CFI will take the controls
C) Verify the student's understanding by asking them to repeat the recovery procedure
D) Begin the maneuver quickly to minimize the student's anxiety
E) Confirm adequate altitude and clear the area for traffic

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Pre-maneuver preparation is the CFI's primary defense against the dual-failure pattern. Briefing the safe envelope (A) gives both parties shared situational awareness. Stating intervention criteria (B) ensures the CFI has predefined triggers rather than relying on in-the-moment judgment. Verifying understanding (C) confirms the student knows the recovery. Clearing the area and confirming altitude (E) ensures adequate safety margin. Option D prioritizes speed over preparation, which increases the risk of both parties being caught off-guard.
