### Q-A11-01 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, AC-2

Your student is on final approach and overshoots the base-to-final turn. The student increases bank angle to 40 degrees and airspeed is decreasing through 70 knots. The stall horn has not activated yet. What is the correct CFI intervention?

A) Wait for the stall horn before intervening -- the student needs to experience the cues
B) Prompt the student to check airspeed and bank angle, and coach them to level wings and go around if they don't immediately respond
C) Take the controls immediately -- this is an emergency
D) Tell the student to add more rudder to tighten the turn

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This is the classic base-to-final stall/spin setup identified by the GAJSC as the leading LOC accident profile. At 40 degrees of bank with decreasing airspeed, the situation is degrading but not yet critical. The CFI should prompt the student to recognize the trend (airspeed, bank angle) and coach toward the safe action (level wings, go around). If the student doesn't respond, the CFI must escalate to direct intervention. Option A waits too long -- the stall horn is a late-stage indicator and in a turning stall, the spin may develop before the horn activates. Option C is premature but defensible. Option D is the worst possible advice -- adding rudder in a steep, slow turn accelerates the spin entry.

### Q-A11-02 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-2, CJ-2

During a power-on stall recovery, your student pushes the nose down aggressively, releases back pressure entirely, and enters a steep descent. What does this reveal about their stall recovery training?

A) The student has a strong stall recovery instinct
B) The student has learned to "break the stall" but not to recover to controlled flight -- they're trained on half the maneuver
C) This is the correct recovery technique for a power-on stall
D) The student panicked and should not attempt stalls until they're calmer

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A proper stall recovery reduces angle of attack to break the stall, then smoothly recovers to a positive rate of climb with minimal altitude loss. A student who pushes aggressively and enters a steep descent has learned only the "break" portion. The CFI needs to teach the complete recovery: reduce AOA, add power, level wings, recover to climb. This is a training gap, not a panic response. Option A rewards an incomplete technique. Option C is incorrect -- the recovery should not result in a steep descent. Option D misidentifies training gaps as emotional problems.

### Q-A11-03 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, RM-2

Your student is flying a gusty crosswind approach. On short final, the student is struggling with control inputs and the approach is clearly unstable -- airspeed fluctuating +/-10 knots, drifting laterally. The student says "I can still make it." What should the CFI do?

A) Allow the student to continue for the learning experience, ready to take controls on landing
B) Direct the student to execute a go-around immediately -- an unstable approach in gusty crosswinds with a struggling student is a LOC setup
C) Coach the student on crosswind technique while continuing the approach
D) Take the controls and land the airplane

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** An unstable approach is a go-around, period. Adding gusty crosswinds and a student who is already behind the airplane makes this a textbook LOC precursor. The student's "I can still make it" is the same overconfidence that drives base-to-final accidents. The CFI must model the discipline to go around when the approach is unstable, regardless of how close to the runway they are. Option A accepts an unacceptable risk. Option C tries to teach during a critical phase of flight. Option D resolves the safety issue but misses the chance to teach go-around decision-making.

### Q-A11-04 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-02)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AC-2, CJ-1

Which of the following are precursors to a base-to-final stall/spin accident that a CFI should teach students to recognize? (Select all that apply)

A) Overshooting the turn from base to final
B) Increasing bank angle to correct toward runway centerline
C) Applying bottom rudder (inside rudder) to tighten the turn
D) Maintaining or increasing back pressure while in a steep turn
E) Maintaining stable airspeed throughout the turn

**Correct:** A, B, C, D
**Explanation:** The base-to-final stall/spin chain typically involves: overshoot (A) leading to increased bank (B), bottom rudder to "help" the turn (C), and increasing back pressure to maintain altitude in the turn (D). Together these create an uncoordinated, low-speed, high-AOA condition at low altitude. Option E -- stable airspeed -- is the opposite: it's an indicator of a safe turn. Teaching students to recognize these precursors as a chain, not isolated events, is the core of LOC prevention.

### Q-A11-05 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-03)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-3, CJ-2

During a training flight, you need to take the controls from your student because of an imminent safety threat. What is the correct procedure?

A) Grab the controls and say "I have it" -- speed is critical
B) Say "I have the flight controls," confirm the student responds "You have the flight controls," then take physical control -- but compress the exchange if safety requires immediate action
C) Say "My controls" and take over
D) Take the controls without verbal exchange to avoid confusing the student

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The standard three-step positive exchange of controls is: "I have the flight controls" / "You have the flight controls" / "I have the flight controls." In an emergency, the CFI may need to compress this but should still verbalize the transfer. AC 61-83K emphasizes that dual accidents often involve confusion about who was flying. Option A is close but uses non-standard phraseology. Option C uses non-standard wording. Option D creates the exact ambiguity that causes dual accidents.

### Q-A11-06 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-03)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, AC-2

A student practicing touch-and-goes is getting progressively sloppier on each pattern. On the fourth circuit, the student turns base early, is low, and is fast. The student hasn't made any dangerous errors yet. What should the CFI prioritize?

A) Let the student continue -- they're legal and safe, and pattern work requires repetition
B) Recognize the degradation trend and stop the pattern work. Discuss what changed from the first pattern to the fourth, and address the root cause before continuing
C) Extend the lesson to give the student more practice
D) Demonstrate a perfect pattern for the student to copy

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Progressive degradation in the pattern is a warning sign. The student may be fatigued, saturated, or reinforcing bad habits through repetition. Continuing the pattern without addressing the trend risks normalizing the degraded performance. The CFI should break the cycle, diagnose the cause (fatigue, diminishing concentration, confusion about a specific element), and either address it or end the lesson. Option A ignores the trend. Option C extends practice when the student's capacity is clearly diminishing. Option D doesn't address why the student is degrading.

**Competencies:** AC-1, RM-2

The GAJSC identified LOC as the number one cause of GA fatalities from 2012 through 2021. A CFI is preparing a safety briefing for a flying club. Which statement most accurately reflects the GAJSC findings?

A) LOC fatalities are primarily caused by mechanical failures beyond pilot control
B) The overwhelming majority of GA LOC accidents are avoidable, and flight instructors can make a significant impact through targeted mentoring and training
C) LOC accidents occur mainly during instrument flight and are not a significant factor in VFR operations
D) LOC prevention is primarily the responsibility of aircraft manufacturers through improved stall protection systems

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11, the GAJSC cited LOC as the number one cause of GA fatalities from 2012-2021, and the overwhelming majority are avoidable. Flight instructors are specifically called out as being able to make a significant impact through targeted mentoring and training. Option A deflects responsibility to mechanical causes. Option C ignores the many VFR LOC profiles (base-to-final, departure, traffic pattern). Option D shifts responsibility away from the training environment.

**Competencies:** AC-2, CJ-1

A CFI is designing a LOC prevention module for their flight school's recurrent training program. Which of the following approaches align with the GAJSC's emphasis on targeted mentoring? (Select all that apply)

A) Tailoring stall/spin awareness training to the specific aircraft types flown at the school
B) Reviewing local accident history to identify LOC patterns relevant to the school's operating environment
C) Requiring all students to memorize the GAJSC accident statistics by year
D) Developing scenario-based discussions around the most common LOC accident profiles

### Q-A11-09 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-05)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-1

A CFI notices that a private pilot seeking a flight review has developed several LOC-risk habits: consistently flying uncoordinated, making steep turns in the pattern, and using excessive back pressure in slow flight. The pilot has 400 hours and flies regularly. What is the CFI's best approach?

A) Sign off the flight review with a recommendation to practice coordination -- the pilot is experienced and current
B) Decline to sign the flight review, explain the specific LOC risk factors observed, and propose a targeted training plan addressing each habit
C) Sign off the flight review but require the pilot to complete an online LOC awareness course
D) Demonstrate proper technique once and sign off if the pilot can replicate it

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Each of the observed habits -- uncoordinated flight, steep pattern turns, excessive back pressure in slow flight -- is a direct LOC precursor identified by the GAJSC. Experience and currency do not equal proficiency. The CFI should use the flight review as the targeted mentoring opportunity it's designed to be: identify specific risks, explain why they matter, and build a training plan. Option A normalizes dangerous habits. Option C substitutes ground knowledge for stick-and-rudder correction. Option D tests mimicry, not understanding.

### Q-A11-10 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-05)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, AC-3

A flight school's insurance company reports that 60% of their LOC incidents in the past five years involved pilots within the first 100 hours after earning their private certificate. As the school's chief instructor, what is the most effective systemic response?

A) Require all new private pilots to complete 10 additional hours of dual instruction before solo cross-country
B) Implement a structured post-certificate mentoring program that pairs new pilots with experienced CFIs for their first 50 hours of PIC time, focused on the specific LOC profiles most common in early PIC flying

The CFI mentors can focus on the LOC profiles most relevant to that transition: first flights in unfamiliar conditions, pressure to complete trips, and degraded skills without the safety net of an instructor. Option A adds hours before the risk window. Option C is passive. Option D addresses the wrong phase of training.

### Q-A11-11 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-06)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, CJ-2

A CFI is conducting a BFR and asks the pilot about their typical flying. The pilot says: "I mostly fly $100 hamburger runs on weekends -- short trips, good weather. I haven't done any stalls or slow flight since my checkride three years ago." What LOC risk does this profile represent?

A) Minimal risk -- the pilot flies regularly and in good conditions
B) Elevated risk -- regular flying maintains takeoff and landing skills but not the slow-flight and unusual-attitude skills needed to recover from LOC events
C) High risk only if the pilot flies a complex aircraft
D) No additional risk beyond any other active pilot

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Currency (regular flying) does not equal proficiency in all skill areas. A pilot who flies frequently but only in a narrow flight envelope -- short trips, good weather, no maneuvering practice -- maintains pattern and cruise skills but allows stall awareness, slow flight control, and unusual attitude recovery skills to atrophy. These are exactly the skills needed when a LOC event develops. The GAJSC data shows LOC is most likely when pilots lack proficiency in these specific areas. Option A confuses currency with comprehensive proficiency. Options C and D underestimate the risk.

### Q-A11-13 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-07)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, AC-2

A CFI is reviewing NTSB accident reports as part of their own professional development. They notice that a recurring theme in LOC fatal accidents is the pilot's failure to recognize the developing situation. How should this finding influence the CFI's teaching approach?

A) Emphasize memorizing the exact conditions that precede each type of LOC accident
B) Focus training on developing the student's ability to recognize trends and degrading conditions in real time, using scenarios that build pattern recognition
C) Increase the number of stall recoveries practiced during each lesson
D) Teach students to rely primarily on aircraft automation to prevent LOC

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The recurring theme of failure to recognize developing situations means the problem is perceptual and cognitive, not procedural. Students need to build pattern recognition -- the ability to notice when airspeed is trending down, bank is increasing, coordination is degrading -- before the situation becomes critical. Scenario-based training builds this recognition skill. Option A teaches facts rather than perception. Option C practices recovery rather than prevention. Option D shifts responsibility to automation that may not exist in training aircraft.

### Q-A11-14 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-07)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-3, RM-2

A senior CFI at a busy flight school notices that several of the school's newer CFIs tend to let students get very close to LOC conditions before intervening, stating they want students to "feel the full stall." What guidance should the senior CFI provide?

A) Support the approach -- students learn best from full stall experience
B) Explain that while experiencing the stall is valuable, the CFI's intervention point must be based on the student's demonstrated ability to recover, the altitude available, and the specific maneuver -- not a one-size-fits-all policy of waiting for the full stall
C) Prohibit any stall training until students have at least 20 hours
D) Require all stall training be conducted in a simulator first

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** LOC prevention training requires the CFI to calibrate their intervention threshold to each student and situation. A blanket policy of waiting for the full stall ignores the variables that determine whether the situation is still recoverable: the student's skill level, altitude, aircraft configuration, and the specific maneuver being practiced. The GAJSC notes that training accidents occur when the instructor fails to recognize developing situations -- this applies to the CFI's own judgment about when to intervene. Option A ignores risk management. Options C and D unnecessarily restrict training.

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-1, CJ-1

A CFI is planning a lesson on LOC prevention for a private pilot who wants to start flying a higher-performance aircraft. Which elements should the CFI include in the targeted mentoring plan? (Select all that apply)

A) Review the specific LOC accident profiles associated with the new aircraft type
B) Practice slow flight and stalls in the new aircraft to establish the pilot's baseline awareness of its handling characteristics
C) Discuss how the higher approach speeds, different power responses, and changed sight picture affect LOC risk in the traffic pattern
D) Skip the ground portion and go straight to flying -- the pilot already has a private certificate
E) Brief the specific departure and arrival procedures that reduce LOC risk at the pilot's home airport

Reviewing type-specific accidents (A) provides context. Practicing slow flight and stalls (B) builds familiarity with the new aircraft's handling near LOC boundaries. Discussing changed approach dynamics (C) addresses traffic pattern LOC risk. Briefing airport-specific procedures (E) grounds the training in the pilot's actual operating environment. Option D skips the critical ground preparation that frames the flight training.
