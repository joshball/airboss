# A.11.7 -- Pilot Proficiency

Studies show LOC is most likely when pilots lack proficiency. AC 61-98 and the Airplane Flying Handbook address this.

---

### Q-A11-77 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-39)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, AC-2

A private pilot who flies 30 hours per year -- mostly weekend local flights -- comes to a CFI for a flight review. The pilot is current per 14 CFR 61.57 (three takeoffs and landings in the past 90 days). During the flight, the CFI observes that the pilot's stall recognition is slow, unusual attitude recovery is hesitant, and crosswind technique is poor. What distinction does this scenario illustrate?

A) The pilot is safe because they meet currency requirements
B) Currency and proficiency are different things. Currency means meeting the minimum regulatory requirements to exercise privileges. Proficiency means possessing the skills to handle the full range of situations the pilot may encounter. This pilot is current but not proficient in the skills most likely to prevent LOC
C) The pilot just needs more flight time to become proficient
D) Currency requirements should be increased to ensure proficiency

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.7, LOC is most likely when pilots lack proficiency. The distinction between currency (meeting regulatory minimums) and proficiency (possessing real skills) is fundamental. Three landings in 90 days satisfies 61.57 but does nothing to maintain stall awareness, unusual attitude recovery, or crosswind skills. CFIs conducting flight reviews should evaluate proficiency across the full range of LOC-relevant skills, not just verify currency. Option A conflates currency with safety. Option C assumes time alone builds skills without targeted practice.

### Q-A11-78 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-39)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-2, AC-1

Which of the following skills are most likely to degrade in a pilot who flies regularly but only in benign conditions (calm winds, VFR, familiar airports)? (Select all that apply)

A) Stall recognition and recovery
B) Crosswind takeoff and landing technique
C) Straight-and-level cruise flight
D) Unusual attitude recognition and recovery
E) Emergency procedures including engine failure response

**Correct:** A, B, D, E
**Explanation:** Skills that are not practiced degrade -- this is the core message of AC 61-83K A.11.7. A pilot who flies only in benign conditions maintains the skills they use (cruise, calm-wind landings at familiar airports) but allows LOC-critical skills to atrophy: stall awareness (A), crosswind technique (B), unusual attitude recovery (D), and emergency procedures (E). These are exactly the skills needed when conditions exceed the pilot's normal envelope. Option C -- straight-and-level cruise -- is the one skill this pilot practices constantly and retains.

### Q-A11-79 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-40)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-3, CJ-2

A CFI is advising a pilot who wants to establish personal minimums. The pilot asks: "Should my personal minimums change over time?" What is the correct guidance?

A) No -- once established, personal minimums should remain fixed for consistency
B) Yes -- personal minimums should be dynamic. They should be more conservative when the pilot's proficiency is lower (hasn't flown recently, hasn't practiced specific skills) and can be carefully relaxed as proficiency increases through targeted practice. They should also be tightened when flying an unfamiliar aircraft, airport, or in conditions the pilot hasn't recently practiced
C) Personal minimums are unnecessary -- the regulatory minimums are sufficient
D) Personal minimums should only be set by the pilot's CFI

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-98 and AC 61-83K A.11.7, personal minimums are a proficiency-management tool. They should reflect the pilot's current skill level, recent experience, and the specific conditions of the planned flight. A pilot who hasn't practiced crosswinds in six months should have higher crosswind minimums than one who practiced last week. The concept is self-assessment: honestly evaluating what you're currently proficient to handle, not what you could handle at your best. Option A makes minimums a fixed rule rather than a dynamic tool. Option C ignores the proficiency gap between currency and competence.

### Q-A11-80 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-40)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, CJ-1

Research on skill degradation shows that complex motor skills (like stall recovery) degrade faster than simple ones (like straight-and-level flight). A pilot who last practiced stall recovery 18 months ago is planning a flight in gusty conditions. What should the CFI recommend?

A) The pilot is fine -- stall recovery is like riding a bicycle
B) The pilot should schedule a proficiency session to practice stall recognition and recovery before flying in gusty conditions. Eighteen months without practice means the pilot's stall recovery skills are likely significantly degraded, and gusty conditions increase the likelihood of encountering an unexpected stall
C) The pilot should review the stall recovery procedure in the POH and that's sufficient
D) Gusty conditions don't increase stall risk

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Skill degradation is real and measurable. Complex motor skills with cognitive components (recognizing the stall, initiating the correct recovery, managing the aircraft through recovery) degrade significantly over 18 months without practice. Gusty conditions increase the chance of encountering wind shear, sudden AOA changes, or airspeed fluctuations that could push the aircraft toward a stall. The combination of degraded recovery skills and increased stall probability is the LOC risk profile AC 61-83K A.11.7 describes. Option A is a dangerous myth -- complex motor skills do degrade without practice. Option C substitutes knowledge for skill.

### Q-A11-81 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-41)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-2, RM-2

AC 61-98 recommends recurrent training beyond the minimum flight review requirements. A pilot asks a CFI: "I already do my flight review every 24 months. Why would I need recurrent training on top of that?" What is the best response?

A) "The flight review is sufficient if you pass it."
B) "The flight review is a snapshot -- it evaluates your skills at one point in time and covers broad areas. Recurrent training targets the specific skills most likely to degrade and most critical for safety, like stall/spin awareness, emergency procedures, and instrument reference skills. Twenty-four months between evaluations leaves long periods where degradation goes undetected."
C) "Recurrent training is only required for commercial pilots."
D) "You can substitute online courses for recurrent training."

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.7 and AC 61-98, the flight review's 24-month interval is a regulatory minimum, not an optimal training schedule. Complex LOC-prevention skills degrade faster than 24 months. Recurrent training -- whether through CFI sessions, proficiency programs like WINGS, or structured self-study with flight practice -- fills the gap between flight reviews. The goal is continuous proficiency maintenance, not periodic pass/fail evaluation. Option A accepts the minimum as the standard. Option C is factually incorrect. Option D may have some value but doesn't address the flight skills component.

### Q-A11-82 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-41)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** CJ-2, AC-3

A CFI is designing a proficiency maintenance program for a flying club. Which elements should be included to address LOC-related skill degradation? (Select all that apply)

A) Quarterly stall/spin awareness practice with a CFI
B) Annual emergency procedures review including simulated engine failures
C) Monthly dues payment verification
D) Biannual instrument reference skills practice for VFR-only pilots (simulated inadvertent IMC)
E) Personalized proficiency goals based on each pilot's flying patterns and identified weak areas

**Correct:** A, B, D, E
**Explanation:** Each of A, B, D, and E directly addresses skill degradation in LOC-critical areas. Quarterly stall practice (A) prevents the most dangerous skill from atrophying. Annual emergency review (B) maintains decision-making under pressure. Instrument reference practice (D) maintains the survival skill for inadvertent IMC. Personalized goals (E) target each pilot's specific weaknesses rather than applying a one-size-fits-all program. Option C is an administrative item, not a proficiency element.

### Q-A11-83 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-42)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, AC-1

A pilot who hasn't flown in four months is planning a trip with their family. The pilot holds a current medical and has met the 90-day currency requirements (three takeoffs and landings were done four months ago, and three more were done recently to re-establish currency). What should the CFI advise?

A) The pilot is legal and can fly whenever they want
B) The pilot should consider a proficiency flight with a CFI before taking passengers. Four months without flying, followed by three landings to re-establish currency, does not restore proficiency in the full range of skills needed for safe passenger-carrying flight. The responsible approach is to fly with a CFI first and verify that all skills -- not just landings -- are at an acceptable level
C) The three recent landings are sufficient preparation
D) The pilot should wait another month before flying with passengers

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This scenario perfectly illustrates the currency-versus-proficiency gap from AC 61-83K A.11.7. Three landings satisfy 61.57 but exercise only one narrow skill. A pilot returning from a four-month break has degraded skills in areas the currency flights didn't address: stall awareness, crosswind technique, emergency procedures, situational awareness, and scan discipline. Adding passengers increases the stakes and the distraction. A CFI proficiency flight is the responsible preparation. Option A is legally correct but operationally reckless. Option C assumes landings equal comprehensive proficiency.

### Q-A11-84 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-42)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, RM-2

A pilot uses the WINGS Pilot Proficiency Program to track and maintain proficiency. Another pilot says: "I don't need WINGS -- I fly every week." How should a CFI respond to the second pilot's statement?

A) "You're right -- frequent flying is all you need."
B) "Frequency doesn't equal breadth. If you fly every week but only do the same local flight, you're maintaining proficiency in one narrow skill set while letting others degrade. WINGS and similar programs provide structure to ensure you're practicing the full range of skills, including the ones you don't use regularly -- which are exactly the ones that save your life in an emergency."
C) "WINGS is mandatory for all pilots."
D) "Weekly flying is better than any proficiency program."

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.7, proficiency means maintaining skills across the full range of conditions a pilot may encounter. A pilot who flies weekly but only in a narrow envelope (same airport, same conditions, same maneuvers) is proficient in that envelope but not in the emergency and unusual-condition skills that prevent LOC. Structured proficiency programs like WINGS force pilots to practice outside their comfort zone. The CFI's role is to help pilots understand that proficiency is multidimensional, not unidimensional. Option A validates a false sense of security.

### Q-A11-85 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-43)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-2, CJ-2

A CFI is conducting a flight review with a pilot who last flew six months ago. The pilot performs takeoffs and landings adequately but struggles with slow flight -- they can't maintain altitude within 100 feet or heading within 10 degrees. The pilot says: "I never do slow flight in real life, so it doesn't matter." How should the CFI respond?

A) "You're right -- slow flight isn't a practical skill for most flying."
B) "Slow flight proficiency is directly tied to LOC prevention. Every approach is slow flight. Every stall recovery begins in slow flight. The skills you practice in slow flight -- precise pitch control, coordination, power management at high AOA -- are the same skills that prevent a stall/spin in the traffic pattern. Your difficulty maintaining altitude and heading in slow flight tells me your precision flying skills have degraded, and those are the skills that keep you alive when conditions get demanding."
C) "We can skip slow flight and focus on landings."
D) "Slow flight is only required for the practical test."

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The pilot's dismissal of slow flight reveals a gap in understanding how skills connect to safety. Slow flight proficiency is directly relevant to every approach, every go-around, every unexpected encounter near stall speed. Per AC 61-83K A.11.7, LOC is most likely when proficiency is low. The inability to maintain parameters in slow flight is a measurable indicator of degraded precision flying skills -- the same skills that prevent LOC during normal operations. The CFI must connect the training exercise to the real-world consequence.

### Q-A11-86 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-43)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-1, AC-3

According to AC 61-83K and AC 61-98, which of the following are recommended strategies for maintaining pilot proficiency between flight reviews? (Select all that apply)

A) Participating in the FAA WINGS Pilot Proficiency Program
B) Scheduling periodic dual instruction focused on specific skill areas
C) Flying the same route repeatedly to build familiarity
D) Setting personal proficiency goals and tracking progress against them
E) Practicing emergency procedures and unusual attitudes with a CFI at regular intervals

**Correct:** A, B, D, E
**Explanation:** Each of these strategies addresses the proficiency maintenance gap between flight reviews. WINGS (A) provides a structured framework. Periodic dual instruction (B) gives expert evaluation and feedback. Personal goals (D) create accountability. Emergency and unusual attitude practice (E) maintain the most perishable LOC-critical skills. Option C -- flying the same route repeatedly -- builds familiarity with that route but does nothing to maintain the breadth of skills needed for LOC prevention. Proficiency requires practicing outside the comfort zone, not deepening the rut.
