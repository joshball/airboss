### Q-A10-01 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1, RM-3

An aircraft owner brings their airplane to you for a flight review of the pilot who flies it. The owner says, "He's a great pilot, just get him through this quickly so we can use the plane this weekend." What is the appropriate response?

A) Expedite the review since the owner is a client and you want to maintain the business relationship
B) Acknowledge the owner's timeline but explain that the flight review must be conducted to standard regardless of external schedule pressure, and that you'll be thorough but efficient
C) Ask the owner to observe the flight review so they can see the pilot's proficiency firsthand
D) Refer the pilot to another CFI who might be faster

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** External pressure to expedite evaluations is one of the most common ethical challenges CFIs face. The professional response is to acknowledge the pressure without yielding to it. A CFI's obligation is to the safety of the flight review, not to the owner's schedule. Option A prioritizes business over safety. Option C introduces a distraction. Option D passes the ethical problem rather than addressing it.

### Q-A10-02 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1

You discover that another CFI at your flight school is endorsing students for checkrides who, in your professional opinion, are not ready. Two of these students have failed their practical tests. What is the most professional course of action?

A) Mind your own business -- each CFI is responsible for their own students
B) Speak with the CFI privately about your observations, expressing concern for student safety and the school's reputation, and escalate to the chief instructor if the pattern continues
C) Report the CFI to the FSDO immediately
D) Warn students not to train with that CFI

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Professionalism includes addressing concerns within the professional community before escalating externally. A private conversation gives the CFI the opportunity to explain or correct their approach. If the pattern continues, escalation to the chief instructor (who has training oversight responsibility in Part 141) or management is appropriate. Option A ignores a safety issue. Option C skips proportional steps. Option D is unprofessional and potentially slanderous.

### Q-A10-03 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1, PS-2

You are a new CFI building hours. A Part 135 operator offers you a job, but you'll need to continue instructing part-time to maintain your skills. A conflict arises: a student needs extra training before a solo cross-country endorsement, but you're scheduled for a 135 trip that pays significantly more. What is the ethical choice?

A) Fly the 135 trip -- the student can wait and the pay difference is substantial
B) Honor your commitment to the student. If you agreed to the training schedule, follow through. Arrange the 135 schedule around your instructional commitments, not the other way around
C) Have another CFI cover the student's lesson
D) Endorse the student for the cross-country now to free up your schedule

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Professional ethics means honoring commitments, especially when a student's progression depends on continuity of instruction. Rearranging instruction around higher-paying work signals that students are secondary. Option A prioritizes money over professional commitment. Option C disrupts the student's continuity without their consent. Option D is the most dangerous -- endorsing a student who needs more training to free up your schedule is exactly the kind of ethical failure that leads to accidents.

### Q-A10-04 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1, RM-3

During a checkride with a DPE, your student makes a mistake that the DPE doesn't notice. The student looks at you. What should you do?

A) Stay silent -- the DPE is the evaluator, and your job is done
B) Note the error and debrief it with the student after the checkride, regardless of the outcome
C) Point out the error to the DPE
D) Signal to the student to correct it

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** During a practical test, the DPE is the evaluator. The CFI's role is as an observer (if present at all). Intervening during the checkride is inappropriate. However, professional integrity requires that you not ignore the error. Debriefing it afterward teaches the student that standards don't change based on who's watching. Option A abandons the teaching responsibility. Option C inappropriately intervenes in the evaluation. Option D is interference with the practical test.

### Q-A10-05 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-03)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** PS-1, PS-2

Which of the following behaviors demonstrate professional ethics for a flight instructor? (Select all that apply)

A) Preparing thoroughly for each lesson, even with students you've taught many times
B) Declining to provide instruction in an aircraft or operation you're not experienced in
C) Accepting a student's social media connection request during training to build rapport
D) Maintaining consistent evaluation standards regardless of personal relationships with students
E) Documenting student performance honestly, including deficiencies

**Correct:** A, B, D, E
**Explanation:** Professionalism is demonstrated through consistent preparation (A), knowing and respecting your limits (B), applying standards equally (D), and honest documentation (E). Option C blurs professional boundaries during an active training relationship. While social connections after training may be appropriate, during training the CFI-student relationship requires professional distance to maintain objectivity in evaluations and honest feedback.

### Q-A10-06 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-04)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1, RC-2

A student asks you to sign off their logbook for a solo cross-country flight they completed last month. They explain they forgot to bring their logbook that day and "just need the entry backdated." You were not present for the flight and did not authorize it in advance. What should you do?

A) Sign the entry since the student seems honest and you have no reason to doubt they flew
B) Refuse to sign, explain that you cannot endorse flights you did not authorize, and document the conversation
C) Sign it but note "entry made after the fact" in the remarks
D) Ask another CFI who was at the airport that day to sign instead

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A CFI's logbook endorsements are legal documents. Signing for a flight you did not authorize or verify is falsification regardless of the student's honesty. Per AC 61-83K, ethics means maintaining standards even when it would be easier not to. Option A and C both involve signing for something you cannot verify. Option D passes the problem to someone else who also wasn't the authorizing instructor. The professional response is to refuse and explain why.

### Q-A10-07 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-04)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-2, CJ-1

You are a CFI at a small flight school. The school owner tells you to stop recommending that students get additional training beyond the minimum hours because "it's costing the school customers -- they go to the cheaper school down the road." How should you respond?

A) Comply with the owner's request since they run the business and you're an employee
B) Find subtle ways to extend training without the student noticing the extra hours
C) Explain that your training recommendations are based on individual student readiness, not minimum hours, and that you cannot compromise student safety for competitive pricing
D) Quit immediately on principle

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K defines a professional as someone compensated for unique skill. That skill includes the judgment to determine when a student is ready. A CFI who recommends training based on competitive pricing rather than student readiness has abandoned professional ethics. Option A subordinates safety judgment to business pressure. Option B is deceptive to the student. Option D is an overreaction -- the professional step is to advocate clearly first.

### Q-A10-08 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-05)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1, OD-2

You discover that a student has been logging PIC time for flights where they were not the sole manipulator of the controls and did not meet the regulatory requirements to log PIC. The student says "everyone does it" and another CFI at the school confirmed this is common practice. What is the ethical response?

A) Accept the explanation since local norms vary and you don't want to cause friction
B) Correct the student on the regulatory requirements for logging PIC time, require them to amend their logbook, and address the broader culture issue with school management
C) Report the student and the other CFI to the FSDO
D) Simply correct this student's entries and move on without raising the broader issue

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Ethics is defined as what a person does when no one is watching -- and when "everyone" is doing the wrong thing. A CFI who encounters systemic logbook inaccuracy has an obligation to correct both the individual error and the culture that produced it. Option A normalizes falsification. Option C skips proportional steps. Option D fixes the symptom without addressing the root cause, which will continue producing inaccurate logbooks.

### Q-A10-09 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-05)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** PS-1, PS-2, RM-3

A CFI sells their own training materials to students at a markup. Which of the following factors would create an ethical conflict of interest? (Select all that apply)

A) The CFI extends training beyond what is needed so the student must purchase additional materials
B) The CFI discloses the markup and offers alternatives from other publishers
C) The CFI requires their proprietary materials as a prerequisite for endorsement
D) The CFI recommends their materials alongside other options and lets the student choose
E) The CFI withholds endorsement from students who use competitor materials

**Correct:** A, C, E
**Explanation:** Conflict of interest arises when a CFI's financial interests compromise their instructional judgment. Extending training to sell materials (A), requiring proprietary materials for endorsement (C), and withholding endorsement based on purchasing decisions (E) all subordinate student interest to instructor profit. Transparent disclosure with alternatives (B) and non-coercive recommendations (D) are ethical because they preserve student choice and separate the financial transaction from instructional authority.

### Q-A10-10 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-06)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1, CJ-2

You are conducting a biennial flight review. Near the end of the flight portion, the pilot makes a series of errors that raise serious concerns about their proficiency. However, the pilot is a close personal friend who flew to your airport specifically for this review. What should you do?

A) Pass the pilot but recommend they get additional practice on their own before their next flight
B) Issue a notice of disapproval and clearly explain the deficiencies, offering to work with them on the specific areas that need improvement
C) Tell your friend you'll pass them this time but they need to be better prepared next time
D) Suggest they come back tomorrow to try again, without documenting today's flight

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K states that flight instructors should maintain the highest level of ethics "most particularly in their professional field." Personal relationships cannot influence evaluation standards. A flight review that overlooks genuine deficiencies puts the pilot and others at risk. Option A and C both result in an unqualified pilot flying. Option D avoids documentation, which is both dishonest and deprives the pilot of actionable feedback on their deficiencies.

### Q-A10-11 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-06)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-2, RC-1

A student pilot who has been training with you for several months confides that they've been flying solo without proper endorsements on days you're not at the airport. They assure you "nothing happened" and ask you to backdate the endorsements. What is the appropriate response?

A) Since no incidents occurred, backdate the endorsements to avoid creating problems for the student
B) Refuse to backdate endorsements, ground the student from solo flight pending a thorough review of their training record and judgment, and consider whether to continue the training relationship
C) Refuse to backdate but allow the student to continue solo flights going forward with proper endorsements
D) Backdate the endorsements but add extra ground training as a consequence

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This situation involves multiple ethical failures: unauthorized solo flight, request for falsification, and demonstrated poor judgment. A CFI cannot endorse what they didn't authorize. More critically, a student who flies solo without endorsement has shown a judgment deficiency that must be addressed before any further solo privileges. Option A and D involve document falsification. Option C ignores the serious judgment issue -- a student who violated the rules once without consequence is likely to do so again.

### Q-A10-12 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-07)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1, AV-1

You overhear a CFI at a safety seminar giving incorrect information about recent regulatory changes to a group of pilots. The CFI appears confident and the pilots are taking notes. What is the most professional response?

A) Say nothing -- correcting a colleague publicly would be unprofessional
B) Wait until the CFI finishes, then approach them privately to discuss the inaccuracy. If they won't correct it, provide the accurate information to the pilots yourself
C) Interrupt immediately and correct the information in front of everyone
D) Post the correct information on social media later, tagging the CFI

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Flight instructors represent "the finest individuals in GA" per AC 61-83K, and that includes ensuring accurate information reaches pilots. Allowing incorrect regulatory information to spread uncorrected is a disservice to the GA community. Option A prioritizes social comfort over safety. Option C is unnecessarily confrontational. Option D is passive-aggressive and may not reach the affected pilots. The professional approach balances respect for the colleague with responsibility to the pilots who received bad information.

### Q-A10-13 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-07)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1, RM-2

A student's father, who is paying for all the flight training, calls you directly and asks for detailed information about his son's training progress, including specific deficiencies. The student is 25 years old. What is the ethical response?

A) Provide the information since the father is paying for the training
B) Share general progress but withhold specific deficiency information
C) Explain that your professional obligation is to the student, and direct the father to discuss training progress with his son, while offering to include the father in a conversation if the student consents
D) Refuse to speak with the father entirely

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** The CFI-student relationship carries professional confidentiality obligations regardless of who is paying. A 25-year-old student is an adult, and their training records belong to them. Financial sponsorship does not grant access to training details. Option A violates the student's privacy. Option B is a half-measure that still discloses without consent. Option D is unnecessarily rigid -- offering a three-way conversation respects everyone's interests while maintaining professional boundaries.

### Q-A10-14 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-08)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-1, OD-1

You notice that the maintenance logbooks for a training aircraft show a recurring squawk that keeps getting signed off as "ops check good" without any corrective action. Students have mentioned the issue to you. The aircraft owner says it's "just a nuisance" and doesn't affect safety. What should you do?

A) Trust the aircraft owner's assessment since they know their airplane best
B) Continue using the aircraft but brief students about the squawk
C) Refuse to use the aircraft for instruction until the squawk receives proper maintenance attention, and document your concern in writing to the aircraft owner or school management
D) Let each student decide whether they're comfortable flying the aircraft

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** A CFI has a professional and ethical obligation to ensure training aircraft are properly maintained. A recurring squawk that is repeatedly signed off without corrective action represents a maintenance culture problem. The CFI's unique position of trust means students rely on the instructor's judgment about aircraft airworthiness. Option A defers professional judgment. Option B acknowledges a problem without acting on it. Option D shifts airworthiness responsibility to students who lack the experience to evaluate it.

### Q-A10-15 (Module 2, Pool A10-M2-08)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** PS-1, PS-2, CJ-1

AC 61-83K states that "ethics has been defined as what a person does when no one is watching." Which of the following CFI actions best exemplify this principle? (Select all that apply)

A) Completing a thorough preflight inspection on a training aircraft even when running behind schedule and the previous instructor said "it's good to go"
B) Accurately recording a student's training deficiencies in lesson notes that only you will review
C) Maintaining personal currency and proficiency requirements even when no one checks or audits your logbook
D) Adjusting a student's stage check score upward because the chief instructor is pressuring you to improve pass rates
E) Conducting a full post-flight debrief after a routine lesson even though the student seems eager to leave

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Ethics when unobserved means maintaining standards regardless of external oversight. Thorough preflights despite schedule pressure (A), honest internal documentation (B), personal currency maintenance without auditing (C), and complete debriefs despite social pressure (E) all demonstrate integrity when shortcuts would go unnoticed. Option D is the opposite -- adjusting scores under pressure compromises the evaluation process and masks student deficiencies, regardless of who is applying the pressure.
