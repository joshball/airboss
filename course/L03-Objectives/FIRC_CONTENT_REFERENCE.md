## Lesson 1 Content Summary (GPS/TAA/Navigation)

### Technically Advanced Airplane (TAA) Definition

Per 14 CFR 61.1 and 61.129(j), a TAA must be equipped with:

1. Electronic Primary Flight Display (PFD) - airspeed, turn coordinator, attitude, heading, altimeter, VSI
2. Electronic Multifunction Display (MFD) - moving map with GPS, aircraft position displayed
3. Two-axis autopilot integrated with nav and heading guidance
4. Display elements must be continuously visible

### TAA Advantages

- Proportionately fewer accidents than legacy GA aircraft
- Fewer fuel management, maneuvering, takeoff/climb phase accidents
- Improved positional awareness

### TAA Disadvantages

- Higher knowledge/proficiency requirement
- Automation Dependency (over-reliance leading to complacency)
- More accidents during landing, go-around, and weather phases

### TAA Training Recommendations

- Realistic, scenario-based, simulation-based
- Integrate "basics" with TAA competence
- Train and test to competence in stick/rudder, mental skills, and risk assessment
- Develop competency in key functions of each avionics system
- Include TAA-specific procedures (e.g., autopilot 180-turn in inadvertent IMC)
- Teach system limitations: low terrain masking satellites, slow weather updates, traffic system accuracy

### GPS Teaching Points

- GPS approved for inflight use since 1993
- "Direct Enter Enter" is not always the answer
- Practice GPS approaches under VMC before IMC

**12 GPS Familiarization Exercises:**

1. RAIM prediction function
2. Insert departure procedure with terminal CDI sensitivity
3. Program destination airport
4. Program and fly overlay approaches (procedure turns, arcs)
5. Change approach after selecting one
6. Program and fly direct missed approaches
7. Program and fly routed missed approaches
8. Enter, fly, and exit holding patterns
9. Program and fly route from holding pattern
10. Program and fly approach with radar vectors
11. Learn RAIM failure indications (before and after FAF)
12. Program radial and distance from VOR

### Key GPS Definitions

- **Active Waypoint** - waypoint to/from which guidance is provided
- **ATD Fix** - distance in NM to active waypoint along track
- **RAIM** - Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitoring; needs at least one extra satellite beyond navigation minimum
- **Fly-By vs Fly-Over Waypoint** - turn anticipation vs must pass over
- **TO-FROM vs TO-TO Navigation** - VOR-like CDI vs localizer-like tracking
- **Turn Anticipation** - system determines where to initiate turn for smooth intercept

### Situational Awareness Concerns

- GPS and moving maps may actually REDUCE positional awareness
- Memory and awareness of passively monitored info is poorer than actively generated info
- GPS + pilotage + dead reckoning + radio nav together = best awareness
- Always plan for GPS failure, have backup navigation
- Simulate automation failure as part of SBT

### SA Incident Examples

- Student pilot can't find airport, violates restricted/Class B airspace
- Private pilot runs out of fuel, forced landing short
- Commercial pilot flies into terrain after altitude misunderstanding

### GBAS (Ground Based Augmentation System)

- Formerly called LAAS (Local Area Augmentation System)
- Ground-based equipment near airports providing additional GPS accuracy
- Currently supports Category I approach minima
- FAA expanding to Category III capabilities
- Intended as alternative to aging ILS systems

## NTSB Case Study (Course Opening)

**NTSB File: ERA23FA008** (Newport News, VA)

- Cessna 172, N97883
- Student pilot on first flight lesson
- Instructor: 333.8 total hours, 75.5 dual given
- Flight instructor observing from back seat
- VMC, daytime, wind 270/7, 10 miles visibility
- Student continued pulling back after rotation
- Left wing stalled at ~200'
- Aircraft impacted ground on belly
- Flight instructor fatally injured
- Student and passenger seriously injured

**Key findings:**

- No pre-takeoff briefing on control exchange
- Neither student nor passenger recalled instruction before takeoff
- Probable cause: instructor's failure to monitor student on takeoff and delayed remedial action

**Teaching point:** With all technology available, we still have students and instructors who forget to fly the airplane. No room for comfort or complacency.

## TSA Requirements Detail (49 CFR Part 1552)

Key operational duties for flight training providers:

1. Cannot provide training until provider establishes individual is U.S. citizen, national, DoD endorsee, or has valid TSA Determination of Eligibility
2. For U.S. citizens/nationals: examine government-issued documentation. Deny if cannot provide acceptable documents.
3. For non-U.S. candidates: use FTSP Portal to confirm valid Determination of Eligibility before training
4. Must terminate training if TSA issues Determination of Ineligibility
5. Must designate Security Coordinator and keep current with TSA
6. Must retain records for at least 5 years after expiration/discontinuance
7. Logbook endorsement option available for citizenship verification
