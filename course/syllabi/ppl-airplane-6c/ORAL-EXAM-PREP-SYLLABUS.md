# PPL Oral Exam Prep Syllabus

This syllabus turns ground-school knowledge into practical-test oral readiness. It is organized around scenarios, source lookup, and ACS knowledge/risk/skill thinking.

Use it after the learner has completed most ground school and before final checkride preparation.

## Philosophy

The oral exam is not a trivia contest. The applicant must show they can act as PIC. Good answers usually include:

- The rule, concept, or aircraft limitation.
- The source that supports it.
- The practical decision.
- The risk control.

Example pattern:

> "Legally, this is allowed under X, but for today's airplane/weather/passenger/airport I would do Y because..."

## Course Structure

Recommended cadence:

- 8 sessions of 60-90 minutes.
- One capstone mock oral of 2-3 hours.
- Short daily flash/oral drills between sessions.

Each session:

1. Source lookup warmup.
2. Scenario questions.
3. Aircraft-specific questions.
4. Weak-code remediation.
5. One-minute PIC explanation.

## Session 1 -- Applicant, Certificate, and Privileges

Objectives:

- Explain student/private pilot eligibility, documents, medical/BASICMED awareness, photo ID, logbook, endorsements, ratings, category/class.
- Explain private pilot privileges and limitations, compensation, pro-rata share, charitable flights, search and rescue, business/incidental use, aircraft sales demos, and passenger carriage.
- Distinguish legal, current, proficient, and wise.

Sources:

- 14 CFR Part 61.
- ACS Area I.
- AC 61-65.

Scenarios:

- Friend offers to pay all costs for a beach trip.
- Employer asks you to fly to a meeting.
- Passenger wants to split fuel and hotel.
- You have not flown in 92 days and want to take two friends.
- You changed address after certificate issuance.

Exit standard:

- Learner can answer "Can I make this flight?" with rule, source, and decision.

## Session 2 -- Aircraft Documents, Airworthiness, and Maintenance

Objectives:

- Prove the aircraft is legal and suitable for the planned flight.
- Explain ARROW-style documents, airworthiness certificate, registration, operating limitations, W&B, required equipment, required inspections, ADs, inoperative equipment, and maintenance records.
- Explain PIC responsibility for airworthiness.

Sources:

- 14 CFR 91.7, 91.9, 91.203, 91.205, 91.207, 91.213, 91.403, 91.409, 91.411, 91.413.
- POH/AFM.
- Aircraft maintenance logs.
- PHAK aircraft documents/systems.

Scenarios:

- Landing light inoperative before a day flight.
- Transponder inspection expired near Class C.
- ELT battery replacement date has passed.
- Annual inspection is current, but a 100-hour is overdue.
- AD compliance is unclear.

Exit standard:

- Learner can walk aircraft documents and maintenance records without guessing.

## Session 3 -- Aircraft Systems, Limitations, and Performance

Objectives:

- Explain the training aircraft systems at a practical level.
- Use the POH/AFM for limitations, normal procedures, emergency procedures, W&B, and performance.
- Explain density altitude, runway margins, fuel planning, CG effects, and operating limitations.

Sources:

- POH/AFM.
- PHAK systems/performance/W&B.
- AFH normal and emergency operations.
- AKTS where test figures are relevant.

Scenarios:

- Hot day, short runway, three passengers, half fuel.
- Alternator failure at night.
- High oil temperature after takeoff.
- Forward vs aft CG loading decision.
- Rough engine over poor landing terrain.

Exit standard:

- Learner can make a performance decision using the actual airplane data.

## Session 4 -- Weather Theory, Products, and Go/No-Go

Objectives:

- Interpret current and forecast weather for a VFR flight.
- Explain fronts, pressure, stability, fog, thunderstorms, turbulence, wind shear, icing awareness, and visibility restrictions.
- Read METARs, TAFs, PIREPs, AIRMETs, SIGMETs, Convective SIGMETs, prog charts, radar, satellite, winds aloft, and briefing products.
- Separate legal VFR from personal minimums.

Sources:

- Aviation Weather Handbook.
- PHAK weather.
- AIM weather services.
- 91.103, 91.155.
- Current weather products.

Scenarios:

- Marginal VFR improving after departure.
- Good departure weather, lowering arrival ceilings.
- Thunderstorms forecast after planned arrival.
- Strong winds aloft and turbulence PIREPs.
- Night VFR with haze and no moon.

Exit standard:

- Learner can brief weather and make a conservative PIC decision.

## Session 5 -- Airspace, Charts, Airports, and Communications

Objectives:

- Interpret sectional/TAC chart features, airspace, special use airspace, TFRs, airport symbols, obstacles, terrain, navaids, and frequencies.
- Explain VFR weather minimums, equipment, communications, speed limits, and entry requirements.
- Use AIM phraseology for towered, non-towered, flight following, emergency, and lost procedures.

Sources:

- 14 CFR 91.126-91.131, 91.155, 91.157, 91.215, 91.225.
- AIM/PCG.
- Chart Supplement.
- VFR charts.
- AC 90-66.

Scenarios:

- Route passes under a Class B shelf.
- Flight crosses an MOA and a restricted area.
- Arrival at an unfamiliar non-towered airport.
- You cannot raise approach control near Class C.
- TFR appears along the route after planning.

Exit standard:

- Learner can point at a chart and state the operational action required.

## Session 6 -- Navigation and Cross-Country Plan Defense

Objectives:

- Defend the checkride cross-country plan.
- Explain route, altitude, checkpoints, headings, times, fuel, alternates, airspace, weather, NOTAMs, performance, W&B, and diversion options.
- Explain pilotage, dead reckoning, GPS/EFB use, VOR basics where applicable, lost procedures, and flight following.

Sources:

- 91.103.
- PHAK navigation.
- AIM navigation aids and ATC services.
- POH/AFM.
- Charts, Chart Supplement, weather, NOTAMs.

Scenarios:

- GPS fails 20 minutes after departure.
- Headwind is stronger than forecast.
- Destination runway closes by NOTAM.
- Passenger airsickness forces a diversion.
- Weather blocks the planned route.

Exit standard:

- Learner can dispatch, monitor, and modify the planned flight.

## Session 7 -- ADM, Human Factors, Emergencies, and Night

Objectives:

- Apply IMSAFE, PAVE, 5P, DECIDE, hazardous attitudes, personal minimums, and external-pressure management.
- Explain hypoxia, hyperventilation, spatial disorientation, fatigue, dehydration, medications, alcohol, carbon monoxide, and night physiology.
- Talk through engine failure, fire, electrical failure, instrument failure, radio failure, inadvertent IMC, off-airport landing, and emergency communications.

Sources:

- RMH.
- PHAK ADM/aeromedical.
- AFH emergency/night operations.
- AIM medical facts, emergency services, airport lighting.
- POH/AFM emergency procedures.
- NTSB Part 830.

Scenarios:

- Family pressure to launch into marginal weather.
- Engine roughness over wooded terrain.
- Lost at dusk with low fuel.
- Passenger panic after door opens.
- Inadvertent IMC during VFR flight.

Exit standard:

- Learner can prioritize aviate, navigate, communicate, and manage risk without freezing.

## Session 8 -- ACS Weak Codes and Rapid-Fire Integration

Objectives:

- Review knowledge-test deficiency codes.
- Review instructor-identified weak topics.
- Practice mixed-topic questions without context clues.
- Build concise, source-backed answers.

Sources:

- ACS.
- Knowledge Test Report.
- Mistake log.
- All core sources.

Scenarios:

- Examiner-style mixed oral.
- "Show me the source."
- "What would make you cancel?"
- "What would you do if this changed in flight?"

Exit standard:

- Learner can answer unfamiliar scenarios by reasoning from sources and PIC priorities.

## Capstone Mock Oral

The capstone should use the learner's actual checkride-style cross-country packet.

Minimum coverage:

- Applicant qualifications.
- Aircraft documents and maintenance records.
- POH/AFM systems, limitations, W&B, and performance.
- Weather brief and go/no-go decision.
- Route, airspace, communications, alternates, NOTAMs, fuel.
- ADM and passenger briefing.
- Emergency/abnormal scenarios.
- Night and human factors.
- Knowledge-test deficiencies.

Debrief categories:

- Knowledge gap.
- Source-location gap.
- Risk-management gap.
- Aircraft-specific gap.
- Communication/clarity gap.

## Daily Oral Drill

Each day, answer five questions:

1. One regulation.
2. One aircraft/POH question.
3. One weather or airspace question.
4. One performance/W&B or navigation question.
5. One ADM/emergency scenario.

Each answer must end with:

- "My source is..."
- "My PIC decision is..."
- "My risk control is..."
