# Private Pilot Airplane Ground School Syllabus

This is a typical U.S. private pilot airplane ground school syllabus for a Part 61 learner, written so it can also be adapted into a Part 141 training-course outline. It is built around the current FAA Private Pilot for Airplane Category ACS, the knowledge areas in 14 CFR 61.105, and the flight proficiency areas in 14 CFR 61.107.

The syllabus assumes 35-45 hours of organized ground instruction, plus homework, oral-practice sessions, and checkride preparation. A very test-focused course can be shorter. A scenario-rich course that includes real cross-country planning, weather triage, maintenance record review, and oral exam practice will naturally run longer.

Commercial textbooks and courses are useful but secondary. ASA, Jeppesen, Gleim, King Schools, Sporty's, Gold Seal, Rod Machado, MzeroA, Pilot Institute, and similar providers can supply readings, videos, test banks, and lesson structure. The FAA/regulatory documents remain the controlling source for standards and privileges.

## Course Outcomes

By the end of ground school, the learner should be able to:

- Pass the FAA Private Pilot Airplane knowledge test with enough margin that weak ACS codes are remediated rather than ignored.
- Plan and brief a realistic VFR cross-country using current weather, NOTAMs, charts, aircraft performance, fuel planning, weight and balance, airspace, alternates, and personal minimums.
- Explain private pilot privileges, limitations, required documents, medical/certificate requirements, aircraft airworthiness, required inspections, and common Part 91 operating rules.
- Use the aircraft POH/AFM to answer systems, limitations, performance, emergency, and operating-procedure questions.
- Speak in ACS language: knowledge, risk management, and skill, not just memorized facts.
- Prepare for the oral portion of the practical test through scenario-based explanations.

## Typical Cadence

Each lesson has four parts:

1. Pre-work: assigned reading and short recall drill.
2. Guided ground: concept instruction, source lookup, and discussion.
3. Scenario: operational application tied to a real or simulated flight.
4. Exit evidence: quiz, planning artifact, oral answer, or instructor sign-off item.

Each week should include at least one "source lookup" drill. The learner should not only know the answer, but know where the answer lives: regulation, ACS, AIM, POH/AFM, chart, Chart Supplement, weather product, or aircraft record.

## Stage 1: Orientation, Rules, and Aircraft Context

### Lesson 1 -- Certification Path and ACS Mindset

Objectives:

- Explain the private pilot training path: student pilot certificate, medical or BasicMed as applicable, solo endorsements, knowledge test, practical test, and certificate privileges.
- Distinguish the knowledge test from the practical test oral and flight.
- Read the ACS structure: Area of Operation, Task, Knowledge, Risk Management, Skill.
- Build a personal training binder or digital workspace.

Primary references:

- FAA-S-ACS-6C: introduction, appendices, task structure.
- FAA-G-ACS-2: ACS purpose and testing philosophy.
- 14 CFR 61.103, 61.105, 61.107, 61.109.
- AC 61-65 for endorsement awareness.

Exit evidence:

- Learner can trace one ACS code to a lesson topic and explain how it might appear on a knowledge test, oral question, and flight task.

### Lesson 2 -- Certificates, Documents, and Pilot Privileges

Objectives:

- Explain student pilot and private pilot privileges and limitations.
- Identify required pilot documents and aircraft documents.
- Explain category, class, type, ratings, endorsements, currency, and proficiency.
- Describe carrying passengers, compensation/pro-rata share, charitable flights, search and rescue, aircraft sales demonstrations, and incidental business use at a private pilot level.

Primary references:

- 14 CFR Part 61, especially Subpart E.
- 14 CFR 61.31, 61.56, 61.57, 61.113.
- PHAK: introduction to flying and flight manuals/documents.
- AC 61-65.

Exit evidence:

- Oral drill: "Can this private pilot make this flight, with these passengers, for this purpose?"

### Lesson 3 -- Aircraft Documents, Airworthiness, and Maintenance Status

Objectives:

- Identify ARROW-style aircraft documents: airworthiness certificate, registration, radio station license if required internationally, operating limitations/POH, weight and balance.
- Explain airworthy as conformity plus condition for safe operation.
- Identify required inspections: annual, 100-hour where applicable, transponder, altimeter/static for IFR, ELT, AD compliance, VOR check when relevant.
- Determine whether an inoperative item grounds the airplane.

Primary references:

- 14 CFR 91.7, 91.9, 91.203, 91.205, 91.207, 91.213, 91.403, 91.409, 91.411, 91.413.
- POH/AFM and equipment list.
- Aircraft maintenance records.
- PHAK: flight manuals and aircraft documents.
- AFH: ground operations/preflight assessment.

Exit evidence:

- Learner reviews sample maintenance records and states whether the airplane is legal and safe for a day VFR training flight.

## Stage 2: Airplane, Aerodynamics, and Performance

### Lesson 4 -- Aircraft Construction, Controls, and Systems

Objectives:

- Explain major airframe components, flight controls, trim, flaps, landing gear, brakes, and common training-aircraft systems.
- Explain engine, propeller, fuel, oil, electrical, ignition, vacuum/pressure, pitot-static, and environmental systems at the POH level.
- Connect system knowledge to abnormal indications and checklist use.

Primary references:

- PHAK: aircraft construction, flight controls, aircraft systems, flight instruments.
- AFH: ground operations and emergency procedures.
- POH/AFM systems, limitations, normal, abnormal, and emergency sections.

Exit evidence:

- Learner briefs a complete "systems walkaround" of the training aircraft, including failure cues and pilot actions.

### Lesson 5 -- Principles of Flight and Stability

Objectives:

- Explain lift, weight, thrust, drag, angle of attack, stalls, load factor, stability, control around all axes, torque effects, left-turning tendencies, and coordination.
- Explain why stall speed changes with weight, bank, load factor, configuration, and CG.
- Relate aerodynamics to slow flight, stalls, steep turns, takeoffs, landings, and go-arounds.

Primary references:

- PHAK: principles of flight and aerodynamics of flight.
- AFH: basic flight maneuvers, energy management, slow flight/stalls, takeoffs/landings.
- Risk Management Handbook for maneuver risk.

Exit evidence:

- Learner explains an accelerated stall in a steep turn and identifies the risk controls before practicing steep turns.

### Lesson 6 -- Weight and Balance

Objectives:

- Calculate weight, arm, moment, CG, useful load, payload, fuel load, and baggage limits.
- Interpret CG envelope and explain forward/aft CG effects on stability, stall speed, elevator authority, and performance.
- Build a loading plan for a real lesson and a passenger cross-country.

Primary references:

- PHAK: weight and balance.
- POH/AFM W&B data and equipment list.
- FAA-CT-8080-2H for testing-style W&B figures.

Exit evidence:

- Completed W&B for three scenarios: solo lesson, dual lesson with bags, family/passenger cross-country with fuel tradeoff.

### Lesson 7 -- Performance, Density Altitude, and Aircraft Limitations

Objectives:

- Use POH/AFM tables or charts for takeoff distance, landing distance, climb performance, cruise performance, range/endurance, fuel burn, and service ceiling.
- Explain density altitude, pressure altitude, temperature, humidity, runway slope, wind, surface condition, and obstacle effects.
- Apply conservative margins and reject "book number equals safe number" thinking.

Primary references:

- PHAK: aircraft performance.
- POH/AFM performance section.
- FAA-CT-8080-2H testing supplement.
- Risk Management Handbook.

Exit evidence:

- Learner makes a go/no-go and loading decision for a hot-day short-field departure.

## Stage 3: Airport, Airspace, Communications, and Operations

### Lesson 8 -- Airports, Runways, Signs, Markings, and Lighting

Objectives:

- Interpret runway numbers, traffic patterns, airport signs, markings, lighting, visual glide indicators, beacons, segmented circles, wind indicators, displaced thresholds, LAHSO, hotspots, and runway incursion risks.
- Use the Chart Supplement to extract runway, lighting, communications, services, pattern, noise, and remarks data.

Primary references:

- AIM: airport operations, signs, markings, lighting.
- PHAK: airport operations.
- AFH: ground operations and airport traffic patterns.
- Chart Supplement and airport diagrams.

Exit evidence:

- Learner briefs an unfamiliar airport diagram and identifies likely taxi/runway-incursion traps.

### Lesson 9 -- Radio Communications and ATC Services

Objectives:

- Use standard phraseology for non-towered and towered airport operations, ground/tower calls, CTAF, ATIS/AWOS/ASOS, flight following, position reports, lost communications, and emergency communications.
- Explain readbacks, hold-short instructions, clearances, advisories, and pilot/controller responsibilities.

Primary references:

- AIM: radio communications, ATC services, airport operations.
- Pilot/Controller Glossary.
- AC 90-66 for non-towered operations.

Exit evidence:

- Learner completes scripted and unscripted radio role-play for departure, en route flight following, arrival, and a radio failure.

### Lesson 10 -- Airspace and VFR Weather Minimums

Objectives:

- Identify Classes A, B, C, D, E, and G airspace on charts.
- Explain entry requirements, equipment, communication requirements, cloud clearances, visibility minimums, speed limits, special VFR, and common local airspace traps.
- Interpret special use airspace, TFRs, MOAs, restricted/prohibited/warning/alert areas, CFAs, MTRs, parachute areas, glider areas, and wildlife/refuge notes.

Primary references:

- 14 CFR 91.126-91.131, 91.155, 91.157, 91.215, 91.225.
- AIM: airspace and special use airspace.
- PHAK: airspace and charts.
- Current VFR sectional/TAC/Flyway charts.

Exit evidence:

- Learner traces a route through complex airspace and states the legal minimums, radio plan, and personal minimums for each segment.

## Stage 4: Weather

### Lesson 11 -- Weather Theory for VFR Pilots

Objectives:

- Explain pressure systems, fronts, air masses, stability, lapse rates, clouds, fog, precipitation, thunderstorms, turbulence, wind shear, mountain weather, microbursts, icing awareness, and visibility restrictions.
- Identify critical weather situations that should trigger delay, reroute, cancellation, or instructor consultation.

Primary references:

- Aviation Weather Handbook.
- PHAK: weather theory.
- Risk Management Handbook.

Exit evidence:

- Learner explains why a plausible VFR forecast can still produce a poor training-flight decision.

### Lesson 12 -- Weather Products and Briefings

Objectives:

- Decode and apply METARs, SPECIs, TAFs, MOS/NBM or equivalent forecast tools as used locally, graphical forecasts, prog charts, radar, satellite, winds aloft, PIREPs, AIRMETs, SIGMETs, Convective SIGMETs, TFR/NOTAM weather implications, and standard/abbreviated/outlook briefings.
- Distinguish decoding a product from making a flight decision.

Primary references:

- Aviation Weather Handbook.
- AIM: preflight briefing and weather services.
- 1800WXBRIEF/Leidos or approved briefing source.
- AviationWeather.gov, NOTAM Search, and EFB weather products as local procedure permits.

Exit evidence:

- Learner produces a weather briefing summary for a local flight and a cross-country, ending with a go/no-go decision and alternates.

## Stage 5: Navigation and Cross-Country Planning

### Lesson 13 -- Charts, Pilotage, Dead Reckoning, and EFB Discipline

Objectives:

- Read VFR chart symbols, terrain, obstacles, airspace, frequencies, airport data, navaids, latitude/longitude, mileage, and chart legends.
- Plan by pilotage and dead reckoning before adding GPS/EFB automation.
- Explain EFB failure modes, database currency, battery/heat issues, and backup planning.

Primary references:

- PHAK: navigation.
- AIM: navigation aids and charting.
- FAA chart user's guide.
- Current VFR charts, plotter, E6B or electronic equivalent.

Exit evidence:

- Learner draws and briefs a paper or digital nav log with checkpoints, headings, times, fuel, alternates, and diversion options.

### Lesson 14 -- Navigation Systems and Lost Procedures

Objectives:

- Explain compass errors, magnetic variation/deviation, VOR basics where applicable, GPS/RNAV basics, moving map use, ADS-B traffic/weather limitations, flight following, and emergency/lost procedures.
- Use the "climb, communicate, confess, comply" style of lost-procedure thinking without making the mnemonic the whole lesson.

Primary references:

- PHAK: navigation.
- AIM: navigation aids, GPS, radar services, emergency services.
- POH/AFM avionics supplements or installed equipment guides.

Exit evidence:

- Scenario: learner becomes uncertain of position near controlled/special-use airspace and briefs the safest recovery plan.

### Lesson 15 -- Full VFR Cross-Country Planning

Objectives:

- Build a complete cross-country plan: route, altitude, airspace, weather, NOTAMs, fuel, W&B, performance, alternates, risk assessment, airport data, communications plan, and passenger briefing.
- Explain 91.103 preflight action in practical terms.
- Prepare for solo cross-country decision-making and instructor review.

Primary references:

- 14 CFR 91.103.
- PHAK: navigation, performance, weather services.
- Chart Supplement, VFR charts, POH/AFM, weather products, NOTAM system.
- Risk Management Handbook.

Exit evidence:

- Complete cross-country package ready for instructor critique.

## Stage 6: Human Factors, Risk, Emergencies, and Special Operations

### Lesson 16 -- ADM, Human Factors, and Personal Minimums

Objectives:

- Explain ADM, risk management, SRM, hazardous attitudes, IMSAFE, PAVE, 5P, DECIDE, external pressures, fatigue, hypoxia, hyperventilation, spatial disorientation, carbon monoxide, alcohol/drugs, medications, dehydration, stress, and passenger pressure.
- Build personal minimums that are more conservative than legal minimums and update them as proficiency changes.

Primary references:

- Risk Management Handbook.
- PHAK: aeronautical decision-making and aeromedical factors.
- AIM: medical facts for pilots.

Exit evidence:

- Learner writes personal minimums for local solo, solo cross-country, passenger flight, night flight, and marginal-weather cancellation.

### Lesson 17 -- Emergency Operations and Abnormal Scenarios

Objectives:

- Explain engine failure, fire, electrical failure, alternator failure, vacuum/pitot-static failure, radio failure, door/window opening, rough engine, low oil pressure, high temperature, inadvertent IMC, emergency descent, off-airport landing, and emergency communications.
- Practice checklist discipline and memory-item boundaries.
- Understand NTSB notification/reporting at a private pilot level.

Primary references:

- AFH: emergency procedures.
- POH/AFM emergency and abnormal procedures.
- PHAK: systems and aeromedical factors.
- NTSB Part 830.
- AIM: emergency services and communications.

Exit evidence:

- Oral scenario: "You are 2,500 feet AGL, rough engine, nearest airport behind you, passenger anxious. Talk through the next 60 seconds."

### Lesson 18 -- Night, Wake Turbulence, Collision Avoidance, and Safety Programs

Objectives:

- Explain night physiology, lighting, illusions, airport lighting, night preflight, night emergencies, and risk controls.
- Explain wake turbulence avoidance, collision avoidance, scanning, ADS-B limitations, runway incursion avoidance, and FAA WINGS/continuing education.

Primary references:

- AFH: night operations.
- AIM: airport lighting, wake turbulence, collision avoidance, medical facts.
- PHAK: aeromedical factors and airport operations.
- AC 90-66 as applicable.

Exit evidence:

- Learner briefs a night pattern lesson and a night cross-country risk assessment.

## Stage 7: Test Preparation and Checkride Integration

### Lesson 19 -- Knowledge Test Review and ACS Deficiency Remediation

Objectives:

- Review weak knowledge areas using ACS codes and FAA testing supplement figures.
- Practice performance, W&B, weather, chart, airspace, and regulation questions under time pressure.
- Prepare for test logistics: FTN, testing authorization/endorsement, ID requirements, allowed materials, and test report.

Primary references:

- FAA-S-ACS-6C.
- FAA-CT-8080-2H.
- FAA airman knowledge test guidance.
- 14 CFR 61.35 and 61.105.
- AC 61-65 for endorsement language.

Exit evidence:

- Instructor documents readiness for the knowledge test and the learner knows how deficient ACS codes will be remediated after the test.

### Lesson 20 -- Oral Exam Scenarios and Checkride Binder

Objectives:

- Build and rehearse a checkride binder or digital equivalent: pilot documents, aircraft documents, maintenance status, W&B, performance, cross-country plan, weather brief, NOTAMs, risk assessment, and POH/AFM tabs.
- Practice scenario-based oral questions across every ACS Area of Operation.
- Convert missed knowledge-test codes into targeted review items.

Primary references:

- FAA-S-ACS-6C.
- FAA-G-ACS-2.
- POH/AFM and aircraft records.
- Current charts, weather, NOTAMs, and Chart Supplement.

Exit evidence:

- Mock oral. Learner can defend the planned flight, aircraft status, personal readiness, and risk controls without relying on rote answers.

### Lesson 21 -- Final Integration: Dispatch a Realistic Flight

Objectives:

- Perform a full dispatch-style decision for a realistic private pilot flight.
- Identify legal, aircraft, weather, airspace, performance, human, passenger, and external-pressure constraints.
- Make a go/no-go/modify decision and brief the decision as PIC.

Primary references:

- All primary references.

Exit evidence:

- Capstone scenario package with instructor debrief and remediation plan.

## Typical Stage Gates

Pre-solo ground readiness:

- Regulations for student pilot privileges and limitations.
- Local airspace, airport operations, radio procedures, runway incursion avoidance.
- Aircraft systems, limitations, emergency procedures, and required documents.
- Weather minimums and solo personal minimums.
- Instructor-administered pre-solo knowledge test and review.

Pre-solo cross-country readiness:

- Cross-country planning, weather briefing, airspace, performance, fuel, W&B, alternates, and lost procedures.
- Solo cross-country endorsements and route review.
- Demonstrated ability to make conservative diversion/cancellation decisions.

Knowledge test readiness:

- Repeated passing practice scores with review of reasoning, not just answer memorization.
- Instructor review against all 61.105 knowledge areas and ACS knowledge codes.
- Endorsement or course completion certificate as applicable.

Practical test readiness:

- Knowledge-test deficiencies reviewed and endorsed.
- Complete aircraft and pilot documents.
- Checkride cross-country plan and aircraft performance package.
- Mock oral and flight review against ACS tasks.

## Common Failure Points to Teach Early

- Treating legal VFR minimums as personal weather minimums.
- Reading weather products as decoded text instead of operational risk.
- Knowing airspace colors but not knowing what communication/equipment/weather action they require.
- Memorizing aircraft systems without connecting them to failures and checklist actions.
- Calculating performance without margins.
- Planning with an EFB without understanding the chart, route, fuel, and alternates.
- Preparing for the knowledge test separately from the oral and flight test.
- Thinking "checkride ready" means maneuver tolerances only, instead of ACS knowledge plus risk management plus skill.
