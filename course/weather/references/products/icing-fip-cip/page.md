# Icing Forecast and Current Icing Product (FIP / CIP)

> Gridded, flight-level-resolved analyses of icing potential. CIP analyzes current icing conditions by fusing radar, satellite, PIREPs, lightning, surface obs, and model background; FIP is the model-forecast version. Both show three separate layers: icing probability, icing severity, and SLD (supercooled large droplet) potential.

## What it is

The Current Icing Product (CIP) and Forecast Icing Product (FIP) are gridded raster products generated by the NWS Aviation Weather Center (AWC). They occupy a different slot in the briefing pack from the text/polygon advisories: instead of a yes/no polygon, they paint a continuous probability and severity field over a 3D grid (latitude, longitude, and flight level in 1000-ft steps from roughly 3,000 ft MSL to 30,000 ft MSL).

- **CIP (Current Icing Product)** is an analysis of current conditions, refreshed hourly. It blends radar reflectivity, satellite imagery, PIREPs, lightning data, surface observations, and the most recent numerical model run into a best estimate of "what is the icing field right now, at each flight level." Because it ingests PIREPs and observations, CIP is closer to ground truth than a pure model forecast.
- **FIP (Forecast Icing Product)** is the model-forecast version. It uses the same algorithm family but runs forward on model output instead of fusing current observations. FIP is published in 3-hour steps out to 18 hours.

Both products expose three separate display layers for any given flight level:

| Layer            | What it shows                                        | Scale                                             |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Probability      | Likelihood that icing exists at this grid cell       | 0% to 100% (continuous color ramp)                |
| Severity         | Expected icing intensity if it occurs                | Trace / Light / Moderate / Heavy / Severe         |
| SLD potential    | Whether supercooled large droplets are likely        | Yes / No (binary overlay)                         |

The SLD layer is the freezing-rain / freezing-drizzle indicator. SLD is a category 14 CFR §91.527 and certified-aircraft handbooks call out specifically -- it can defeat boots and unprotected airframes very quickly.

The graphical counterpart on aviationweather.gov is interactive: a flight-level slider scrubs through altitude in 1000-ft steps, and the three layers can be toggled independently. This is the briefing surface, not raw GRIB.

## When you read it

- **Preflight, any IFR flight in cold-season cloud.** If the route punches any IMC and freezing temps exist anywhere between the surface and your cruise altitude, FIP/CIP belong in the briefing. Especially winter and shoulder-season flights with stratiform precipitation, frontal systems, or upslope cloud.
- **Preflight, even VFR**, if the freezing level is low enough that climb/descent profiles or terrain push you into freezing cloud.
- **Pair with AIRMET Zulu and SIGMETs.** AIRMET Zulu is the regulatory area advisory for moderate icing; SIGMETs cover severe icing. FIP/CIP give you the gradient and altitude resolution that the polygon advisories flatten -- the advisory tells you a polygon is "ice"; FIP/CIP tell you which 1000-ft band inside that polygon is worst and where SLD risk sits.
- **En route, via EFB.** Most EFBs render FIP/CIP layers. Use them to evaluate climb / descent options when a PIREP or controller call surprises you.

What it decides:

- **Altitude selection** -- the primary use. "File at 8,000 or 10,000?" becomes a layer-by-layer question.
- **Go / no-go for non-deiced aircraft** -- a route with moderate-or-greater severity at every available altitude is a no-go.
- **Equipment match** -- a route with SLD potential at cruise altitude is a no-go for any aircraft not specifically certified for it (most piston singles and twins).

## How to read it

The product is a chart, not text. On aviationweather.gov the interface is a map with three independent controls:

| Control            | Range / values                                        | Function                                                                 |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Flight-level slider| 3000 to 30000 ft MSL in 1000-ft steps                 | Picks the grid layer being displayed. CIP/FIP are 3D; you read one layer at a time. |
| Layer selector     | Probability / Severity / SLD potential                | Toggles which of the three fields is colored on the map.                 |
| Time selector      | CIP: current (hourly). FIP: +3, +6, +9, +12, +15, +18 hr | CIP shows analysis time; FIP scrubs forward to each forecast step.       |

The three layers each have their own color scale; legends are inline on the AWC page. A few rules of thumb:

- **Probability** is a continuous ramp, typically gray (low) through yellow to red (high). Above roughly 50-70% the cell is "likely iced" for planning purposes; below 25% it's a low-likelihood band, not zero.
- **Severity** is a stepped scale: trace (lightest shade), light, moderate, heavy, severe. Moderate and above is the threshold for AIRMET Zulu coverage. Severe is SIGMET territory.
- **SLD potential** is a binary overlay (typically a hatched or distinctly colored shading). Treat presence as critical: SLD is freezing-rain / freezing-drizzle physics, defeats most ice-protection systems, and is a no-go for non-SLD-certified aircraft.

You read the product by:

1. Picking your filed flight level on the slider.
2. Scanning probability first to find the corridors of likely icing.
3. Switching to severity to grade those corridors.
4. Switching to SLD to flag any band of supercooled-large-droplet potential.
5. Repeating at +/- 2,000 to 4,000 ft to see whether an altitude shift escapes the worst.

## Annotated example(s)

### Example 1 -- Winter stratiform setup over the Great Lakes

A January morning over Michigan and Ohio. A slow-moving low pressure system is dragging stratiform precipitation across the lakes, surface temps in the upper 20s F, freezing level at the surface or just above. You're filing IFR from KDTW to KCLE, planning 6,000 ft.

You pull up CIP for the current hour. At 4,000 ft and 6,000 ft, probability is solidly red across your route -- 70% to 90% for the entire length. You switch to the severity layer at 6,000 ft: moderate over most of the route, with a band of moderate-to-heavy along the lake shore. You switch to SLD: the SLD overlay is on, covering roughly the Toledo to Cleveland leg between 4,000 and 8,000 ft. You scrub the slider up:

- 4,000 ft -- moderate severity, SLD on. Worst layer.
- 6,000 ft -- moderate severity, SLD on. Your filed altitude.
- 8,000 ft -- moderate severity, SLD on.
- 10,000 ft -- light severity, SLD off. Cleaner.
- 12,000 ft -- trace severity, SLD off. Clean.

Decision walk-through: filing 6,000 ft puts you in the worst of the layer with SLD present. For a non-deiced piston single this is straight no-go; SLD alone is disqualifying. For a deiced known-icing-certified airframe, 10,000 ft or above is a workable cruise altitude (light, no SLD), but climb and descent will still pass through the moderate / SLD band -- plan a continuous climb with the boots ready and a quick descent into KCLE timed for minimum exposure. Cross-check AIRMET Zulu (which should be active for this synoptic pattern) and any PIREPs in the area for ground truth on the FIP/CIP picture.

### Example 2 -- Clear-air pre-frontal day with a narrow freezing-level band

A late October afternoon, a cold front approaching from the northwest. Skies are mostly clear, no significant cloud below FL250 except a narrow band of stratocumulus along the frontal boundary. Freezing level is around 9,000 ft MSL across your route from KMKE to KMSP, except where the front pushes it down to 5,000 ft along a 50-NM corridor near La Crosse.

You pull up FIP for the planned departure time. At 8,000 ft probability is essentially zero across the entire route -- the air is dry and warm. At 10,000 ft the only cell of any probability is a small patch along the frontal band (15% probability, trace severity, SLD off). At 12,000 ft the same patch shows 40% probability, light severity, SLD off. At 14,000 ft the front has dropped freezing temps below your altitude and the cell is gone.

Decision walk-through: this is a very different problem from the Great Lakes case. The route is mostly free of icing risk; the only exposure is the frontal band, and even there severity stays at light or below. Two viable plans: file at 8,000 ft to stay below the freezing level on the bulk of the route (the freezing level is locally 5,000 ft at the front, but exposure is brief at altitude), accepting a 50-NM segment with localized risk and the option to descend; or file at 14,000 ft to top the frontal cloud entirely. Either works. The decision shifts from "can I fly this at all" to "which leg of the cruise altitude trade do I prefer." Pair the FIP read with the freezing-level chart and a PIREP scan to verify the localized band's behavior.

## Common gotchas

- **Probability and severity are SEPARATE layers.** A cell with 80% probability and "trace" severity is a different problem from a cell with 20% probability and "severe" severity. Read both, then synthesize. Never collapse them into "icing yes/no."
- **SLD is the freezing-rain / freezing-drizzle indicator.** Treat any SLD overlay as critical. SLD physics defeats most ice-protection systems and is a known-icing-certification line item. For non-SLD-certified airframes, SLD presence anywhere on the route at any altitude you might pass through is a no-go.
- **CIP is current-conditions ANALYSIS, not a forecast.** CIP tells you what the picture looks like right now. FIP is the forecast counterpart. Don't use a CIP image as a forecast for your 3-hour-out departure -- pull FIP for that.
- **Both products are GRIDDED, 1000-ft altitude resolution.** You read the flight-level band you're filing, not a region or polygon. An altitude shift of 2,000 ft routinely changes the picture from "no-go" to "workable" and vice versa.
- **FIP/CIP do not replace AIRMET Zulu or SIGMETs.** They are advisory analyses and forecasts; AIRMET Zulu is the regulatory area advisory, and SIGMETs are the regulatory severe-icing notice. Use FIP/CIP for resolution and planning; use the advisories for the regulatory picture.
- **Probability does not equal "definitely iced."** A 70% probability cell still has a 30% chance of being ice-free. Plan for the cell to be iced; verify with PIREPs en route.
- **Severity is a forecast distribution, not a measurement.** "Moderate" on FIP/CIP is the expected intensity if icing occurs in the cell, not a guarantee that an aircraft will experience moderate. Aircraft type, exposure time, and droplet behavior all modulate the actual experience.
- **The product covers CONUS and adjacent areas.** Alaska, Hawaii, and oceanic routes have different products. Outside the lower 48 + offshore, check the regional icing products.

## Triage

When you have 60 seconds with FIP/CIP, your eyes go to four things in order:

1. **Scrub the altitude slider to your filed altitude.** Where on the route does probability go above 50% and severity reach moderate or higher? Those are the bands of concern.
2. **Check 2,000 to 4,000 ft above and below.** Does an altitude shift escape the worst of the layer? If the worst layer is 2,000 ft thick and clean air sits above and below, an altitude change is the easy fix.
3. **Toggle to the SLD layer.** Where on the route is SLD potential indicated? SLD presence anywhere along your altitude band is a hard decision point. For most piston aircraft, SLD in the route is no-go.
4. **Cross-check with PIREPs.** Pull PIREPs along the route in the time window. Do they confirm the FIP/CIP picture, or are they reporting clean air where FIP forecasts moderate? PIREPs are ground truth; absence of confirming PIREPs is not evidence the forecast is wrong, but presence of confirming PIREPs upgrades the read from "forecast says" to "it is."

The go/no-go drivers:

- **SLD potential at any altitude you'll pass through** is a no-go for any non-SLD-certified aircraft.
- **Moderate or greater severity at every available altitude** is a no-go for any non-deiced aircraft, and a strong caution even for deiced.
- **An icing layer with clean air above and below** is workable: climb above, descend below, or change cruise altitude to escape the layer.

## Related products

- [airmet](../airmet/page.md) -- AIRMET Zulu is the regulatory text advisory for moderate icing and freezing level; the polygon counterpart to the FIP/CIP raster.
- [sigmet](../sigmet/page.md) -- SIGMETs cover severe icing as an all-aircraft hazard; FIP/CIP severity "severe" maps to SIGMET territory.
- [pirep](../pirep/page.md) -- pilot reports of icing are the ground-truth check against FIP/CIP forecasts; CIP itself ingests PIREPs into its analysis.
- [freezing-level](../freezing-level/page.md) -- standalone freezing-level chart; FIP/CIP gives you the icing field above it, the freezing-level product gives you where the layer starts.
- [gfa](../gfa/page.md) -- Graphical Forecasts for Aviation; displays AIRMET Zulu polygons spatially alongside FIP/CIP-style data in the same surface.
