# A 250-hour PPL walks in and asks for an instrument rating

## The question

> "A current private pilot, 250 hours total, current Class 3 medical, no recent IFR training of any kind, walks into your school Monday and says 'I want my instrument rating.' Walk me through every regulatory check, every counting question, every endorsement I'm going to write, and every twist of [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) they're going to try to talk you into bending. They're paying tuition. The CFII cert is yours."

This is the qualifications-side capstone. The pilot side of the relationship has a clear destination -- the IR practical. The CFII side is the regulated side. Every hour the candidate logs has to map cleanly to a [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) sub-requirement. Every shortcut is a paperwork problem that becomes a real problem at the practical when the DPE goes line by line through the IACRA application.

## What this is testing

- Whether you know [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) cold -- eligibility, aero-experience, the long XC, the simulator credit, the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d) endorsement
- Whether you keep [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026) properly aligned -- what counts toward what
- Whether you handle the FFS / FTD / AATD / BATD substitution rules under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(h) and (i) without confusing the four device classes
- Whether you write the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(a)(6) endorsement only when the standard is met, with AC 61-65 wording
- Whether you recognize and refuse the common counting tricks (pre-PPL "instrument time," night-XC stacking, FTD-as-AATD)
- Whether you recognize when the candidate's path requires an IPC instead

## Model answer (full walkthrough)

A defensible answer has eight stages. Order matters: eligibility before training, training before the long XC, the long XC before the test endorsement.

### Stage 1: Eligibility -- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(a) and (b)

> [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) **(a) General.** A person who applies for an instrument rating must:
>
> (1) Hold at least a current private pilot certificate, or be concurrently applying for a private pilot certificate, with an airplane, helicopter, or powered-lift rating appropriate to the instrument rating sought;
>
> (2) Be able to read, speak, write, and understand the English language. ...
>
> (3) Receive and log ground training from an authorized instructor or accomplish a home-study course of training on the aeronautical knowledge areas of paragraph (b) of this section that apply to the instrument rating sought;
>
> (4) Receive a logbook or training record endorsement from an authorized instructor certifying that the person is prepared to take the required knowledge test;
>
> (5) Pass the required knowledge test on the aeronautical knowledge areas of paragraph (b) of this section ...
>
> (6) Receive and log training on the areas of operation of paragraph (c) of this section from an authorized instructor in an aircraft, or in a flight simulator or flight training device ...
>
> (7) Receive a logbook or training record endorsement from an authorized instructor certifying that the person is prepared to take the required practical test; and
>
> (8) Pass the required practical test on the areas of operation in paragraph (c) of this section ...

**Plain reading:** The candidate must (1) hold a PPL, (2) speak English, (3) get knowledge-area ground training, (4) get the written-test endorsement, (5) pass the written, (6) get IFR flight training in the right Areas of Operation, (7) get the practical-test endorsement, (8) pass the practical.

**What I'd say in the oral:**

> "[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(a) is the eight-step framework. PPL held -- yes. English -- yes. Ground training and written test endorsement come early. Flight training and practical test endorsement come at the end. Two endorsements I'm going to write: the written prep endorsement under (a)(4), and the practical prep endorsement under (a)(7). AC 61-65 has both -- A.31 for the IRA written, A.32 for the IRA practical. I'm not signing either until the candidate has actually met the standards each one certifies."

**Common pitfall:** Treating eligibility as "they have a PPL, we're good." (a) has eight elements; the practical-test endorsement at the end is the most-checked item by the DPE.

### Stage 2: The flight time clock -- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d) aero-experience

> [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) **(d) Aeronautical experience for the instrument-airplane rating.** A person who applies for an instrument-airplane rating must have logged:
>
> (1) Except as provided in paragraph (g) of this section, **50 hours of cross-country flight time as pilot in command**, of which 10 hours must have been in an airplane; and
>
> (2) **40 hours of actual or simulated instrument time** in the areas of operation listed in paragraph (c) of this section, of which **15 hours must have been received from an authorized instructor** who holds an instrument-airplane rating, and the instrument time includes:
>
> (i) **Three hours of instrument flight training** from an authorized instructor in an airplane that is appropriate to the instrument-airplane rating within 2 calendar months before the date of the practical test; and
>
> (ii) **Instrument flight training on cross-country flight procedures, including one cross-country flight** in an airplane with an authorized instructor, that is performed under instrument flight rules, when a flight plan has been filed with an air traffic control facility, and that involves ---
>
> (A) A flight of 250 nautical miles along airways or by directed routing from an air traffic control facility;
>
> (B) An instrument approach at each airport; and
>
> (C) Three different kinds of approaches with the use of navigation systems.

**Plain reading -- five sub-requirements:**

| Requirement                                                                                                   | Number                                                                       | Source                                                   |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Cross-country PIC time                                                                                        | 50 hours total, 10 in an airplane                                            | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)(1)     |
| Total actual or simulated instrument time                                                                     | 40 hours                                                                     | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)(2)     |
| Of which, instrument time received from an authorized CFII                                                    | 15 hours                                                                     | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)(2)     |
| Of which, instrument flight training in an airplane with a CFII within 2 calendar months before the practical | 3 hours                                                                      | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)(2)(i)  |
| The long IFR cross-country with a CFII                                                                        | 1 flight, 250 nm, 3 approaches at 3 airports, 3 different navigation systems | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)(2)(ii) |

These are five separate boxes that all have to be checked. They overlap (training given for the long XC counts toward the 15 hours of CFII-given time, which counts toward the 40 total instrument hours) but they don't substitute for each other.

**What I'd say in the oral:**

> "Five requirements under (d). 50 hours XC PIC, 10 of those in an airplane. 40 hours total instrument time, 15 of those from a CFII. 3 hours of CFII-given instrument time in the airplane within 2 months of the practical -- the 'fresh prep' requirement. The long XC: 250 nm, three approaches, three different kinds of navigation systems, IFR flight plan, with me. They all overlap; none substitute. I'm building a tracker on day one that has all five rows and updating it after every lesson."

**Common pitfall:** Confusing the 40 with the 15 with the 3. They're nested. 3 hours of recent-with-CFII counts toward 15 hours of CFII-given, counts toward 40 hours total. Reverse direction does not work: 25 hours of solo simulated instrument time toward the 40 does not count toward the 15 or the 3.

### Stage 3: Plot twist 1 -- the candidate has 49 PIC XC hours

The candidate's logbook shows 49.0 hours of cross-country PIC. They want to start IFR training Monday. What do you do?

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)(1) requires 50 hours of cross-country PIC time. The 50 must be logged before the practical test, not before training begins. The candidate can start IFR training Monday with 49 PIC XC hours in the log.

But the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) cross-country definition for the *instrument rating* is different from the standard cross-country definition. For most ratings, "cross-country" is "flight that includes a point of landing more than 50 nautical miles from the original point of departure." For the instrument rating, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026)(b)(3)(ii) -- review the precise paragraph -- adds: "is conducted in an aircraft, that includes a landing at an airport that is at least a straight-line distance of more than 50 nautical miles from the original point of departure."

This is important: a flight from KPAO (Palo Alto, CA) to a touch-and-go at KSQL (San Carlos, CA) and back -- those airports are very close together -- isn't cross-country for any rating. A flight from KPAO to a full-stop landing at KSBP (San Luis Obispo, CA, 145 nm) and back is cross-country for *both* the standard ratings and the instrument rating.

**What I'd say in the oral:**

> "49 hours XC PIC, instrument-rating definition. They start IFR training Monday and we plan a couple of XC flights as part of the build-up. The 50th hour will land naturally during instrument training. The trap is the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) definition -- 'cross-country' for the instrument rating still requires a landing at an airport more than 50 nm from departure. Touch-and-go doesn't count for the instrument rating. The candidate's prior 49 hours might include some flights that don't qualify if they were trying to count them under a less strict definition. We audit the logbook on day one and re-categorize anything that's tagged XC but doesn't meet the instrument definition. Better to find that on day one than at the practical."

**Common pitfall:** Assuming the candidate's logbook is correct. The DPE will go through it on the day of the practical. Inflated XC hours are a documented self-injury; the DPE may decline to administer the practical until the logbook is reconciled, which means rebooking, paying again, and explaining to the candidate why this happened.

### Stage 4: Plot twist 2 -- "I logged a lot of pre-PPL instrument in IMC with a friend"

The candidate says: "Before I got my PPL, I flew a few flights with a friend who's instrument-rated. He'd file IFR, we'd be in actual for half the flight, I was the only one manipulating the controls. I logged 8 hours as instrument time. Counts toward the 40, right?"

Two separate questions: (a) was the candidate legally able to log instrument time, and (b) does it count toward [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)(2)?

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026)(g) governs:

> [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026) **(g) Logging instrument flight time.**
>
> (1) A person may log instrument flight time only for that flight time when the person operates the aircraft solely by reference to instruments under actual or simulated instrument flight conditions.

A person logs instrument time when they were the one operating the aircraft solely by reference to instruments. If the candidate was sole manipulator of the controls during the IMC portion of the flight -- and the friend was acting PIC because the candidate was a non-instrument-rated PPL or pre-PPL student -- the candidate logs the time as instrument time per (g)(1).

Does it count for [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)? Yes, toward the 40 hours of total instrument time under (d)(2). It does *not* count toward the 15 hours from a CFII, because the friend wasn't an authorized CFII. It does *not* count toward the 3 hours within 2 calendar months of the practical, unless the flights happened recently *and* with a CFII.

**What I'd say in the oral:**

> "8 hours of pre-PPL instrument time with a friend in IMC, sole manipulator -- yes, those count toward the 40 hours under (d)(2). They do not count toward the 15 hours of CFII-given time because the friend wasn't a CFII. They do not count toward the 3 hours of recent-with-CFII because the friend wasn't a CFII and they weren't recent. So we have 8 hours toward 40 -- 32 to go on the unrestricted bucket -- and 0 toward 15 -- 15 to go on the CFII bucket -- and 0 toward 3 -- 3 to go on the recent-prep bucket. The 8 hours is real, but it doesn't substitute for the structured training. I take the candidate's word on the entries provisionally and verify the logbook entries actually exist with dates and aircraft tail numbers; if there's no logbook entry or it's vague, the time doesn't go in the column."

**Common pitfall:** Auto-discounting the time as "not legitimate." [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026)(g)(1) is permissive about who can log instrument time; the candidate doesn't need to be instrument-rated to log it. But the time goes in the right bucket.

### Stage 5: Plot twist 3 -- "Can I credit my FTD hours toward the 40?"

The candidate has 12 hours of "simulator time" from a previous flight school. You ask which simulator. They show you a photo of a fixed BATD-class device -- a Redbird MCX or similar. Some "simulator time" was logged in a desktop ATD that may or may not be FAA-approved.

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) has different substitution rules for different device classes:

| Device class                             | What it can substitute for                                                    | Per [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) |
| ---------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Full Flight Simulator (FFS)              | Up to 30 hours of the 40 hours total instrument time, with a CFII             | (g) and (h)                                        |
| Flight Training Device (FTD)             | Up to 20 hours, with a CFII                                                   | (g) and (h)                                        |
| Advanced Aviation Training Device (AATD) | Up to 20 hours, with a CFII; also written test endorsement after specific use | (h) and (i)                                        |
| Basic Aviation Training Device (BATD)    | Up to 10 hours, with a CFII                                                   | (h) and (i)                                        |

**Important:** All substitution requires a CFII to be present and the device has to have an FAA letter of authorization. Solo time in any of these devices, no matter how realistic, does not count toward [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d). And the device class must match the FAA letter -- a manufacturer who labels their device an "AATD" but holds only a BATD letter of authorization is letter-class BATD for [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) purposes.

**What I'd say in the oral:**

> "I need three things from the prior school. One: the device's FAA letter of authorization, which fixes the device class. Two: the candidate's training records signed by the CFII who was present. Three: the candidate's logbook entries for those sessions. If any of those is missing, the hours don't count. If they're all clean and the device is BATD, up to 10 hours can credit toward the 40. AATD, up to 20. FTD, up to 20. FFS, up to 30. The candidate cannot stack credits -- if 8 hours were on the BATD with the prior CFII and the BATD-cap is 10, only 10 BATD hours can credit, period, even if the candidate also did sessions on a separate AATD elsewhere. I track this on the same five-bucket sheet from the start."

**Common pitfall:** Letting candidates self-classify their device. AATD, BATD, FTD, FFS each map to a specific FAA approval. A candidate who says "AATD" because the box says AATD might be wrong; the FAA letter is the source of truth.

### Stage 6: Plot twist 4 -- "Let's do the long XC at night"

The candidate proposes: "Let's combine the long XC with my [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(b) night currency. We do the long XC at night, three full-stop landings, knock out two requirements at once."

This is well-intentioned and almost works. Walk it carefully:

| Requirement                                                                                     | Met by night long XC?                                                                                    |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)(2)(ii)(A): 250 nm flight, IFR plan, with CFII | Yes if 250 nm                                                                                            |
| (B): instrument approach at each airport                                                        | Yes if approaches are flown                                                                              |
| (C): three different kinds of approaches                                                        | Yes if three different navigation systems are used                                                       |
| [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(b): three full-stop landings to a night landing  | Yes only if all three are *full-stop* and during the period 1 hour after sunset to 1 hour before sunrise |

But there are operational concerns layered on top: a 250 nm long XC at night with a non-instrument-rated candidate (because the candidate is the *student*, not a rated pilot) is a different risk profile than the same flight in daylight. AC 61-98 doesn't speak directly to this, but most working CFIIs sequence it: long XC in daylight, night currency restored separately, because the long XC is also a *teaching event* and night reduces the visual cues the candidate uses to anchor IFR-task performance.

**What I'd say in the oral:**

> "On paper, yes -- a 250 nm IFR XC at night with three full-stop landings would satisfy [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)(2)(ii) and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(b). The regulations stack cleanly. In practice, I separate them. The long XC is high-density teaching -- briefings, in-flight check-ins, post-flight debrief on six approaches' worth of performance. Night adds workload that competes with the teaching. I tell the candidate: 'We do the long XC in daylight so the teaching gets the bandwidth it deserves. We restore your night currency on a different evening, three patterns to full-stop at the home airport, no IFR overhead.' Same regulations, separate flights, better outcomes. The candidate can disagree, but the CFII sets the curriculum."

**Common pitfall:** Letting the candidate optimize the syllabus for hour-stacking. Hour-stacking is legal under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026); pedagogical staging is the CFII's call. Don't be a checkbox CFII.

### Stage 7: The endorsements -- written and practical

Two CFII endorsements get written:

**Written test endorsement** -- AC 61-65 Endorsement A.31 (current revision):

> **A.31 Aeronautical knowledge test: § 61.35(a)(1), § 61.65(a) and (b).**
>
> I certify that [First name, MI, Last name] received the required training of § 61.65(b). I have determined that [he or she] is prepared for the Instrument Rating-Airplane (or other appropriate rating) knowledge test.

The endorsement only certifies *preparation for the test*. It does not certify the candidate as having passed.

**Practical test endorsement** -- AC 61-65 Endorsement A.32:

> **A.32 Flight proficiency / practical test: § 61.65(a)(6) and (a)(7).**
>
> I certify that [First name, MI, Last name] has received the required training of § 61.65(c) and (d). I have determined [he or she] is prepared for the Instrument-Airplane practical test.

The endorsement is the gate. It says: in my professional judgment, this person is ready. The DPE's practical test then evaluates against the IFR ACS / PTS. The CFII's endorsement and the DPE's evaluation are independent gates.

**What I'd say in the oral:**

> "Two endorsements, both AC 61-65 verbatim. A.31 for the written, after I've taught the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(b) knowledge areas and run the candidate through prep until I'd bet money on a pass. A.32 for the practical, after the long XC is logged, the 3-hour-within-2-months has happened, all five buckets are full, and the candidate has passed my mock oral and mock checkride. Both endorsements have my signature, my CFII number, and my expiration date. And I keep a corresponding [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/189?at=2026) instructor record for both. 5-year retention on my side."

**Common pitfall:** Writing A.32 because the calendar is running out. The endorsement is the CFII's certification of readiness, not a date stamp. If the candidate isn't ready, the CFII doesn't sign and they keep training. The 2-calendar-month clock on the 3 hours of fresh prep is a real deadline; if it lapses while the candidate isn't ready, the candidate flies more dual hours, not faster.

### Stage 8: When this isn't [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026), it's an IPC

What if the candidate -- as told -- *already had* an instrument rating, lapsed for years, and "wants their IR back"? That's not [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026); that's [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(d) IPC territory.

The IPC has its own AC 61-98 content (parallel to the flight review's AC 61-98 content), its own AC 61-65 endorsement (A.4), and a different scope than initial training. The candidate doesn't take a new written, doesn't apply for a new rating, doesn't go to a DPE. The CFII does the IPC, signs A.4, and the candidate is current to fly IFR PIC again.

**What I'd say in the oral:**

> "Worth confirming day one: is this initial IR training, or a return to IFR after lapse? If they hold a current IR but they're past the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(c) 6-month window plus the 6-month grace, they need an IPC, not initial training. AC 61-65 A.4 is the endorsement, not A.32. Different regulation, different paperwork. I ask explicitly on day one and verify the IR by looking at their cert; people sometimes call any return-to-IFR 'getting my IR' colloquially when they technically already hold the rating."

**Common pitfall:** Charging for initial-IR training to a candidate who actually needs an IPC. The candidate ends up with hours they don't need; the CFII ends up with a paperwork story that doesn't match the candidate's certificate.

## Compressed answer (under 90 seconds)

> "Eight checks. **One**: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(a) eight-step framework -- PPL held, English, ground training, written endorsement, written pass, flight training, practical endorsement, practical pass. **Two**: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d) five buckets -- 50 hr XC PIC (10 in airplane), 40 hr instrument total, 15 hr from CFII, 3 hr in airplane with CFII within 2 cal months of practical, 1 long XC (250 nm, 3 approaches, 3 navigation systems). **Three**: 49 PIC XC hours -- start training, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) instrument-XC definition is 50 nm landing, audit prior log entries. **Four**: pre-PPL IMC with a friend -- counts toward 40 per [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026)(g)(1) (sole manipulator under instruments), does not count toward 15 (no CFII) or 3 (not recent / no CFII). **Five**: FTD/AATD/BATD substitution -- need FAA letter of authorization, CFII present, device class matches caps (FFS 30, FTD 20, AATD 20, BATD 10). Self-classification by candidate is not authoritative. **Six**: long XC at night to stack with [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(b) -- regulatorily compatible, pedagogically separate; long XC in daylight, night currency separately. **Seven**: AC 61-65 A.31 (written prep) and A.32 (practical prep) -- both verbatim, both kept in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/189?at=2026) records 5 years. Endorsement is readiness, not deadline. **Eight**: confirm initial IR vs. IPC on day one -- different regulation, different endorsement (A.4), different scope."

## What goes wrong (failure modes)

| Failure mode                                                                                    | Diagnosis                                                                                                                                             |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Recites total numbers (40, 50, 15) without the nesting                                          | The buckets are nested. 3 hr of recent-with-CFII counts toward 15 hr CFII-given counts toward 40 hr total. Reverse direction does not work.           |
| Counts touch-and-go XC toward the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)(1) 50 hours | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) instrument-XC definition requires a landing more than 50 nm from departure                              |
| Discounts pre-PPL instrument time as illegitimate                                               | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026)(g)(1) permits sole-manipulator-under-instruments logging regardless of cert level held                 |
| Lets the candidate self-classify FFS / FTD / AATD / BATD                                        | The FAA letter of authorization fixes the class; manufacturer marketing terms are not authoritative                                                   |
| Counts solo simulator time toward [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(d)             | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(h) substitution requires a CFII present                                                                |
| Stacks the long XC with night currency without separating teaching from operations              | Regulatorily compatible, pedagogically conflicting; the CFII sets the syllabus                                                                        |
| Writes A.32 because the calendar is running out                                                 | The endorsement certifies readiness, not a date target                                                                                                |
| Treats long-lapsed IR holder as initial-IR candidate                                            | They need [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(d) IPC and AC 61-65 A.4, not [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) initial training |
| Forgets the 2-calendar-month freshness on the 3 hours                                           | The 3-hr-with-CFII-in-the-airplane window is rolling; if the candidate is not ready when the prior 3-hr session ages out, redo                        |
| Misses [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/189?at=2026) instructor record retention              | 5-year retention on the CFII side; missing record is a separate violation                                                                             |

| "Same candidate, but their PPL was issued under sport-pilot rules, not standard PPL."                                                                                     | Sport pilots cannot pursue an IR -- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026)(a)(1) requires a private pilot certificate or concurrent application. Sport pilot must upgrade to PPL first under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/103?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/109?at=2026).                                                                                                                                                                     |
| "Same candidate, but they have 49 PIC XC hours and want to count a flight where they were sole manipulator under the hood with another rated PPL as 'cross-country PIC.'" | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026)(e)(1)(i) -- sole manipulator logs PIC. The flight is XC if the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) XC definition is met. So yes, that flight counts toward the 50 PIC XC. The sole-manipulator-under-the-hood arrangement is conceptually fine but requires the safety pilot to be acting PIC under (e)(1)(iii) for the under-the-hood portion -- the candidate logs PIC for the time they were sole manipulator. |
| "Same candidate, but mid-training the candidate's medical lapses."                                                                                                        | Training continues. Hicks letter -- the CFII is acting as PIC during instruction, the candidate doesn't need a current medical to receive instruction. The candidate cannot solo or take the practical without a current medical. Renew before the practical.                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| "Same candidate, but you (the CFII) are not current on the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(c) IFR currency yourself."                                      | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026) doesn't directly require the CFII to be IFR-current to give instrument instruction, but [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(c) governs *acting as PIC under IFR*. If you're acting as PIC during the long XC, you must be IFR-current. Restore your own currency before the long XC.                                                                                                                            |
