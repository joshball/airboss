# Transponder and ADS-B Out

The transponder rule is old. The ADS-B Out rule is new. They live next door in subpart C and they answer the same question -- "what makes this airplane visible to the surveillance system?" -- but the surveillance system is different in each case, and the airspace where each is required is *not the same*.

This is the lesson where students confuse "Mode C veil" with "ADS-B rule airspace" because the two overlap heavily but not perfectly. They also confuse the airspace tables in 91.215 and 91.225, which look similar but are not identical.

ADS-B Out is **the rule of the decade**. It became effective January 1, 2020 (after a roll-in period that started in 2010). For modern flying it has become as fundamental as the transponder. Examiners and ramp checks audit it routinely.

## What you'll be able to do

- State which airspace requires a Mode C transponder (91.215) and which airspace requires ADS-B Out (91.225) cold, including the small-but-real differences between the two lists
- Distinguish Mode A, Mode C, and Mode S transponders
- Apply 91.227 performance requirements to determine whether a specific aircraft's ADS-B Out installation satisfies the rule
- Walk a pilot through the deviation provisions in 91.215(d) and 91.225(g)
- Locate the ADS-B Out exemptions and special-airspace authorizations

## Why this matters

The transponder and ADS-B Out rules are pure airspace-and-equipment lookups, but they are checkride targets because misreading the airspace tables ends a flight. A pilot who flies into Class B without an operating transponder commits a 91.215(c) violation; without ADS-B Out, a 91.225(b) violation. ATC sees both immediately on their displays, and the deviation report writes itself.

Beyond enforcement, these rules are the price of admission to the modern ATC system. A pilot who understands what these systems *do* operates more effectively in busy airspace -- they understand why ATC sees a target with altitude, why TIS-B traffic appears on the tablet, why the system gives more useful traffic call-outs in some places than others.

## The discovery question

You're planning a flight from KSLE (Salem, OR) to KAST (Astoria, OR) in a 1976 Cessna 172. The route is mostly Class E with a brief brush past KEUG's Class C airspace if you choose the southern routing. Your transponder works (Mode C). Your airplane has *no ADS-B Out*. The route is below 10,000 ft MSL, and you avoid all Class B / C / D airports entirely (no transitions, no overflights into the Mode C veils). Today is 2026.

Pause.

Can you legally fly this trip in 2026?

Read 91.225(d). The "rule airspace" for ADS-B Out is similar to the Mode C airspace but not identical. ADS-B Out is required in:

- Class A, B, and C airspace
- Above the lateral boundary of Class B / C up to 10,000 ft MSL
- Class E airspace at and above 10,000 ft MSL (excluding airspace below 2,500 ft AGL)
- Within 30 nm of the primary airport of a Class B (the "Mode C veil")
- Class E airspace over the Gulf of Mexico (specific carve-out)

Your KSLE-KAST route avoids all of these. It stays below 10,000 ft, never enters Class B/C, and is nowhere near a Mode C veil. So 91.225 does *not* require ADS-B Out for this flight. The flight is legal in 2026 without ADS-B Out.

The companion question: same flight, but you now want to land at KEUG (Class C). 91.225(d)(2) requires ADS-B Out within Class C. The flight is no longer legal without ADS-B Out unless you obtain a deviation under 91.225(g). The deviation provision exists; we cover it below.

This is the daily question for owners of older airplanes that haven't been upgraded. ADS-B Out is required in much of the airspace pilots care about, but not all of it. A careful flight plan can keep an unequipped airplane viable in many regions of the U.S.

## What 91.215 actually says

```text
§91.215  ATC transponder and altitude reporting equipment and use.

(a) All airspace: U.S.-registered civil aircraft. For operations not
    conducted under part 121 or 135 of this chapter, ATC transponder
    equipment installed must meet the performance and environmental
    requirements of any class of TSO-C74b (Mode A) or any class of
    TSO-C74c (Mode A with altitude reporting capability) ... or the
    appropriate class of TSO-C112 (Mode S).

(b) All airspace. Unless otherwise authorized or directed by ATC, no
    person may operate an aircraft in the airspace described in
    paragraphs (b)(1) through (b)(5) of this section, unless that
    aircraft is equipped with an operable coded radar beacon
    transponder having either Mode 3/A 4096 code capability,
    replying to Mode 3/A interrogations with the code specified by
    ATC, or a Mode S capability, replying to Mode 3/A interrogations
    with the code specified by ATC and intermode and Mode S
    interrogations in accordance with the applicable provisions
    specified in TSO C-112, and that aircraft is equipped with
    automatic pressure altitude reporting equipment having a Mode C
    capability that automatically replies to Mode C interrogations
    by transmitting pressure altitude information in 100-foot
    increments. This requirement applies --

  (1) All aircraft. In Class A, Class B, and Class C airspace areas;

  (2) All aircraft. In all airspace within 30 nautical miles of an
      airport listed in appendix D, section 1 of this part, from
      the surface upward to 10,000 feet MSL [the Mode C veil];

  (3) Notwithstanding paragraph (b)(2) of this section, any aircraft
      which was not originally certificated with an engine-driven
      electrical system or which has not subsequently been
      certified with such a system installed, balloon, or glider
      may conduct operations in the airspace within 30 nautical
      miles of an airport listed in appendix D, section 1 of this
      part provided such operations are conducted --

    (i) Outside any Class A, Class B, or Class C airspace area; and
    (ii) Below the altitude of the ceiling of a Class B or Class C
         airspace area designated for an airport or 10,000 feet MSL,
         whichever is lower; and

  (4) All aircraft in all airspace above the ceiling and within the
      lateral boundaries of a Class B or Class C airspace area
      designated for an airport upward to 10,000 feet MSL; and

  (5) All aircraft except any aircraft which was not originally
      certificated with an engine-driven electrical system or which
      has not subsequently been certified with such a system
      installed, balloon, or glider --
    (i) In all airspace of the 48 contiguous States and the
        District of Columbia at and above 10,000 feet MSL,
        excluding the airspace at and below 2,500 feet above the
        surface; and
    (ii) In the airspace from the surface to 10,000 feet MSL within
         a 10-nautical-mile radius of any airport listed in
         appendix D, section 2 of this part, excluding the airspace
         below 1,200 feet outside the lateral boundaries of the
         surface area of the airspace designated for that airport.

(c) Transponder-on requirement. While in the airspace as specified in
    paragraph (b) of this section or in all controlled airspace, each
    person operating an aircraft equipped with an operable ATC
    transponder maintained in accordance with §91.413 of this part
    shall operate the transponder, including Mode C equipment if
    installed, and shall reply on the appropriate code or as assigned
    by ATC.

(d) ATC authorized deviations. Requests for ATC authorized deviations
    must be made to the ATC facility having jurisdiction over the
    concerned airspace within the time periods specified as follows:

  (1) For operation of an aircraft with an inoperative transponder
      to the airport of ultimate destination, including any
      intermediate stops, or to proceed to a place where suitable
      repairs can be made or both, the request may be made at any
      time.

  (2) For operation of an aircraft that is not equipped with a
      transponder, the request must be made at least 1 hour before
      the proposed operation.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The structure to internalize:

```text
Mode C transponder required in:
  - Class A, B, C
  - Mode C veil (30 nm of a Class B primary, surface to 10,000 MSL)
  - Above the ceiling of Class B / C, up to 10,000 MSL
  - All airspace at or above 10,000 MSL in the 48 contiguous states
    (excluding airspace below 2,500 AGL)
  - Within 10 nm of certain Class B primaries (Appendix D Section 2)

Exempt:
  - Aircraft not originally certificated with engine-driven electrical
    systems (think Champ, Cub, certain antique airplanes)
  - Balloons and gliders
  ... when operated outside Class A/B/C and below the relevant
  Class B/C ceilings
```

The "no engine-driven electrical system" exemption is real. A Champ on a private strip, hopping over to a neighbor 20 nm away under a Class B that goes to 4,000 ft, can fly the Mode C veil if it stays under the Class B floor and outside the Class B itself. The exemption disappears the moment the airplane gets retrofitted with an alternator.

### Mode A vs. Mode C vs. Mode S

The transponder generations:

| Generation | What it transmits                                   | Where required |
| ---------- | --------------------------------------------------- | -------------- |
| Mode A     | A 4096-code beacon (the squawk code) only           | Legacy; not sufficient alone for Mode-C airspace |
| Mode C     | Mode A + pressure altitude (encoded altimeter)      | The "Mode C" airspace requirement is for a Mode-A-and-C-equipped transponder |
| Mode S     | Mode A + Mode C + a unique 24-bit aircraft address + datalink | Required for ADS-B Out installations using a 1090ES transponder; also the standard for newer transponders |

A pilot who has a Mode A transponder only -- no altitude reporting -- is *not* compliant with 91.215(b). The "Mode C airspace" rule is named for the altitude-reporting capability, not for any specific frequency band. A non-altitude-reporting Mode A transponder is operationally indistinguishable from no transponder for the purposes of 91.215(b).

### 91.215(c) -- the transponder-on rule

If your airplane has an operable transponder, **you must operate it** while flying in any of the 91.215(b) airspace **or in any controlled airspace**. The transponder-on requirement extends beyond the equipment-required airspace. A pilot flying VFR at 8,000 ft in Class E (no Mode C requirement) with a working transponder must still have it on, set to the appropriate code (1200 if VFR, the assigned code if on flight following).

A common question: "If my transponder breaks mid-flight in Class E airspace, do I have to land?" 91.215(c) says you "shall operate the transponder" -- but the rule is for operable transponders. An inflight failure converts the airplane to a no-transponder airplane mid-flight. The pilot is no longer in 91.215(c) violation if they continue to a destination at which the airspace doesn't require the transponder. If the destination requires a transponder, the pilot needs an ATC deviation under 91.215(d).

## The 91.215(d) deviation provision

The deviation provisions are operationally important:

```text
For an inop transponder:
  - Request can be made at any time.
  - ATC authorizes the deviation for the rest of the flight (or to
    a repair facility).

For a no-transponder operation in Mode C airspace:
  - Request must be made at least 1 hour before the proposed
    operation.
  - ATC authorizes specific timing and routing.
```

The 1-hour notice for non-equipped aircraft is the figure pilots forget. A pilot showing up at the airport intending to depart and discovering they need a Mode C deviation has to wait the hour out. ATC will give the authorization, but they need the time to coordinate.

The deviation is requested through the FSDO or the controlling ATC facility. In practice, calling the relevant TRACON or center is the path; the FSS phone tree can route the request.

## What 91.225 actually says

```text
§91.225  Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B) Out
equipment and use.

(a) After January 1, 2020, and unless otherwise authorized by ATC,
    no person may operate an aircraft in Class A airspace unless the
    aircraft has equipment installed that --

  (1) Meets the performance requirements in TSO-C166b, Extended
      Squitter Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B)
      ...; and

  (2) Meets the requirements of §91.227.

(b) After January 1, 2020, and unless otherwise authorized by ATC,
    no person may operate an aircraft below 18,000 feet MSL and in
    airspace described in paragraph (d) of this section unless the
    aircraft has equipment installed that --

  (1) Meets the performance requirements in --
    (i) TSO-C166b; or
    (ii) TSO-C154c, Universal Access Transceiver (UAT) Automatic
         Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B) Equipment
         Operating on the Frequency 978 MHz...; and
  (2) Meets the requirements of §91.227.

(c) Operators with equipment, installed before January 1, 2020, that
    does not meet the requirements of paragraph (a) of this section
    must upgrade or remove the equipment, otherwise the aircraft is
    prohibited from operating in airspace described in paragraphs
    (a) and (d) of this section.

(d) Airspace.

  (1) Class B and Class C airspace areas;

  (2) Class E airspace areas at and above 10,000 feet MSL over the
      48 contiguous United States and the District of Columbia,
      excluding the airspace at and below 2,500 feet above the
      surface;

  (3) Class E airspace at and above 3,000 feet MSL over the Gulf of
      Mexico from the coastline of the United States out to 12
      nautical miles;

  (4) Around those airports identified in appendix D, section 1 of
      this part [the Mode C veils], from the surface upward to
      10,000 feet MSL within a 30-nautical-mile radius of the
      primary airport;

  (5) Class E airspace areas above the airspace in paragraph (d)(4)
      of this section.

(e) ... (deviation provisions for unequipped aircraft)

(f) ... (operations on a discrete code)

(g) ATC authorized deviations.  Requests for ATC authorized
    deviations from the requirements of this section must be made
    to the ATC facility having jurisdiction over the concerned
    airspace within the time periods specified as follows:

  (1) For operation of an aircraft with an inoperative ADS-B Out,
      to the airport of ultimate destination, including any
      intermediate stops, or to proceed to a place where suitable
      repairs can be made, the request may be made at any time.

  (2) For operation of an aircraft that is not equipped with ADS-B
      Out, the request must be made at least 1 hour before the
      proposed operation.
```

### The rule airspace

```text
ADS-B Out required in:
  - Class A airspace                                      (paragraph (a))
  - Class B and Class C                                   (d)(1)
  - Class E at and above 10,000 MSL over the 48 contiguous states,
    excluding airspace at and below 2,500 AGL              (d)(2)
  - Mode C veils (30 nm around Class B primaries, surface to
    10,000 MSL)                                            (d)(4)
  - Above the ceiling of Class B / C up to 10,000 MSL      (d)(5)
  - Class E above 3,000 MSL over the Gulf of Mexico (within 12 nm
    of U.S. coastline)                                     (d)(3)
```

### Mode C airspace vs. ADS-B rule airspace

The two airspaces are similar -- but not identical. The differences:

| Feature                                        | 91.215 (Mode C)       | 91.225 (ADS-B Out)    |
| ---------------------------------------------- | --------------------- | --------------------- |
| Class A                                        | Required              | Required              |
| Class B, C                                     | Required              | Required              |
| Mode C veil (30 nm around Class B primaries)   | Required              | Required              |
| Above Class B/C ceiling up to 10,000 MSL       | Required              | Required              |
| All airspace at and above 10,000 MSL (48 contig states) | Required        | Class E only          |
| Within 10 nm of Appendix D Section 2 airports  | Required (transponder) | Not specifically called out (covered by Class C/B if applicable) |
| Class E over Gulf of Mexico                    | Not specifically      | Required at and above 3,000 MSL within 12 nm of coast |
| Carve-outs for non-electrical aircraft         | Yes (91.215(b)(3))    | Yes (91.225 implicit; pre-2020 aircraft) |

The biggest practical difference: the 91.215 Mode C requirement covers *all airspace* at and above 10,000 MSL in the contiguous 48; 91.225 covers Class E at and above 10,000 MSL in the same area. The two rules align in practice (above 10,000 MSL most airspace is Class E or higher), but the 91.225 wording is more specific.

### 91.227 -- performance requirements

91.227 specifies the technical requirements for an installed ADS-B Out:

```text
§91.227  Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B) Out
equipment performance requirements.

(a) Definitions ...

(b) ADS-B Out performance requirements for NIC, NACp, NACv, SIL, and
    SDA. After January 1, 2020, the ADS-B Out equipment installed
    on the aircraft must meet the performance requirements specified
    in this paragraph (b) when broadcasting:

  (1) NIC = 7 or greater (navigation integrity category)
  (2) NACp = 8 or greater (navigation accuracy category for position)
  (3) NACv = 1 or greater (navigation accuracy category for velocity)
  (4) SIL = 3 (source integrity level)
  (5) SDA = 2 (system design assurance)

(c) Minimum broadcast message element set ...
(d) Aircraft installation ...
(e) Position source ...
(f) Position source. The aircraft must have on board ... a position
    source that has been authorized by the FAA Administrator for
    use as a source of GPS position information for ADS-B Out.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The performance numbers are the FAA's way of specifying that the GPS source feeding the ADS-B transmitter must be accurate, certified, and integrity-monitored. A typical certified GPS WAAS unit (Garmin GTN 650 / 750, GTX 345 with the right WAAS source, Avidyne IFD-series with the right setup) satisfies the 91.227 performance requirements. A non-WAAS GPS or an uncertified consumer GPS does not.

The "rebroadcast" terminology: an aircraft transmitting ADS-B Out also receives ADS-B traffic and weather information through ADS-B In (TIS-B and FIS-B), but ADS-B In is *not required* by 91.225. A pilot can comply with 91.225 with ADS-B Out only and have no ADS-B In capability.

### Two physical-layer choices: 1090ES and 978 UAT

ADS-B Out can be implemented two ways:

```text
1090ES (1090 MHz Extended Squitter):
  - Same physical layer as Mode S transponder
  - Can be added to an existing Mode S transponder
  - Required for operations above 18,000 MSL (Class A)
  - Standard worldwide

978 UAT (Universal Access Transceiver):
  - Different physical layer (978 MHz)
  - U.S.-only solution
  - Limited to operations below 18,000 MSL
  - Carries some additional services (FIS-B weather)
```

A pilot operating exclusively below 18,000 MSL (most GA pilots) can use either solution. A pilot who plans flight in Class A must have 1090ES.

The wrinkle: the 91.225(b) airspace below 18,000 MSL accepts either 1090ES or 978 UAT. The 91.225(a) airspace (Class A) accepts only 1090ES.

### 91.225(g) -- deviation provisions

The deviation structure parallels 91.215(d):

```text
For inop ADS-B Out:
  - Request can be made at any time.
  - ATC authorizes the deviation for the rest of the flight or to a
    repair facility.

For no-ADS-B Out operation in rule airspace:
  - Request must be made at least 1 hour before the proposed operation.
  - ATC authorizes specific timing and routing.
```

The 1-hour deviation request is the planning trap for owners of un-equipped airplanes. The pilot needs to call the ATC facility responsible for the route (typically the relevant TRACON or center) and request the deviation. Most facilities are accommodating but the request and the response take time.

The 91.225(g) deviation is officially called the "ADS-B equipment deviation." Many GA pilots do not know it exists and assume their unequipped airplane is grounded for any flight into rule airspace. It is not -- the deviation is available, and many ferry flights, training flights, and even routine arrivals at Class C airports are conducted under 91.225(g) deviations.

## The "operable" requirement and post-flight diagnostics

Both 91.215(c) and 91.225 require the equipment to be *operating*. A transponder that's been turned off, or an ADS-B Out that's failed, is not operating.

The FAA publishes ADS-B performance reports through their Public Performance Reports tool: a pilot can request a report of their tail number's ADS-B Out broadcasts on a specific date. The reports show NACp, NIC, etc., for each minute of broadcast. A pilot whose broadcasts are below the 91.227 thresholds (e.g., NACp = 7 instead of 8) is in violation of 91.225 even if the equipment "appears to work." Post-flight reviews of the report are how some operators discover their installation drifted out of spec.

## Common misreadings

- **"The Mode C veil is the same as the ADS-B rule airspace."** Almost, but not exactly. The two airspaces overlap heavily but not perfectly -- 91.215 covers all airspace at and above 10,000 MSL in the contiguous 48; 91.225 covers Class E at and above 10,000 MSL. For practical purposes the answer is the same in nearly every case, but the wording differs.
- **"ADS-B Out is required everywhere now."** Wrong. It's required in the 91.225(d) rule airspace. A flight that stays in Class E below 10,000 MSL, away from Mode C veils, away from Class B/C, does not require ADS-B Out.
- **"If my transponder works, I have ADS-B Out."** Wrong. ADS-B Out is a separate function. A Mode S transponder *can* support 1090ES ADS-B Out if the right software upgrade and a WAAS GPS feed are installed, but a transponder alone does not satisfy 91.225.
- **"978 UAT works in Class A."** Wrong. 91.225(a) requires 1090ES for Class A. UAT is below 18,000 only.
- **"Mode A is enough for Class B."** Wrong. 91.215(b) requires "Mode 3/A 4096 code capability ... and ... automatic pressure altitude reporting equipment having a Mode C capability." A Mode A transponder without altitude encoding is non-compliant.
- **"I have to use the transponder only when ATC tells me to."** Wrong. 91.215(c) requires the transponder to be on whenever the airplane is in 91.215(b) airspace *or in any controlled airspace*. Setting 1200 in Class E and leaving the transponder on is the rule.
- **"If my ADS-B fails, the flight is over."** Wrong. 91.225(g) provides a deviation pathway. An inflight failure can be reported to ATC; they will routinely accommodate (the controller can see your transponder return without ADS-B Out, and they can vector you out of the rule airspace if needed). For pre-departure equipment failures, the 1-hour-notice deviation is available.
- **"NACp 7 is fine for ADS-B."** Wrong. 91.227(b)(2) requires NACp = 8 or greater. NACp 7 is 0.1 NM accuracy; NACp 8 is 0.05 NM. The threshold matters.
- **"My old loran-based GPS is good enough for ADS-B."** Wrong. 91.227(f) requires an FAA-authorized position source for ADS-B Out. Generally that means WAAS GPS. Loran-based or non-WAAS GPS is insufficient.
- **"Above 10,000 ft MSL means everywhere."** Wrong. 91.225(d)(2) excludes airspace at and below 2,500 ft AGL when in the 10,000+ MSL band -- the rule is "above 10,000 MSL but not at or below 2,500 AGL." This matters in mountainous terrain where the surface is high but a pilot might be flying low above it.

### Apply the rules

> A pilot in a Mode-C-equipped 172 (no ADS-B Out) plans a flight from KCCR (within the SFO Class B veil) to KSAC (Class C). The route is at 4,500 ft, well below the Class B floor in the SFO area. Is the flight legal in 2026?

Answer: No, without a deviation. KCCR is inside the SFO Mode C veil (30 nm around KSFO's Class B primary). 91.215(b)(2) requires Mode C in that veil -- the airplane has Mode C, so 91.215 is satisfied. But 91.225(d)(4) requires ADS-B Out in the same Mode C veil airspace, and the airplane has no ADS-B Out. Without a 91.225(g) deviation, the flight is not legal even before reaching KSAC.

To make the flight legal, the pilot must request a 91.225(g) deviation at least 1 hour before departure, specifying the Mode C veil and Class C transitions. ATC will typically authorize specific timing and routing.

> A pilot in a 1946 Champ (no electrical system, no transponder, no ADS-B) is doing pattern work at a private grass strip 20 nm inside a Mode C veil. The pattern stays below 1,200 ft AGL. Is this legal?

Answer: Yes. 91.215(b)(3) provides a carve-out for aircraft not originally certificated with engine-driven electrical systems, allowing operation within a Mode C veil provided they stay outside Class A/B/C and below the relevant ceiling (Class B floor or 10,000 MSL, whichever is lower). The Champ in pattern work at 1,200 AGL satisfies these conditions. Similar logic for ADS-B Out: the airplane was not originally certificated with an electrical system, so it falls under the equivalent ADS-B carve-out. Legal as long as the pattern stays under the relevant ceilings.

> A pilot's ADS-B Out fails 30 minutes into a cross-country at 8,500 ft MSL en route to a Class B arrival. What should the pilot do?

Answer: Notify ATC. 91.225(g)(1) allows an inflight deviation request for an inop ADS-B Out -- the request can be made at any time. ATC will authorize the deviation for the remainder of the flight (or to a repair facility). The transponder typically remains operating (assuming it's not the same equipment that failed); ATC retains radar surveillance through the Mode C return.

If the destination is the Class B primary, the deviation may need to specify the arrival; if it's a feeder field outside the rule airspace, the deviation may be unnecessary. The pilot communicates with ATC to clarify.
