# Maintenance responsibilities and inspections

The maintenance subpart (Part 91 subpart E) is short, dense, and almost entirely operational. Five questions get answered here:

1. **Who is responsible for maintaining the aircraft?** -- 91.403
2. **What does the owner/operator have to do?** -- 91.405
3. **What does the pilot do after maintenance, preventive maintenance, rebuilding, or alteration?** -- 91.407
4. **Which inspections are required, and when?** -- 91.409
5. **What records prove all of this?** -- 91.417 / 91.419 (lesson 05)

This lesson takes the first three plus 91.409 (the inspection regime). Lesson 05 takes the records.

The 100-hour vs. annual distinction in 91.409 is the trap that defines this lesson. Pilots who don't have a clean mental model of when the 100-hour applies and when it doesn't fail dispatch decisions and -- worse -- defend illegal operations as legal because they're confused about which rule applies.

## What you'll be able to do

- Locate the owner/operator's maintenance responsibilities in 91.403 and 91.405
- Apply 91.407 to the post-maintenance return-to-service question, including the test-flight requirement
- Distinguish a 91.409(a) annual inspection from a 91.409(b) 100-hour inspection cold; predict which applies to a specific aircraft and operation
- Recall the 91.409(c) and (d) progressive and continuous airworthiness maintenance program alternatives by name and locate them
- Recognize the cases where a 100-hour inspection requirement does *not* apply (the most common trap)

## Why this matters

The 100-hour inspection rule is the single most-misapplied maintenance rule in general aviation. Pilots and CFIs routinely think every airplane being used for instruction needs a 100-hour. The actual rule is narrower -- and the narrowness is the point. Understanding which airplanes need 100-hours and which don't is dispatch-critical for flight schools, leasebacks, and self-employed CFIs.

91.405 makes the operator responsible for compliance with airworthiness rules, but 91.407 puts the *pilot* in the loop for return-to-service. A pilot who flies an airplane after maintenance without verifying the proper sign-offs and the operational test (where required) is exposed to a 91.7 charge if the airplane's actually unairworthy.

The maintenance regime is also the reason airplanes stay airworthy decade after decade. A 1956 172 still flying today does so because someone has signed an annual inspection every year for 70 years. The system works, but it works because people understand the responsibilities.

## The discovery question

You CFI for a small flight school that owns three airplanes. A renter walks up to the C152 you teach in and pulls up the airframe logbook. The most recent inspection entry is dated October 18, 2025. Today is April 29, 2026. You have 1 hour of dual scheduled. You also coincidentally know the airplane is just under its next 100-hour interval.

What inspections matter for this airplane today, and which timer is closest?

Pause.

For this airplane, two timers run:

1. **Annual** -- 91.409(a). The airplane needs an annual inspection within the preceding 12 calendar months. October 18, 2025 + 12 calendar months = October 31, 2026. The annual is good.
2. **100-hour** -- 91.409(b). The airplane needs a 100-hour inspection if it is used to "carry any person, other than a crewmember, for hire" or to give "flight instruction for hire." A flight school C152 used for instruction-for-hire qualifies. The interval is 100 hours of time-in-service since the last 100-hour or annual.

The 100-hour timer is hours-based; the annual is calendar-based. They run independently. A pilot needs to check both before launch.

The trap a lot of pilots fall for: they see a current annual and assume that's enough. For an instruction-for-hire airplane, the 100-hour is independent and equally binding. An annual that's 11 months old and a 100-hour that's at 99.5 hours both must be addressed.

This is the question every flight-school dispatcher answers ten times a day.

## What 91.403 actually says

```text
§91.403  General.

(a) The owner or operator of an aircraft is primarily responsible
    for maintaining that aircraft in an airworthy condition,
    including compliance with part 39 of this chapter [airworthiness
    directives].

(b) No person may perform maintenance, preventive maintenance, or
    alterations on an aircraft other than as prescribed in this
    subpart and other applicable regulations, including part 43.

(c) No person may operate an aircraft for which a manufacturer's
    maintenance manual or instructions for continued airworthiness
    has been issued that contains an airworthiness limitations
    section unless the mandatory replacement times, inspection
    intervals, and related procedures specified in that section or
    alternative inspection intervals and related procedures set
    forth in an operations specification approved by the
    Administrator under part 121 or 135 of this chapter or in
    accordance with an inspection program approved under §91.409(e)
    are complied with.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The structure to internalize:

1. **Owner/operator is "primarily responsible."** Not the mechanic (the mechanic is responsible for the *quality* of the maintenance they perform). Not the pilot (the pilot is responsible for *contemporaneous* airworthiness under 91.7). The *owner/operator* is the one whose responsibility is to keep the airplane airworthy over time.
2. **AD compliance** is part of "maintaining airworthy condition." 14 CFR Part 39 is the AD subchapter. Owners track ADs against their airplane's tail number, comply with them, and document compliance.
3. **The "airworthiness limitations" section** of a maintenance manual is binding. If the manufacturer specifies a mandatory inspection or replacement interval (a wing-spar inspection at 6,000 hours, an AD-mandated cylinder inspection, etc.), the operator must comply.

The 91.403(a) "primarily responsible" language is significant. In an enforcement action, the FAA looks first to the owner/operator. A leaseback owner whose airplane goes out of annual is the FAA's first call. The renter who takes off in the airplane is exposed under 91.7, but the owner is exposed under 91.403.

## What 91.405 actually says

```text
§91.405  Maintenance required.

Each owner or operator of an aircraft --

(a) Shall have that aircraft inspected as prescribed in subpart E of
    this part and shall between required inspections, except as
    provided in paragraph (c) of this section, have discrepancies
    repaired as prescribed in part 43 of this chapter;

(b) Shall ensure that maintenance personnel make appropriate
    entries in the aircraft maintenance records indicating the
    aircraft has been approved for return to service;

(c) Shall have any inoperative instrument or equipment, permitted
    by §91.213(d)(2) to be inoperative, repaired, replaced, removed,
    or inspected at the next required inspection; and

(d) When listed discrepancies include inoperative instruments or
    equipment, shall ensure that a placard has been installed as
    required by §43.11 of this chapter.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The 91.405 structure:

| Paragraph | Owner/operator obligation                                                |
| --------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| (a)       | Ensure required inspections are performed                                |
| (a) cont. | Have discrepancies repaired (between inspections) per Part 43           |
| (b)       | Ensure maintenance personnel make the return-to-service entry            |
| (c)       | At the next inspection, deal with any items deferred under 91.213(d)    |
| (d)       | Ensure placards are installed for inoperative items deferred under 91.213(d) |

The "next required inspection" in (c) is the loop-closure for the no-MEL deferral pathway from lesson 02. A pilot who defers an inop item under 91.213(d) does not have an unlimited deferral period; the next annual or 100-hour must address the deferred item (repair, replace, remove, or inspect).

## What 91.407 actually says

```text
§91.407  Operation after maintenance, preventive maintenance,
rebuilding, or alteration.

(a) No person may operate any aircraft that has undergone maintenance,
    preventive maintenance, rebuilding, or alteration unless --

  (1) It has been approved for return to service by a person
      authorized under §43.7 of this chapter; and
  (2) The maintenance record entry required by §43.9 or §43.11, as
      applicable, of this chapter has been made.

(b) No person may carry any person (other than crewmembers) in an
    aircraft that has been maintained, rebuilt, or altered in a
    manner that may have appreciably changed its flight
    characteristics or substantially affected its operation in
    flight until an appropriately rated pilot with at least a
    private pilot certificate flies the aircraft, makes an
    operational check of the maintenance performed or alteration
    made, and logs the flight in the aircraft records.

(c) The aircraft does not have to be flown as required by paragraph
    (b) of this section if, prior to flight, ground tests, inspection,
    or both show conclusively that the maintenance, preventive
    maintenance, rebuilding, or alteration has not appreciably
    changed the flight characteristics or substantially affected the
    flight operation of the aircraft.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

Three pieces:

```text
After ANY maintenance, preventive maintenance, rebuilding, or
alteration:

  (a) Two requirements before any flight at all:
      1. Approved for return to service by an authorized person
         (typically the certificated mechanic who performed
         the work)
      2. Entry made in the maintenance record per 43.9 or 43.11

  (b) For maintenance/rebuild/alteration that may have appreciably
      changed flight characteristics or substantially affected
      flight operation:
      No passenger flight until a pilot has flown an operational
      check (test flight). The pilot must hold at least a private
      certificate, be appropriately rated, and log the flight in
      the aircraft records.

  (c) The (b) test flight is not required if ground tests or
      inspection conclusively show the work has NOT appreciably
      changed the flight characteristics.
```

The (b) test-flight requirement is the high-yield trap. A flight school that just had a 100-hour completed *might* need a test flight under (b) before flying with students, depending on what was done during the inspection. A control-rigging adjustment would trigger (b); a tire change would not. The mechanic and the operator decide whether (c) applies.

The classic case: a Cessna 172 with a new engine (replacement, not just an overhaul). 91.407(b) clearly applies -- a new engine appreciably changes flight characteristics and substantially affects flight operation. The first flight after the engine swap must be solo (no passengers other than crewmembers), an appropriately rated PIC must conduct the operational check, and the flight is logged in the aircraft records as the post-maintenance test flight.

The "appropriately rated" requirement is interpreted broadly: the pilot needs to be qualified for the airplane and the operation, not for the specific maintenance performed. A private pilot qualified in the C172 can be the test pilot for a C172 after maintenance.

The test flight does not require a passenger; it requires the *absence* of one. A pilot who takes a passenger up before the operational check is in 91.407(b) violation.

### Preventive maintenance the pilot may perform

14 CFR Part 43 Appendix A lists items pilots may perform on their own airplane (oil changes, filter changes, certain tire-pressure work, etc.) under "preventive maintenance." When a pilot performs preventive maintenance, the pilot:

- Makes the entry in the maintenance record per 43.9
- Does *not* require approval-for-return-to-service from a mechanic (the pilot's own action approves the airplane for return to service for items in Appendix A)
- Must still satisfy 91.407(b) if the work appreciably changed flight characteristics (rare for preventive maintenance items)

The Appendix A list is short, specific, and narrow. A pilot who attempts work outside Appendix A is performing maintenance, not preventive maintenance, and needs a certificated mechanic to sign return-to-service.

## What 91.409 actually says

```text
§91.409  Inspections.

(a) Except as provided in paragraph (c) of this section, no person
    may operate an aircraft unless, within the preceding 12 calendar
    months, it has had --

  (1) An annual inspection in accordance with part 43 of this
      chapter and has been approved for return to service by a
      person authorized by §43.7 of this chapter; or

  (2) An inspection for the issuance of an airworthiness certificate
      in accordance with part 21 of this chapter.

  No inspection performed under paragraph (b) of this section may
  be substituted for any inspection required by this paragraph
  unless it is performed by a person authorized to perform annual
  inspections and is entered as an annual inspection in the
  required maintenance records.

    may operate an aircraft carrying any person (other than a
```

### The 91.409(b) trigger -- when 100-hour applies

The 100-hour rule applies in two specific cases:

1. **The aircraft is used to carry any person other than a crewmember for hire.** Examples: a charter flight, a sightseeing flight, a banner-tow operation carrying paying passengers. (Many of these are also Part 135 -- but if the operation is Part 91 and the airplane is for-hire, the 100-hour applies.)
2. **The aircraft is used to give flight instruction for hire AND the instructor provides the aircraft.** Examples: a flight school where the school owns the airplane and bills students for instruction. The instructor is providing the airplane, so 91.409(b) applies.

The trap: an instructor who is teaching in *the student's airplane* (the student owns it, the instructor is just teaching for hire) is **not** triggering 91.409(b) on that airplane. The 100-hour rule applies when *the instructor provides the aircraft*. A renter-instructor relationship where the school's airplane is used triggers 91.409(b); a CFI hired to teach in the student's personal airplane does not.

Many CFIs do not know this exception. The result: students who own their own airplanes are flying with CFIs who incorrectly tell them their airplane needs a 100-hour. It does not. (It still needs an annual under 91.409(a). And the owner can voluntarily perform 100-hours for their own purposes.)

### The 100-hour 10-hour ferry overrun

91.409(b) allows the 100-hour limitation to be exceeded by up to 10 hours **while en route to reach a place where the inspection can be done**. The 10 hours is for ferry, not for use. A flight school that runs an airplane to 105 hours doing student flights cannot defend with the 10-hour overrun -- the overrun is only for the ferry trip to the maintenance base.

The overrun is *charged against the next interval*. If a 100-hour was completed at 99 hours and the airplane runs to 109 to ferry to the maintenance base, the next 100-hour is due not at 209 (109 + 100) but at 199 (99 + 100). The overrun reduces the next interval by the same number of hours.

### Annual vs. 100-hour content

The annual and 100-hour inspections are scope-identical -- both follow the inspection scope laid out in 14 CFR Part 43 Appendix D. The difference is who can sign:

- **Annual** must be signed by an Inspection Authorization (IA) holder. Not every A&P can sign annuals.
- **100-hour** can be signed by any A&P. (IAs can sign 100-hours too, of course.)

A 100-hour inspection performed by an IA who chooses to log it as an annual *does* count as an annual under 91.409(a) -- the regulation explicitly allows this in 91.409(a) ("No inspection performed under paragraph (b) of this section may be substituted for any inspection required by this paragraph unless it is performed by a person authorized to perform annual inspections..."). The reverse is not true: an IA's annual completed at the 100-hour interval substitutes for the 100-hour. An A&P's 100-hour does not substitute for the annual.

Many flight schools ask their IAs to sign the 100-hour as an annual when the timing works, which then resets *both* clocks at once. This is operationally efficient.

### 91.409(c) -- exceptions

```text
Aircraft NOT subject to 91.409(a) annual or 91.409(b) 100-hour:

  (c)(1) Special flight permit, experimental, light-sport, or
         provisional airworthiness certificates
  (c)(2) Aircraft on an approved Part 125, 135, or 137 inspection
         program
  (c)(3) Aircraft subject to (d) progressive or (e) continuous
         airworthiness program
  (c)(4) Turbine rotorcraft on (e) inspection program
```

Light-sport and experimental airplanes follow their own inspection regimes (typically the experimental airplane has a "condition inspection" annually, often performed by the airplane's builder if the builder holds repairman certificate for that airplane).

### 91.409(d) -- progressive inspection

A progressive inspection program splits the annual inspection into smaller blocks performed across the calendar year. Each block keeps the airplane in the airworthy state without ever requiring a single big shop visit. Owners with high-utilization airplanes use progressive programs to minimize ground time.

Progressive programs require FAA approval (the application is at 91.409(d)). The mechanism is operationally similar to a Part 135 inspection program, sized for a single airplane.

### 91.409(e) and (f) -- large/turbine programs (CAMP)

For large airplanes (other than Part 125 airplanes), turbojet multi-engine airplanes, turbopropeller-powered multi-engine airplanes, and turbine rotorcraft, a continuous airworthiness maintenance program (CAMP) is required or available. These are not GA airplanes; corporate and Part 91-K (fractional) operators use CAMP.

We name and locate. We don't deep-lesson on CAMP.

## Common misreadings

- **"Every airplane used for instruction needs a 100-hour."** Wrong. 91.409(b) requires a 100-hour only when *the instructor provides the aircraft*. A CFI teaching in the student's personal airplane does not trigger 91.409(b). Many CFIs do not know this.
- **"A 100-hour can substitute for an annual."** Half-true. A 100-hour inspection can be performed *by an IA* and logged as an annual, which then satisfies both. An A&P's 100-hour cannot substitute for an annual; only an IA can sign annuals.
- **"The 10-hour overrun lets me fly 110 hours before the 100-hour."** Wrong. 91.409(b) allows up to 10 hours overrun *while en route to a maintenance base*. The overrun is for the ferry flight, not for student flights or other revenue use.
- **"After maintenance, I can fly with passengers as long as the mechanic signed it off."** Wrong. 91.407(a) allows return-to-service flight; 91.407(b) requires a test flight (no non-crew passengers) for maintenance/rebuild/alteration that may have appreciably changed flight characteristics. The signoff is necessary but not sufficient.
- **"91.405 makes the pilot responsible for maintenance."** Wrong. 91.405 (and 91.403) make the *owner/operator* responsible. The pilot is responsible for *contemporaneous airworthiness* under 91.7. Different actors, different responsibilities.
- **"Annuals are calendar-based; 100-hours are also calendar-based."** Wrong. Annuals are calendar-based (12 calendar months). 100-hours are time-in-service based (100 hours).
- **"An expired annual just means I have to fly straight to the IA."** Wrong. An expired annual means the airplane is not airworthy. The pilot cannot fly it without a special flight permit (ferry permit) under 91.213(c) and 21.197.
- **"Preventive maintenance is whatever the pilot wants to do."** Wrong. Preventive maintenance is the items listed in 14 CFR Part 43 Appendix A. Anything outside Appendix A is maintenance and requires an A&P.
- **"After replacing an engine, I can fly with my CFI and a student."** Wrong. 91.407(b) requires the operational check be flown without non-crew passengers. A CFI plus a student likely qualifies (both are crewmembers in the dual context), but a CFI alone is the safer interpretation. A test flight after a major maintenance event is solo or two-pilot crew, not a paying passenger flight.
- **"AD compliance is the mechanic's job."** Wrong. 91.403(a) makes AD compliance the owner/operator's responsibility. The mechanic typically researches and performs the AD work, but the owner is the responsible party for ensuring compliance.

### Apply the rules

> A 1976 C172 owned by a private individual is used exclusively for the owner's personal flying. The last annual was 11 months ago. The airplane has 96 hours of time-in-service since the annual. The owner wants to fly today. Are any inspection currencies binding?

Answer: The annual under 91.409(a) is at 11 months -- still good (within 12 calendar months). 91.409(b) does not apply -- the airplane is private use, not for hire and not used for instruction-for-hire. The 100-hour interval is irrelevant for this airplane. Legal to fly.

> Same airplane, same 96 hours since annual. The owner now hires a CFI to teach instrument flying in this airplane. Does the 100-hour now apply?

Answer: No. 91.409(b) requires the 100-hour when the instructor *provides* the aircraft. The student is providing the airplane (the student is the owner). The CFI is teaching for hire, but in the student's airplane. 91.409(b) does not apply. Annual still applies under 91.409(a).

> A flight school's C152 had its 100-hour at 1240 hours of time-in-service. The airplane is now at 1339 hours. The dispatcher wants to send it on an instructional flight. Legal?

Answer: 1339 - 1240 = 99 hours of use. 100-hour is at 1340 hours. There is 1 hour of margin. Legal to launch a flight that won't reach 1 hour of additional time-in-service. A 1.5-hour planned flight would put the airplane past 1340.5 -- in 91.409(b) violation absent the 10-hour ferry overrun (which doesn't apply for instructional use). Dispatch a flight ≤ 1 hour or wait for the 100-hour.

> A C172 just had a top overhaul (cylinder replacement on all four cylinders). The mechanic signed the airplane back into service. The owner picked up the keys and wants to fly with their spouse. Legal?

Answer: No, not yet. 91.407(b) requires a post-maintenance operational check before passenger carriage when the work *may* have appreciably changed flight characteristics. A top overhaul (new or rebuilt cylinders) qualifies -- the engine could behave differently, mixture distribution could change, baseline power could shift. The owner needs to fly the airplane solo (or with crew only) for an operational check, log the flight in the records as the post-maintenance test flight, and only then carry the spouse.

The judgment call: was the work substantial enough to trigger (b)? A top overhaul is a clear yes. A spark-plug change is a clear no. A cylinder compression test is borderline. When in doubt, a 30-minute solo test flight is cheap insurance.

> An owner does the oil change on their C172 themselves. They make the entry in the maintenance record. Do they need a CFI or A&P signoff before the next flight?

Answer: No. The oil change is on the 14 CFR Part 43 Appendix A list of preventive maintenance items the pilot may perform on their own airplane. The pilot's own entry per 43.9 satisfies 91.405(b). Return to service is implicit. Legal to fly.

> A flight school IA does an annual on their C172 at 945 hours and signs it as an annual. Is the 100-hour also satisfied?

Answer: Yes. An annual signed by an IA also resets the 100-hour clock under 91.409(a) -- the airplane is good for the next 100 hours of time-in-service or 12 calendar months, whichever comes first.
