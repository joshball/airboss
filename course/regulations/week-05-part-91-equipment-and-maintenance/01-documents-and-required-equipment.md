# Documents and required equipment

Two questions every pilot answers before every flight, often without realizing they're answering them:

1. Are the right *documents* in the airplane?
2. Is the right *equipment* installed and working for this operation?

The first question lives in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/203?at=2026). The second lives in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/205?at=2026). They are both short sections. They both get misread.

This is the lesson where the AROW mnemonic and the ATOMATOFLAMES / FLAPS / GRABCARD mnemonics earn their keep, but only if you understand what they index. The mnemonics are a retrieval aid, not the rule. The rule is the cumulative structure of 91.205 -- and that structure is what we build here.

## What you'll be able to do

- State the AROW documents from memory and locate the rule that requires each one
- Build the 91.205 equipment list for VFR day, VFR night, and IFR cumulatively, by understanding *why* each block of equipment gets added when the operation changes
- Recognize that ATOMATOFLAMES / FLAPS / GRABCARD are mnemonics indexing 91.205(b), 91.205(c), and 91.205(d) respectively
- Distinguish 91.205 ("required by certification and operation") from 91.213 ("what to do when something required is broken")

## Why this matters

The pre-flight legal check is one of the lowest-cost, highest-yield habits a pilot ever builds. A pilot who can't answer "are the documents aboard?" and "is the required equipment working?" before each flight is one ramp check away from a deviation file. A CFI who hasn't built the habit into the student is one signed-off solo away from owning a violation chain.

The reason this section is checkride bait is that the answer changes by operation. Same airplane, same pilot, same CFI sign-off; the legal answer to "is this airplane legal for the flight?" depends on whether the flight is VFR day, VFR night, or IFR. The pilot who only knows ATOMATOFLAMES will pass the day VFR question and fail the night question.

## The discovery question

You are about to fly a 1976 Cessna 172N from KPAO to KSBA, departing at 4:30 a.m. local in February. Sunrise at KPAO is 6:48 a.m. The route crosses the coastal hills with a forecast 4000-ft broken. The airplane is brand new to you (you just rented it). Your renter walks you to the door, hands you the keys, and walks back to the desk.

Before you climb in, what are you checking, and where is each item required?

Pause.

The flight begins before sunrise. By 91.205 we are in **VFR night** territory until 6:48. The IFR question doesn't apply (we have no IFR plan, and the cloud bases give us comfortable VFR margin), so we're VFR-day-plus-VFR-night equipment. We need every ATOMATOFLAMES item plus every FLAPS item, all installed and working. We need the AROW documents in the airplane: airworthiness, registration, operating limitations, weight and balance. We need to verify that no required equipment is inoperative or, if it is, that we've worked through 91.213 (covered in lesson 02). And under 91.7 we, as PIC, are personally responsible for determining the airplane is in airworthy condition before flight, regardless of what the maintenance logs say.

That last part is the one that surprises new pilots. The mechanic signs off the airworthiness inspections; the *pilot* certifies airworthiness for each flight. 91.7 is the section that puts that on us.

## What 91.203 actually says

```text
§91.203  Civil aircraft: Certifications required.

(a) Except as provided in §91.715, no person may operate a civil
    aircraft unless it has within it the following:

  (1) An appropriate and current airworthiness certificate.
      Each U.S. airworthiness certificate used to comply with this
      subparagraph (except a special flight permit, a copy of the
      applicable operations specifications issued under §21.197(c)
      of this chapter, appropriate sections of the air carrier
      manual required by parts 121 and 135 of this chapter
      containing that portion of the operations specifications
      issued under §21.197(c), or an authorization under §91.611)
      must be displayed at the cabin or cockpit entrance so that
      it is legible to passengers or crew.

  (2) An effective U.S. registration certificate issued to its
      owner or, for operation within the United States, the second
      copy of the Aircraft Registration Application as provided
      for in §47.31(c), a Certificate of Aircraft Registration as
      provided in part 48, or a registration certification issued
      under the laws of a foreign country.

(b) No person may operate a civil aircraft unless the airworthiness
    certificate required by paragraph (a) of this section or a
    special flight authorization issued under §91.715 is affixed
    to the aircraft.  ...

(c) No person may operate an aircraft with a fuel tank installed
    within the passenger compartment or a baggage compartment
    unless the installation was accomplished pursuant to part 43
    of this chapter, and a copy of FAA Form 337 ... is on board
    the aircraft.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The mnemonic AROW points at the documents the pilot must have aboard:

| Letter | Document                              | Where required                                  |
| ------ | ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| A      | Airworthiness certificate              | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/203?at=2026)(a)(1)         |
| R      | Registration certificate               | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/203?at=2026)(a)(2)         |
| O      | Operating limitations (POH / AFM and placards) | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/9?at=2026)               |
| W      | Weight and balance data                | Type-certificate / 14 CFR 91 implicit via 91.9  |

Two important notes on the mnemonic:

1. **The "O" and "W" do not live in 91.203.** They live in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/9?at=2026), which requires the operating limitations to be in the aircraft and made available to the pilot, and the weight-and-balance data is required by the type certificate and operationally by 91.9. The AROW mnemonic is a memory device that conflates two regulations into one phrase. That's fine for retrieval, but for the checkride answer cite both: "documents are required by 91.203 and 91.9 -- 91.203 covers the airworthiness and registration certificates, 91.9 covers the operating limitations and the weight-and-balance which forms part of those limitations."

2. **The airworthiness certificate must be visible to passengers or crew.** Most rentals tape it to the doorframe or the back of the rear-seat headrest. Examiners check.

The often-overlooked clause: the registration certificate is normally a paper document, but 14 CFR 47 expires registrations every 7 years (since 2010). A pilot who has been flying the same airplane for 5 years may not have noticed that the registration paperwork in the airplane is *the new one* and not the original from purchase. This is a common ramp-check finding.

## What 91.7 actually says

```text
§91.7  Civil aircraft airworthiness.

(a) No person may operate a civil aircraft unless it is in an
    airworthy condition.

(b) The pilot in command of a civil aircraft is responsible for
    determining whether that aircraft is in condition for safe
    flight. The pilot in command shall discontinue the flight when
    unairworthy mechanical, electrical, or structural conditions
    occur.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

This is the section that converts the maintenance signature into pilot responsibility. The mechanic returned the airplane to service after the annual; that creates a presumption of airworthiness on the date of the inspection. **Each subsequent flight, the PIC re-affirms it.** A maintenance discrepancy that develops between flights, that the PIC notices on preflight, is the PIC's call -- not the mechanic's, not the dispatcher's, not the FBO's.

The legal weight of 91.7 is heavy. Cases on 91.7 routinely cite the pilot for "operating an unairworthy aircraft" even when a mechanic had recently signed it off, because the pilot's responsibility is contemporaneous with the flight, not delegated to the maintenance signature.

The companion concept: **91.7 is a continuing responsibility.** A PIC who launches with an airworthy airplane and then notices something concerning in flight (oil temperature climbing, a vibration, a smell) has a 91.7(b) duty to "discontinue the flight." That doesn't mean immediate emergency descent; it means the flight should not be continued under conditions that may make the airplane unairworthy. The line between continuing and discontinuing is judgment.

## What 91.205 actually says

```text
§91.205  Powered civil aircraft with standard category U.S.
airworthiness certificates: Instrument and equipment requirements.

(a) General. Except as provided in paragraphs (c)(3) and (e) of this
    section, no person may operate a powered civil aircraft with a
    standard category U.S. airworthiness certificate in any operation
    described in paragraphs (b) through (f) of this section unless
    that aircraft contains the instruments and equipment specified
    in those paragraphs (or FAA-approved equivalents) for that type
    of operation, and those instruments and items of equipment are
    in operable condition.

(b) Visual-flight rules (day). For VFR flight during the day, the
    following instruments and equipment are required:
    [the ATOMATOFLAMES list -- see below]

(c) Visual-flight rules (night). For VFR flight at night, the
    following instruments and equipment are required:
    (1) Instruments and equipment specified in paragraph (b) ...
    (2) Approved position lights.
    (3) An approved aviation red or aviation white anticollision
        light system ...
    (4) ... adequate source of electrical energy for all installed
        electrical and radio equipment.
    (5) One spare set of fuses, or three spare fuses of each kind
        required, that are accessible to the pilot in flight.

(d) Instrument flight rules. For IFR flight, the following
    instruments and equipment are required:
    (1) Instruments and equipment specified in paragraph (b) ...,
        and, for night flight, instruments and equipment specified
        in paragraph (c) of this section.
    (2) Two-way radio communication and navigation equipment
        suitable for the route to be flown.
    (3) Gyroscopic rate-of-turn indicator (turn coordinator) ...
    (4) Slip-skid indicator.
    (5) Sensitive altimeter adjustable for barometric pressure.
    (6) A clock displaying hours, minutes, and seconds with a sweep-
        second pointer or digital presentation.
    (7) Generator or alternator of adequate capacity.
    (8) Gyroscopic pitch and bank indicator (artificial horizon).
    (9) Gyroscopic direction indicator (directional gyro or
        equivalent).

(e), (f), (g) ... (additional rules for category II ops, certain
helicopters, and equipment-deviation procedures)

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The structural insight that makes 91.205 navigable: the section is **cumulative**.

```text
VFR day      = (b) the ATOMATOFLAMES list
VFR night    = (b) + (c) the FLAPS additions
IFR          = (b) + (c-night) + (d) the GRABCARD additions
```

Each operation adds to the previous. There is no "IFR day" list separate from "IFR night"; if you're IFR you have all the VFR day items, all the VFR night items if it's night, and the IFR items. The mnemonics are pneumonic shortcuts; the rule is the cumulative structure.

## ATOMATOFLAMES -- the (b) list, VFR day

Each letter indexes a 91.205(b) item:

| Letter | Item                                       | Why required |
| ------ | ------------------------------------------ | ------------ |
| A      | Airspeed indicator                         | Energy management; stall awareness |
| T      | Tachometer for each engine                 | Engine power monitoring |
| O      | Oil pressure gauge for each engine         | Lubrication monitoring |
| M      | Manifold pressure gauge for each constant-speed prop / supercharged engine | Power management |
| A      | Altimeter                                  | Vertical position; airspace |
| T      | Temperature gauge for each liquid-cooled engine | Cooling-system monitoring |
| O      | Oil temperature gauge for each air-cooled engine | Same |
| F      | Fuel quantity gauge for each tank          | Fuel state |
| L      | Landing gear position indicator (retractables) | Configuration |
| A      | Anti-collision light system (post-1971; required if installed)| Conspicuity. See 91.205(b)(11) wrinkle below |
| M      | Magnetic direction indicator               | Heading |
| E      | ELT (when required by 91.207)              | Search and rescue. Cross-references 91.207 |
| S      | Seat belts (and shoulder harnesses for seats post-1978) | Crashworthiness |

The wrinkle on the anti-collision light: the mnemonic puts "A" (anti-collision) in the day list, but the regulation under 91.205(b)(11) lists it for aircraft *certificated after a specific date* (the rule has been amended). The night-flight anti-collision-light rule is in 91.205(c)(3) and applies to *all* operations at night. For checkride defense: cite both -- "an anticollision light is required by 91.205(b)(11) for aircraft certificated after the effective date, and by 91.205(c)(3) for all night operations."

The mnemonic is a memory aid. The regulation is the authority. When the two diverge, cite the regulation.

## FLAPS -- the (c) additions for VFR night

Night adds to the VFR day list. The (c) additions:

| Letter | Item                                       |
| ------ | ------------------------------------------ |
| F      | Fuses (one spare set or three spare of each kind required) |
| L      | Landing light (if for hire; otherwise recommended but not required by this section) |
| A      | Anticollision lights (red or white)         |
| P      | Position lights (red, green, white)         |
| S      | Source of electrical power adequate for all installed equipment |

The "L" trap: the FLAPS mnemonic includes a landing light, and pilots routinely think a working landing light is *required* for VFR night under 91.205(c). It is not -- 91.205(c) does not require a landing light for non-commercial Part 91 night ops. A landing light is required by 91.205(c)(2) only "if the aircraft is operated for hire." For private Part 91 night flying, a landing light is good practice and absolutely recommended for landing, but its absence does not violate 91.205(c).

The (c) additions are mostly about being *seen* (position and anticollision lights) and being *able to operate the airplane in the dark* (electrical power source, fuses for the cockpit lighting and the radios). The structure makes intuitive sense once you see the pattern: VFR day requires the basic engine-and-flight-instrument suite; night adds the see-and-be-seen lighting and the redundancy to operate it.

## GRABCARD -- the (d) additions for IFR

IFR adds to the cumulative VFR night list. The (d) additions:

| Letter | Item                                       |
| ------ | ------------------------------------------ |
| G      | Generator or alternator of adequate capacity |
| R      | Radios (two-way comm and nav appropriate for route) |
| A      | Attitude indicator (gyroscopic pitch and bank) |
| B      | Ball (slip-skid indicator)                  |
| C      | Clock with hours, minutes, seconds         |
| A      | Altimeter (sensitive, barometric-adjustable; replaces the basic altimeter from (b)) |
| R      | Rate-of-turn indicator (turn coordinator)  |
| D      | Directional gyro (gyroscopic heading indicator) |

The IFR additions answer the question "what does a pilot need to fly the airplane without outside visual reference?" Attitude (A), heading (D), turn-and-bank (R), altitude reference (B), and the radios (R) to talk and navigate. The clock (C) is for timing approach segments where DME or GPS isn't authorized. The generator/alternator (G) is the redundant electrical to make all of the above survive a battery-only emergency.

For modern glass-cockpit airplanes, several of these items are integrated into the PFD/MFD. The integration is FAA-approved equivalents under 91.205(a) -- a G1000 satisfies the attitude indicator, heading indicator, altimeter, airspeed indicator, and slip-skid items in the integrated display, with appropriate redundancy in the standby instruments.

## The structural insight -- why the operation drives the equipment

The 91.205 structure is not arbitrary. Each block answers a question:

- **(b) -- VFR day.** "Can the pilot fly the airplane and monitor the engines?" -- basic engine instruments, basic flight instruments, basic safety equipment.
- **(c) -- VFR night.** "Can the pilot still operate when external visual reference is degraded by darkness?" -- adds lighting (see and be seen), adds power redundancy.
- **(d) -- IFR.** "Can the pilot fly the airplane *without* external visual reference at all?" -- adds the attitude/heading/turn instruments and the radio nav equipment.

The reason this structural understanding matters: a checkride examiner who asks "why does 91.205 require an attitude indicator for IFR but not for VFR day?" is not asking for a mnemonic. They're asking whether you understand that VFR pilots use the horizon for attitude and IFR pilots have to substitute an instrument for the missing visual reference. The mnemonic is the answer to "what" -- the structural understanding is the answer to "why."

## Where it sits

91.205 lives in subpart C of Part 91 (equipment, instrument, and certificate requirements). The subpart C order:

```text
§91.201  (Reserved)
§91.203  Civil aircraft: Certifications required.
§91.205  Powered civil aircraft with standard category U.S.
         airworthiness certificates: Instrument and equipment
         requirements.
§91.207  Emergency locator transmitters.
§91.209  Aircraft lights.
§91.211  Supplemental oxygen.
§91.213  Inoperative instruments and equipment.
§91.215  ATC transponder and altitude reporting equipment and use.
§91.217  Data correspondence between automatically reported pressure
         altitude data and the pilot's altitude reference.
§91.219  Altitude alerting system or device: Turbojet-powered civil
         airplanes.
§91.221  Traffic alert and collision avoidance system equipment and
         use.
§91.223  Terrain awareness and warning system.
§91.225  Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B) Out
         equipment and use.
§91.227  Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B) Out
         equipment performance requirements.
```

Subpart C reads as a single answer to "what equipment does the airplane need and how must it work?" Section 91.203 (documents) sits at the front because it's the threshold question. 91.205 is the workhorse. 91.207, 91.209, 91.211, 91.213, 91.215 each address a specific piece of equipment. 91.225 / 91.227 are the modern bolt-on for ADS-B Out.

## Common misreadings

- **"AROW is in 91.203."** Half-true. 91.203 covers the A and the R (airworthiness and registration certificates). The O (operating limitations) and W (weight and balance) live in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/9?at=2026). For a checkride answer, cite both sections.
- **"ATOMATOFLAMES is the equipment list for VFR day."** The mnemonic is *an indexing aid* to 91.205(b). The regulation is 91.205(b). When the mnemonic and the regulation disagree (the anti-collision light wrinkle), the regulation wins.
- **"FLAPS adds a landing light requirement for night."** Wrong. 91.205(c)(2) requires a landing light only for aircraft operated for hire. For Part 91 private night flight, a landing light is recommended but not required by 91.205. The mnemonic mistakenly suggests it is.
- **"IFR doesn't need any of the VFR equipment because IFR is its own list."** Wrong. 91.205(d)(1) explicitly says IFR requires the instruments and equipment specified in (b) "and, for night flight, instruments and equipment specified in paragraph (c)." The structure is cumulative.
- **"If a glass cockpit replaces the steam-gauge instruments, 91.205 doesn't apply."** Wrong. 91.205(a) accepts "FAA-approved equivalents" -- a G1000 (or equivalent integrated display) is approved as the equivalent for the listed instruments. The instruments are still required; their *form* is approved by the type certificate / STC.
- **"91.7 is just a definitional section."** Wrong. 91.7(b) is the operative pilot-responsibility section. Most 91.7 enforcement actions are paragraph (b) cases -- pilots who launched with conditions they had reason to know were unairworthy.
- **"The airworthiness certificate doesn't have to be visible."** Wrong. 91.203(a)(1) requires it be displayed at the cabin or cockpit entrance, legible to passengers or crew. Ramp checks verify this.
- **"Spare fuses are only required if the airplane uses fuses (modern airplanes use circuit breakers)."** Mostly correct -- 91.205(c)(5) reads "one spare set of fuses, or three spare fuses of each kind required, that are accessible to the pilot in flight." Aircraft using only circuit breakers do not require spare fuses (there are no fuses required to spare). But many airplanes have *some* fuses (avionics, accessory circuits) and those still trigger the 91.205(c)(5) spare-fuse requirement.

### Apply the rules

> A 1972 Cessna 172 with the panel as delivered (round-gauge) is being preflighted for a VFR night flight at 7 p.m. The position lights work, the anti-collision beacon works, the landing light is burned out, and there are no spare fuses in the airplane. Is the airplane legal for this flight?

Answer: The position lights and the anti-collision satisfy 91.205(c)(2) and (c)(3). The landing light is *not* required for Part 91 private night ops -- 91.205(c)(2) requires it only "if the aircraft is operated for hire." Spare fuses are required by 91.205(c)(5) "if the aircraft has fuses." A 1972 Cessna 172 typically has fuses for some accessory circuits; if so, the missing spare fuses are a 91.205(c)(5) violation and the airplane is not legal until spares are aboard. If the airplane has been re-wired with all circuit breakers (uncommon for that model), 91.205(c)(5) does not apply and the airplane is legal. The pilot needs to know which case applies for this specific airplane.

> A pilot is preflighting a Cirrus SR22 for a VFR cross-country at noon. The MFD shows a "TAS FAIL" message. The PFD attitude, airspeed, altimeter, and engine indications all work. The pilot is rated and current. Is the airplane legal?

Answer: TAS (true airspeed) is a derived parameter, not an instrument required by 91.205(b). For VFR day, 91.205(b) requires an *airspeed indicator* (the A in ATOMATOFLAMES) -- which displays IAS, not TAS. As long as IAS is presented correctly on the PFD, 91.205(b) is satisfied. The TAS failure does not ground the flight under 91.205. The pilot still has to clear the inop annunciator under the no-MEL pathway in 91.213(d) (covered in lesson 02), but the underlying equipment requirement is satisfied.

> Same pilot, same airplane, planning IFR. Now the slip-skid ball at the bottom of the PFD is showing "INVALID." All other PFD indications are valid. Is the airplane legal for IFR?

Answer: 91.205(d)(4) requires a slip-skid indicator for IFR. An invalid ball is a non-functioning slip-skid indicator. The airplane is not legal for IFR until the indicator is restored or the no-MEL pathway under 91.213(d) is followed (covered in lesson 02). For VFR day, 91.205(b) does not list a slip-skid indicator separately, so the airplane is legal VFR day -- but the IFR plan must change.
