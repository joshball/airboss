# Week 9 -- Enforcement and NTSB Part 830

What happens when things go wrong. The Compliance Program, certificate action, post-incident reporting obligations, and the career-enders.

This is the sober week. Every previous week has been about the rules a working pilot operates inside. This week is about what happens when those rules are broken: who knocks on the door, what they're allowed to ask, what you have to say, what you don't, and which violations end careers vs. which get resolved with a phone call and a remedial training endorsement.

The frame for the week: **the FAA mostly does not want to suspend your certificate**. Since 2015 the agency has run a Compliance Program that prefers training and counseling over punishment for inadvertent, non-criminal violations. But "mostly does not want to" is not the same as "will not." Some violations are categorically excluded from the Compliance Program. Some pilots talk themselves into certificate action that the FAA wasn't going to pursue. The pilot who knows the structure walks through the post-event landscape with rights intact; the pilot who doesn't, doesn't.

## What you'll be able to do by end of week

- Distinguish the FAA Compliance Program (FAA Order 8000.373, formerly "compliance philosophy") from legal enforcement
- Walk the enforcement progression: warning notice -> letter of investigation -> Notice of Proposed Certificate Action -> certificate action -> NTSB appeal
- Apply Pilot's Bill of Rights and Pilot's Bill of Rights 2 protections at each stage
- Know when ASRS provides waiver protection and when it does not (the "accident" disqualifier)
- Cite and apply the career-enders: 61.16, 91.17, 67.403, and the §61.15 alcohol/drug-conviction reporting rule
- Apply 49 CFR Part 830 -- accident vs. incident, the §830.5 immediate-notification list, the §830.15 ten-day written report, and §830.10 wreckage preservation
- Use 91.123(b) and 91.3(b) deliberately when ATC issues an instruction you cannot safely comply with
- Recognize when remedial training in lieu of certificate action is on the table and how to ask for it

## How this week relates to the rest of the course

Weeks 10 brings everything together with capstone integration orals -- the [gear-up-night-ifr](../orals/gear-up-night-ifr.md) capstone that closes Week 10 specifically tests post-incident reporting; **this week's oral complements that one** by focusing on the certificate-action defense angle, not the post-event reporting angle. Read both.

The career-enders touched on briefly in Week 2 (61.15, 67.403 in [06-medical-certificates.md](../week-02-part-61-deep/06-medical-certificates.md)) and Week 4 (91.17 alcohol/drug operating rule) get their full treatment here.

## Front-to-back reading

Read NTSB Part 830 in full. It's short -- about ten substantive sections, less than a long lesson's worth of text. Most pilots have never read it. Read it once carefully now and you will never confuse "accident" with "incident" again, and you will recognize which scenarios trigger immediate notification vs. the 10-day report vs. neither.

Skim Chapter 1 of FAA Order 2150.3 (the FAA's enforcement handbook). Chapter 1 frames the agency's enforcement posture and introduces the Compliance Program. The rest of the document is operational guidance for the FAA's own personnel; pilots don't need it. Chapter 1 is enough to demystify the agency's process.

## Drills

The week's drill format: "Given this incident, what reports are required, by whom, by when?" The drills mix NTSB 830 triggers, ASRS submissions, internal company reports, and ATC-side pilot deviations. The skill being exercised is *triage*: the post-event landscape has many possible obligations, only some of which apply to any given event, and the pilot who can't sort them quickly will either over-report (volunteering information that creates new exposure) or under-report (missing a 10-day deadline that turns a manageable event into a §830.15 violation on top of the original event).

## Oral

The Week 9 oral is the **certificate-action defense** walkthrough: the FAA is calling, what do you do? Pause, what to say, what not to say, when to invoke counsel, when ASRS is and isn't a defense, what the NOPCA looks like, the NTSB appeal timeline. This is *not* the post-incident reporting oral -- that's the [gear-up-night-ifr](../orals/gear-up-night-ifr.md) capstone in Week 10. This oral picks up *after* the immediate post-event obligations are met and the FAA has indicated they're investigating.

## Posture

The course is sober about this material because the consequences of mishandling it are heavy. Some of these violations end careers. Some of these procedural choices, made wrong in a moment of panic, convert a Compliance Program candidate into a certificate-action target. Most pilots will never need any of this material; the small fraction who do, on the worst day of their flying career, do not get a second chance to learn it then.

We treat enforcement the same way we treat emergencies elsewhere in the course: rehearse it cold, in the calm, so that when the call comes the response is procedural rather than improvised.
