# The career-enders

Most FAA enforcement is procedural. A pilot who busts an airspace, deviates from a clearance, or misreads a rule will, in most cases, end up with a Compliance Action and a counseling letter. The certificate stays. The career continues.

Some violations do not work that way. They fall outside the Compliance Program by category. They draw emergency revocation rather than 30-day suspension. They generate criminal referrals to the U.S. Attorney's Office in addition to administrative certificate action. A pilot who triggers one of these has not had a bad day; they have ended a career.

This lesson covers the three primary career-enders for general aviation pilots:

- **§61.16** -- refusal to submit to drug or alcohol test
- **§91.17** -- operation while under the influence (8-hour, 0.04 BAC, no influence)
- **§67.403** -- falsification of medical applications, certificates, or records

There are adjacent rules (§61.15 alcohol/drug *conviction* reporting, §91.19 carriage of narcotics, §61.59 falsification of certificates and logbooks) that deserve attention. The category is "violations the FAA will not negotiate."

## What you'll be able to do

- State each career-ender's elements cold (what the agency must prove)
- Distinguish §61.15 (conviction reporting) from §91.17 (operating rule) from §61.16 (refusal to test)
- Apply the §91.17 8-hour / 0.04 / no-influence test to specific scenarios
- Identify the consequences for each violation: emergency revocation, criminal referral, lifetime bans
- Counsel a pilot through the disclosure obligations following an alcohol/drug arrest, regardless of whether they were flying
- Articulate why these violations are categorically excluded from the Compliance Program and ASRS waiver

## Why this matters

These rules carry the heaviest sanctions in 14 CFR. Revocation, not suspension. Criminal prosecution, not just administrative action. In some cases lifetime ineligibility for a new certificate.

A pilot who knows the rules can avoid them. The §91.17 8-hour rule is famously easy to follow if you know it. The §61.15 60-day reporting requirement after an alcohol-related conviction is famously easy to forget if you don't. The §67.403 medical-application standard is easy to comply with by being thorough on Form 8500-8 and easy to violate by signing the form without reading the questions carefully.

These violations are not procedural traps. They are bright-line rules. The pilot who reads them once and treats them seriously thereafter will not encounter them. The pilot who doesn't, often does.

## The discovery question

A 28-year-old commercial pilot is arrested for DUI on a Saturday night driving home from a friend's wedding. They blow 0.09 on the roadside test, are arrested, booked, and released the next morning. The matter is set for arraignment in 30 days. They are not scheduled to fly until Monday.

Pause. Three questions:

1. Are they currently in violation of any FAA regulation?
2. If so, which?
3. What do they have to do, by when?

The answer to (1) is conditional. They were not flying when arrested. They were not within 8 hours of flying. §91.17 was not violated. **However**, §61.15(c) requires the pilot to file a written report with the FAA Civil Aviation Security Division within 60 calendar days of *any* motor vehicle action involving alcohol or drugs (DUI, DWI, DWAI, refusal to test in a vehicle context, license suspension or revocation arising from an alcohol or drug action). The arrest is a motor vehicle action triggering §61.15(c).

The 60-day clock started Sunday morning. The pilot has until day 60 to file the report.

If the pilot does not file the §61.15(c) report within 60 days, the *failure to report* is a separate FAA violation that can independently support enforcement action -- on top of any sanction stemming from a conviction.

The answer to (2) is §61.15(c) reporting requirement.

The answer to (3) is file Form Aviation Safety Reporting (the Civil Aviation Security Division mailing address; current contact through faa.gov) within 60 calendar days, and start coordinating with counsel about the implications of any conviction.

This is a typical and underappreciated trap. Pilots who get DUI arrests often think "I wasn't flying, this isn't an FAA matter." That's wrong. §61.15(c) makes the arrest itself an FAA matter. The 60-day window is binding regardless of whether the pilot is convicted, has the case dismissed, or pleads down to a non-DUI charge.

## §61.15 -- Offenses involving alcohol or drugs

```text
§61.15  Offenses involving alcohol or drugs.

(a) A conviction for the violation of any Federal or State statute
    relating to the growing, processing, manufacture, sale, disposition,
    possession, transportation, or importation of narcotic drugs,
    marihuana, or depressant or stimulant drugs or substances is grounds
    for --

  (1) Denial of an application for any certificate, rating, or
      authorization issued under this part for a period of up to 1
      year after the date of final conviction; or

  (2) Suspension or revocation of any certificate, rating, or
      authorization issued under this part.

(b) ... (similar consequences for alcohol-related driving convictions)

(c) Each person holding a certificate issued under this part shall
    provide a written report of each motor vehicle action to the FAA,
    Civil Aviation Security Division ... not later than 60 days after
    the motor vehicle action. The report must include --

  (1) The person's name, address, date of birth, and airman certificate
      number;

  (2) The type of violation that resulted in the conviction or the
      administrative action;

  (3) The date of the conviction or administrative action;

  (4) The State that holds the record of conviction or administrative
      action; and

  (5) A statement of whether the motor vehicle action resulted from
      the same incident or arose out of the same factual circumstances
      related to a previously reported motor vehicle action.

(d) ... (failure to file timely is itself grounds for action)

(e) ... (failure to file or false statement is grounds for action)

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The §61.15 structure:

- **§61.15(a) and (b)** -- Convictions for drug or alcohol offenses are grounds for FAA action against pilot certificates. Drug convictions: up to 1-year denial of certificate applications + suspension/revocation. Alcohol-related DMV actions: similar consequences.
- **§61.15(c)** -- The 60-day reporting rule. *Each motor vehicle action* (conviction or administrative DMV action) must be reported in writing within 60 days.
- **§61.15(d)/(e)** -- Failure to file the report timely, or filing a false report, is itself separate grounds for action.

### "Motor vehicle action" -- what triggers the report

A motor vehicle action under §61.15(c) means:

- A conviction for DUI / DWI / DWAI / similar
- A conviction for vehicular alcohol or drug offense not involving a moving vehicle (open container while driving, etc.)
- An administrative DMV action: license suspension, revocation, or restriction arising from an alcohol or drug-related event
- A refusal to take a roadside breath test that results in DMV action

The trigger is *the action*, not just the conviction. A pilot whose case is later dismissed or pled down still files the report on the original administrative DMV action (which often happens immediately, before the criminal case is resolved).

### What the report does

The report goes in the pilot's FAA record. It does not, by itself, trigger a certificate action. It is informational disclosure to the agency.

What follows the report:

1. The FAA may open an enforcement case under §61.15(a) or (b) if the conviction is for a covered offense
2. The FAA may not open a case if the offense is not covered (an administrative-only DMV action without a criminal conviction sometimes does not draw enforcement)
3. The pilot's medical certificate process flags the disclosure on the next renewal -- the AME asks about prior alcohol/drug events on Form 8500-8

The report is the regulatory floor. What happens after depends on the conviction outcome and the pilot's medical posture.

### What "convicted" means

Conviction includes:

- Pleading guilty
- Pleading no contest (nolo contendere)
- Being found guilty after trial
- Receiving a deferred judgment / deferred adjudication where the pilot accepted a plea but the court withheld formal entry of judgment

The deferred-judgment trap is real. Many state DUI programs allow first-time offenders to plead guilty and receive a deferred judgment that doesn't formally enter as a conviction if the pilot completes program requirements. From the FAA's perspective, the original guilty plea is the conviction; the §61.15 reporting requirement applies even if the state later expunges or vacates the judgment.

## §61.16 -- Refusal to submit to alcohol or drug test

```text
§61.16  Refusal to submit to an alcohol test or to furnish test results.

A refusal to submit to a test to indicate the percentage by weight of
alcohol in the blood, when requested by a law enforcement officer in
accordance with §91.17(c), or a refusal to furnish or authorize the
release of the test results requested by the Administrator in
accordance with §91.17(c) or (d), is grounds for --

(a) Denial of an application for any certificate, rating, or
    authorization issued under this part for a period of up to 1 year
    after the date of that refusal; or

(b) Suspension or revocation of any certificate, rating, or
    authorization issued under this part.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

§61.16 is short and absolute. A pilot who refuses the §91.17(c) test (when a law enforcement officer requests it after a flight) faces certificate denial / suspension / revocation. Up to a 1-year denial for new applicants; suspension or revocation for current certificate holders.

The §91.17(c) test is the *aviation* test, requested when a law enforcement officer suspects the pilot was operating an aircraft under the influence. It is distinct from a roadside DUI test, which is governed by state law and triggers §61.15(c) reporting (above) but not §61.16.

A refusal of the §91.17(c) test is treated by the FAA as equivalent to a positive test result. The agency does not have to prove the pilot was actually impaired; the refusal itself is the violation.

### When does §91.17(c) testing apply

§91.17(c) testing is requested when a law enforcement officer (federal, state, or local) suspects, based on reasonable cause, that the pilot was operating an aircraft under the influence of alcohol or drugs. The officer must be authorized; the request must be lawful.

The pilot's right to consult counsel before testing is governed by state law and is generally narrow (testing is time-sensitive, and delays diminish evidentiary value). The right to refuse exists, but exercising it triggers §61.16. There is no good outcome for refusing. The right answer is: do not be in a position where this question arises.

## §91.17 -- Alcohol or drugs

```text
§91.17  Alcohol or drugs.

(a) No person may act or attempt to act as a crewmember of a civil
    aircraft --

  (1) Within 8 hours after the consumption of any alcoholic beverage;

  (2) While under the influence of alcohol;

  (3) While using any drug that affects the person's faculties in any
      way contrary to safety; or

  (4) While having an alcohol concentration of 0.04 or greater in a
      blood or breath specimen.

(b) Except in an emergency, no pilot of a civil aircraft may allow a
    person who appears to be intoxicated or who demonstrates by manner
    or physical indications that the individual is under the influence
    of drugs (except a medical patient under proper care) to be carried
    in that aircraft.

(c) A crewmember shall, on request of a law enforcement officer, submit
    to a test to indicate the percentage by weight of alcohol in the
    blood.

(d) ... (further test-result rules)

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The §91.17(a) test has four independent prongs:

| Prong  | Condition                                                             |
| ------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| (a)(1) | 8 hours from "bottle to throttle"                                     |
| (a)(2) | No flying "under the influence" (regardless of timing or BAC)         |
| (a)(3) | No flying while using any drug affecting faculties contrary to safety |
| (a)(4) | BAC less than 0.04                                                    |

A pilot must satisfy *all four* simultaneously. Failing any single prong is a §91.17 violation.

### The "8-hour rule" misnomer

Pilots commonly call this the "8-hour rule," but the 8 hours is only one of four prongs. A pilot who has 8 hours since their last drink but is still under the influence (still impaired) is in violation under (a)(2). A pilot who is at 0.039 BAC (just under the (a)(4) limit) but is impaired in judgment or coordination is in violation under (a)(2). A pilot who is at 0.05 BAC after 9 hours since drinking is in violation under (a)(4).

The right operational framing: 8 hours is the *floor*. "Under the influence" and "0.04 or greater" are independent ceilings. A responsible pilot considers all three and does not fly within 8 hours of drinking, does not fly while impaired regardless of timing, and does not fly with a measurable BAC.

Many airlines and operators impose stricter rules: 12 hours, 24 hours, or zero-tolerance "no measurable BAC." Those are operator policies, not §91.17. A pilot subject to operator policy is bound by both.

### Drugs other than alcohol

§91.17(a)(3) covers drugs broadly. The prohibition is on flying while using any drug "that affects the person's faculties in any way contrary to safety." This includes:

- Recreational drugs (almost universally disqualifying)
- Sedating prescription medications (some disqualifying, some allowed -- consult AME)
- Cannabis -- federally Schedule I, FAA's position is zero tolerance regardless of state legalization
- Over-the-counter sedating medications (Benadryl, etc.) -- typically prohibited if effects are active

The FAA publishes "Allowed" and "Do Not Issue" medication lists; AMEs use these during medical exams and pilots can consult them before self-grounding. A pilot taking a "Do Not Issue" medication is per se in violation of §91.17(a)(3) if they fly while it's active.

The cannabis question is operationally important and frequently misunderstood. Cannabis remains federally illegal (Schedule I). The FAA's position has been explicit: even in states where cannabis is legal, FAA-certificated pilots may not use it. A pilot who tests positive for cannabis on a §91.17(c) test or any required drug test is in §91.17 violation regardless of state law. The §61.15(a) drug-conviction consequences also attach if there's a federal or state cannabis conviction.

### "Carriage of narcotics" -- §91.19 adjacent rule

§91.19 prohibits operating an aircraft carrying narcotics, marijuana, or depressant/stimulant drugs (other than for proper medical purposes). The carriage is the violation; the pilot need not be using.

§91.19 violations carry similar consequences to §91.17: certificate suspension or revocation, plus potential criminal exposure under federal drug-trafficking statutes. Pilots flying for compensation in states where cannabis is legal need to know that carrying cannabis as cargo, even legally under state law, is a §91.19 federal violation.

## §67.403 -- Falsification of medical applications

```text
§67.403  Applications, certificates, logbooks, reports, and records:
Falsification, reproduction, or alteration; incorrect statements.

(a) No person may make or cause to be made --

  (1) Any fraudulent or intentionally false statement on any application
      for a medical certificate or on a request for any reconsideration
      thereof;

  (2) Any fraudulent or intentionally false entry in any logbook,
      record, or report that is required to be kept, made, or used
      to show compliance with any requirement for the issuance or
      exercise of the privileges, of any certificate issued under this
      part;

  (3) Any reproduction for fraudulent purpose of any medical certificate
      issued under this part; or

  (4) Any alteration of any medical certificate issued under this part.

(b) The commission by any person of an act prohibited under paragraph
    (a) of this section is a basis for --

  (1) Denial of an application for a medical certificate or request for
      any reconsideration thereof; or

  (2) Suspension or revocation of all airman, ground instructor, and
      medical certificates and ratings held by that person.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

§67.403 is the medical-fraud rule, mirrored on the airman-certificate side by §61.59 (falsification, reproduction, or alteration of certificates, logbooks, etc.).

### The "intentionally false" standard

The standard for §67.403(a)(1) is "fraudulent or intentionally false." The agency must prove intent. Innocent error or genuine misunderstanding is a defense; willful concealment is the violation.

In practice, "intentionally false" is read broadly. A pilot who *knew* of a medical condition the form asks about (a diagnosed mental health condition, a medication regimen, a prior hospitalization) and *did not disclose* on Form 8500-8 has acted intentionally for §67.403 purposes even if they thought "it didn't apply" or "the FAA wouldn't care." The "knew or should have known" standard from §61.53 (medical deficiency, covered in [Week 2's medical lesson](../week-02-part-61-deep/06-medical-certificates.md)) carries through to §67.403's intent analysis.

### Consequences

A §67.403 violation carries the heaviest administrative sanction in the airman regulations:

- Revocation of *all* certificates: pilot, instructor, ground instructor, medical
- Up to 1-year denial of any new application
- Criminal referral to the U.S. Attorney's Office (false statement to a federal agency under 18 USC 1001 carries up to 5 years imprisonment)
- Reputational consequences -- the violation is on the public record

This is the heaviest career-ender. A §67.403 conviction is a permanent feature of the pilot's history; future applications, employment, and insurance underwriting all reflect it.

### How to avoid it

Read Form 8500-8 questions carefully. Disclose anything within the question's scope. When in doubt, disclose more rather than less and let the AME or FAA medical office determine relevance. The FAA's special-issuance pathways routinely accommodate disclosed conditions (diabetes, depression with treatment, certain cardiac conditions); the same conditions concealed and later discovered draw §67.403 sanctions.

The trap is "this won't matter." A pilot who decides the question's scope is narrower than written, and skips disclosure on that basis, has crossed the line. The right rule: disclose, and let the agency decide what matters.

## §61.59 -- Falsification of pilot certificates and logbooks

The airman-certificate analog to §67.403:

```text
§61.59  Falsification, reproduction, or alteration of applications,
certificates, logbooks, reports, or records.

(a) No person may make or cause to be made --

  (1) Any fraudulent or intentionally false statement on any application
      for a certificate, rating, authorization, or duplicate thereof,
      issued under this part;

  (2) Any fraudulent or intentionally false entry in any logbook,
      record, or report that is required to be kept, made, or used
      to show compliance with any requirement for the issuance or
      exercise of the privileges of any certificate, rating, or
      authorization under this part;

  ... (other paragraphs on reproduction, alteration)

(b) The commission of an act prohibited under paragraph (a) is a basis
    for suspending or revoking any airman certificate, rating, or
    authorization held by that person.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

§61.59 covers logbook falsification (the most common variant), application fraud (the airman-certificate version of medical-application fraud), and certificate alteration. The sanction structure mirrors §67.403: revocation of certificates, criminal referral for the false statement, lifetime impact.

The most common §61.59 trap: pilots logging time they did not fly. Logbooks are scrutinized during checkrides, hiring processes, and any enforcement investigation. Fabricated entries are detectable through Hobbs/Tach reconciliation, scheduling records, and witness testimony. A pilot who pads logbook hours risks revocation of all certificates if discovered.

## Why these are categorically excluded

Each of these career-enders falls outside the Compliance Program (8000.373B section 3) by category:

| Career-ender         | Compliance Program exclusion       | ASRS waiver exclusion |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------- | --------------------- |
| §61.16 refusal       | Lack of qualification + criminal   | Criminal offense      |
| §91.17 alcohol/drugs | Reckless or intentional + criminal | Criminal offense      |
| §61.15 conviction    | Criminal (for the underlying)      | Criminal offense      |
| §67.403 fraud        | Reckless or intentional + criminal | Criminal offense      |
| §61.59 fraud         | Reckless or intentional + criminal | Criminal offense      |
| §91.19 narcotics     | Criminal                           | Criminal offense      |

Counseling and remedial training do not address the underlying issues. The FAA does not "train someone out of" intoxicated flying or medical fraud. Certificate action is the only available administrative tool.

## Where it sits

```text
14 CFR Part 61 (pilot certification)
  §61.15  Offenses involving alcohol or drugs (the conviction-reporting rule)
  §61.16  Refusal to submit to alcohol or drug test
  §61.59  Falsification (airman side)

14 CFR Part 67 (medical certification)
  §67.403  Falsification (medical side)

14 CFR Part 91 (operating rules)
  §91.17  Alcohol or drugs (the operating rule)
  §91.19  Carriage of narcotic drugs

18 USC 1001 (federal false-statement statute)
  Criminal exposure for false statements to a federal agency

49 USC 46306, 46315  Aviation-specific federal criminal statutes
  Operating under influence, falsifying records
```

The pilot side, the medical side, and the operating side are all separate. A pilot can violate one without the others. A pilot who lies on Form 8500-8 has a §67.403 problem; a pilot who flies drunk has a §91.17 problem; a pilot whose DUI license suspension goes unreported has a §61.15(c) problem. Each draws separate sanction.

## Common misreadings

- **"§61.15 only applies if I'm convicted."** Wrong. §61.15(c) requires reporting any *motor vehicle action*, which includes administrative DMV actions independent of criminal conviction. A license suspension following a DUI arrest, even if the criminal case is later dismissed, triggers the 60-day report.
- **"The 8-hour rule is the only §91.17 prong."** Wrong. §91.17(a) has four independent prongs: 8 hours, no influence, no impairing drug, BAC less than 0.04. All four must be satisfied. The 8-hour count is necessary but not sufficient.
- **"Cannabis is legal in my state, so I can use it."** Wrong, for FAA-certificated pilots. The FAA's position is zero tolerance regardless of state law. Cannabis use can support §91.17(a)(3) violations, §61.15 drug conviction consequences, and medical-certificate denials.
- **"§67.403 only matters if I lie about something serious."** Wrong. Any "fraudulent or intentionally false" statement on the medical application falls under §67.403. The agency reads "intentional" broadly. Failing to disclose a documented mental health diagnosis, an arrest, a hospitalization, or a medication is intentional even if the pilot thought it wouldn't matter.
- **"I can refuse the §91.17(c) test if I'm not sure I should consent."** Wrong. Refusing triggers §61.16 sanctions equivalent to a positive test. There is no procedural advantage to refusing.
- **"A deferred judgment isn't a conviction so I don't need to report under §61.15."** Wrong. The FAA reads "convicted" to include guilty pleas, no-contest pleas, and deferred judgments where a guilty plea was entered. State expungement does not unwind the §61.15 obligation.
- **"§91.19 only applies to flying under contract."** Wrong. §91.19 applies to *any* flight carrying narcotics or controlled drugs (other than for proper medical purposes). A private pilot carrying cannabis on a personal flight, even on a single-state route in a state where cannabis is legal, violates federal §91.19.
- **"The Compliance Program will work for my DUI."** Wrong. §61.15 / §61.16 / §91.17 are categorically excluded from the Compliance Program. The disposition is administrative certificate action, possibly with criminal exposure on top.
- **"ASRS waiver covers a §91.17."** Wrong. Criminal offenses are categorically excluded from ASRS. Filing the report does not protect against a §91.17 prosecution.
- **"§61.59 is just a paperwork rule."** Wrong. Logbook falsification is one of the FAA's hardest-line violations. The agency revokes certificates over logbook fraud routinely. The "small lie" of adding 5 unsanctioned hours to qualify for an experience minimum is treated the same way as a wholesale fabrication.

### Apply

> A pilot blows 0.05 on a roadside DMV test. The state suspends their license. They were not flying. Do they file a §61.15(c) report?

Answer: Yes. Within 60 calendar days. The administrative DMV action (license suspension) is itself a "motor vehicle action" under §61.15(c) regardless of whether the criminal case is resolved or pursued. File the written report with the FAA Civil Aviation Security Division.

> A pilot has 9 hours since their last drink, blows 0.02 on a §91.17(c) test after a flight. Are they in violation of §91.17?

Answer: Possibly. The 8-hour prong is satisfied (9 > 8). The 0.04 prong is satisfied (0.02 < 0.04). The "under the influence" prong (§91.17(a)(2)) is independent and not directly tested by BAC alone -- if the pilot was demonstrably impaired (slurred speech, coordination, judgment errors during the flight), §91.17(a)(2) can still apply. Practical: a pilot at 0.02 is unlikely to be impaired enough to support an (a)(2) charge, but the determination is fact-specific.

> A 27-year-old commercial pilot is taking a prescribed sedating antidepressant. They have been on the medication for 6 months, with their AME's awareness, under a special issuance. They want to fly tomorrow. Are they in §91.17 violation?

Answer: Probably not, if the medication is approved under their special issuance. §91.17(a)(3) prohibits flying while using "any drug that affects the person's faculties in any way contrary to safety." Approved medications (with special issuance authorization, typically with stable dosing for an established period) are operationally compatible with PIC duty. The AME and FAA Aerospace Medicine office set the conditions; the pilot follows them. Flying while taking a medication *outside* the special issuance terms (different dose, different drug, recent changes) would be the violation.

> A pilot fails to disclose a 10-year-old DUI on a Form 8500-8 medical application, thinking "it's old, doesn't matter." The AME issues a third-class medical. The pilot flies for 2 years before an unrelated FAA investigation surfaces the prior DUI. What's the §67.403 exposure?

Answer: §67.403(a)(1) is in play. The medical application asked about prior alcohol-related convictions; the pilot intentionally did not disclose. The disclosure was knowingly false. The FAA can revoke the medical, all airman certificates, and pursue criminal referral under 18 USC 1001 for false statement to a federal agency. The "I thought it was old enough not to matter" defense fails because the form's question (current language) covers all prior alcohol/drug events without time limit; the agency reads the failure as intentional. This is the §67.403 archetype.

> A 121 first officer is arrested for a DUI on personal time. Their employer has zero-tolerance termination for DUI. They are terminated. Their FAA exposure?

Answer: Separate from the employment consequence: §61.15(c) requires the 60-day report. §61.15(a)/(b) consequences apply if they're convicted. The first-class medical for ATP duties may be threatened by the conviction (FAA medical reviews alcohol-related convictions). If the pilot pleads down to a non-DUI charge (reckless driving, for example), the §61.15(c) reporting still applies (the underlying motor vehicle action triggers it), but §61.15(a)/(b) consequences depend on the specific offense conviction. They consult an aviation attorney specifically; this case is not DIY.

> A 600-hour CFI signs a flight review endorsement for a pilot they did not actually fly with -- the pilot was a friend, the CFI was confident the pilot was current, and they signed the endorsement without conducting the review. The pilot later has an incident, FAA investigates, the false endorsement surfaces. CFI exposure?

Answer: §61.59(a)(2) is the violation: false logbook entry required to show compliance with §61.56. The CFI's airman certificate (CFI-A and any underlying private/commercial) is at risk for revocation. Criminal referral under 18 USC 1001 is plausible because the endorsement constitutes a statement on a record required by federal regulation. Loss of the entire flight instructor career. This is a §61.59 archetype.

> A pilot is stopped at a ramp inspection. The inspector smells alcohol on the pilot's breath and requests a §91.17(c) breath test. The pilot says "I want my lawyer first." Inspector says "you have to consent now or I'll treat it as a refusal." What's the right answer?

Answer: There is no good answer. Refusing triggers §61.16 sanctions (revocation, 1-year denial). Consenting risks a positive test. The right outcome was for the pilot to not be in this position. Practically, the pilot's only available choice is consent (avoiding §61.16) and contest the test results, the lawful basis for the request, and the chain of custody through counsel later. Refusal is per se a violation; consent at least preserves procedural defenses.
