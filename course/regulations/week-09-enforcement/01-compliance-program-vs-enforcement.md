# The Compliance Program vs. legal enforcement

When a pilot's flight attracts FAA attention, the agency picks one of two doors. Behind door one is the Compliance Program, an administrative path that resolves with counseling, training, or a written plan and *no certificate action*. Behind door two is legal enforcement, an adversarial path that can end with suspension or revocation of the certificate.

Most pilots assume that getting a Letter of Investigation in the mail means the second door. Most of the time, it doesn't. The agency has formally preferred the Compliance Program for inadvertent, non-criminal violations since 2015, and it now resolves the majority of pilot violations administratively. But "most" is not "all," and the criteria that send a case down the legal-enforcement path are specific and largely under the pilot's control in the moments after the event.

This lesson establishes the bifurcation. Subsequent lessons walk the pipeline of legal enforcement (lesson 02), the protections that travel with the pilot through that pipeline (lessons 02 and 03), and the categories of violation that are categorically excluded from the Compliance Program door (lesson 04).

## What you'll be able to do

- State the FAA's two-track enforcement posture cold, including which order document defines each track
- Predict whether a given pilot violation is Compliance-Program-eligible based on the published exclusion criteria
- Articulate why the Compliance Program is in the FAA's interest as well as the pilot's
- Recognize the moments in a post-event interaction where pilot conduct shifts the case from the Compliance Program toward legal enforcement
- Explain why understanding this framework *before* the event is operational, not theoretical, knowledge

## Why this matters

The Compliance Program is the single most important thing a working pilot can know about FAA enforcement, and almost no pilot knows it before the day they need it.

Before 2015, the FAA's default response to a violation was legal enforcement: a Notice of Proposed Certificate Action, a suspension or revocation period, an NTSB appeal if the pilot wanted to fight it. The agency suspended a lot of certificates. Many of those suspensions were over inadvertent procedural lapses (a missed altitude assignment, an airspace nick, a logbook discrepancy) where the pilot self-identified the problem, accepted responsibility, and was clearly not a safety risk going forward. Suspending the certificate of a pilot like that does nothing for safety; it just punishes the pilot.

In 2015, the FAA published Order 8000.373 and shifted the default. The agency's stated preference now is to resolve violations through *training and counseling* rather than punishment when the pilot is willing to engage and the violation is inadvertent. Most LOIs (Letters of Investigation) issued today close with the Compliance Program rather than certificate action.

A pilot who knows this:

- Does not panic when the FSDO calls
- Does not deny the event happened
- Does not lawyer up so aggressively that the agency reads it as adversarial posture
- Does engage cooperatively, take responsibility, and offer to do remedial training
- Does retain counsel before making written statements, but uses counsel to *shape* a Compliance-Program disposition rather than to fight a fight that hasn't started

A pilot who doesn't know this:

- Reads "Letter of Investigation" and assumes their certificate is gone
- Either over-defensively refuses to engage (pushing the case toward legal enforcement)
- Or over-cooperatively volunteers statements that get used as evidence
- Or files a NASA ASRS report and assumes total immunity (it isn't, and we cover the actual scope in [03-asrs-asap-and-self-disclosure.md](03-asrs-asap-and-self-disclosure.md))

The framework is procedural; the response to it has to be calm. That requires having the framework already in your head when the call comes.

## The discovery question

A pilot busts the Bravo shelf around a Class B airport by 200 feet of altitude on a VFR flight to a Class D airport beneath the shelf. ATC notices, files a pilot deviation report, and the FSDO calls a week later. The pilot is a 400-hour private pilot, current on flight review and recency, with no prior FAA enforcement history. The deviation lasted about 90 seconds. Nobody died, no traffic conflict, no other consequences.

Pause. Two questions:

1. Is this case more likely to resolve through the Compliance Program or through legal enforcement?
2. What about the pilot's *response* to the FSDO over the next two weeks could shift it the other direction?

The case is exactly the Compliance Program archetype. Inadvertent (the pilot didn't *plan* to bust the shelf), non-criminal, no consequence beyond the deviation itself, willing-to-engage pilot, no prior history. Order 8000.373 calls this case out as the kind the agency wants to resolve administratively.

What can shift it toward legal enforcement:

- Pilot lies about the event ("I was at 4000, not 4500")
- Pilot refuses to engage with the FSDO entirely
- Pilot's prior history shows a pattern (this is the third bust in two years)
- Investigation surfaces that the pilot was not actually current on flight review
- Pilot's response posture is hostile rather than learning-oriented

The framing "is this Compliance-Program-eligible?" is partly a question about the violation itself and partly about the pilot. The agency's discretion runs through both axes.

## What FAA Order 8000.373 actually says

FAA Order 8000.373 (current revision: 8000.373B, 2018) is the agency's published Compliance Program policy. Read directly:

```text
FAA Order 8000.373B  Compliance Program  (paraphrased excerpt)

1. Purpose. This Order establishes the FAA's Compliance Program, which
   reflects the agency's preference for using compliance tools to address
   noncompliance whenever a regulated entity is willing and able to
   comply, the noncompliance does not appear deliberate, and there is
   no significant risk to safety.

2. Compliance Tools. Compliance tools are non-enforcement actions
   designed to identify and correct noncompliance through training,
   education, and counseling. They include:
     a. On-the-spot correction
     b. Counseling
     c. Additional training (remedial training)
     d. Compliance Action with documented training plan

3. When Legal Enforcement Action is Required. The Compliance Program
   does not apply when noncompliance is:
     a. Reckless or intentional
     b. A criminal act
     c. A demonstrated lack of qualification
     d. Repetitive (the same or similar conduct recurring after a
        prior compliance disposition)
     e. A failure to meet a previously imposed compliance commitment

4. Posture. FAA personnel will use the Compliance Program as the
   first option whenever the noncompliance and the regulated entity's
   posture support its use.

Note: paraphrase of the policy structure. Read the actual Order before
referencing in exam.
```

Three things to extract from the literal text:

1. **The Compliance Program is the FAA's preferred default.** This is not a back-pocket option the agency reluctantly uses; it is the *first* option the FAA inspector evaluates when a violation surfaces.
2. **Eligibility is bounded by four exclusion categories.** Reckless, intentional, criminal, demonstrated-lack-of-qualification, repetitive, or failure-to-meet-prior-commitment. Anything in those categories goes legal enforcement, period.
3. **"Willing and able to comply"** is a posture test. The pilot's behavior during the investigation matters. A pilot who refuses to engage, lies about facts, or shows no interest in remedial training is signaling unwillingness, which the order treats as disqualifying for the Compliance Program track.

### What the Compliance Program is *not*

It is not amnesty. The pilot still faces an FAA investigation, a documented disposition, and possibly a Compliance Action that goes in their file. Future violations will be evaluated against the prior Compliance Action; the "repetitive" exclusion in section 3(d) means a pilot whose first bust was Compliance-Program-resolved is more likely to face legal enforcement on a similar second bust.

It is also not a defense the pilot invokes. The agency makes the Compliance vs. enforcement decision based on its own evaluation. A pilot cannot demand "I want the Compliance Program for this." They can demonstrate posture and willingness; the FSDO inspector and supervisor decide.

## What it actually means -- the working-pilot frame

Translate the policy into the operational reality:

| Track             | Trigger                                                  | Outcome                                                              |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Compliance Action | Inadvertent, non-criminal, willing pilot, no recent priors | Counseling, training, written commitment. No certificate action.     |
| Legal enforcement | Reckless, intentional, criminal, repeat, or lack of qual | Notice of Proposed Certificate Action -> suspension/revocation. NTSB-appealable. |

The choice between the two tracks gets made early in the FSDO's review, often before the pilot even submits a written response to the LOI. That means **the pilot's first interaction with the FSDO matters**. It is not the time to be defensive, evasive, or hostile. It is also not the time to volunteer a confession of every speculative misstep that may have contributed.

The right posture in the first interaction:

1. Acknowledge that you got the LOI and are taking it seriously.
2. Confirm you will provide a written response within the requested timeframe (typically 10 business days).
3. Indicate willingness to engage in remedial training if appropriate.
4. **Do not narrate the event in detail.** That goes in the written response, prepared with counsel.
5. **Do not deny the event happened.** The agency already has data; denial reads as either dishonesty or detachment.

That posture maximizes the chance the case routes to the Compliance Program door.

### What "willing and able to comply" looks like in practice

The order requires the regulated entity to be both willing (cooperative) and able (qualified). The "able" test is the lack-of-qualification exclusion. It surfaces when the violation reveals a gap in pilot competence the FAA cannot resolve with a counseling letter. Examples:

- Pilot's logbook reveals they were never properly endorsed for the airplane they were flying
- Pilot's medical was expired at the time of the event
- Pilot's flight review had lapsed by 18 months
- Pilot's response demonstrates they don't understand the rule they violated and don't believe they need to

In any of those cases, the agency cannot in good conscience close the file with "additional training" because the pilot's qualifications are themselves the issue. The case routes to legal enforcement, where certificate suspension or revocation can address the underlying qualification gap.

The "willing" test is simpler: the pilot either engages with the investigation or doesn't. Refusing to respond to the LOI, refusing to participate in remedial training, or affirmatively contesting the agency's findings on contested facts all signal unwillingness. The agency interprets unwillingness as the pilot's choice of the legal enforcement track.

## The Pilot's Bill of Rights interaction

The Pilot's Bill of Rights (PBR, 2012) and Pilot's Bill of Rights 2 (PBR2, 2016) give the pilot specific procedural protections during an enforcement investigation. The interaction with the Compliance Program:

The Compliance Program is not a *substitute* for PBR/PBR2 protections. It is the alternative *track* the agency may run alongside its investigation. A pilot under PBR2 protections retains the right to:

- Be notified that they are the subject of an FAA investigation
- Obtain ATC tapes and FAA records relevant to the investigation
- Have legal counsel present during agency interactions
- Be informed that statements may be used in a future enforcement action

The pilot exercises these rights on either track. Specifically: requesting ATC tapes and FAA records under PBR2 is *not* an act of unwillingness to engage. The order distinguishes between the pilot using their procedural rights (fine) and the pilot refusing to engage substantively with the agency's questions (unwilling). A pilot can request all the records they're entitled to *and* indicate willingness to participate in remedial training; the two are not in conflict.

We treat PBR2 in detail in [02-enforcement-action-pipeline.md](02-enforcement-action-pipeline.md). Here we just note that the protections travel along the path, and that exercising them early is wise on either track.

## Where it sits

Order 8000.373 is administrative agency policy, not a CFR. It does not appear in 14 CFR. It appears in the FAA's Order series, alongside Order 2150.3 (the FAA's enforcement handbook, which is the operational document for FSDO inspectors and lawyers running enforcement cases).

The Order series structure:

```text
FAA Order 2150.3  FAA Compliance and Enforcement Program
  (the operational handbook for inspectors and counsel)
  Chapter 1  Compliance Program (program overview, posture, philosophy)
  Chapters 2-N  Specific procedures by violation type, sanction guidelines

FAA Order 8000.373  Compliance Program (policy statement)
  (the policy document the agency cites externally)
```

Both are public documents. The pilot reads 8000.373 (short, clear) for the framework and skims Chapter 1 of 2150.3 for the operational application. The remaining chapters of 2150.3 are sanction-guideline tables (how many days of suspension a given violation typically draws) which are useful only to defense counsel and pilots in active enforcement cases.

The CFR-side anchor for legal enforcement is broad. The catch-all "careless or reckless" provision lives at [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/13?at=2026):

```text
§91.13  Careless or reckless operation.

(a) Aircraft operations for the purpose of air navigation. No person
    may operate an aircraft in a careless or reckless manner so as to
    endanger the life or property of another.

(b) Aircraft operations other than for the purpose of air navigation.
    No person may operate an aircraft, other than for the purpose of
    air navigation, on any part of the surface of an airport used by
    aircraft for air navigation, in a careless or reckless manner so
    as to endanger the life or property of another.
```

§91.13 is the agency's universal rider. Nearly every certificate-action case attaches §91.13 alongside the specific section the pilot violated, because §91.13 does not require the agency to prove a specific operational rule was broken; it only requires the agency to prove the operation was "careless or reckless." This is the section pilots quietly fear, because the standard is broad and the agency has substantial discretion in applying it.

The Compliance Program defangs §91.13 in the inadvertent-violation context: the order's explicit preference for non-enforcement resolution means a single inadvertent §91.13 case is far more likely to draw a counseling letter than a suspension. But §91.13 attached to a *reckless* or *intentional* violation does not get the Compliance Program treatment.

## Common misreadings

- **"The Compliance Program means the FAA can't suspend my certificate."** Wrong. The Compliance Program is the FAA's *preference*, not a binding limit. The agency retains full enforcement authority. If the violation falls in the exclusion categories (reckless, intentional, criminal, repeat, qualification gap), the Compliance Program does not apply and certificate action is available.
- **"If I don't get a phone call from the FSDO, the Compliance Program kicked in automatically."** Wrong. Silence is not a Compliance Action. Most enforcement cases begin with a Letter of Investigation, written. If you didn't get one, either no investigation is open or one is open and you haven't received notice yet. Don't assume.
- **"I should call the FSDO before they call me to get ahead of it."** Mostly wrong. Self-disclosure has its place (NASA ASRS, ASAP for 121/135) but unsolicited "I think you might be looking into me" calls to the FSDO are rarely the right move. They commit you to a narrative before you have counsel or all the facts. The general rule: respond to what the FAA asks; don't volunteer more.
- **"The Compliance Program covers everything as long as I'm cooperative."** Wrong. Cooperation is necessary but not sufficient. The exclusion categories (reckless, intentional, criminal, repeat, lack of qualification) are categorical. A pilot who flies drunk is not Compliance-Program-eligible no matter how cooperative they are afterward; §91.17 violations are excluded by the criminal-act category and §91.13 is excluded by the reckless category.
- **"NASA ASRS protects me from enforcement so I don't need the Compliance Program."** Wrong. ASRS provides a *limited* waiver of certificate action under specific conditions, not total immunity, and the conditions specifically exclude accidents, criminal violations, lack of qualification, and repeat offenders. The Compliance Program and ASRS are independent; some events qualify for both, some for neither, some for one but not the other. We treat ASRS in detail in [03-asrs-asap-and-self-disclosure.md](03-asrs-asap-and-self-disclosure.md).
- **"The order is just guidance; the FAA can ignore it whenever they want."** Mostly wrong. Internal agency orders bind the agency's own personnel. An FSDO inspector who routes a Compliance-Program-eligible case to legal enforcement without justification is acting against agency policy and is subject to internal review. The order is not a defense in court for a pilot, but it is operationally binding inside the agency.

### Apply

> A 200-hour private pilot transponder-codes 1200 into a Class C airport without contacting approach. A 90-second incursion. They land at the towered airport, ATC asks them to call the FSDO, and they do. The FSDO logs a pilot deviation. The pilot has no priors. The pilot's flight review and medical are current. Compliance Program or legal enforcement?

Answer: Compliance Program. Inadvertent (no plan to bust the C), non-criminal, no consequence beyond the deviation, willing pilot (called the FSDO when asked), no priors, qualifications intact. The order's archetype.

> Same pilot, but the deviation was after they had already received a counseling letter for a similar Bravo bust 14 months earlier.

Answer: Legal enforcement is now likely. The "repetitive" exclusion in 8000.373B section 3(d) treats the same or similar conduct recurring after a prior compliance disposition as disqualifying. The first bust got a Compliance Action; the second bust shifts the calculus. The agency views the prior counseling as having been given a chance.

> Same first scenario, but during the FSDO call, the pilot's first instinct is to argue that ATC's radar must have been wrong because they're "sure" they were below the C ceiling.

Answer: The pilot is converting a Compliance-eligible case into a legal-enforcement risk through their posture. The order requires "willing and able to comply" -- arguing about contested facts in the first call signals unwillingness. The agency has the radar tape; arguing the data is going to lose anyway. Better posture: "I take this seriously. I'll review the data with counsel and submit a full written response to the LOI."

> A commercial pilot flies a Part 135 charter while their medical certificate has been expired for 6 weeks. The flight is uneventful. The expiration surfaces during a routine ramp check. Compliance Program or legal enforcement?

Answer: Likely legal enforcement. The medical expiration is a qualification gap (lack of qualification exclusion) and the operation was for compensation under Part 135 (heightened scrutiny). The agency does not "train and counsel" a commercial pilot back into having a current medical -- the pilot already had the qualification rule and broke it. Certificate suspension is the typical outcome, with the suspension period set under FAA Order 2150.3 sanction guidelines.

> A pilot lands gear-up on a night IFR cross-country with substantial damage to the airplane. No injuries. The pilot files an ASRS report the next morning and waits for the FSDO call.

Answer: Likely legal enforcement, and the ASRS waiver does not apply. ASRS waiver is excluded for accidents under 14 CFR 91.25 / AC 00-46 -- a gear-up landing with substantial damage is an aircraft accident under 49 CFR 830.2, which removes the ASRS waiver eligibility. The Compliance Program *might* apply (depends on facts -- was it pilot inattention, was there a contributing mechanical issue, was the pilot's training trajectory adequate) but the case starts with an accident-level event, which is heavier than the typical Compliance candidate. The pilot needs counsel and a careful response strategy. We work this scenario fully in the [gear-up-night-ifr](../orals/gear-up-night-ifr.md) capstone.
