# NTSB Part 830 -- accidents, incidents, and reporting

The post-event reporting landscape spans two agencies and two titles of the Code of Federal Regulations. The FAA's enforcement posture (Title 14) is one piece. The NTSB's reporting requirements (Title 49, Part 830) are the other. They are *independent* obligations: a pilot can have a Part 830 reporting duty even when no FAA enforcement is contemplated, and a pilot can face FAA enforcement on an event that triggers no Part 830 obligation.

49 CFR Part 830 is short. It defines what counts as an "accident" and what counts as an "incident," lists the events requiring immediate notification of the NTSB, sets deadlines for written reports, and requires preservation of wreckage. The whole part is roughly ten substantive sections. Most pilots have never read it. Reading it once, carefully, removes the most common confusions: "is this an accident?" "do I have to call NTSB?" "do I have to call the FAA?" "can I move the airplane?"

The instructions are binding on operators. The "operator" includes the pilot in command in most general-aviation contexts. The pilot's failure to comply with §830.5 (immediate notification) or §830.15 (10-day written report) is itself a violation that can attach to any underlying enforcement case.

## What you'll be able to do

- State the NTSB definitions of "aircraft accident" and "incident" cold (49 CFR 830.2)
- Walk the §830.5 immediate-notification list and predict which events trigger it
- Apply the §830.15 10-day written report deadline
- Apply the §830.10 wreckage preservation rule and explain its scope
- Distinguish FAA notification from NTSB notification and identify when each is owed
- Identify the most common pilot errors in post-event reporting

## Why this matters

The post-event minutes are when most reporting errors happen. The pilot who has just landed gear-up, watched the airplane slide to a stop, evacuated the passengers, and called for fuel is also the pilot the regulations expect to make decisions about NTSB notification, FAA notification, wreckage preservation, and ASRS filing. The pilot who has rehearsed Part 830 cold makes those decisions procedurally. The pilot who hasn't, makes them under panic and improvised.

The most common errors:

- **Treating "accident" as a colloquial term.** §830.2 has a precise definition. A precise event either is or is not an accident; the pilot's gut feel doesn't determine.
- **Confusing FAA and NTSB obligations.** They are separate agencies. NTSB notification under Part 830 is required even if the FAA pursues no enforcement. FAA notification is customary but not required by Part 830 specifically.
- **Moving the wreckage.** §830.10 requires preservation. Moving the airplane to "see what happened" is itself a violation independent of the original event.
- **Missing the 10-day written report deadline under §830.15.** The deadline is hard. Missing it is a separate violation.
- **Overreporting.** A non-accident event reported as an accident triggers an NTSB investigation that wasn't required and locks the pilot into a narrative they may not fully understand yet.

## The discovery question

A 350-hour private pilot, on a long cross-country, encounters severe turbulence at cruise. The autopilot disconnects, the airplane is briefly upset, the pilot recovers within 100 feet of altitude loss, and the flight continues uneventfully. On landing the pilot inspects the airplane and finds no visible damage. A passenger reports neck pain that resolves within an hour with no medical care.

Pause. Three questions:

1. Is this a Part 830 accident?
2. Is this a Part 830 incident requiring §830.5 immediate notification?
3. What does the pilot owe in writing, and by when?

The accident definition (§830.2) requires either *substantial damage*, *serious injury*, or *death*. None of those applies here. The airplane is undamaged; the passenger's neck pain is not "serious injury" (which is defined in §830.2 to require hospitalization more than 48 hours, fracture, severe internal injury, etc.). Not an accident.

The §830.5 immediate-notification list includes specific kinds of in-flight events: flight control system malfunction or failure, inability of any required flight crewmember to perform their duties, in-flight fire, aircraft collision in flight, damage to property other than the aircraft of more than $25,000, etc. An autopilot disconnect during turbulence is not on the list. Not an §830.5 incident.

So: nothing owed under Part 830. The pilot's regulatory duty stops here.

What the pilot might still do operationally: file a NASA ASRS report (if there's any concern of a procedural lapse), document the event in their flight notes, brief their next CFI on the upset and recovery for training purposes, possibly file a Pilot Report (PIREP) on the turbulence area. None of that is required by Part 830.

The discovery teaches the bidirectional rule: not every concerning event is reportable, and the regulations give clear definitions for what is.

## §830.2 -- the definitions

Read directly from 49 CFR §830.2:

```text
49 CFR §830.2  Definitions.

Aircraft accident means an occurrence associated with the operation
of an aircraft which takes place between the time any person boards
the aircraft with the intention of flight and all such persons have
disembarked, and in which any person suffers death or serious injury,
or in which the aircraft receives substantial damage. ...

Fatal injury means any injury which results in death within 30 days
of the accident.

Incident means an occurrence other than an accident, associated with
the operation of an aircraft, which affects or could affect the
safety of operations.

Operator means any person who causes or authorizes the operation of
an aircraft, such as the owner, lessee, or bailee of an aircraft.

Serious injury means any injury which:
  (1) Requires hospitalization for more than 48 hours, commencing
      within 7 days from the date of the injury was received;
  (2) Results in a fracture of any bone (except simple fractures of
      fingers, toes, or nose);
  (3) Causes severe hemorrhages, nerve, muscle, or tendon damage;
  (4) Involves any internal organ; or
  (5) Involves second- or third-degree burns, or any burns affecting
      more than 5 percent of the body surface.

Substantial damage means damage or failure which adversely affects
the structural strength, performance, or flight characteristics of
the aircraft, and which would normally require major repair or
replacement of the affected component. Engine failure or damage
limited to an engine if only one engine fails or is damaged, bent
fairings or cowling, dented skin, small punctured holes in the skin
or fabric, ground damage to rotor or propeller blades, and damage
to landing gear, wheels, tires, flaps, engine accessories, brakes,
or wingtips are not considered "substantial damage" for the purpose
of this part.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The four definitions every pilot needs cold:

| Term                | Bright-line test                                                          |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Aircraft accident   | Death OR serious injury OR substantial damage during operation            |
| Fatal injury        | Death within 30 days of the event                                         |
| Serious injury      | >48 hr hospitalization, fracture (not finger/toe/nose), severe damage     |
| Substantial damage  | Adverse effect on strength / performance / flight, requires major repair |
| Incident            | Anything other than accident that affects or could affect safety          |

### What "substantial damage" excludes

§830.2's substantial-damage definition has a *negative list* that pilots routinely miss. Specifically excluded:

- Engine failure or damage limited to a single engine on a single-engine airplane (the ONE engine failing is excluded)
- Bent fairings or cowling
- Dented skin, small punctures
- Ground damage to props or rotors
- Landing gear, wheels, tires, flaps, engine accessories, brakes, wingtips

This means a pilot who suffers a single-engine failure in flight, gliders to a successful off-airport landing, and damages the prop on touchdown has *not* incurred substantial damage. The engine failure itself doesn't count (one-engine exception). The prop strike doesn't count (specifically excluded). If the airframe is otherwise intact, this event is not an accident under §830.2.

Now the gear-up landing case from the [capstone oral](../orals/gear-up-night-ifr.md): a gear-up landing typically involves *both* a prop strike *and* fuselage damage from contact with the runway. The prop strike alone is excluded. The fuselage skin damage may or may not be "substantial" -- if it's just a dented skin, it's specifically excluded. But a typical gear-up event also bends the firewall, twists structure, and may damage the engine mount through prop-strike load transfer. *Those* damages are substantial. And the prop strike, while specifically excluded as "ground damage to propeller blades," typically requires engine teardown per the manufacturer's prop-strike inspection -- the engine itself becomes damaged through the strike's load on the crankshaft.

The judgment call: was there structural damage that would normally require major repair? If yes, accident. If no, possibly just an incident.

### The 30-day fatal-injury rule

A passenger who dies 25 days after the event makes the event a *fatal* accident under §830.2 even if they were apparently fine immediately after. This matters in events where injuries are initially minor but later become serious or fatal due to complications.

## §830.5 -- immediate notification

```text
49 CFR §830.5  Immediate notification.

The operator of any civil aircraft, or any public aircraft not operated
by the Armed Forces or an intelligence agency of the United States,
or any foreign aircraft shall immediately, and by the most expeditious
means available, notify the nearest National Transportation Safety
Board (NTSB) office, when:

(a) An aircraft accident or any of the following listed serious
    incidents occur:

  (1) Flight control system malfunction or failure;

  (2) Inability of any required flight crewmember to perform normal
      flight duties as a result of injury or illness;

  (3) Failure of any internal turbine engine component that results in
      the escape of debris other than out the exhaust path;

  (4) In-flight fire;

  (5) Aircraft collision in flight;

  (6) Damage to property, other than the aircraft, estimated to exceed
      $25,000 for repair (including materials and labor) or fair market
      value in the event of total loss, whichever is less;

  (7) For large multiengine aircraft (more than 12,500 pounds maximum
      certificated takeoff weight):

      (i) In-flight failure of electrical systems which requires the
          sustained use of an emergency bus powered by a back-up source
          such as a battery, auxiliary power unit, or air-driven
          generator to retain flight control or essential instruments;

      (ii) In-flight failure of hydraulic systems that results in
           sustained reliance on the sole remaining hydraulic or
           mechanical system for movement of flight control surfaces;

      (iii) Sustained loss of the power or thrust produced by two or
            more engines; and

      (iv) An evacuation of an aircraft in which an emergency egress
           system is utilized.

  (8) Release of all or a portion of a propeller blade from an aircraft,
      excluding release caused solely by ground contact;

  (9) A complete loss of information, excluding flickering, from more
      than 50 percent of an aircraft's cockpit displays known as:

      (i) Electronic Flight Instrument System (EFIS) displays;

      (ii) Engine Indication and Crew Alerting System (EICAS) displays;

      (iii) Electronic Centralized Aircraft Monitor (ECAM) displays; or

      (iv) Other displays of this type, which generally include a
           primary flight display (PFD), primary navigation display
           (PND), and other integrated displays;

  (10) Airborne Collision and Avoidance System (ACAS) resolution
       advisories issued either:

       (i) When an aircraft is being operated on an instrument flight
           rules flight plan and compliance with the advisory is
           necessary to avert a substantial risk of collision between
           two or more aircraft; or

       (ii) To an aircraft operating in class A airspace.

  (11) Damage to helicopter tail or main rotor blades, including
       ground damage, that requires major repair or replacement of
       the blade(s);

  (12) Any event in which an aircraft operated by an air carrier:

       (i) Lands or departs on a taxiway, incorrect runway, or other
           area not designed as a runway; or

       (ii) Experiences a runway incursion that requires the operator
            or the crew of another aircraft or vehicle to take
            immediate corrective action to avoid a collision.

(b) An aircraft is overdue and is believed to have been involved in an
    accident.

Note: paraphrase of §830.5. Read the actual reg before referencing
in exam.
```

The §830.5(a) list is the *exact* list of events triggering immediate NTSB notification beyond the accident-itself trigger. Pilots typically remember about half of it. Read the full list once now; you will not get this wrong on a checkride or in the moment after.

### The "general aviation" subset

For typical general-aviation single-engine ops, the relevant §830.5 triggers are:

- An accident (any of the §830.2-defined kinds)
- Flight control system malfunction or failure (§830.5(a)(1))
- Required crewmember unable to perform duties (§830.5(a)(2))
- Engine debris escape other than exhaust (§830.5(a)(3))
- In-flight fire (§830.5(a)(4))
- Mid-air collision (§830.5(a)(5))
- Damage to non-aircraft property over $25,000 (§830.5(a)(6))
- Propeller blade release in flight (§830.5(a)(8))
- Overdue aircraft (§830.5(b))

The large-airplane and air-carrier triggers (§830.5(a)(7), (9), (10), (11), (12)) typically don't apply to GA ops, but pilots transitioning to type-rated airplanes need to know they exist.

### "Immediately, and by the most expeditious means available"

The §830.5 standard. In practice this means a phone call to the nearest NTSB regional office (or the FSDO, which will coordinate). The NTSB maintains a 24-hour duty officer. The contact information is published; current numbers are at ntsb.gov.

The same call typically goes to the FSDO. The FAA expects to be notified for any accident even though Part 830 doesn't specifically require FAA notification (Part 830 is an NTSB rule). FSDO notification is customary; in many cases the FSDO and NTSB coordinate, and a single call to either reaches both.

What "immediate" means: as soon as it is operationally safe and reasonable to make the call. A pilot in the active rescue phase doesn't have to call mid-evacuation. A pilot in the FBO an hour after a survivable accident, taking care of passengers and securing the airplane, makes the call there. Letting the rest of the day pass before calling fails the "immediate" standard.

## §830.10 -- preservation of wreckage

```text
49 CFR §830.10  Preservation of aircraft wreckage, mail, cargo, and
records.

(a) The operator of an aircraft involved in an accident or incident
    for which notification must be given is responsible for preserving
    to the extent possible any aircraft wreckage, cargo, and mail
    aboard the aircraft, and all records, including all recording
    mediums of flight, maintenance, and voice recorders, pertaining
    to the operation and maintenance of the aircraft and to the
    airmen until the Board takes custody thereof or a release is
    granted pursuant to §831.12(b).

(b) Prior to the time the Board or its authorized representative takes
    custody of aircraft wreckage, mail, or cargo, such wreckage, mail,
    or cargo may not be disturbed or moved except to the extent
    necessary:

  (1) To remove persons injured or trapped;

  (2) To protect the wreckage from further damage; or

  (3) To protect the public from injury.

(c) Where it is necessary to move aircraft wreckage, mail, or cargo,
    sketches, descriptive notes, and photographs shall be made, if
    possible, of the original positions and condition of the wreckage
    and any significant impact marks.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The preservation rule. The pilot does not move the airplane more than necessary to clear safety hazards (block on the runway, fire risk, etc.). The pilot does not "investigate" by moving controls, opening cowlings, or repositioning components. Photographs are fine; touching is not.

The rule applies until the NTSB releases the wreckage. In practice, NTSB releases of GA accidents happen within days to weeks after the on-site investigation. Until then, the airplane stays where it stopped, or moves only as required.

The "what about insurance" question: the insurance adjuster cannot move the airplane until the NTSB releases. This is one of the operational reasons NTSB releases happen quickly for routine GA accidents -- the agency's investigation interest is bounded and the wreckage is needed for owner / insurance disposition.

### What "preservation" includes beyond the airplane

§830.10(a) requires preservation of:

- Aircraft wreckage
- Cargo and mail
- Records: flight records, maintenance records, voice recorders, recording media

This means the pilot's logbook, the airplane's maintenance logs, the GPS unit's flight track, ADS-B data, EFB devices, any cockpit camera footage. All of it gets preserved. None of it gets edited, deleted, or "cleaned up" before NTSB receives it.

The rule is binding on the operator. A pilot who deletes a GPS track to "remove personal data" before NTSB arrives has violated §830.10. The agency reads such deletions as obstruction.

## §830.15 -- written report

```text
49 CFR §830.15  Reports and statements to be filed.

(a) Reports. The operator of an aircraft shall file a report on Board
    Form 6120.1 within 10 days after an accident, or after 7 days if
    an overdue aircraft is still missing. A report on an incident for
    which immediate notification is required by §830.5(a) shall be
    filed only as requested by an authorized representative of the
    Board.

(b) Crewmember statement. Each crewmember, if physically able at the
    time the report is submitted, shall attach a statement setting
    forth the facts, conditions, and circumstances relating to the
    accident or incident as they appear to him. ...

(c) Where to file the reports. The operator of an aircraft shall file
    any report with the field office of the Board nearest the accident
    or incident.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The written report:

- Form 6120.1 -- the NTSB's accident/incident reporting form
- 10 days for accidents (7 days for overdue aircraft)
- For §830.5(a) incidents (immediate-notification incidents), only filed if the NTSB asks
- Crewmember statement attached (if able)
- Filed with the nearest NTSB field office

Pilots typically miss the §830.15 deadline because they're focused on the post-event chaos and forget the 10-day clock. Counsel involved in the case calendars this aggressively.

The form itself is straightforward: aircraft data, pilot data, flight data, narrative of events, contributing factors. Counsel typically helps draft the narrative because it goes into the public record and may be used in any subsequent enforcement action.

## How Part 830 interacts with FAA enforcement

Part 830 is NTSB. FAA enforcement is Title 14. They are independent.

| Event class                                  | NTSB notification (Part 830) | FAA notification (custom) | FAA enforcement (potential) |
| -------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------- | ------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Accident under §830.2                        | Required immediately          | Customary                 | Possible                    |
| §830.5(a) incident (e.g., in-flight fire)    | Required immediately          | Customary                 | Possible                    |
| Other incident affecting safety              | Not required                  | Customary if FAA-noticed  | Possible                    |
| FAA-noticed deviation, no injury, no damage  | Not required                  | Required (LOI response)   | Likely                      |

The two reporting systems answer different questions. NTSB is interested in safety -- understanding what happened and preventing recurrence. FAA is interested in compliance -- whether rules were broken and what to do about it. They share information but have separate rules.

A pilot in an accident has both obligations: NTSB notification under §830.5 AND a likely FAA enforcement investigation (LOI to follow). The post-event response addresses both tracks in parallel.

## Common misreadings

- **"An accident is whatever the pilot or witnesses think looked bad."** Wrong. §830.2 has a precise definition: death, serious injury, or substantial damage. A bad-looking event without those elements is not an accident.
- **"Substantial damage means any damage."** Wrong. §830.2's definition is bounded: structural strength, performance, or flight characteristics, requiring major repair. Specifically excluded: dented skin, prop strikes, single-engine failures, gear/wheel/tire/flap/wingtip damage, etc. Read the negative list.
- **"A prop strike alone is substantial damage."** Wrong, technically. §830.2 excludes "ground damage to rotor or propeller blades." The prop strike itself is excluded. *However*, the strike usually requires engine teardown under the manufacturer's prop-strike inspection, and the engine teardown often reveals damage that meets the substantial-damage threshold. The event becomes an accident not because of the prop, but because of the engine that the prop strike damaged.
- **"NTSB notification is the same as FAA notification."** Wrong. Different agencies, different rules. NTSB notification under §830.5 is required for accidents and the listed serious incidents. FAA notification is customary but not specifically required by Part 830; it happens as part of FSDO investigation procedures.
- **"I should call NTSB whenever something goes wrong."** Wrong. Over-reporting triggers an investigation that wasn't required and locks the pilot into a narrative. Report under §830.5 when the event meets the definition; otherwise don't.
- **"The 10-day report is for any incident."** Wrong. §830.15 requires the 10-day Form 6120.1 only for *accidents*. For §830.5(a) incidents, the written report is filed only when the NTSB requests it. Pilots commonly mis-file Form 6120.1 for events that don't require it; counsel coordinates.
- **"I can taxi the airplane after a gear-up to get it off the runway."** Mostly wrong. Moving the airplane is permitted under §830.10(b) only as needed for personnel rescue, further-damage protection, or public safety. "Off the runway to the FBO" is sometimes within (b)(3) (public safety -- runway clearance), but the airplane is towed, not taxied under power. A prop-struck engine cannot be operated until torn down per the manufacturer's prop-strike SB. A bent gear may be unsafe under power.
- **"NTSB releases the wreckage when their investigation is complete."** Right. For routine GA accidents, this is days to weeks. For complex events (fatalities, multiple aircraft, type investigations), it can be months. Insurance and owner disposition wait.
- **"§830.10 means I have to leave the airplane on the runway."** Wrong. The rule allows moves to clear the runway and protect the public. The standard is "to the extent necessary" -- not zero movement, but no investigation-style examination.

### Apply -- accident, incident, or neither?

> A pilot taxis into a hangar door with the wingtip during a windy taxi. The wingtip is damaged. No injuries.

Answer: Not an accident under §830.2. Wingtip damage is specifically excluded from "substantial damage." Likely just a maintenance event. Notification not required under §830.5. FAA may still investigate as a potential §91.13 careless-or-reckless or §91.123 (if at a towered airport with taxi instructions). Document; respond if FAA asks.

> A pilot suffers an in-flight engine failure on a single-engine airplane. They glide to a successful off-airport landing in a field. The airplane is undamaged. No injuries.

Answer: Not an accident under §830.2. The engine failure on a single-engine is in the "substantial damage" exclusion list (one engine failing is excluded). Airplane undamaged means no substantial damage. No serious injury or death. Not an accident. Likely not a §830.5(a) incident either (the engine debris exception in §830.5(a)(3) is for *internal turbine* debris escape, not piston engines). FAA notification customary; NTSB notification not strictly required.

> Same engine failure scenario, but the pilot lands hard, bending the gear and damaging the firewall.

Answer: Now an accident. The bent firewall is structural damage adversely affecting strength; the bent gear plus firewall together constitute substantial damage. §830.5 immediate notification applies. §830.10 preservation applies. §830.15 10-day written report due.

> A pilot is upset by wake turbulence in cruise, loses 800 feet of altitude, recovers without incident. No damage. One passenger sprains a wrist trying to brace; no medical attention needed.

Answer: Not an accident. The injury is not "serious" (no hospitalization >48 hr, no fracture, no severe damage). No substantial damage to the airplane. No §830.5(a) trigger (this isn't on the list). Not Part-830-reportable. Pilot may file ASRS for the wake encounter and PIREP the wake turbulence. No NTSB notification.

> An aircraft has a complete electrical failure in IMC at FL250 in a turbine twin. The pilot uses backup battery power to maintain instruments and communicates with ATC; lands without incident.

Answer: §830.5(a)(7)(i) trigger -- in-flight failure of electrical systems requiring sustained use of an emergency bus / backup source for flight control or essential instruments, in a large multiengine aircraft (over 12,500 lb MGW). Immediate notification required. Whether it's also an "accident" depends on whether substantial damage or serious injury occurred (sounds like neither here), but the §830.5(a) incident-list trigger applies independently. Notify NTSB.

> A gear-up landing at a towered airport. Prop strike, dented belly skin, no other damage. Engine teardown later reveals crankshaft displacement requiring engine replacement.

Answer: Almost certainly an accident. The gear-up itself was the event; the engine damage discovered later confirms substantial damage. The prop strike alone was excluded but the resulting engine damage is structural to the aircraft system. NTSB notification required immediately upon understanding the event involves substantial damage (which often happens during the initial post-event inspection, not weeks later). §830.10 preservation; §830.15 10-day report. This is the [capstone oral](../orals/gear-up-night-ifr.md) scenario.

> A passenger develops a cardiac event during cruise flight. The pilot diverts; the passenger is hospitalized for 5 days but recovers.

Answer: §830.2 "serious injury" -- hospitalization more than 48 hours. The injury occurred during operation (between boarding-with-intent-of-flight and disembarkation). Accident under §830.2. NTSB notification required, even though the cause was medical and not aircraft-related. The accident involves the aircraft only because the passenger was aboard during the operation.

> A pilot suffers an in-flight fire from an electrical short. The fire is contained and extinguished; the pilot lands safely. Some interior panels are heat-damaged.

Answer: §830.5(a)(4) trigger -- in-flight fire. Immediate notification required regardless of damage extent. The interior panel damage may or may not be substantial (probably not), but the in-flight fire alone triggers §830.5. NTSB notified immediately; Form 6120.1 filed only if NTSB requests (per §830.15(a) for §830.5(a) incidents that aren't accidents).

> A solo student pilot lands hard at their home airport. Tire damage but no other damage. They taxi to the ramp.

Answer: Not an accident. Tire damage is in the §830.2 exclusion list. Not on the §830.5(a) incident list. Not Part 830-reportable. The CFI evaluates whether further training is warranted; the regulatory file is clean.

> A pilot lands at the wrong airport (uncontrolled, similar layout, 8 miles from intended). No damage, no injury, no other consequence.

Answer: Not an accident. Not on the §830.5(a) list (the air-carrier wrong-airport / wrong-runway trigger in §830.5(a)(12) does not apply to private GA). Not Part 830-reportable. May draw FAA attention as a §91.103 or §91.13 question (preflight planning, careless operation). The pilot files ASRS as protection if there's any deviation concern.
