# NASA ASRS, ASAP, and self-disclosure as protection

The FAA wants to know about safety problems before they become accidents. The agency cannot, however, get pilots to volunteer information about their own near-misses if doing so risks their certificates. The resolution is the self-disclosure ecosystem: programs that grant the pilot conditional immunity in exchange for safety-relevant reports.

There are two principal programs. **NASA ASRS** (Aviation Safety Reporting System) is open to every pilot, every operation, every certificate level. **ASAP** (Aviation Safety Action Program) is a sponsored program available to Part 121 and Part 135 operators that runs in parallel with the airlines' internal safety systems. They have different scopes, different protections, and different filing mechanics.

Both share a critical feature: protection is conditional. ASRS does not provide blanket immunity from enforcement. It provides a one-time waiver of certificate action for specific kinds of violations, subject to specific exclusions, when the report is filed within a specific window. Pilots who file an ASRS report and assume they are bulletproof are mistaken; pilots who file an ASRS report and *do not* know what protection it actually provides are also mistaken.

This lesson teaches the actual scope of each program, the filing mechanics, and the moments where self-disclosure is the right move vs. the moments where it is not.

## What you'll be able to do

- State the protections ASRS provides and the four conditions for waiver eligibility
- Apply the 10-day filing rule and explain why the wall-clock matters
- Distinguish ASRS protection from ASAP protection and identify which population each serves
- Recognize when ASRS does and does not apply -- the accident exclusion in particular
- Decide, given a near-miss event, whether ASRS filing is the right move and why
- Articulate the limits of self-disclosure as a defense (it is not a substitute for the Compliance Program or for counsel)

## Why this matters

ASRS is the pilot's most universally available enforcement protection. It costs nothing, takes 20 minutes, and can convert a 30-day suspension into a counseling letter for a substantial fraction of inadvertent violations. Pilots who do not understand it lose protection they were entitled to, simply by not filing.

ASRS is also the pilot's most-misunderstood enforcement protection. Pilots who *over*-rely on it think they are immune from enforcement entirely; pilots who *under*-rely on it think it doesn't apply to them and skip the filing. Both errors leave the pilot worse off.

A working pilot files ASRS reports for any event that *might* have been a deviation, near-miss, or procedural lapse, on the assumption that if it turns out the FAA noticed, the report will help; if it turns out the FAA didn't notice, the report did no harm. The 20-minute filing buys real protection at zero cost in the bad case and zero cost in the good case.

## The discovery question

A 600-hour private pilot, returning from a $100 hamburger flight, accepts a runway crossing instruction from ground control after landing, doesn't quite hear the runway number, and crosses the wrong runway. ATC catches it, says nothing remarkable on frequency, the pilot continues taxi to the FBO, parks, and goes in for lunch.

That afternoon, the pilot replays the sequence in their head and realizes they probably committed a runway incursion. Nobody was on the runway they crossed. They have no idea if ATC reported it. The FSDO has not called.

Pause. Three questions:

1. Should the pilot file a NASA ASRS report?
2. By when?
3. What does it protect against if filed?

The answer to (1) is yes. Even if the FAA never opens a case, the report is a low-cost insurance policy.

The answer to (2) is *within 10 calendar days of the event*. The clock starts at the event, not at when the pilot realizes a violation occurred. Today is the day of the event; the filing should happen today or within 10 days at most.

The answer to (3) is conditional waiver of certificate action under 14 CFR 91.25 / AC 00-46 if the violation:

- Was inadvertent and not deliberate
- Did not involve a criminal offense
- Did not involve an accident (49 CFR 830.2)
- Did not show a lack of qualification or competency
- Has not been the subject of a prior ASRS waiver in the preceding 5 years

A taxi-time runway crossing without anyone on the runway, by an otherwise-qualified pilot, with no prior ASRS waivers in the last 5 years, almost certainly qualifies. The waiver, if granted, means that even if the FAA later identifies the violation, the agency will not impose certificate action.

This is the typical case. The pilot doesn't always know whether the FAA noticed. The 10-day window closes either way. The cost of filing is 20 minutes. The cost of not filing, in the bad case, is whatever certificate action the agency would have imposed.

## NASA ASRS -- the program

ASRS is administered by NASA, not the FAA. This is structural: a pilot reporting to the FAA reports to the agency that enforces against them; a pilot reporting to NASA reports to a third party that strips identifying information before sharing aggregate data with the FAA.

The program exists under FAA Advisory Circular 00-46 (current revision: AC 00-46F, 2021). The waiver authority lives in 14 CFR §91.25.

### The CFR -- §91.25

```text
§91.25  Aviation Safety Reporting Program: Prohibition against use of
reports for enforcement purposes.

The Administrator of the FAA will not use reports submitted to the
National Aeronautics and Space Administration under the Aviation
Safety Reporting Program (or information derived therefrom) in any
enforcement action except information concerning accidents or
criminal offenses which are wholly excluded from the Program.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

§91.25 is short and absolute *for what it covers*: the FAA cannot use the ASRS report itself or information derived from it as evidence in enforcement. But §91.25 only prohibits *use of the report*. It does not prohibit the FAA from pursuing the same case through evidence the agency obtains independently (ATC tapes, radar, witness statements). The protection is not "the violation never happened"; it is "we won't use your safety report against you."

### The waiver -- AC 00-46

AC 00-46 is where the actual *waiver of certificate action* lives. Read carefully:

```text
FAA AC 00-46F  Aviation Safety Reporting Program  paraphrased

The FAA will consider the filing of a report with NASA concerning an
incident or occurrence involving a violation of 49 USC subtitle VII
or 14 CFR to be indicative of a constructive attitude. Such an attitude
will tend to prevent future violations.

Therefore, the Administrator may, in determining the type and extent
of any enforcement action, consider the filing of an ASRS report. The
Administrator will not impose a civil penalty or certificate action if
the violation was:

  (1) Inadvertent and not deliberate;

  (2) Not a criminal offense, accident, or one of the items specifically
      excluded;

  (3) Not indicative of a lack of qualification or competency; and

  (4) The reporter has not been found to have committed a violation
      under 49 USC or 14 CFR for a period of 5 years prior to the
      occurrence;

AND the report was filed in a timely manner (within 10 days of the
occurrence).

Note: paraphrase of AC 00-46 substance. Read the actual AC before
referencing in exam.
```

The four eligibility tests + the timing rule are the entire scope. Memorize them. They come up in every checkride conversation about ASRS, and they come up again in every actual ASRS filing the pilot ever makes.

### The four eligibility tests, unpacked

**1. Inadvertent and not deliberate.**

A pilot who busts an altitude assignment because they misread the altimeter qualifies. A pilot who busts an altitude assignment because they intentionally climbed without clearance to dodge weather without coordinating with ATC does not -- the action was deliberate even if the reasoning was reasonable. (The remedy in that case is to declare an emergency under §91.3(b), which is a different protection; we cover this in [06-deviations-and-remedial-training.md](06-deviations-and-remedial-training.md).)

The line is whether the action was *intended at the moment of the violation*, not whether the *outcome* was intended.

**2. Not a criminal offense, accident, or specifically excluded.**

The criminal exclusion catches §91.17 (alcohol/drug operating violations), §61.16 (refusal to test), §67.403 (medical fraud), and any willful or reckless conduct that the U.S. Attorney's Office could prosecute.

The accident exclusion is the most-missed: 49 CFR 830.2 defines "aircraft accident" as an event involving substantial damage or serious injury. A gear-up landing with prop strike is almost certainly an accident under §830.2; ASRS does not waive enforcement for accident-level events. (The report is still useful to file for confidentiality and as a posture signal, but the waiver of certificate action does not apply.)

The "specifically excluded" category includes operations conducted under 14 CFR Parts 121, 129, and 135 by an air carrier (those operators have ASAP instead) and certain other specifically named operations.

**3. Not indicative of a lack of qualification or competency.**

The qualification gap exclusion. A pilot who flew a complex airplane without the §61.31(e) endorsement is not just inadvertently noncompliant; they were *not qualified* to fly the airplane in the first place. ASRS does not paper over qualification gaps.

This exclusion overlaps with the Compliance Program's lack-of-qualification exclusion in 8000.373B. The frame is the same: if the violation reveals the pilot wasn't supposed to be doing the operation at all (vs. they were qualified but made a mistake during the operation), neither program applies.

**4. No prior violation found in the preceding 5 years.**

The 5-year clean-record requirement. A pilot who has had a prior FAA enforcement action found in the last 5 years cannot use the ASRS waiver again until 5 years have passed since the prior finding. The waiver is intended for first-time inadvertent violations, not as a recurring shield for repeat offenders.

A NASA-noted ASRS *report* in the prior 5 years does not disqualify; only a *finding* of violation does. The distinction is real: a pilot can file ASRS reports for events that turned out to be non-violations (or events the FAA never opened a case on) without burning their 5-year clock. The 5 years runs from a finding, not from a filing.

### The 10-day filing window

The wall-clock starts at the event and runs for 10 calendar days. Filing on day 11 forfeits the waiver eligibility. There is no "I didn't realize until later" extension.

Two operational consequences:

- **File when you suspect, not when you confirm.** A pilot who thinks they may have busted an altitude doesn't have time to investigate fully. File the report based on the best available understanding; the report can be updated, but the timing rule is binary.
- **Calendar from the event date.** Day 1 is the day after the event. Day 10 is 10 days after. Pilots who file "within 10 business days" rather than 10 calendar days lose the waiver. The CFR uses calendar days.

The filing itself takes 15-25 minutes via the NASA ASRS website (asrs.arc.nasa.gov). The pilot fills out the standard form, submits, and receives a date-stamped confirmation. The confirmation is the pilot's evidence that the report was filed in time; pilots keep the confirmation in their flight bag or with their logbook.

### Confidentiality

NASA strips identifying information from the report before any data leaves NASA. The de-identified report goes into the public ASRS database for safety research; the original report (with identifying details) is destroyed by NASA after a defined retention period.

The FAA has no access to the un-redacted report. If the FAA later asks "did this pilot file an ASRS report on this event?" -- NASA will not confirm. The pilot, however, retains a copy of their confirmation receipt and can present it to the FAA during an enforcement action to invoke the waiver.

This is the protection mechanic: the pilot proves the filing using their own copy of the confirmation; the agency, recognizing the filing, applies the waiver if the conditions are met.

## ASAP -- Aviation Safety Action Program

ASAP is the airline / air carrier analog. It runs under FAA AC 120-66 and applies to operators conducting flights under Parts 121, 135, and certain other commercial operations.

The structure is different from ASRS:

- ASAP is *operator-sponsored*. The airline / 135 operator establishes a program with the FAA and a labor union.
- Reports go to a joint Event Review Committee (ERC) staffed by management, labor, and the FAA.
- The ERC determines whether the event qualifies for ASAP protection (closed without enforcement) or whether it's outside the program (referred to enforcement).
- Reports must be filed within a defined window (typically 24 hours for operational events).

ASAP protection is broader than ASRS in one direction (it can cover multiple events per pilot per year if the program rules allow) and narrower in another (it is conditional on the operator's program agreement remaining in good standing with the FAA).

For a private GA pilot, ASAP is not relevant. For commercial pilots flying for a 121 or 135 operator, ASAP is typically the *primary* self-disclosure tool, with ASRS as a backup for events the ERC kicks out of ASAP. Pilots in this regime file *both* an ASAP report through their operator's program and an ASRS report through NASA, because the protections are independent and the windows are different (ASAP's 24 hours is tighter than ASRS's 10 days).

The 24-hour wall-clock rule for ASAP is procedurally critical. Pilots in 121/135 operations who don't file ASAP within 24 hours of an event lose ASAP eligibility, period. Their backup is ASRS (10 days) but ASAP is the higher-protection program when available.

## Self-disclosure beyond the formal programs

There are other self-disclosure paths the pilot can use:

- **Voluntary report to FAA inspector at ramp check.** The "I'd like to bring something to your attention" approach. Limited protection but can support a Compliance-Program disposition.
- **Internal report to a flight school or club's safety officer.** No regulatory weight directly; can support a culture of learning that improves the operator's ASAP-equivalent posture.
- **Report at next flight review.** A pilot mentioning a near-miss to their CFI during the flight review prompts the CFI to incorporate it into training. No regulatory protection, but useful for the proficiency arc.

None of these substitute for ASRS in the inadvertent-violation case. ASRS is the protection of record because it is published in the CFR (§91.25) and codified in AC 00-46.

## Where it sits

The ASRS protection is structured across three documents:

```text
14 CFR §91.25       Prohibition against use of ASRS reports in enforcement
AC 00-46F           The waiver of certificate action policy
49 USC 40123        Statutory protection for voluntarily provided safety
                     information (background statute)
```

The CFR section (§91.25) is the regulatory anchor; the AC (00-46F) is the operational implementation; the statute is the underlying authority. A pilot's counsel cites all three when invoking the waiver.

ASAP runs under AC 120-66 (current revision varies) and operator-specific Memoranda of Understanding (MOUs) between the FAA, the operator, and the union. The MOU is the binding document for any specific operator's program; the AC is the framework.

## Common misreadings

- **"Filing an ASRS report makes me immune from FAA enforcement."** Wrong. The waiver is conditional on the four eligibility tests and the timing rule. It also does not prevent the FAA from pursuing enforcement; it prevents *certificate action* (and civil penalty) for the violation. The investigation can still occur; the disposition is what's bounded.
- **"ASRS works for accidents."** Wrong. The accident exclusion (49 CFR 830.2) is hard. Accident-level events do not get ASRS waiver protection. File the report anyway for posture, but rely on counsel and the Compliance Program for disposition.
- **"I have 30 days to file ASRS."** Wrong. 10 calendar days from the event. The 30-day rule is the §61.15 alcohol/drug *conviction* reporting window, which is unrelated.
- **"ASRS filings are anonymous."** Mostly. NASA strips identifying information from the version that goes public, but the original retains identity. NASA does not share identity with the FAA, but the pilot's own copy of the confirmation does identify them; the pilot uses it to invoke the waiver.
- **"ASRS protects against criminal prosecution."** Wrong. ASRS specifically excludes criminal offenses. A pilot who files an ASRS report for an event involving §91.17 violations or §67.403 fraud is not protected from criminal referral; they may even be supplying additional evidence.
- **"The 5-year clean-record requirement starts from when I last filed an ASRS report."** Wrong. It starts from the date of the prior *finding* of violation. Filing ASRS reports does not consume the 5-year window; only being found to have committed a violation does.
- **"ASAP and ASRS are the same."** Wrong. Different programs, different scopes, different filing windows. Commercial pilots flying for 121/135 operators with active ASAP file *both* (ASAP for the program-specific protection, ASRS as backup). Private pilots only have ASRS.
- **"If the FAA didn't notice, ASRS doesn't matter."** Wrong logic. The pilot doesn't always know whether the FAA noticed. The 10-day filing window closes regardless of agency awareness. The right operational rule: file when you suspect, because the cost is 20 minutes and the protection is real if the agency does end up looking.
- **"The waiver is automatic if I file in time."** Mostly right with a wrinkle. The waiver applies when the four eligibility tests are met. If the agency disputes whether the event was "inadvertent and not deliberate" (for example), the waiver may not apply. Counsel argues the eligibility test if it's contested. The filing alone does not resolve the case.

### Apply

> A pilot busts an altitude assignment by 200 feet during an IFR climb. ATC says "verify maintaining 7,000" and the pilot corrects to 7,000. ATC says nothing further. The pilot is unsure whether ATC reported it. Does the pilot file ASRS?

Answer: Yes. Within 10 calendar days. Inadvertent (the bust was a misread, not deliberate), non-criminal, non-accident, no qualification gap, no recent priors. All eligibility tests met. Cost: 20 minutes. Benefit: if the FAA opens a case, the waiver applies; if not, no harm done.

> A 121 first officer notices during cruise that the captain is not pulling the correct checklist for a configuration change. They flag it on the post-flight debrief. Should they file ASAP, ASRS, or both?

Answer: Both. ASAP first (within the operator's program window, typically 24 hours) for the procedural lapse. ASRS as backup. The protections are independent. ASAP gets the higher-quality disposition; ASRS provides backup if ASAP's ERC declines the case.

> A pilot is involved in a runway incursion at a towered airport. The other aircraft was holding short. There was no contact, no near-miss, no consequence. The pilot files an ASRS report on day 8. The FAA opens an investigation on day 25. Does ASRS waiver apply?

Answer: Likely yes, if all four eligibility tests are met. Day 8 was within the 10-day filing window. The event sounds inadvertent, non-criminal, non-accident (no substantial damage, no injury), and presuming no qualification gap. The pilot's counsel presents the ASRS confirmation and argues the waiver. The FAA can investigate but generally cannot impose certificate action.

> A pilot flies a Bonanza without the §61.31(e) high-performance endorsement. They file an ASRS report on day 5. Does the waiver apply?

Answer: No. The "lack of qualification or competency" exclusion catches this. The pilot was not qualified to fly the airplane under §61.31(e); the event reveals that. ASRS does not waive certificate action when the violation is the qualification gap itself. File the report anyway for posture, but the waiver is unlikely.

> A pilot lands gear-up at night with substantial damage to the airplane. They file ASRS on day 3. Does the waiver apply?

Answer: No. Substantial damage triggers the 49 CFR 830.2 accident definition, which is one of the categorical ASRS exclusions. File the report anyway for confidentiality and as a posture signal -- the agency may still consider the filing as evidence of constructive attitude in a Compliance Program disposition -- but the formal waiver of certificate action does not apply. This is exactly the case worked in [orals/gear-up-night-ifr.md](../orals/gear-up-night-ifr.md).

> A pilot was suspended for 30 days in 2023 for a Class B incursion. In 2026 they have a similar Class B incursion. They file ASRS on day 4. Does the waiver apply?

Answer: No. The 5-year clean-record requirement is not satisfied. The 2023 finding starts a 5-year clock; the 2026 violation is well within it. The pilot files anyway for posture (the ASRS report can support a less-aggravated sanction) but the waiver of certificate action does not apply. The 2026 case is also a "repeat" exclusion under the Compliance Program (8000.373B section 3(d)), which makes legal enforcement the likely path.

> A pilot violates §91.17 (operates under the influence). They file ASRS on day 2. Does the waiver apply?

Answer: No. §91.17 violations are criminal under federal law and are categorically excluded from ASRS waiver. The pilot's exposure is not just certificate action; it includes criminal prosecution. Filing ASRS does not protect against either. We treat §91.17 in [04-career-enders.md](04-career-enders.md).

> A pilot deviates from an ATC clearance to avoid a Cb embedded in a line. They had no time to coordinate the deviation with ATC before turning. They notify ATC immediately after. Should they file ASRS?

Answer: Maybe. The deviation may have been authorized under §91.3(b) emergency authority -- if so, no violation occurred and there is nothing to report against. But emergency authority is asserted, not granted; the FAA may later question whether the deviation was actually emergency-grade. Filing ASRS as backup, within 10 days, costs nothing and provides protection if the agency disputes the §91.3(b) assertion. The right move: file ASRS, write the §91.3(b) deviation report under §91.123(d) within 48 hours if requested, and consult counsel if the FAA opens an investigation. We cover §91.123 in [06-deviations-and-remedial-training.md](06-deviations-and-remedial-training.md).
