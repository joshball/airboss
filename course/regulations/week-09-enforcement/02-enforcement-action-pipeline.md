# The enforcement action pipeline

When a case routes to legal enforcement rather than the Compliance Program, the agency follows a defined pipeline. Each stage is procedural; each stage has documents the pilot receives, deadlines that bind, and rights that attach. The pipeline is not arbitrary; it is the implementation of the FAA's enforcement authority under 49 USC and the procedural protections Congress put in place via the Pilot's Bill of Rights (2012) and Pilot's Bill of Rights 2 (2016).

This lesson walks the pipeline stage by stage. The pilot who has rehearsed it once, in calm, knows what each piece of paper is, what it asks for, what it requires, and what it does not require.

## What you'll be able to do

- Name each stage of the FAA enforcement pipeline in order, with the document the agency issues and the response the pilot owes
- State the Pilot's Bill of Rights protections that attach at each stage
- Identify the statute of limitations for FAA enforcement and the deadline-based traps inside the pipeline
- Articulate when to retain counsel and what counsel does at each stage
- Recognize which stages are appealable to the NTSB and which are not
- Predict the typical timeline from event to disposition

## Why this matters

The pipeline runs on deadlines. Missing a deadline at any stage forfeits the protection that stage offered. A pilot who lets the LOI response window expire without engaging has signaled unwillingness, removed any Compliance Program possibility, and weakened their substantive position at the NOPCA stage. A pilot who lets the NTSB appeal window expire (typically 20 days) has waived the right to appeal the agency's certificate action, period. The certificate suspension stands.

Pilots who lose certificates over otherwise-defensible incidents almost always lose them at procedure: a missed response, a casual phone call to the FSDO that supplied evidence the agency didn't have, a written response submitted without legal review that conceded points the agency would not have proven on its own.

The framework is rehearsable. The response is procedural. Cold knowledge of the pipeline is what converts panic into structure.

## The discovery question

A pilot receives a Letter of Investigation in the mail. It arrives on a Tuesday. It is dated the previous Friday. It asks for a written response within 10 calendar days. It identifies a specific date and flight and names the rule the agency believes was violated. It includes the statement "any response you provide may be used in a subsequent enforcement action."

Pause. What does the pilot do today?

- They do not respond today. They do not call the FSDO today. They do not write back today.
- They do retain a defense counsel today (an aviation attorney; many pilots use AOPA legal services if they're members or a private aviation lawyer).
- They request the relevant ATC tapes and FAA records under Pilot's Bill of Rights 2 today.
- They review the LOI carefully, identifying the specific factual claim the agency is making and the specific rule the agency cites.
- They calendar the response deadline, work backward, and plan the written response with counsel.
- They file a NASA ASRS report if not yet filed and within the 10-day window from the date of the event (we cover this in [03-asrs-asap-and-self-disclosure.md](03-asrs-asap-and-self-disclosure.md)).

The 10-day response window is real, but it is also a *requested* window. The agency will typically grant a reasonable extension on request, particularly for counsel coordination. The pilot's counsel asks for an extension if needed; the pilot does not just let the deadline pass.

```text
  - Often issued for minor violations or as Compliance Action outcome
  |
  | (or, more serious case routes to:)
  |
  v

  - Notifies pilot they are subject of investigation

  |

  - Formal proposal of suspension/revocation
  - States proposed sanction (e.g., 30-day suspension)
  - Pilot has options: accept, request informal conference, appeal to NTSB
  |
  | (if pilot appeals or rejects):
  |
  v
ORDER OF CERTIFICATE ACTION (suspension/revocation order)
  - Formal sanction issued
  - Pilot has 20 days to appeal to NTSB
  |
  | (if pilot appeals):
  |
  v

  - Administrative Law Judge hears the case
  - FAA must prove its case by preponderance of evidence
  - ALJ issues decision; either party can appeal
  |
  | (if appealed):
  |
  v
NTSB BOARD APPEAL
  - Five-member NTSB Board reviews ALJ decision
  - Board issues final decision
  |
  | (if pilot loses and exhausts admin remedies):
  |
  v

  - Pilot can petition for review in U.S. Court of Appeals
  - Court reviews for legal error or arbitrary action
  |

```

That is the full pipeline. Most cases settle or close at the LOI stage or via Compliance Action. The cases that go to NOPCA usually settle before formal certificate action issues. The cases that reach NTSB hearings are the small minority where the pilot and the agency genuinely disagree on facts or law.

## Stage 1 -- Warning notice or Letter of Correction

The lowest tier of administrative response. The agency documents the violation, places the documentation in the pilot's file, and takes no certificate action. Used for minor infractions where even a Compliance Action's training requirement is overkill.

A pilot receiving a Warning Notice has no required response and no appeal. The notice is informational. Future violations will reference it.

This stage is rare. Most cases either resolve internally without issuing anything to the pilot (informal closure) or route directly to LOI / Compliance Action.

## Stage 2 -- Letter of Investigation (LOI)

The first formal document in most enforcement timelines. The LOI does several things at once:

1. Notifies the pilot they are the subject of an FAA investigation
2. Identifies the specific event (date, flight, location)
3. States the rule the agency believes was violated
4. Requests a written response within a specified window (typically 10 calendar days)
5. Includes the PBR2-required notification that statements may be used in enforcement action

### Pilot's Bill of Rights 2 protections at LOI

The LOI is the trigger for explicit PBR2 obligations on the agency:

```text
49 USC 44703 note (Pilot's Bill of Rights 2)  paraphrased

The FAA shall, upon notification to a person that the person is the
subject of an investigation:

  (1) Provide timely written notification of the investigation;

  (2) State the specific Federal Aviation Regulation alleged to have
      been violated;

  (3) Inform the person that any response may be used in a
      subsequent enforcement action;

  (4) Inform the person of the right to receive air traffic control
      data, weather data, and FAA flight information system data
      relevant to the investigation;

  (5) Inform the person of the right to legal counsel.

Note: paraphrase of statutory protections. Read the actual statute
before referencing in exam.
```

The agency's LOI must contain these notifications. If it doesn't, that's a procedural defect the pilot's counsel will note.

### What the pilot does at the LOI stage

The right sequence:

1. **Calendar the response deadline.** Note the date the LOI was issued, the date received, and the response deadline. If the deadline is unrealistic, plan to request an extension.
2. **Retain counsel.** AOPA Legal Services Plan members can call AOPA legal; non-members retain private aviation defense counsel. Some pilots get away without counsel for clearly Compliance-Program-eligible cases; for any case where certificate action is plausible, counsel is the right call.
3. **Request the FAA records.** Under PBR2, request ATC tapes, weather data, and FAA flight data relevant to the investigation. Counsel writes the request; the agency must respond.
4. **File a NASA ASRS report** if within the 10-day filing window from the event (covered in [03-asrs-asap-and-self-disclosure.md](03-asrs-asap-and-self-disclosure.md)). This may or may not provide waiver protection depending on the violation type, but the report has confidentiality value either way.
5. **Draft the written response with counsel.** The response acknowledges receipt, states the pilot's position on the alleged violation (with care), and indicates willingness to engage in remedial training if appropriate.
6. **Submit the response on time.** Or request an extension before the deadline expires.

What the pilot does *not* do:

- Call the FSDO to "explain"
- Volunteer information not requested
- Concede facts the agency has not yet proven
- Argue with contested facts on the phone (do that in writing, with counsel, with documentation)
- Ignore the LOI and hope it goes away

### What "right to silence" means here

The PBR2 right to be informed that statements may be used in enforcement action is the agency-side acknowledgment that the pilot can choose what to say. The pilot does not have a Fifth-Amendment-style right to remain silent in the criminal sense (this is administrative, not criminal), but the pilot is not obligated to volunteer narrative or supply evidence that the agency does not already have.

The right posture: respond substantively to specific factual questions the agency is entitled to ask (date, flight, what the pilot was doing) while declining to speculate, narrate broadly, or characterize intent. Counsel shapes the line.

## Stage 3 -- Enforcement decision point

After the FSDO has the pilot's LOI response and reviewed it alongside ATC and FAA evidence, the case routes one of three ways:

| Disposition       | Outcome                                                                          |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Closed            | No further action. Pilot's file noted that an investigation occurred and closed. |
| Compliance Action | Counseling, training, written commitment. No certificate action.                 |
| Legal enforcement | Case routes to FAA Regional Counsel for NOPCA preparation.                       |

Most cases close or route to Compliance Action. The cases that route to legal enforcement are the ones with categorical exclusions (reckless, intentional, criminal, repeat, lack of qualification) or the cases where the pilot's posture made administrative resolution untenable.

## Stage 4 -- Notice of Proposed Certificate Action (NOPCA)

The first document with teeth. The NOPCA:

1. States the specific rule violated (often §91.13 catch-all alongside the operative section)
2. States the proposed sanction (typically a suspension period in days, or revocation)
3. Identifies the FAA Regional Counsel attorney handling the case
4. Offers the pilot three options:
   - Accept the proposed sanction
   - Request an informal conference with the Regional Counsel
   - Appeal to the NTSB (waive the informal conference and go directly)

### The informal conference

The informal conference is exactly what it sounds like: a meeting (often by phone or video) between the pilot, their counsel, and the FAA Regional Counsel attorney. The purpose is to discuss the case, present the pilot's evidence and position, and potentially negotiate a settlement. Many cases settle at the informal conference for a reduced sanction or a Compliance Action outcome.

The informal conference is not a hearing. There is no judge, no rules of evidence, no formal record. It is a negotiation. The pilot's counsel does the talking; the pilot is present but generally listens.

### Sanction guidelines

The FAA's typical sanctions for common violations are published in FAA Order 2150.3 sanction guidelines tables. Examples (illustrative, not authoritative):

| Violation                                              | Typical sanction range          |
| ------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Inadvertent airspace incursion (single, no aggravators) | Counseling to 30-day suspension |
| Operating without required medical                     | 30-90 day suspension            |
| Falsified logbook entry                                | Revocation                      |
| Operating under influence (91.17)                      | Revocation, criminal referral   |
| Repeat violations after prior Compliance Action        | Doubled or revocation           |

Counsel uses these tables to assess the agency's leverage and negotiate. A pilot offered a 60-day suspension when the table suggests 30 has room to push back; a pilot offered a 30 when the table suggests 90 should probably take it.

## Stage 5 -- Order of Certificate Action

If the pilot does not accept the NOPCA and does not settle at informal conference, the agency issues the formal Order of Certificate Action: the actual suspension or revocation. The pilot has **20 days** from receipt to appeal to the NTSB.

Twenty days is a hard deadline. Missing it forfeits appeal rights and the order takes effect. Counsel calendars this aggressively.

## Stage 6 -- NTSB Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) hearing

If the pilot appeals, the NTSB conducts a formal hearing before an Administrative Law Judge. This is an actual evidentiary hearing:

- FAA Counsel presents the agency's case
- Pilot's counsel presents the defense
- Both sides examine and cross-examine witnesses
- ATC controllers, FAA inspectors, and the pilot may all testify
- Rules of evidence apply (relaxed but present)
- The ALJ rules from the bench or in a written decision

The FAA bears the burden of proof: preponderance of the evidence (more likely than not). The pilot does not have to prove innocence; the agency has to prove the violation. This is materially different from the Compliance / Enforcement decision earlier in the pipeline, where the pilot is somewhat in defensive posture even on contested facts.

ALJ decisions are appealable to the NTSB Board.

## Stage 7 -- NTSB Board appeal

The five-member NTSB Board reviews the ALJ's decision on appeal. The Board can affirm, modify, or reverse the ALJ. The Board can also remand for further fact-finding.

NTSB Board decisions are the final administrative ruling. The pilot's only further appeal is to a U.S. Court of Appeals.

## Stage 8 -- Federal Circuit Court appeal

The pilot may petition for review in the appropriate U.S. Court of Appeals. The court reviews for:

- Substantial-evidence error (was there sufficient evidence to support the finding?)
- Legal error (did the agency or NTSB apply the wrong rule?)
- Arbitrary or capricious agency action

This is a deferential review. Most pilot petitions to circuit courts fail because courts give administrative agencies wide latitude. But the path exists, and appellate decisions occasionally reset important questions of regulatory interpretation.

## Statute of limitations

The FAA must initiate enforcement action within **6 years** of the alleged violation (28 USC 2462, the general federal statute of limitations for civil penalties). For accidents, the clock can extend; for fraud (§67.403), the clock starts when the agency discovers the fraud rather than when it occurred.

In practice, FAA enforcement action almost always begins within months of the event. The 6-year limit is a backstop, not a typical timeline.

## Where it sits

The pipeline operates under multiple authorities:

- **49 USC 44701** et seq. -- the FAA's enforcement authority (statutory)
- **49 USC 44703 note** -- Pilot's Bill of Rights / Pilot's Bill of Rights 2
- **14 CFR Part 13** -- the FAA's enforcement procedures (regulatory)
- **49 CFR Part 821** -- NTSB rules of practice (the appeal hearings)
- **FAA Order 2150.3** -- internal operational guidance (administrative)
- **FAA Order 8000.373** -- Compliance Program policy (administrative)

The pilot does not need to memorize these citations. The pilot's counsel does.

## Common misreadings

- **"The LOI means I'm losing my certificate."** Wrong. Most LOIs close without certificate action. The LOI is the *start* of the investigation, not the end. A measured, posture-aware response improves the probability of administrative closure.
- **"I have to respond to the LOI within 10 days exactly."** Mostly. The 10-day window is requested by the agency, but extensions are routinely granted on request, particularly when counsel is involved. Letting the deadline pass without engaging or requesting an extension is what causes problems.
- **"PBR2 means I can refuse to talk to the FAA."** Partial. PBR2 establishes notification and records-access rights. It does not give the pilot Fifth-Amendment-style silence. The pilot can decline to volunteer narrative or speculate, but they cannot ignore an LOI without consequence.
- **"NTSB appeals are easy to win."** Wrong. The agency wins most NTSB appeals, in part because the agency is careful which cases it advances and in part because the deferential standard of review favors the agency. NTSB appeals are absolutely worth pursuing in genuine factual disputes; they are not a "free shot" at reversing the agency.
- **"If I miss the 20-day NTSB appeal window, I can ask for an extension."** Wrong. The 20-day window is statutory, not administrative. Missing it forfeits the appeal. Counsel calendars this on day one of receiving the Order.
- **"I can call the FAA Regional Counsel attorney directly to negotiate."** Wrong; counsel does that. The pilot represents themselves only at significant risk. Once a NOPCA issues, the case is in formal legal posture and the pilot's lawyer talks to the agency's lawyer; the pilot does not.
- **"The FAA's enforcement is criminal."** Wrong. FAA enforcement is administrative (certificate action, civil penalties). Criminal prosecution is a separate path, pursued by the U.S. Attorney's Office, typically only for §67.403 fraud, §91.17 alcohol/drug operations resulting in death, or willful and reckless conduct. Most enforcement is administrative; the pilot's exposure is the certificate, not the prison.
- **"A 30-day suspension just means I don't fly for 30 days."** Mostly right, with a wrinkle. The suspension period is a regulatory event in your record. Insurance underwriters notice. Future employers notice. Future enforcement evaluates against priors. The 30 days off is the small part of the cost.

### Apply

> A pilot receives an LOI on April 1, 2026, with a 10-day response window. They retain counsel on April 3. Counsel needs more time to obtain records and prepare a response. What does counsel do?

Answer: Counsel writes the FSDO requesting an extension of the response window, citing the need for records review under PBR2 and counsel-coordination time. Extensions of 30 days or more are routinely granted on request. The request goes in writing before the original deadline expires.

> A pilot accepts a 30-day suspension at the NOPCA stage rather than appealing. Six months later, they are cited for a similar violation. The agency now seeks revocation. Why?

Answer: The "repetitive" exclusion under 8000.373B and the sanction-doubling pattern under FAA Order 2150.3 sanction guidelines. A pilot with a recent administrative disposition who repeats the same conduct is no longer Compliance-Program-eligible and faces an aggravated sanction. The first incident's quick acceptance was probably the right call; the second incident is now in serious legal posture.

> A pilot loses at the NTSB ALJ. They have how many days to appeal to the NTSB Board?

Answer: Per 49 CFR Part 821, the appeal window from an ALJ decision to the Board is typically 10 days for a notice of appeal, with 50 days for the appeal brief (subject to the Board's specific procedural rules at the time -- counsel verifies the current deadline). Failing to appeal within the window makes the ALJ's decision the final administrative ruling.

> A pilot is offered an informal conference with the Regional Counsel attorney. The pilot's counsel believes the agency's evidence is weak. What's the strategy?

Answer: Counsel typically goes to the informal conference. Three reasons: (1) the agency may settle for a reduced sanction or even close the case after seeing the defense's posture, (2) the pilot's evidence and arguments get presented in a non-binding setting where the agency can update its position without losing face, and (3) skipping the conference and going directly to NTSB appeal can be read as adversarial posture, which won't help if the case does end up before an ALJ. The conference is "free" in the sense that nothing said is binding and the pilot retains all appeal rights regardless.

> A pilot receives a Letter of Correction. They want to appeal it. What's the process?

Answer: There is no appeal. A Letter of Correction is administrative, not certificate action. It documents the violation, places the documentation in the file, and is non-appealable. The pilot's only recourse is to ensure no further violations occur (the LoC will aggravate any future case) and possibly to write a rebuttal letter for the file (which the agency can choose to attach but is not required to consider).

> A pilot gets an LOI for a §91.13 careless-or-reckless allegation tied to a near-miss event. The other aircraft involved was a corporate jet. The corporate jet's pilots have already filed a NASA ASRS report. Should our pilot also file?

Answer: Yes, if within the 10-day filing window from the event (we cover ASRS in [03-asrs-asap-and-self-disclosure.md](03-asrs-asap-and-self-disclosure.md)). The other pilots' ASRS reports do not protect our pilot. ASRS waiver eligibility is per-pilot. Whether ours qualifies for the waiver depends on whether the event was an accident (§830.2), criminal, or showed lack of qualification. For a near-miss without injury or substantial damage, the waiver typically applies if the other criteria are met.
