# Week 9 oral -- "The FAA is calling. Walk me through your defense."

## The question

> "You're a 600-hour private pilot. Two weeks ago you flew a flight where you may have busted a Class B shelf -- maybe by 200 feet, maybe by more, you weren't sure at the time. Today you got a Letter of Investigation in the mail. The FSDO inspector wants to talk. Walk me through what you do, in order, from the moment you open the envelope until the case is closed. I want to hear how you keep your certificate."

This is the certificate-action defense oral. It is *not* the post-incident reporting oral -- that's the [gear-up-night-ifr](../orals/gear-up-night-ifr.md) capstone. This oral picks up at the moment a developing case lands on the pilot's desk and walks the response.

## What this is testing

- Whether you know the enforcement pipeline cold and what each document means
- Whether you keep Compliance Program eligibility intact through your posture
- Whether you exercise PBR2 protections deliberately rather than panicking
- Whether you know what to say, what not to say, and when to invoke counsel
- Whether you understand what ASRS does and does not protect against
- Whether you can describe a Compliance Action / remedial-training outcome and how to position for it
- Whether you keep calm. The pilot who can rehearse this cold can also execute it under stress.

## Model answer (full walkthrough)

A defensible answer takes about 18-20 minutes spoken. Twelve stages, in order.

### Stage 1: Open the envelope, do not respond yet

> "First thing I do not do is respond. I do not call the FSDO. I do not write back. I do not text my flight school CFI to ask. The LOI is not a deadline that requires action *today*; it's a deadline that requires *action within the window* -- typically 10 days. My first move is to read the letter carefully, identify what it says, and *not* commit myself to any narrative."

Demonstrates the discipline. The pilot who responds in the first hour, while panicked, is the pilot who supplies evidence the agency didn't have.

### Stage 2: Identify what the LOI tells me

> "The LOI should contain -- per Pilot's Bill of Rights 2 -- specific information: it identifies me as the subject of an investigation, names the date and flight, states the rule the FAA believes I violated, informs me that any response may be used in enforcement, and informs me of my right to obtain ATC tapes and FAA records and my right to legal counsel. I read all of those. If any of those are missing, my counsel will note the procedural defect. I take careful notes on what the agency is alleging vs. what facts they're claiming."

Demonstrates knowledge of PBR2 and the structure of the document.

### Stage 3: Calendar the response deadline

> "I open my calendar. I note the date the LOI was issued (on the document), the date I received it, and the response deadline. I also calendar a date 7 days before the response deadline so my counsel and I have time to prepare. If the deadline is unreasonable -- say, 10 days when I need to obtain records that won't arrive in time -- I plan to request an extension *before* the deadline expires."

Concrete and procedural.

### Stage 4: Retain counsel

> "Today, I retain aviation defense counsel. AOPA Legal Services Plan if I'm a member. If not, a private aviation attorney -- there are several reputable firms that specialize. I do not represent myself. The agency's investigators and lawyers are professionals; mine should be too. The cost of counsel for a single LOI response is in the low four figures; the cost of mishandling a Class B incursion case is potentially a 30 to 60-day suspension on my record forever, plus insurance-rate consequences, plus future-employment consequences. The math is clear."

Names the cost and the rationale. The agency rewards the pilot who shows up represented; it does not punish them.

### Stage 5: File NASA ASRS if within window

> "If I'm within the 10-day window from the original event, I file a NASA ASRS report immediately. This event is two weeks old, so the window has closed -- I can't get the waiver. If I had filed within 10 days, I'd have potentially had certificate-action waiver protection under §91.25 / AC 00-46, assuming the violation was inadvertent, non-criminal, non-accident, no qualification gap, and no prior finding within 5 years. But the window's closed, so I move on. Lesson for next time: file ASRS the day of the event."

Honest about the missed protection (in pedagogically apt prose) and identifies the rule for future events.

### Stage 6: Request records under PBR2

> "Counsel writes the FSDO and requests, under Pilot's Bill of Rights 2, all ATC tapes, weather data, radar data, ADS-B data, and any other FAA documentation relevant to the investigation. The agency must respond. We don't draft a substantive response until we know what evidence the agency has. The pilot who responds without seeing the agency's evidence is responding to an imagined case, which often differs from the actual case."

Procedural and tactical.

### Stage 7: Review evidence and assess

> "Counsel and I review what the agency provides. Maybe the radar data shows I was actually 50 feet below the shelf the whole time -- in which case my response is straightforward. Maybe the data shows I was 300 feet above the shelf for 90 seconds -- in which case I have a clear deviation to address. Maybe the data is ambiguous and I have a defense on the question of whether I actually busted. The evidence drives the response. Until we see it, we don't know what we're responding to."

Demonstrates that the response is evidence-driven, not narrative-driven.

### Stage 8: Compliance Program assessment

> "Counsel and I assess Compliance Program eligibility. The bust was inadvertent. Not criminal. Not an accident. I don't have prior FAA findings. My flight review and medical were current. My posture is willing-to-engage. All of that points to Compliance Program eligibility under FAA Order 8000.373B. The exclusion categories -- reckless, intentional, criminal, repeat, lack of qualification -- don't apply. So I'm probably looking at a Compliance Action outcome rather than legal enforcement, *if* I respond well. The posture I take in the response either preserves that eligibility or shifts the case toward legal enforcement."

Names the relevant order and the four exclusion criteria.

### Stage 9: Draft the written response

> "Counsel drafts the written response. The structure: acknowledge receipt of the LOI, acknowledge that an event occurred (without conceding more than the evidence shows), state my account of what happened, identify what I now understand differently, and propose a remedy. The proposed remedy is the lever for the Compliance Program disposition.
>
> Specifically I propose remedial training in lieu of certificate action: 'I propose to complete 8 hours of dual instruction with a designated CFI focused on Class B procedures, with an emphasis on shelf identification, sectional currency, and pre-flight planning. I have already identified CFI Smith at the local flight school, who has agreed to provide the training. I will complete the training within 60 days and file the CFI's logbook endorsement with the FSDO upon completion.'
>
> That proposal does several things: it acknowledges the seriousness of the event, demonstrates I've thought about what went wrong and how to fix it, takes initiative on the remedy rather than waiting for the agency to impose one, and gives the FSDO an off-ramp from certificate action that costs them nothing. The agency has discretion to accept; they often do for cases like this."

Concrete remedy proposal. The agency loves a pilot who proposes the right outcome before the agency has to decide.

### Stage 10: Submit on time

> "We submit the response within the window. If we need an extension, we request it in writing before the deadline. The agency typically grants reasonable extension requests. We do not let the deadline pass without engaging."

Hard procedural rule.

### Stage 11: What I do *not* do

> "Just to make this concrete -- here's what I do not do at any stage:
>
> One, I do not call the FSDO inspector to 'just explain.' Whatever I say goes in the record under the PBR2 notice that statements may be used in enforcement. Casual phone narratives turn into agency exhibits.
>
> Two, I do not deny the event happened. The agency has data. Denial reads as either dishonesty (which removes Compliance Program eligibility under the 'willing' posture test) or detachment from facts.
>
> Three, I do not volunteer information beyond what the agency requested. The LOI is the agency's chance to ask specific questions; my response addresses those questions. I do not narrate adjacent events, prior similar incidents, or my emotional state.
>
> Four, I do not file ASRS now expecting it to provide retroactive immunity. The 10-day window from the event closed. ASRS doesn't apply.
>
> Five, I do not represent myself unless the case is genuinely trivial. This case isn't.
>
> Six, I do not panic. The procedural framework exists exactly so this is rehearsable rather than improvised."

The negative space. What the disciplined pilot does not do.

### Stage 12: Possible outcomes and how I respond to each

> "After the response, several outcomes are possible.
>
> *Best case*: agency closes the case at Compliance Action with the proposed remedial training. I complete the training, file the endorsement, the case closes. No certificate action. A counseling letter goes in my file but no suspension. Insurance and future employers see a documented training event, not a suspension.
>
> *Likely case*: agency proposes a Letter of Correction or counseling letter without remedial training, or accepts the remedial training but doubles the hours. Counsel evaluates and we accept or counter.
>
> *Less common*: agency proceeds toward NOPCA. Counsel requests an informal conference with Regional Counsel, presents the case for Compliance Action, negotiates. Most NOPCAs settle at the informal conference.
>
> *Worst case*: agency issues NOPCA proposing certificate suspension, no settlement at informal conference. Counsel evaluates: do we have a defensible position for NTSB appeal? If yes, we appeal within 20 days of the formal Order. If no, we accept the suspension and move on. The 20-day window is hard; counsel calendars it on day one.
>
> Through all of these, I do not contact the FAA Regional Counsel attorney directly. Counsel does. I do not testify or give recorded statements without counsel present. I do not discuss the case publicly or on social media -- the agency reads social media in the digital age."

Names the realistic outcome distribution. The pilot who walks in expecting "best case" and the pilot who walks in expecting "worst case" are both miscalibrated; the realistic distribution is the working pilot's frame.

## Compressed answer (under 3 minutes)

For an examiner who wants the integration without the deep dive:

> "**One**: do not respond today. Calendar the deadline. **Two**: retain aviation counsel. **Three**: file ASRS if within 10 days of the event (this case, the window's closed). **Four**: request ATC tapes and FAA records under PBR2. **Five**: review the evidence with counsel; assess Compliance Program eligibility under FAA Order 8000.373B. **Six**: draft a written response that acknowledges the event without overconceding, proposes remedial training in lieu of certificate action with a specific CFI and hours, and addresses what I'll do differently. **Seven**: submit on time or request a written extension before the deadline. **Eight**: through all of this, do not call the FSDO inspector to 'just explain,' do not deny what the data shows, do not volunteer beyond the agency's questions, do not represent myself.
>
> Most cases like this -- inadvertent, no priors, willing pilot, no consequences -- close at Compliance Action with remedial training. The Compliance Program is the FAA's preference for this archetype. The pilot's posture is what preserves eligibility. PBR2 protections travel with me throughout. The 20-day NTSB appeal window from any formal Order is hard.
>
> What goes wrong: panicking, calling without counsel, supplying evidence the agency didn't have, missing the response deadline, or refusing to engage entirely. Each of those shifts the case from Compliance Action toward legal enforcement."

That answer cites the relevant FAA Order, the PBR2 framework, the §91.25 / AC 00-46 ASRS structure, the Compliance Program exclusion categories, the NOPCA pipeline, the 20-day NTSB appeal, and the remedial-training outcome. Working-level expectation.

## What goes wrong (failure modes)

| Failure mode                                                              | Diagnosis |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------- |
| Calls the FSDO immediately to "explain"                                    | Self-incrimination. Bypasses PBR2 protections. Supplies the agency evidence it didn't have. Removes Compliance Program eligibility through the "willing posture without preparation" trap. |
| Denies the event happened despite agency data                              | Reads as dishonesty. Triggers the Compliance Program exclusion for "unwilling." Pushes the case toward legal enforcement. |
| Files ASRS expecting retroactive immunity 14 days after event              | Misreads ASRS. The 10-day window is binary; outside it the waiver doesn't apply. ASRS is not a defense; it's a pre-filing protection. |
| Ignores the LOI hoping it goes away                                        | Removes Compliance Program eligibility entirely (unwilling). Forfeits any possibility of administrative resolution. The agency proceeds to NOPCA on the agency's evidence with no pilot input. |
| Represents themselves at all stages                                        | Misses procedural protections, mishandles the Regional Counsel negotiation, fails to use sanction-guideline tables effectively. The cost saved on counsel is dwarfed by the suspension that could have been avoided. |
| Misses the 20-day NTSB appeal window                                       | Forfeits all appeal rights. The Order takes effect. No do-overs. |
| Files NASA ASRS but doesn't keep the confirmation                          | Cannot prove timely filing. The waiver only applies if the pilot can prove the report was filed; the confirmation is the pilot's evidence. |
| Treats the LOI as if certificate action is automatic                       | Most LOIs close at Compliance Action. Treating it as automatic suspension causes panic responses that make the suspension self-fulfilling. |
| Does not propose remedial training in the response                          | The agency may impose a sanction it would not have imposed if remedial training had been on the table. The pilot's proposal is the lever for the Compliance Action outcome; not proposing leaves the lever unused. |
| Discusses the case on social media or pilot forums                          | The agency monitors. Statements made publicly become agency exhibits. The "venting" supplies evidence and damages posture. |
| Concedes intent or recklessness in the written response                    | Removes Compliance Program eligibility. Reckless / intentional are categorical exclusions. Counsel-drafted responses contest characterization while acknowledging facts. |
| Forgets the §61.15(c) 60-day reporting requirement after a non-aviation arrest | Independent violation on top of the underlying event. Failure to file is itself enforcement-actionable. |

## Variant prompts

| Variant                                                                               | What changes |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------ |
| "Same scenario, but the deviation was a Class B incursion that resulted in ATC vectoring traffic for separation" | Aggravating factor: actual safety consequence. Compliance Program still likely (no accident, inadvertent, no priors), but sanction guidelines push higher. Negotiate harder for remedial training. |
| "Same scenario, but you have a 30-day suspension on your record from 18 months ago"      | Repeat exclusion under 8000.373B section 3(d). Compliance Program likely unavailable. Counsel's job shifts from Compliance Action negotiation to NOPCA defense. Sanction guidelines doubled. |
| "Same scenario, but during the LOI response window you remember that your medical actually expired 3 weeks before the event" | Adds a §61.23 lack-of-qualification problem on top of the original violation. Compliance Program excluded under qualification gap. Counsel calls; this case is now serious. |
| "Same scenario, but the FSDO inspector calls you and you take the call"                  | The pilot has now talked. Whatever they said is on record. Counsel evaluates damage; if no statements were prejudicial, the case continues. If prejudicial statements were made, counsel manages that as part of the response. |
| "Same scenario, but the event involved a TFR violation, not a Class B incursion"          | TFR violations are heavily enforced and often involve security-related concerns. Compliance Program possible for first-time inadvertent but the agency's posture is stricter. Counsel essential; remedial training proposal still appropriate. |
| "Same scenario, but you also smelled alcohol on a passenger when they boarded -- you flew anyway" | Adds §91.17(b) (carrying a person who appears intoxicated) potential. Possibly excluded from Compliance Program if the pilot's awareness rises to "reckless." Counsel-driven analysis. |
| "Same scenario, but during the response, you realize you forgot to file an ASRS report on a separate altitude bust 6 months ago" | The other event is not in scope of this LOI. The pilot doesn't volunteer it in this response. Their counsel may advise filing ASRS now (outside the 10-day window so no waiver) for posture purposes only. |
