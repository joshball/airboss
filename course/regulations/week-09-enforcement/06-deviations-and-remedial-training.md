# Deviations and remedial training

Two themes close out the week. First: when ATC issues an instruction the pilot cannot safely comply with, what does the regulation actually require? §91.123 governs ATC compliance, but the section is shorter and more pilot-protective than most pilots realize. The "unable" call is in the regulation, and §91.3(b) provides emergency authority that overrides ATC instructions when needed.

Second: when a pilot does deviate (with or without authority), the FAA may receive a "pilot deviation" report from ATC. The agency's response is calibrated, and remedial training in lieu of certificate action is the most common positive outcome for inadvertent deviations. The pilot who knows how to ask for it, when to ask, and what the program looks like has a meaningful tool for steering a developing case toward an outcome that preserves the certificate.

These are the two pieces a working pilot encounters most often after an event: how to refuse a clearance correctly *during* the flight, and how to negotiate the post-event response toward training rather than punishment.

## What you'll be able to do

- Quote §91.123 and apply it to specific ATC scenarios involving "unable"
- Distinguish §91.3(b) emergency authority from §91.123 routine deviation
- Know what an ATC pilot deviation report contains and how it's transmitted to the FSDO
- Recognize when remedial training in lieu of certificate action is on the table
- Articulate how the pilot positions for the remedial-training outcome
- Apply §91.123(d) post-deviation reporting requirements

## Why this matters

§91.123 is misread on both sides. Some pilots think every ATC instruction is mandatory and any refusal triggers enforcement. Others think "unable" is a magic word that allows them to ignore any clearance they find inconvenient. Both readings are wrong. The regulation specifies *when* deviation is permitted (emergency, can't comply safely) and *what the pilot owes* afterward (notification, written report if requested).

Remedial training is the most underappreciated tool in the post-event landscape. It exists at the intersection of the Compliance Program (8000.373B) and FAA Order 2150.3 sanction guidelines. A pilot whose case is heading toward a 30-day suspension can often negotiate that into a 10-hour training program with a designated CFI, no certificate action, and a documented training record that improves their FAA standing. The pilot who doesn't know to ask gets the suspension; the pilot who knows to ask, often gets the training.

This is operational knowledge. It compounds: a pilot who has navigated one event well is in a better posture for any future event. The skills are rehearsable and worth rehearsing.

## The discovery question

Tower issues "Cessna Two-Three Tango, hold short of runway 17, traffic on 5-mile final." The Cessna is on a 1-mile final to runway 17 itself, on a normal stabilized approach, with no convenient way to hold short of 17 without abandoning the approach. The pilot can't physically hold short of the runway they're landing on.

Pause. What does the pilot say, and what does the regulation say they should do?

The right answer is: "Tower, Two-Three Tango, unable hold short of 17, on short final 17." Tower then either issues a different instruction (cleared to land, go around to runway 35, etc.) or coordinates with the conflicting traffic.

The pilot's "unable" is not a violation. §91.123(a) requires compliance with ATC instructions *except* in emergency or when the instruction cannot be safely complied with. The pilot retains command authority for safety; ATC retains separation authority. When the two clash, the pilot says "unable" and gives ATC a chance to issue a different instruction.

§91.123(b) requires the pilot to *follow* ATC instructions in controlled airspace except in emergency or when an alternate clearance is obtained. "Unable" is the pilot's invitation to ATC for an alternate clearance.

A pilot who silently abandons the approach without communicating violates §91.123(b). A pilot who silently lands when ATC has said "hold short" violates §91.123(b). The pilot who says "unable" does not. The vocabulary matters.

This is the routine case. §91.3(b) emergency authority is the heavier hammer; we cover it next.

## §91.123 -- compliance with ATC clearances and instructions

```text
§91.123  Compliance with ATC clearances and instructions.

(a) When an ATC clearance has been obtained, no pilot in command may
    deviate from that clearance unless an amended clearance is obtained,
    an emergency exists, or the deviation is in response to a traffic
    alert and collision avoidance system resolution advisory. However,
    except in Class A airspace, a pilot may cancel an IFR flight plan
    if the operation is being conducted in VFR weather conditions.
    When a pilot is uncertain of an ATC clearance, that pilot shall
    immediately request clarification from ATC.

(b) Except in an emergency, no person may operate an aircraft contrary
    to an ATC instruction in an area in which air traffic control is
    exercised.

(c) Each pilot in command who, in an emergency, or in response to a
    traffic alert and collision avoidance system resolution advisory,
    deviates from an ATC clearance or instruction shall notify ATC
    of that deviation as soon as possible.

(d) Each pilot in command who (though not deviating from a rule of this
    subpart) is given priority by ATC in an emergency, shall submit a
    detailed report of that emergency within 48 hours to the manager
    of that ATC facility, if requested by ATC.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The §91.123 structure:

- **§91.123(a)** -- The general rule for *clearances*: comply unless you have an amended clearance, an emergency, or a TCAS RA. Cancellation of IFR is permitted in VFR conditions outside Class A. Uncertain clearances must be clarified.
- **§91.123(b)** -- The general rule for *instructions* (not clearances): comply except in emergency.
- **§91.123(c)** -- The notification rule: emergency or RA deviations must be reported to ATC as soon as possible.
- **§91.123(d)** -- The 48-hour written report rule: if the pilot received priority in an emergency and ATC requests a written report, it goes to the ATC facility manager within 48 hours.

### "Clearance" vs. "instruction"

The regulation uses both words, and the distinction matters slightly:

- **Clearance** = an ATC authorization to proceed (e.g., "cleared to land," "cleared direct DUMBA," "cleared for the ILS 28R approach"). §91.123(a) governs.
- **Instruction** = an ATC directive that doesn't authorize a flight phase (e.g., "turn left heading 270," "hold short of runway 17," "report passing 4,000"). §91.123(b) governs.

The compliance obligation is similar for both, but the deviation conditions differ slightly: §91.123(a) explicitly allows TCAS RA deviation from a clearance, while §91.123(b) only mentions emergency. In practice, an RA-driven turn is also defensible as emergency authority under §91.3(b), so the distinction rarely matters operationally.

### "Unable" -- the pilot's right to refuse

The word "unable" doesn't appear in §91.123. But the regulation contemplates the pilot's ability to refuse: "When a pilot is uncertain of an ATC clearance, that pilot shall immediately request clarification" (§91.123(a) last sentence). "Uncertain" includes unable to safely comply.

The operational protocol:

1. ATC issues an instruction or clearance the pilot cannot safely comply with.
2. The pilot says "unable" and briefly states the reason (length of final, fuel state, weather, equipment, terrain, traffic).
3. ATC issues an alternative.
4. If no alternative is forthcoming and safety requires immediate action, the pilot exercises §91.3(b) emergency authority.

The "unable" call is *not* a violation. ATC's job after hearing it is to find a different solution. The pilot's job is to communicate clearly and continue safe operation while the alternate clearance arrives.

### §91.123(c) -- the post-deviation notification

If the pilot deviates from a clearance or instruction in an emergency or following a TCAS RA, they must notify ATC of the deviation "as soon as possible." This is not the post-flight written report; it's the on-frequency notification.

In a TCAS RA case: "Tower, Two-Three Tango, RA, climbing." That call satisfies §91.123(c).

In an emergency case: "Approach, Two-Three Tango, deviating left for thunderstorm cell." That call satisfies §91.123(c).

The notification is operational. The §91.123(d) 48-hour written report is the documentary follow-up if requested.

### §91.123(d) -- the 48-hour written report

If ATC requests a written report following an emergency-priority deviation, the pilot has 48 hours to file it with the facility manager. The report describes the emergency, the deviation, and the resolution.

ATC does not always request the report. For routine TCAS RAs and for clearly emergency-driven deviations, ATC may close the file without requesting documentation. For ambiguous deviations or those that interfered with traffic separation, ATC routinely requests the report.

The pilot's response to the request is to file. Refusing or ignoring the request is itself a violation. The written report is filed within 48 hours; counsel typically helps draft it because the report goes into the FAA's enforcement record if the agency opens an investigation.

## §91.3(b) -- emergency authority

```text
§91.3  Responsibility and authority of the pilot in command.

(a) The pilot in command of an aircraft is directly responsible for,
    and is the final authority as to, the operation of that aircraft.

(b) In an in-flight emergency requiring immediate action, the pilot
    in command may deviate from any rule of this part to the extent
    required to meet that emergency.

(c) Each pilot in command who deviates from a rule under paragraph
    (b) of this section shall, upon the request of the Administrator,
    send a written report of that deviation to the Administrator.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

§91.3(b) is the broad emergency-authority rule. It allows the pilot to deviate from *any* rule of Part 91 -- altitude, route, equipment, fuel, you name it -- "to the extent required to meet" the emergency. It is the pilot's strongest single regulatory protection.

### Conditions for §91.3(b)

The protection has two conditions:

1. **In-flight emergency requiring immediate action.** Genuine emergency, not "I'm running late."
2. **Deviation to the extent required.** Proportional, not blanket. The pilot deviates as much as needed and no more.

A pilot using §91.3(b) to climb through assigned altitude to avoid a thunderstorm uses it correctly. A pilot using §91.3(b) to skip an arrival procedure because the weather is "not great" probably does not.

The agency reviews §91.3(b) assertions on the same standard the pilot applied: was there a genuine emergency, was the deviation proportional? The Administrator can request a written report under §91.3(c) if the agency questions the deviation. The pilot's report is their explanation; the agency makes its determination.

### "Emergency" -- what counts

The regulation does not define emergency. Case law and Chief Counsel interpretations have built up a working definition:

- **Genuine threat to safety** -- weather, equipment failure, medical event, fuel exhaustion threat, terrain conflict, traffic conflict
- **Immediate** -- requires action now, not after consultation
- **In-flight** -- the rule specifically addresses in-flight situations (a pre-flight gate emergency is different)

An emergency need not be life-threatening to qualify, but it must be operationally serious. A pilot's failure to plan adequately for fuel does not become a §91.3(b) "emergency" just because the fuel state is now critical -- the agency may treat this as a §91.13 careless-or-reckless violation. But genuine fuel exhaustion threat from unforecast headwinds, encountered at altitude, where the pilot must descend and divert without ATC's permission to do so, is a §91.3(b) case.

### Declared vs. undeclared

A pilot can declare an emergency on frequency: "Mayday, Mayday, Mayday, Two-Three Tango, fuel emergency." The declaration is operational; it tells ATC to give priority and clear the airspace.

§91.3(b) does not require the pilot to *declare* the emergency. The pilot can use emergency authority without using the word "Mayday." The agency reads §91.3(b) on the underlying conditions, not on the radio call.

Operationally, declaring is the right move when:

- The pilot needs ATC priority (clear the airspace, expedite vectors)
- The pilot wants ATC's resources (search and rescue, fire trucks rolling, dedicated controller)
- The pilot wants the post-event treatment to clearly recognize emergency authority

Operationally, *not* declaring is sometimes preferred when:

- The deviation is small and self-resolving (a 100-foot altitude bust to avoid wake)
- ATC's involvement is unnecessary
- The pilot prefers post-event handling without the formality of a declared emergency record

Both are valid uses of §91.3(b). Counsel routinely advises clients on which framing to use.

## ATC pilot deviation reports

When ATC observes a pilot doing something they think violates a regulation (busting a clearance, busting airspace, missing a hold-short, etc.), the controller files a *pilot deviation report*. This is an internal ATC document that flows to the FAA's enforcement system.

The pilot deviation report includes:

- Date, time, location
- Aircraft identification (typically tail number)
- Pilot name (if known) and certificate number (if known)
- Description of the alleged deviation
- ATC's relevant communications
- Radar data (if the deviation involved airspace or altitude)

The report goes to the FSDO. The FSDO opens an investigation, which typically begins with a phone call or letter to the pilot. Depending on the severity and the pilot's history, the case routes to:

- Closed without action (least serious)
- Counseling letter (informal)
- Compliance Action (training/counseling) -- the typical outcome for inadvertent deviations
- Letter of Correction or Warning Notice
- Letter of Investigation -> Notice of Proposed Certificate Action -> certificate action (most serious)

Most pilot deviation cases close at Compliance Action level. Severe cases (multiple aggravators, prior history, denial of clear facts) escalate.

### What the pilot does upon learning of a pilot deviation

The pilot may learn of the deviation:

- Immediately on frequency ("call this number when you land")
- By phone from the FSDO within days
- By LOI in the mail within weeks

The right response in any of these cases:

1. Acknowledge receipt; do not narrate the event substantively yet.
2. File ASRS within 10 days of the original event if the window is still open.
3. Retain counsel.
4. Request ATC tapes and FAA records under PBR2.
5. Draft a written response with counsel.

The "call this number when you land" interaction is the most operationally treacherous. Pilots who call the number from the FBO, freshly post-flight, with adrenaline still up, often volunteer narrative that supplies evidence the agency didn't have. Better posture: call the number, identify yourself, acknowledge the call, indicate you'll be sending a written response after consultation. Decline to discuss the event in detail on the spot.

## Remedial training in lieu of certificate action

The most important post-event tool. Remedial training is a Compliance-Program-style outcome where the FAA requires the pilot to complete specific training (often with a designated CFI) instead of suspending or revoking the certificate.

### What it looks like

Typical remedial training outcomes:

- 5-10 hours of dual instruction with a designated CFI in the type of operation involved
- Specific topic coverage (the rule that was violated, the operational context)
- Written agreement between pilot and FSDO documenting the training plan
- CFI's logbook endorsement on completion
- FSDO closes the case after endorsement received

Variants:

- **Pilot-initiated remedial training** -- the pilot offers it during the LOI response: "I propose to complete 8 hours of dual instruction with a designated CFI focusing on Class B operations." The FSDO accepts or counters.
- **FSDO-imposed remedial training** -- the agency identifies specific training as part of a Compliance Action.
- **Settlement-based remedial training** -- counsel negotiates remedial training as part of a NOPCA settlement, converting a proposed suspension into training.

### Statutory authority

Remedial training is authorized under 49 USC 44709(d) (which requires the FAA to consider remedial training in lieu of suspension when appropriate) and FAA Order 8000.373B (Compliance Program). The agency has broad discretion to use it, and the policy preference since 2015 has been to use it heavily for inadvertent first-time violations.

### Position to ask

The pilot positions for remedial training by:

1. **Acknowledging the event** in the LOI response (without conceding intent or recklessness)
2. **Demonstrating learning** -- what does the pilot now understand that they didn't before? What will they do differently?
3. **Proposing specific training** -- not "I'll do whatever you want," but "I propose to complete X hours of dual instruction with a designated CFI focusing on Y."
4. **Identifying a CFI** -- if possible, the pilot identifies a CFI in advance who has agreed to provide the training. This signals seriousness.

The pilot who proposes remedial training in the LOI response is more likely to receive it than the pilot who waits to see what the agency proposes. The agency's decision-makers like to see the pilot taking initiative on the remedy; the proposal demonstrates the "willing and able to comply" posture the Compliance Program rewards.

### When remedial training is *not* on the table

The exclusion categories from the Compliance Program apply. Remedial training is unavailable for:

- Reckless or intentional violations
- Criminal violations (§91.17, §61.16, §67.403, §61.59)
- Repeat violations within the program window
- Lack of qualification cases (where the pilot wasn't supposed to be doing the operation at all)

For these cases, certificate action is the available remedy. Remedial training is post-violation training; it does not address the structural problem in those categories.

## Common misreadings

- **"I have to comply with every ATC instruction or it's a violation."** Wrong. §91.123(a) and (b) both allow deviation in emergency or with alternate clearance. The "unable" call is the pilot's path to alternate clearance; the regulation expects pilots to refuse instructions they cannot safely comply with.
- **"§91.3(b) emergency authority is unlimited."** Wrong. The deviation is bounded by "to the extent required to meet that emergency." A proportionality test. A pilot who uses §91.3(b) to ignore a clearance for a mild inconvenience has not used emergency authority correctly.
- **"I have to declare an emergency on frequency to use §91.3(b)."** Wrong. §91.3(b) does not require declaration. The pilot can use emergency authority and explain afterward. Declaration is operationally useful for ATC priority but not legally required.
- **"§91.123(c) only applies if I had a TCAS RA."** Wrong. §91.123(c) applies to any emergency-driven or RA-driven deviation from clearance or instruction. The pilot must notify ATC "as soon as possible."
- **"The 48-hour written report rule means I have to write a report after every emergency."** Wrong. §91.123(d) requires the report only if ATC requests it. ATC may close the case without a request. If they request, file within 48 hours.
- **"A pilot deviation report means certificate action is coming."** Wrong. Most pilot deviations close at Compliance Action level (counseling and training). The report is the start of an investigation, not a sanction.
- **"I should call the FSDO immediately to explain the deviation."** Mostly wrong. Acknowledging receipt is fine; substantively narrating the event without preparation is not. Counsel-coordinated written response is the better path.
- **"Remedial training is for low-time pilots."** Wrong. Remedial training is the agency's preferred Compliance-Program outcome for inadvertent first-time violations regardless of pilot experience. Senior commercial and ATP-rated pilots receive remedial training when they violate inadvertently.
- **"I can decline remedial training and just take the suspension."** Right, but probably not what you want. A 30-day suspension is in your file forever; remedial training closes the case without certificate action. The training is fewer days, less impactful on insurance and employment, and resets the pilot's regulatory record favorably.
- **"The CFI giving remedial training is on the FAA's side."** Mostly wrong. The CFI is a designated independent instructor; their job is to assess and train, not to investigate. The training is supportive; the CFI's endorsement closes the case favorably for the pilot.

### Apply

> Tower says "Two-Three Tango, hold short of runway 35 right." You're on a 1-mile final to runway 35 right. You can't comply.

Answer: Say "Tower, Two-Three Tango, unable hold short of 35 right, on short final 35 right." Tower will issue an alternate clearance (cleared to land if traffic permits, go around if not). The "unable" is not a §91.123 violation; it's the regulation's expected response when compliance is unsafe.

> ATC vectors you into a rapidly-developing thunderstorm cell. You can see the cell visually and on board radar. You ask for a deviation; ATC says "unable, traffic." What do you do?

Answer: Declare an emergency or use §91.3(b) authority and turn. "Approach, Two-Three Tango, deviating left for weather, this is an emergency." You don't fly into a thunderstorm because ATC denied a deviation. ATC's "unable" creates an emergency that justifies §91.3(b) authority. After the deviation, notify ATC under §91.123(c) and file the §91.123(d) written report if requested. File ASRS as backup.

> You bust an altitude assignment by 200 feet inadvertently. ATC says "Two-Three Tango, verify maintaining 5,000." You realize you're at 5,200, descend to 5,000, and acknowledge.

Answer: Inadvertent deviation; not §91.3(b) emergency. ATC may file a pilot deviation report. File ASRS within 10 days. If LOI arrives, retain counsel and prepare a written response that acknowledges the deviation, identifies what went wrong (autopilot misset, distraction, etc.), proposes remedial training if appropriate (e.g., 2 hours of altitude-management focused training with a CFII). Likely outcome: Compliance Action, no certificate action.

> You file an IFR flight plan from KPAO to KSBA, get cleared as filed. En route, weather at SBA goes below mins. You ask for diversion to KSMX, ATC vectors you direct. Did you deviate?

Answer: No. You operated under an amended clearance (the diversion vector), which §91.123(a) explicitly allows. The original clearance was modified; you complied with the modified clearance. No violation, no §91.123(c) or (d) reporting required, no §91.3(b) emergency. Routine IFR operation.

> You receive an LOI for an inadvertent Class B incursion. The agency proposes a 30-day certificate suspension. Your counsel reviews and identifies that the agency's evidence is weak on whether you were aware of the airspace boundary. What's the negotiating position?

Answer: Counsel writes a written response that: (1) acknowledges receipt and the seriousness; (2) provides the pilot's account, which differs on the awareness point; (3) proposes remedial training in lieu of suspension -- "8 hours of dual instruction with a designated CFI focused on Class B procedures, completion within 60 days, CFI endorsement filed with FSDO"; (4) requests an informal conference if the agency does not accept the proposal. Likely outcome: settled at remedial training, no certificate action.

> You have a TCAS RA in cruise IFR, climb 200 feet to comply. Tower asks "verify altitude." You confirm and explain. Tower says nothing further. Do you need to file anything?

Answer: §91.123(c) notification is satisfied by the on-frequency confirmation. §91.123(d) 48-hour written report is required only if ATC requests it; if tower didn't request and the case closes, no written report owed. File NASA ASRS as routine practice (the RA is a near-miss event worth documenting). No further action.

> You experience a partial engine failure in cruise. You declare an emergency, divert, land safely. Tower asks for a written report under §91.123(d). When is it due?

Answer: 48 hours from the emergency. Submitted in writing to the manager of the ATC facility (the tower). Counsel typically helps draft because the report goes into the FAA's record if an investigation opens. NTSB reporting under §830.5 may also apply if the engine failure resulted in substantial damage; the engine event itself is excluded from "substantial damage" on a single-engine, but if other damage was sustained during the diversion, the §830.5 trigger may attach. Coordinate FSDO and NTSB notification.
