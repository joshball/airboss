# Aeronautical experience -- the parallel hour requirements

A pilot progresses through three certificate levels (private, commercial, ATP). Each level has an aeronautical-experience requirement that lives in its subpart's analog of §61.109. The requirements *rhyme* across levels -- once you see the pattern, you stop having to memorize it.

This lesson treats the three sections together so you can see the parallel. The deep wrinkles (cross-country definition, simulator credit, complex/TAA substitution) all live in §61.1 and §61.51.

## What you'll be able to do

- Recall the topline hour requirements for private, commercial, and ATP without notes
- Distinguish the §61.1 cross-country definitions for the certs (50 nm general; instrument rating uses 50 nm with a landing at a non-departure airport)
- Identify what §61.51 lets you log toward each requirement (PIC vs dual-given vs solo vs simulator)
- Apply the post-2018 TAA / complex equivalence for the commercial cert

## Why this matters

Pilots, parents of pilots, and flight schools spend a lot of money pursuing hour milestones. A CFI who doesn't know the hour requirements cold gives bad advice -- "you need 250 to take the commercial checkride" is wrong about half the time because the pilot has already met the *total* hour but is short on a sub-requirement (instrument time, complex/TAA, night cross-country). The aero-experience sections are written as a *list of requirements*, not a single number; reading them as a single number is the most expensive mistake a CFI can make.

## The discovery question

A pilot you fly with at the local field has 280 total hours, all in a 172. They'd like to start commercial training. Are they ready to sit the commercial checkride next month?

Pause.

The total-time number says yes (250 minimum); but the §61.129(a) sub-requirements almost certainly say no. They need:

- 100 hours PIC of which 50 are cross-country (probably yes if they've been flying)
- 10 hours of instrument training (probably no -- 172 hours but not necessarily logged)
- 10 hours of training in a complex, TAA, or turbine airplane (likely no for a 172-only pilot)
- One day VFR cross-country of at least 100 nm with three landings (likely yes)
- One night VFR cross-country of at least 100 nm with three landings (probably yes)
- 5 hours night VFR (probably yes)
- 3 hours of instrument training within 60 days of the practical test (depends on timing)
- 10 hours of solo or "performing the duties of pilot in command" (yes)

The pilot is short on instrument training and complex/TAA time. They're not ready. The total number was a red herring.

This is what reading 61.129 *as a list* rather than *as a single number* protects you from.

## Private pilot -- §61.109

The private pilot aeronautical experience for a single-engine airplane:

```text
40 hours total flight time as a pilot, that includes at least:
  20 hours of flight training from an authorized instructor that includes at least:
     3 hours of cross-country flight training (>50 nm, with landing)
     3 hours of night flight training that includes:
       1 cross-country flight of >100 nm total distance
       10 takeoffs and 10 landings to a full stop, each separately
     3 hours of flight training by reference to instruments
     3 hours of flight training in preparation for the practical test, within
       the 60-day calendar period preceding the date of the test
  10 hours of solo flight time in a single-engine airplane that includes at least:
     5 hours of solo cross-country
     1 solo cross-country of >150 nm total distance, full-stop landings at three points,
       and one segment of the flight is >50 nm between takeoff and landing locations
     3 takeoffs and landings to a full stop at an airport with an operating
       control tower
```

Read the structure: 40 total hours, broken into a 20-hour dual minimum and a 10-hour solo minimum, with sub-requirements stamping further detail onto each. The 20+10 doesn't add to 40 because the remaining 10 hours can be anything (more dual, more solo, ground reference work).

Part 141 schools graduate at 35 total hours instead because the §141.55 syllabus structure compresses the hours required without compromising the practical-test gate. §141 graduates take the same FAA practical test the §61 graduates take.

### The cross-country definition trap

§61.1 defines cross-country *for the private pilot* as:

> any flight that includes a point of landing that was at least a straight-line distance of more than 50 nautical miles from the original point of departure.

That's the §61.1(b)(3)(ii) flavor. Note "more than 50" -- exactly 50 isn't cross-country.

§61.1 *also* defines cross-country *for the instrument rating* as:

> any flight that includes a landing at a point that was at least a straight-line distance of more than 50 nautical miles from the original point of departure.

Same wording. They're equivalent for §61.65 (instrument rating) and §61.109 (private pilot).

The trap is in §61.1(b)(3)(i), which defines a *third* flavor of cross-country for the airline transport pilot certificate: the same 50-nm threshold but it does *not* require a landing -- a flight that just *includes* a point more than 50 nm from departure counts. ATPs accumulating cross-country time get to count flights that pass through a 50-nm-distant point but don't land there.

## Commercial pilot -- §61.129

Commercial pilot aeronautical experience for a single-engine airplane:

```text
250 hours of flight time as a pilot, that includes at least:
  100 hours of flight time in powered aircraft, of which 50 hours
    must be in airplanes
  100 hours of pilot-in-command flight time, that includes at least:
    50 hours in airplanes
    50 hours of cross-country flight, of which at least 10 hours
      must be in airplanes
  20 hours of training:
    10 hours of instrument training in single-engine airplane (5 in actual flight,
      1 cross-country in single-engine airplane that meets §61.65 requirements)
    10 hours of training in an airplane that has a retractable landing gear,
      flaps, and a controllable pitch propeller; OR is turbine-powered;
      OR meets the requirements for a Technically Advanced Airplane (TAA)
    one 2-hour cross-country in a single-engine airplane in daytime conditions,
      consisting of a total straight-line distance of more than 100 nm
    one 2-hour cross-country in a single-engine airplane at night, with a
      straight-line distance of more than 100 nm
    3 hours of training in single-engine airplane in preparation for the
      practical test, within the 60-day calendar period preceding the date
      of the test
  10 hours of solo flight time in a single-engine airplane (or 10 hours of
    "performing the duties of pilot in command" in single-engine airplane
    with an authorized instructor on board), on the areas of operation listed
    under §61.127(b)(1), of which at least:
    one cross-country flight of >300 nm total distance with landings at a
      minimum of three points (one of which must be at least 250 nm
      from the original departure point)
    5 hours in night VFR conditions, with 10 takeoffs and 10 landings (with
      each landing involving a flight in the traffic pattern) at an airport
      with an operating control tower
```

That's a lot of sub-requirements. The pattern: total time, then PIC time, then training time, then solo or "performing the duties" time. Each layer has its own internal structure.

### The 2018 TAA substitution

Pre-2018, §61.129(a)(3)(ii) required 10 hours in a *complex* airplane (retractable gear, flaps, controllable-pitch prop). The complex requirement caused a real problem -- the supply of GA complex aircraft is shrinking, and many flight schools no longer have complex aircraft to rent.

The 2018 amendment added two alternatives: turbine-powered, or TAA. A TAA is defined in §61.129(j):

> An airplane equipped with an electronically advanced avionics system that includes a primary flight display (PFD), a multifunction display (MFD), and a two-axis autopilot integrated with the navigation and heading guidance system.

Most modern G1000 / G3000 / G2000 / Avidyne-equipped airplanes meet this definition. An aspiring commercial pilot training in a 172 G1000 is meeting the §61.129(a)(3)(ii) requirement *every flight* -- whereas under the pre-2018 rule the same training time wouldn't count.

The trap: a TAA is *not* the same as a §61.31(e) high-performance airplane (>200 HP) or a §61.31(f) tailwheel airplane. A pilot can have 100 hours in a TAA and still need a high-performance endorsement to fly a 200+ HP airplane.

### "Performing the duties of pilot in command"

§61.129(a)(4) introduces an alternative phrasing: instead of solo time, the regulation accepts "performing the duties of pilot in command in a single-engine airplane with an authorized instructor on board." The student is the manipulator and is operating the airplane as PIC for the purpose of the training, but the instructor is physically present.

This wording matters because it lets a school accumulate the 10 hours required even when the student isn't soloing -- valuable when insurance won't underwrite solo in a high-performance complex airplane, for example.

## ATP -- §61.159

The airline transport pilot aeronautical experience for a single-engine or multiengine airplane:

```text
1500 hours of total time as a pilot that includes at least:
  500 hours of cross-country flight time
  100 hours of night flight time
  50 hours of flight time in the class of airplane for the rating sought
  75 hours of instrument flight time, in actual or simulated instrument
    conditions, subject to subdivisions:
    not more than 25 hours of which may be obtained in a flight simulator
      or flight training device
  250 hours of flight time as PIC, or as second-in-command performing the
    duties of PIC while under the supervision of a PIC, or any combination
    thereof
```

The cross-country definition for ATP loosens, per §61.1(b)(3)(i): the flight only has to *include* a point more than 50 nm from departure; no landing required.

The 1500-hour rule was strengthened post-Colgan (2009) by Public Law 111-216 and codified into §61.159 in 2013. Before Colgan, a regional airline first officer needed only a commercial cert; after Colgan, an ATP-equivalent is required to act as a Part 121 SIC.

### The R-ATP exception

§61.160 carves out a Restricted-ATP for pilots with structured training: 1000 hours total for graduates of accredited four-year aviation programs (with specific course content), or 1250 hours for two-year aviation programs, or 750 hours for military-trained pilots. The R-ATP allows the holder to act as SIC under Part 121 but not PIC.

R-ATP recipients still need ATP-CTP (the Airline Transport Pilot Certification Training Program) before sitting the ATP knowledge test.

## §61.51 -- the logging rules that feed all of this

The aero-experience sections specify *how many hours* of each type. §61.51 specifies *what counts as which type*. The interaction is the source of most logging confusion.

### What §61.51 requires

§61.51(b) lists the minimum required entries: date, total time, type and identification of aircraft, point of departure, point of arrival, and the purpose of flight. Plus type of pilot experience or training (solo, dual, PIC, simulated instrument, etc.).

The reg doesn't require *every* flight be logged -- only flights that the pilot wants to count toward "currency, certification, additional ratings, or for the FAA's recordkeeping." A casual pilot can fly without logging; a career-track pilot logs everything.

### What counts as PIC time -- §61.51(e)

The PIC-time question is the most-litigated aspect of pilot logging. §61.51(e)(1) lists three independent ways to log PIC:

- **(i) -- Acting as PIC.** The pilot is the *legal* PIC of the flight (designated by the airplane operator or by being the only certificated pilot on board). Acting-as-PIC and logging-as-PIC line up here.
- **(ii) -- Sole manipulator of an aircraft for which the pilot is rated.** A pilot rated in the category and class of the airplane logs PIC for whatever portion of the flight they were the sole manipulator. The Hicks (2010) Chief Counsel letter clarifies this: the rating is the gate, not the act of being PIC.
- **(iii) -- Operating in operations requiring more than one pilot.** A pilot in a two-pilot operation logs PIC time when they are the designated PIC.

The trap: §61.51(e)(1)(ii) lets a pilot log PIC even when they're not legally responsible for the flight. A private pilot rated single-engine land receiving instruction in a complex airplane (which they're not endorsed for) is still rated SEL; they are sole manipulator for the dual-given portion of flight; they log that time as PIC. Their *instructor* is also logging PIC for the same time -- under §61.51(e)(3), an authorized instructor logs PIC for the full duration of the dual.

This is intentional. The reg is doing accounting for the pilot's resume; both pilots can rightly count the time, because both pilots were exercising PIC-type skill (the student manipulating, the instructor responsible). The only place this creates friction is when a hiring airline asks for "PIC time" without the §61.51 nuance -- HR readers can mistake a logbook entry for "sole-PIC time" when it's actually "sole-manipulator-with-instructor PIC time." Pilots learn to annotate their logbooks to disambiguate.

### What counts as cross-country -- the multiple definitions

§61.1(b)(3) is where the cross-country definitions live, and they are *not* the same:

- **§61.1(b)(3)(i)** -- For ATP cross-country: "more than a straight-line distance of more than 50 nautical miles from the original point of departure." No landing required.
- **§61.1(b)(3)(ii)** -- For private pilot, commercial pilot, and instructor certificates: "a point of landing that was at least a straight-line distance of more than 50 nautical miles from the original point of departure."
- **§61.1(b)(3)(iii)** -- For instrument rating: same as (ii). Landing required.
- **§61.1(b)(3)(iv)** -- For sport pilot: includes a point of landing more than *25 nm* from departure.

A pilot accumulating hours toward both their commercial and their ATP must log carefully: a flight that flies from KPAO to a point 60 nm out and returns without landing counts as ATP cross-country but *not* commercial cross-country (no landing). The same logbook entry yields different counts depending on the certificate the pilot is filing toward.

### Simulator and FTD time

§61.51(g) governs flight simulator and flight training device time. The summary:

- **Full-flight simulators (FFS).** Up to 50 hours can be credited toward the §61.129 commercial requirement, and up to 25 hours toward the §61.159 ATP requirement.
- **Flight training devices (FTD), advanced.** Limits depend on the cert; usually less than full-flight simulator credit.
- **Aviation training devices (ATD), basic and advanced.** Up to 10 hours toward instrument rating, 20 hours toward ATP. For the private pilot, 2.5 hours of ATD instrument training counts toward the 3 hours of instrument-by-reference required at §61.109(a)(3).

The simulator credit rules are wrinkled enough that working CFIs always check the section directly when planning a flight log. Memorizing them is fragile.

## Common misreadings

- **"Total time is the only number that matters."** Wrong. Every aero-experience section has sub-requirements. A pilot can have 270 hours and not be eligible for the commercial because they're short on instrument training, complex/TAA, or night cross-country.
- **"Logging PIC requires being the legal PIC."** Wrong. §61.51(e)(1)(ii) allows logging PIC as sole manipulator if rated, even when the pilot isn't the legal PIC. The Hicks letter is canon on this point.
- **"Cross-country always means a 50-nm landing."** Mostly right, but ATP cross-country (§61.1(b)(3)(i)) doesn't require a landing. Sport-pilot cross-country uses a 25-nm threshold.
- **"Simulator time counts toward total time."** Generally false. Simulator time counts toward specific requirements (instrument time, certain training requirements) up to specified limits, but does not count toward the total flight-time requirement except in narrow cases.
- **"Complex is required for the commercial."** Outdated. The 2018 amendment added TAA as an alternative; many modern G1000-equipped airplanes meet the requirement.

### Apply the rules

> A 200-hour PPL in a 172 G1000 wants to start commercial training. They have 25 hours of cross-country PIC and 5 hours of instrument training in their logbook (all PPL training). Walk through what they still need under §61.129.

Answer outline: 50 more PIC hours (need 100), 25 more cross-country PIC (need 50), 5 more instrument (need 10), they're already in a TAA so the TAA hours are accumulating organically, they need both day and night long cross-countries, they need the 300-nm solo cross-country, and they need to take and pass the knowledge test before the practical test.

> A 1200-hour pilot wants to know if they qualify for an R-ATP. What questions do you ask?

Answer outline: Was their training in an accredited 4-year aviation program with the required course content (1000-hour eligibility), a 2-year program (1250-hour), or military (750-hour)? Did they complete ATP-CTP? If yes to one of the structured programs and yes to ATP-CTP, they're eligible. If no, they need 1500.
