# Walking Part 61 -- subparts A through G

This is the front-to-back commentary on Part 61. Read it slowly. The point is not to memorize sections; the point is to feel the *shape* of the part so that when a question hits you in three weeks, you already have a directional sense of where the answer lives.

We walk subparts A through G in order. Subparts H, I, J, K we name and locate; we'll deep-dive H next week.

## What you'll be able to do

- Recite the subparts of Part 61 with their topic and a one-sentence purpose
- Predict, given a question type, which subpart owns the answer
- Recognize the *parallel structure* across the cert-level subparts (E for private, F for commercial, G for ATP)
- Locate the most-cited sections (61.51, 61.56, 61.57, 61.58, 61.113) without scanning a table of contents

## Why this matters

Part 61 has roughly 130 sections. Memorizing them is brittle and almost nobody who works in this field actually does it. What working CFIs have is a *structural map*: they know "logging lives in 61.51" because they know subpart A is general and 61.51 is the second-most-cited section in the part. They know that "privileges and limitations for a private pilot" lives in 61.113 because subpart E covers private pilots and 61.113 sits next to 61.117 (which is basically the same thing for the limitations side). The map is durable; the recall isn't.

Front-to-back reading installs the map.

## The shape of Part 61

```text
Part 61 -- Certification: Pilots, Flight Instructors, and Ground Instructors

Subpart A -- General                                   (sections 61.1 to 61.31)
  Definitions, applicability, the universal rules that touch every pilot
  regardless of cert level. The "every pilot" subpart.

Subpart B -- Aircraft Ratings and Pilot Authorizations (sections 61.39 to 61.71)
  Logging, currency, flight reviews, the practical-test prereqs, the
  aeronautical-knowledge prereqs. The "what you've done counts as what" subpart.

Subpart C -- Student Pilots                            (sections 61.81 to 61.95)
  The student pilot certificate, solo prerequisites, solo cross-country
  endorsements, solo limitations.

Subpart D -- Recreational Pilots                       (sections 61.96 to 61.101)
  The cert nobody gets anymore. Name + locate; do not memorize.

Subpart E -- Private Pilots                            (sections 61.102 to 61.117)
  The private pilot certificate. Eligibility, aero experience, knowledge
  test, practical test, privileges, limitations. The first cert most pilots
  hold.

Subpart F -- Commercial Pilots                         (sections 61.121 to 61.135)
  Same shape as Subpart E, one cert level up.

Subpart G -- Airline Transport Pilots                  (sections 61.151 to 61.171)
  Same shape, one cert level up again.

Subpart H -- Flight Instructors                        (sections 61.181 to 61.201)
  The CFI subpart. Week 3 is this entire subpart.

Subpart I -- Ground Instructors                        (sections 61.211 to 61.219)
  CFI-G. Name + locate.

Subpart J -- Sport Pilots                              (sections 61.301 to 61.327)
  LSA pathway. Name + locate.

Subpart K -- Flight Instructors with a Sport Pilot Rating (61.401 to 61.429)
  CFI-S. Name + locate.
```

The structural pattern: subparts A-B are universal, subpart C is student, D is recreational (vestigial), E-G walk private -> commercial -> ATP, H-I are instructors, J-K are sport pilot. Once you see the pattern you can find any cert-level rule by knowing only the cert level.

## Subpart A -- General (61.1 to 61.31)

Subpart A is the floor. The rules here apply to every pilot regardless of certificate level. The most-quoted sections:

### §61.1 -- Applicability and definitions

Read the definitions list once. It defines "aeronautical experience," "authorized instructor," "complex airplane," "high-performance airplane," "training time," "PIC time," "solo flight time" -- terms that show up across the rest of Part 61 and across all of Part 91. Many examiners will quiz you on whether a particular flight counts as "training time" vs. "PIC time" -- 61.1 is where those definitions live.

### §61.3 -- Requirement for certificates, ratings, and authorizations

The section that says "you must hold the right cert to do the thing." Most pilots never read 61.3 carefully because it states the obvious. The trap is paragraph (l): a pilot must present their pilot certificate, photo ID, and medical certificate to the FAA, NTSB, or law enforcement *upon request*.

### §61.5 -- Certificates and ratings issued under this part

Lists the certificates Part 61 issues and the ratings each one supports. Useful when a question asks "what rating exists for X?" -- the answer is in 61.5's enumeration.

### §61.13 -- Issuance of airman certificates

Mostly procedural -- how the FAA issues a cert, the form, the duration, etc. Skim.

### §61.15 -- Offenses involving alcohol or drugs

The career-ender. A drug or alcohol conviction triggers reportable consequences within 60 days. We come back to this in Week 9.

### §61.17 -- Temporary certificate

The piece of paper an examiner gives you on the day of your checkride. Valid 120 days.

### §61.19 -- Duration of pilot and instructor certificates

Most pilot certs do not expire (private, commercial, ATP). Instructor certs *do* (24 calendar months -- subpart H rules govern renewal). Medical certs expire under Part 67.

### §61.23 -- Medical certificates: requirement and duration

The medical certificate rules. First class for ATP-required ops, second class for commercial, third class for private and recreational. Class durations vary with age. BasicMed is in §61.113(i) and Part 68. We treat the medical fully in [06-medical-certificates.md](06-medical-certificates.md).

### §61.31 -- Type rating, additional training, and authorization requirements

The famous endorsement section. High-performance, complex (rolled into HP for new aircraft post-1997), high-altitude (pressurized over FL250), tailwheel, glider tow. Each requires a one-time training and logbook endorsement.

The trap on 61.31: the "complex" definition was technically retired for new aircraft post-1997 but the section still lives, and the training endorsement still appears in many logbooks because the airplanes the endorsement was written for still fly.

## Subpart B -- Aircraft Ratings and Pilot Authorizations (61.39 to 61.71)

Subpart B is "what counts as what for what." It is the most-cited subpart in this entire part because nearly every cert progression and every recurring currency requirement passes through it.

### §61.39 -- Prerequisites for practical tests

The eight-or-so conditions you must satisfy before sitting a practical test. Includes the famous "logbook endorsement from your instructor stating you are prepared," the 60-day knowledge-test window for some, the recent-flight-experience floor.

### §61.51 -- Pilot logbooks

The logging section. Required entries, what counts as PIC time, the difference between PIC time and "sole manipulator" time, the dual-given convention for instructors, the simulator/FTD logging rules. **This is the most-tested section in Part 61**, full stop. We deep-dive in [02-aeronautical-experience.md](02-aeronautical-experience.md).

### §61.56 -- Flight review

Every 24 calendar months, every pilot. One hour of ground, one hour of flight, completed by an authorized instructor with a logbook endorsement. Equivalents in §61.56(d) (a new cert or rating practical test in the same category and class), §61.56(e) (a sport pilot proficiency check), §61.56(f) (a phase of an FAA-sponsored pilot proficiency program). Deep-dive in [04-flight-review-and-equivalents.md](04-flight-review-and-equivalents.md).

### §61.57 -- Recent flight experience: pilot in command

The currency section. Three currencies live here:

- **(a) -- Passenger currency.** Three takeoffs and landings within 90 days, in the same category, class, and (if applicable) type, to act as PIC carrying passengers.
- **(b) -- Night passenger currency.** Same three takeoffs and landings, but the period closes one hour after sunset to one hour before sunrise, with a *full stop* requirement (touch-and-goes don't count).
- **(c) -- IFR currency.** "Six-six-HIT": six instrument approaches, holding procedures, intercepting and tracking courses, within the preceding six calendar months. Lapsed currency triggers a six-month grace where the pilot is non-current but can re-establish; longer than that and an instrument proficiency check (IPC) is required.

Deep-dives in [03-currency-vs-recency-vs-proficiency.md](03-currency-vs-recency-vs-proficiency.md) and [05-ifr-currency.md](05-ifr-currency.md).

### §61.58 -- Pilot-in-command proficiency check: operation of aircraft requiring more than one pilot

The §61.58 PIC proficiency check is *not* the same as §61.57. It applies only to pilots acting as PIC of an aircraft that *requires* more than one pilot under its certification (typically a type-rated airplane or one with a "two-pilot" certification basis). One PIC proficiency check every 12 calendar months in the type, plus an additional check every 24 months in another type if the pilot is multi-typed. Most GA pilots never touch this section; ATPs and corporate pilots live in it.

### §61.65 -- Instrument rating requirements

The aero-experience and knowledge-test gates for the instrument rating. 50 hours PIC cross-country, 40 hours actual or simulated instrument, of which 15 with a CFII, plus a long IFR cross-country meeting specific distance and approach criteria. The trap: cross-country here uses the §61.1 *instrument-rating cross-country* definition (50 nautical miles minimum, with a landing at a point other than departure), not the §61.1 general cross-country definition.

### §61.69 -- Glider and unpowered ultralight vehicle towing

Niche. Name + locate.

### §61.71 -- Graduates of an approved Part 141 training course

The bridge between Part 61 and Part 141 -- when a 141 graduate sits a Part 61 practical, the 141 grad's training counts. This section is why a 141 school can graduate a private at 35 hours instead of the Part 61 minimum of 40.

## Subpart C -- Student Pilots (61.81 to 61.95)

The student pilot certificate, solo flight, and the endorsements that authorize a student to fly without their instructor in the cockpit.

### §61.83 -- Eligibility for a student pilot certificate

16 years old (14 for glider/balloon), able to read, speak, write, and understand English, with a third-class medical (or BasicMed under specific narrow conditions, or none at all for sport-pilot ops). The medical or BasicMed timing matters: a student pilot may obtain the cert *before* the medical, but cannot solo without the medical (or the BasicMed/sport-pilot exception) in hand.

### §61.87 -- Solo requirements for student pilots

The endorsement structure: the instructor signs the student off for *each make and model* the student will solo. The endorsement specifies the conditions under which solo is authorized. §61.87 is the spine of the student-pilot endorsement bible (AC 61-65).

### §61.89 -- General limitations on student pilots

What student pilots may *not* do: carry passengers, fly for compensation or hire, fly in furtherance of a business, fly in IMC or solo at night unless specifically endorsed, fly internationally without specific endorsement.

### §61.93 -- Solo cross-country flight requirements

The CFI sign-off chain that gates each solo cross-country: a one-time endorsement plus a per-flight endorsement after the instructor reviews the route, weather, weight and balance, and performance. The per-flight endorsement is what makes the cross-country legal even with a one-time endorsement on file.

### §61.95 -- Operations in Class B airspace

Student pilots cannot solo into Class B airspace except at airports specifically permitted (a list FAA publishes), and only with the right endorsements. The two endorsements (general Class B and airport-specific Class B) are the trap; AC 61-65 spells them out.

## Subpart D -- Recreational Pilots (61.96 to 61.101)

Recreational pilot is a vestigial certificate. Created in 1989 to be a "lighter" private pilot, almost no one earned it. Sport pilot (subpart J) replaced it operationally. Name and locate; do not memorize.

If a question is about recreational pilot privileges, look in 61.101. The privileges are narrow (single-engine under 180 HP, four-seat or less, daytime, within 50 nm of departure airport, no flight at night, no IFR, no Class B/C/D without endorsement). Working CFIs almost never train at this cert level.

## Subpart E -- Private Pilots (61.102 to 61.117)

The first "real" cert most pilots hold. Subpart E is structured as:

- **§61.102 -- applicability**
- **§61.103 -- eligibility requirements (the floor)**: 17 years old, English, third-class medical, certain prerequisites
- **§61.105 -- aeronautical knowledge**: the topics the knowledge test covers
- **§61.107 -- flight proficiency**: the maneuvers the practical test will test
- **§61.109 -- aeronautical experience**: the famous "40 hours minimum" requirement
- **§61.111 -- cross-country flights: pilots who are not flight instructors**: niche
- **§61.113 -- private pilot privileges and limitations: pilot in command**: the daily-life section
- **§61.115 -- balloon rating: limitations**: niche
- **§61.117 -- private pilot privileges and limitations: second in command of aircraft requiring more than one pilot**: also niche

**§61.113** is the most-quoted section in subpart E. It defines what a private pilot may do for compensation (basically nothing -- with narrow exceptions in (b) for incidental compensation and (c) for cost-sharing). The Mangiamele letter (2009) is the canonical Chief Counsel interpretation of cost-sharing under §61.113(c). The Walker and Hicks letters address other aspects.

**§61.113(i)** is the home of BasicMed -- a private pilot may operate certain aircraft under §61.113 without a third-class medical certificate by complying with Part 68 and 49 USC 44703 instead.

**§61.109** is the aero-experience requirement. 40 hours total time, of which 20 are with a CFI, of which 3 are dual cross-country, plus 10 hours of solo with specific subset minimums (5 cross-country, 1 long cross-country meeting distance and landing criteria, 3 hours within 60 days of the practical test). A Part 141 school graduates at 35 hours instead because 141 syllabi compress the time without compromising the practical-test standards.

## Subpart F -- Commercial Pilots (61.121 to 61.135)

Same shape as subpart E, one level up. The parallel:

| Topic                              | Private (E) | Commercial (F) |
| ---------------------------------- | ----------- | -------------- |
| Eligibility                        | §61.103     | §61.123        |
| Aeronautical knowledge             | §61.105     | §61.125        |
| Flight proficiency                 | §61.107     | §61.127        |
| Aeronautical experience            | §61.109     | §61.129        |
| Privileges and limitations (PIC)   | §61.113     | §61.133        |
| Privileges and limitations (SIC)   | §61.117     | §61.135        |

**§61.129** -- 250 hours minimum, 100 PIC, 50 cross-country PIC, 10 of which by airplane, plus specific instrument time, complex/TAA time, night cross-country, and so on. The complex aircraft requirement (§61.129(a)(3)(ii)) was modified in 2018 to allow TAA (Technically Advanced Aircraft) as an alternative to a true complex.

**§61.133** -- the commercial privileges section. A commercial pilot may act as PIC for compensation or hire, subject to limits. The trap: a commercial cert without an instrument rating restricts the commercial pilot to daytime, within 50 nm of departure (§61.133(b)). Most working commercial pilots have an IR for this reason.

## Subpart G -- Airline Transport Pilots (61.151 to 61.171)

Same shape again, one level up. The parallel continues:

| Topic                              | Commercial (F) | ATP (G)  |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------- | -------- |
| Eligibility                        | §61.123        | §61.153  |
| Aeronautical knowledge             | §61.125        | §61.155  |
| Flight proficiency                 | §61.127        | §61.157  |
| Aeronautical experience            | §61.129        | §61.159 (airplane) / §61.161 (helicopter) |
| Privileges and limitations (PIC)   | §61.133        | §61.167  |

**§61.159** -- 1500 hours total, with subset requirements that include 500 cross-country, 100 night, 75 instrument, 250 PIC, and the famous ATP-CTP (ATP Certification Training Program) introduced after Colgan in 2013.

**§61.165** is a niche addition: type ratings issued concurrent with the ATP cert. This is where a typical airline pilot gets their first 737 / A320 / etc. type rating.

## Subparts H, I, J, K -- name and locate

We deep-dive subpart H next week. The remaining subparts are name-and-locate:

- **Subpart H -- Flight Instructors.** §61.181 to §61.201. The CFI subpart. Eligibility (§61.183), aeronautical experience (§61.185, §61.187), privileges (§61.193), limitations (§61.195), renewal (§61.197). Week 3 spends the entire week here.
- **Subpart I -- Ground Instructors.** §61.211 to §61.219. The non-flying instructor cert. Skim if asked; otherwise skip.
- **Subpart J -- Sport Pilots.** §61.301 to §61.327. The light-sport pathway. Privileges live in §61.315; aircraft-specific privileges and limitations follow. Skim.
- **Subpart K -- Flight Instructors with a Sport Pilot Rating.** §61.401 to §61.429. The light-sport instructor cert. Skim.

## How to use the walk

This commentary is not the regulation. The regulation is on eCFR; that's authoritative. What this commentary gives you is the *shape*: which subpart owns what, which sections are most-cited, which are vestigial.

When a checkride question hits you, route it through the shape:

- "Can this pilot do X for compensation?" -> the privileges-and-limitations section in their cert's subpart (E for private, F for commercial). For private specifically, §61.113.
- "What's the time minimum to add Y?" -> the aero-experience section (E -> §61.109, F -> §61.129, G -> §61.159).
- "When is this pilot's flight review due?" -> §61.56.
- "When is this pilot's IFR currency due?" -> §61.57(c).
- "Does this pilot need an endorsement to fly Z?" -> §61.31 (HP, complex, tailwheel, high-altitude) or §61.93 (solo cross-country) or §61.87 (solo) for student-level cases.
- "Is this CFI eligible to renew?" -> §61.197 (subpart H, deep-dive Week 3).

After the front-to-back walk, the rest of Week 2 zooms in on the high-traffic sections.

## Common misreadings

- **"61.51 logs everything that flies."** Wrong. §61.51 is a minimum-required-entries rule. A pilot can log anything they want; the FAA only requires logging for *currency, certification, and rating purposes*. Whether a flight is logged "for the FAA" is different from whether it's recorded "for the pilot." Many ATP-track pilots log every flight; many recreational fliers log only what's required.
- **"PIC time is the same as sole-manipulator time."** Wrong, mostly. Under §61.51(e), a private pilot logs PIC time when *acting* as PIC (regardless of who's flying) or when *sole manipulator of the controls of an aircraft for which the pilot is rated*. A commercial student under instruction in a complex airplane they're not yet endorsed for may log PIC as sole manipulator (if they're rated in the category and class) but cannot *act* as PIC of that flight. The trap: rated in category/class is the sole-manipulator gate; an endorsement (HP, complex) is the *acting-as-PIC* gate, not the *logging-as-PIC* gate.
- **"Flight reviews and IFR currency reset together."** Wrong. §61.56 (flight review) and §61.57(c) (IFR currency) are independent timers. A pilot can have a current flight review and lapsed IFR currency, or vice versa. They reset on different events.
- **"Subpart D is for recreational pilots and is alive."** Technically alive, operationally vestigial. Almost no one earns recreational pilot today; the FAA published it in 1989 as a stepping stone and replaced it functionally with sport pilot (subpart J) in 2004.
- **"BasicMed lives in Part 61."** Partial. The privilege to operate without a third-class medical is mentioned in §61.113(i), but the requirements (medical exam, online course, restrictions on aircraft and flight conditions) live in **Part 68**, with the underlying statutory authority in **49 USC 44703**.
