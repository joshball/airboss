# Currency, recency, proficiency

There are three different ideas pilots routinely call "current," and they are not the same thing. The regulation only addresses one of them. The other two live in the pilot's own judgment and the pilot's own logbook.

This lesson disambiguates them, because most checkride failures on this topic come from a pilot answering the wrong question.

## What you'll be able to do

- State precisely what the FAA means by "current" for each currency type
- Distinguish currency (legal floor) from recency (your own usage) from proficiency (actual capability)
- Apply §61.57(a) (passenger), §61.57(b) (night passenger), §61.57(c) (IFR) to a specific pilot
- Articulate why the regulatory floor is necessary but not sufficient for safe flight

## Why this matters

A pilot who says "I'm current" without specifying *for what* has just told you they don't know what currency is. A pilot who says "I'm current and proficient" but only shows you a logbook that satisfies §61.57(a) has confused the regulatory floor with the operational standard. CFIs who train this distinction sloppily produce pilots who fly to the legal minimum and crash.

The accident chain on a "perfectly current" pilot who has not flown in 89 days, takes three friends up in marginal weather, and gets disoriented because the last 90 days had no time-in-the-system actual flying -- this exact case is in NTSB reports more often than any of us are comfortable with.

## The discovery question

You're at the airport, your friend asks you to come fly to lunch tomorrow. They tell you, confidently, "I'm current." Before you say yes or no, what do you ask?

Pause.

Reasonable questions to ask in order:

1. *Current for what?* Passengers? Night passengers? IFR? All three are different timers.
2. *Current means: when did you last do the takeoffs and landings, or the approaches?* The bar is "within the preceding 90 days for passengers" not "I've flown recently."
3. *In what aircraft?* Currency is by category, class, and (where applicable) type. Three takeoffs and landings in a 172 don't make you current to fly the friend's PA-28R-201.
4. *Are we flying at night?* Different timer (full-stop landings, not touch-and-goes; one-hour-after-sunset window).
5. *Is the flight IFR?* Different timer entirely (six-six-HIT instead of 3-takeoffs-and-landings).
6. *Are you also proficient, not just legal?* (Proficiency is *your* judgment, not the FAA's. We come back to this.)

A pilot who answers "I'm current" without unpacking which currency they're claiming has not earned a yes from you yet.

## Three different ideas

| Concept           | Defined by                            | Source of truth      | Time-bound?               |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------- | -------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **Currency**      | Federal regulation                    | §61.56, §61.57, §61.58 | Yes, by clock + activity  |
| **Recency**       | Your own logbook                      | The pilot's habit    | Soft -- no specific bar    |
| **Proficiency**   | Your own honest judgment + objective skill | The pilot's flying  | Continuous, not boolean    |

### Currency -- the regulatory floor

Currency is binary and time-bound. Either you've satisfied §61.56 (flight review) and the relevant subsections of §61.57 (passenger, night passenger, IFR) and §61.58 (PIC proficiency check, where applicable), or you haven't. The FAA cares about currency because it's the only auditable proxy they have for "this pilot has done a minimum amount of flying recently."

Currency is necessary for legal flight. Currency is not sufficient for safe flight.

### Recency -- the personal habit

Recency is whether you've flown the *kind of flight* you're about to fly *recently enough that your hands and eyes are warm*. The FAA does not regulate recency. The pilot's habit and the pilot's logbook do.

A pilot who is current per §61.57 but hasn't flown an actual cross-country in eight months is technically legal to take their family to a 200-nm destination. Whether they should is a *recency* question, not a *currency* question. Many CFIs counsel students to set their own recency floor higher than the regulatory currency requirement.

### Proficiency -- the operational standard

Proficiency is the actual question: can the pilot fly the airplane to the practical-test standard, today, in the conditions they're going to encounter? The FAA has no way to measure this between checkrides. Whether the pilot is proficient is the pilot's call (with input from any CFI they fly with, any check airman in their org, any line of business with stricter standards than the FARs).

The FAA codified the *currency floor* not because it equals proficiency, but because no better proxy exists short of a checkride every six months for every pilot.

## §61.56 -- Flight review

The flight review is the most-failed-to-track currency item.

```text
Except as provided in paragraphs (b) and (c) of this section, no person may
act as pilot in command of an aircraft unless, since the beginning of the
24th calendar month before the month in which that pilot acts as pilot
in command, that person has --

  (1) Accomplished a flight review given in an aircraft for which that
      pilot is rated by an authorized instructor; and

  (2) A logbook endorsement from an authorized instructor who gave the
      review certifying that the person has satisfactorily completed
      the review.

Note: paraphrase of §61.56(c). Read the actual reg before referencing in
exam.
```

The structure: every 24 calendar months, in an aircraft the pilot is rated for, with an authorized instructor, with a logbook endorsement at the end. One hour ground + one hour flight is a paraphrase of §61.56(a) -- read it directly.

The "calendar month" math: a flight review accomplished on March 15, 2024 is good through March 31, 2026. The 24 calendar months count from the beginning of the 24th month before the current operation; pilots get the back-end calendar month for free.

We deep-dive equivalents (§61.56(d) practical test, §61.56(e) sport-pilot proficiency check, §61.56(f) Wings program phase) in [04-flight-review-and-equivalents.md](04-flight-review-and-equivalents.md).

## §61.57(a) -- Passenger currency

```text
No person may act as a pilot in command of an aircraft carrying passengers
or of an aircraft certificated for more than one pilot flight crewmember
unless that person has made at least 3 takeoffs and 3 landings within the
preceding 90 days, and --

  (i) The person acted as the sole manipulator of the flight controls; and

  (ii) The required takeoffs and landings were performed in an aircraft
       of the same category, class, and type (if a type rating is required),
       and, if the aircraft to be flown for those operations is an
       airplane with a tailwheel, the takeoffs and landings must have been
       made to a full stop in an airplane with a tailwheel.

Note: paraphrase of §61.57(a)(1). Read the actual reg before referencing
in exam.
```

The structure:

1. **3 takeoffs and 3 landings**
2. **Within the preceding 90 days**
3. **Sole manipulator** (the pilot performing the takeoff/landing -- can't be receiving instruction with the CFI handling controls during the rollout)
4. **Same category, class, and (if applicable) type.** A pilot rated single-engine land needs the takeoffs/landings in single-engine land. A pilot rated airplane multi-engine land needs them in airplane multi-engine land. Type matters when the airplane requires a type rating (12,500+ lbs, jet, etc.).
5. **Tailwheel airplanes -- to a full stop.** Touch-and-goes don't count for tailwheel passenger currency. The wording is in §61.57(a)(1)(ii).

### What "carrying passengers" means

The reg uses "passengers" -- a term §1.1 doesn't define. Courts have read this broadly: anyone other than the required flight crewmembers. A non-pilot family member is a passenger. Another rated pilot is a passenger if they're not part of the required crew. A CFI giving instruction is *not* a passenger because they are not aboard for transport -- they're aboard for the conduct of training.

### The math of "preceding 90 days"

"Within the preceding 90 days" means within 90 calendar days *backward* from the moment of the proposed flight. A pilot whose last takeoff and landing was 91 days ago is not current. A pilot whose third-most-recent takeoff and landing was 91 days ago is also not current -- they need *all three* within the 90-day window.

The 90 days is a rolling window. Pilots recovering currency typically do three takeoffs and three landings on a single solo flight (or with an instructor, who is not a passenger), then the clock resets.

## §61.57(b) -- Night passenger currency

```text
Except as provided in paragraph (e) of this section, no person may act as
pilot in command of an aircraft carrying passengers during the period
beginning 1 hour after sunset and ending 1 hour before sunrise, unless
within the preceding 90 days that person has made at least 3 takeoffs and
3 landings to a full stop during the period beginning 1 hour after sunset
and ending 1 hour before sunrise, and --

  (i) That person acted as sole manipulator of the flight controls; and

  (ii) The required takeoffs and landings were performed in an aircraft
       of the same category, class, and type (if a type rating is
       required).

Note: paraphrase of §61.57(b). Read the actual reg before referencing in
exam.
```

The structural difference from §61.57(a):

- The window is 1 hour after sunset to 1 hour before sunrise. (Note: this is *not* "civil twilight"; pilots conflate them. The §61.57(b) window is sun-clock-only.)
- The takeoffs and landings must be **to a full stop**. Touch-and-goes do not count for night passenger currency.
- All three of the night T&Ls must be within the 90-day rolling window, similarly to (a).

### The full-stop trap

A pilot who does three night touch-and-goes within 90 days has not satisfied §61.57(b). They've satisfied §61.57(a) (touch-and-go counts as one takeoff and one landing) but not (b). To carry passengers at night they need three night full-stop landings.

A common misconception: "night currency" combines (a) and (b) into a single timer. It does not. They are independent currencies. A pilot can be current for daytime passengers (recent T&Ls during the day) but not night (no recent full-stop landings at night).

## §61.57(c) -- IFR currency

This is "six-six-HIT" -- the most-quoted memory aid in pilot training. We deep-dive in [05-ifr-currency.md](05-ifr-currency.md). The summary:

- Within the **preceding 6 calendar months**:
- **6 instrument approaches**
- **Holding** procedures
- **Intercepting and tracking** courses

If lapsed, the pilot has a **6-month grace** during which they cannot exercise IFR PIC privileges but can re-establish currency by performing the required procedures. Past the grace, an **instrument proficiency check** (IPC) is required.

The grace period is a trap. Once you exit the grace period, you cannot re-establish without an IPC. CFIs see pilots arrive 13 months out of currency convinced they "just need to do six approaches" -- they need an IPC.

## §61.58 -- PIC proficiency check (different thing)

§61.58 applies *only* to pilots acting as PIC of an aircraft that requires more than one pilot under its certification basis -- typically type-rated airplanes. The check is annually in the type, with an additional check every 24 months in another type if the pilot is multi-typed.

For most GA pilots, §61.58 doesn't apply -- they don't fly aircraft requiring more than one pilot. ATP and corporate pilots live under it. The trap is *thinking it does apply* -- a single-pilot Cessna 172 PIC is not subject to §61.58, even though they need §61.56 and §61.57.

## The independence trap

These currencies all run on independent clocks:

| Currency       | Section     | Clock              | Reset event                                  |
| -------------- | ----------- | ------------------ | -------------------------------------------- |
| Flight review  | §61.56      | 24 calendar months | Completion of review or equivalent           |
| Passengers     | §61.57(a)   | 90 days rolling    | Each individual T&L                          |
| Night passengers | §61.57(b) | 90 days rolling    | Each individual full-stop landing            |
| IFR            | §61.57(c)   | 6 calendar months  | Each approach + holding + tracking event     |
| Class B (student) | §61.95   | Endorsement-based  | Re-endorsement (no fixed time)               |
| PIC proficiency | §61.58     | 12 calendar months | Completion of check                          |

A pilot can be:
- Current for §61.56 but not §61.57(a) -- flight review valid, but no recent takeoffs.
- Current for §61.57(a) but not §61.57(c) -- recent T&Ls but no recent approaches.
- Current for §61.57(a) but not §61.57(b) -- recent daytime T&Ls but no night full-stop landings.

The pilot must hit each independent currency requirement that applies to the proposed operation. None of them resets the others.

## Currency does not equal proficiency

§61.56 and §61.57 set a *legal floor*. They do not produce proficiency. Three takeoffs and three landings to a full stop on a clear, calm day in November might be 12 minutes of flying spread across one trip to the practice area. That 12 minutes does not make a pilot proficient to fly a passenger to a 5,000-foot grass strip with 18-knot crosswind on a hazy summer afternoon.

What proficiency requires varies with the flight:

| Flight                                                     | Proficiency standard implies                                                  |
| ---------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| VFR pattern work in an unfamiliar tower-controlled airport | Recent radio work, recent T&Ls, comfort with the airport diagram              |
| 200-nm cross-country VFR with passengers                   | Recent cross-country work, fuel-stop planning, weather assessment, Class C/D  |
| 400-nm cross-country IFR in IMC                            | Recent approaches in IMC, partial-panel familiarity, comfort with ATC routes  |
| Mountainous flight in 8,000-ft density-altitude            | Mountain-flying training, density-altitude awareness, terrain awareness       |
| Float operations on glassy water                           | Float-plane training, glassy-water landing practice, recent float currency    |

The FAA cannot regulate proficiency. The PIC must self-regulate it.

### The personal minimums frame

CFIs commonly counsel pilots to maintain *personal minimums* that exceed the regulatory currency floor. Examples:

- Three takeoffs and landings within 30 days (instead of 90), particularly for less-experienced pilots
- Six approaches *in actual* every six months, not just six approaches in any conditions
- A flight review every 12 months instead of every 24
- A specific currency by airplane type (you've flown the 172 in the last 14 days; you've not flown the Cherokee 6 in 90, so you ride with a CFI before going)

Personal minimums are not regulation. They are the pilot's own judgment. The lesson is to teach pilots to *have* personal minimums and to articulate them.

## Common misreadings

- **"I'm current" without specifying which currency.** Pilots conflate §61.56, §61.57(a), §61.57(b), §61.57(c), §61.58 into a single concept. Each is a separate timer.
- **"Touch-and-goes count for night currency."** Wrong. §61.57(b) requires full-stop landings.
- **"Civil twilight is the night currency window."** Wrong. The §61.57(b) window is sun-clock-only -- 1 hour after sunset to 1 hour before sunrise. Civil twilight ends 30 minutes after sunset (or starts 30 minutes before sunrise) by definition; it doesn't appear in §61.57.
- **"Passenger currency in a 172 covers me to fly the friend's Cherokee 6."** Wrong if the Cherokee 6 is in a different *type* of certification (it's not, here -- both are airplane single-engine land), but partially right that they're both ASEL. The trap arises with multiengine, type-rated, or tailwheel airplanes -- a SEL pilot is not current to act as PIC carrying passengers in a multiengine, even with recent SEL T&Ls.
- **"Currency equals proficiency."** No. Currency is the legal floor; proficiency is the operational standard. The FAA writes the floor; the pilot writes the standard.
- **"The FAA grants a grace period for lapsed currency."** Only for IFR (§61.57(d)). There is no grace period for §61.57(a) or §61.57(b). If the pilot is past 90 days, they cannot carry passengers until they fly again.

## Drills

### Locate the section

| Question                                                                        | Section            |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------ |
| Where is the flight review requirement?                                          | §61.56             |
| Where is daytime passenger currency?                                             | §61.57(a)          |
| Where is night passenger currency?                                               | §61.57(b)          |
| Where is IFR currency?                                                           | §61.57(c)          |
| Where is the IFR currency grace period?                                          | §61.57(d)          |
| Where is the PIC proficiency check (multi-pilot type-rated aircraft)?            | §61.58             |
| Where is the flight review *equivalent* (practical test counts)?                | §61.56(d)          |

### Apply the rules

> A 200-hour PPL flew three takeoffs and landings on March 1. Today is May 30. Is this pilot legal to take a friend up?

Answer: Day is March 1 -> May 30 is 90 days exactly (not "within the preceding 90"). Depending on time of day, this pilot is right at or just past the cutoff. They should fly three more takeoffs and landings before the trip with the friend. The reg says "within the preceding 90 days," which means strictly less than 90 in nearly every reading. Safer answer: no, not legal.

> A pilot did three night touch-and-goes on April 1. They want to take a passenger flying tomorrow night, May 15. Are they current?

Answer: For §61.57(a) -- yes (touch-and-goes count as T&L for daytime passenger currency, and we're within 90 days). For §61.57(b) -- no. Touch-and-goes don't count for night currency. Three full-stop night landings within 90 days are required.

> A pilot completed an instrument proficiency check on January 15, 2024. Last instrument-flight currency event: April 10, 2026 (three approaches in actual). Today is November 5, 2026. Are they current for IFR?

Answer: Last currency event was 209 days ago (or roughly seven calendar months). They're past the 6-month bar (April 10 -> October 10 was the deadline) and into the §61.57(d) grace period. They cannot exercise IFR PIC privileges currently. They have until April 10, 2027 (12 calendar months from the last currency event) to re-establish via §61.57(c) procedures; after that an IPC is required.

> An ATP-rated pilot acting as captain of a Citation Mustang (single-pilot type-rated) is two months out from their last §61.58 check. Are they legal?

Answer: A Citation Mustang has a single-pilot certification basis, so §61.58 (which applies only to airplanes *requiring* more than one pilot) does not apply. The pilot needs §61.56 (flight review every 24 months) and §61.57 if exercising PIC privileges with passengers. §61.58 is for airplanes requiring two pilots in their cert basis.
