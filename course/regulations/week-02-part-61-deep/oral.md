# Week 2 oral -- "Walk me through Part 61"

## The question

> "Walk me through Part 61. I want to see the structural map, the parallel hour requirements across cert levels, and how you keep currency, recency, and proficiency separate when you're advising a pilot."

This oral integrates all three Week 2 lessons. It tests whether the structural map of Part 61 is in your head, whether you can use it to diagnose a pilot's gap in seconds, and whether you keep three commonly-confused concepts (currency, recency, proficiency) cleanly separated.

## What this is testing

- Whether you can name Part 61's subparts and predict which subpart owns a given question
- Whether you understand the parallel structure across cert-level subparts (E private, F commercial, G ATP) and can recall the topline aero-experience numbers cold
- Whether you keep currency ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/58?at=2026)), recency ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)), and proficiency (not regulatory) cleanly separated
- Whether you locate the most-cited sections ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/58?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026)) without scanning a table of contents
- Whether you give advice that diagnoses against sub-requirements, not against totals

## Model answer (full walkthrough)

A defensible answer takes about 12-15 minutes spoken.

### Stage 1: The frame (one sentence)

> "Part 61 is the pilot subchapter -- it answers 'who can fly?' Subparts A through D are general and special-population: A general rules, B aircraft ratings, C student pilots, D recreational. Subparts E through G are the cert-level subparts, and they rhyme: E for private, F for commercial, G for ATP. Each cert subpart has the same anatomy -- eligibility, aero-experience, training requirements, privileges and limitations -- so if I learn the pattern once for private, I can navigate commercial and ATP by analogy. Subpart H is CFIs -- my home for the next year. I, J, K cover light-sport and special cases."

This opening earns trust. You named the part, the structural pattern, and identified your home as a CFI candidate.

### Stage 2: The most-cited sections (the hot list)

> "Five sections come up over and over: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026) for logging, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) for the flight review, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) for recency, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/58?at=2026) for the PIC proficiency check, and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026) for private pilot privileges. If a question is about a working pilot's day-to-day legality, it's almost certainly in one of those five. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026) is the endorsement section -- high-performance, complex, tailwheel, high-altitude. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) is the part's dictionary -- definitions of cross-country, complex, TAA, etc."

Demonstrates that the structural map has the high-yield sections marked.

### Stage 3: The parallel hour requirements

> "[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/109?at=2026) is private aero-experience. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/129?at=2026) is commercial. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/159?at=2026) is ATP. They're written as *lists of sub-requirements*, not as totals. The total-time number is the floor; the sub-requirements almost always bind first. For private: 40 hours total, 20 with an instructor of which 3 instrument and 3 night, 10 solo of which 5 cross-country plus a long XC, 3 instrument, 3 hours within 2 calendar months of the practical. For commercial: 250 total, 100 PIC of which 50 XC, 10 instrument training, 10 in a complex/TAA/turbine, day VFR XC at least 100 nm with 3 landings, night VFR XC at least 100 nm with 3 landings, 5 night, 3 instrument within 60 days of the practical, 10 solo or performing the duties of PIC. For ATP: 1,500 hr with the carve-outs in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/160?at=2026) -- 1,000 military, 1,250 four-year aviation degree from a 141 program, 1,500 otherwise. The cross-country *definition* changes for the instrument rating: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) gives 50 nm with a landing at a non-departure airport instead of the standard 50 nm to a different point."

The hour numbers and the rhyme between cert levels are the high-yield content. The instrument-XC distinction is the most common trap.

### Stage 4: Currency, recency, proficiency

> "These three words get mixed up constantly, but they answer different questions:
>
> **Currency** is the regulatory window for a privilege. The flight review under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) every 24 calendar months. The annual PIC proficiency check under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/58?at=2026) for large or turbojet airplanes. Currency answers 'is this pilot legally allowed to act as PIC at all?'
>
> **Recency** is the rolling-window rules in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026). 3 takeoffs and landings in the preceding 90 days for daytime passenger ops. 3 *full-stop* landings *at night* for nighttime passenger ops. The 6-6-HIT for IFR. Recency answers 'is this pilot legally allowed to do *this specific operation*?' A pilot can be current ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) flight review valid) but not recent (no night landings) and therefore unable to take pax at night.
>
> **Proficiency** is whether the pilot can actually fly the mission. The regulations don't define it. Currency-and-recency-passing is the floor; proficiency is the ceiling. A pilot who hasn't flown the airplane in 6 months is current and recent and probably not proficient. A working CFI who advises only against the regulatory test is being negligent -- the regulatory test is an *insufficient* test for whether the flight is safe.
>
> The IFR currency machinery has a wrinkle: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) gives 6 months of currency from the last 6/6/HIT. After that there's a 6-month grace where the pilot can re-establish by completing the 6/6/HIT in actual or simulated instrument conditions with a safety pilot. After the grace expires, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) requires an IPC."

Cleanly separates the three concepts, names the sections, and articulates the proficiency ceiling -- which is the part most pilots miss.

### Stage 5: Endorsements and the §61.31 ladder

> "[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026) is where the endorsements live: high-performance ((f) -- more than 200 hp), complex ((e) -- retractable, flaps, controllable prop, with the post-2018 commercial-substitution wrinkle), tailwheel ((i)), high-altitude pressurized ((g) -- service ceiling or max op altitude above FL250), and the type-specific endorsements. AC 61-65 is the *recommended language* for the endorsements -- it's guidance, not regulation, but FSDOs expect the AC 61-65 phrasing. The endorsement requirement is in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026); the recommended wording is in AC 61-65."

Names the canonical AC for endorsements and clarifies regulatory vs. guidance status.

### Stage 6: Privileges and limitations

> "[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026) is private privileges -- the 'no compensation' rule and the cost-sharing carve-out under (c) for pro-rata costs and a common purpose (Mangiamele, 2009 is the canonical interpretation). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/117?at=2026) is the limitations side. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/133?at=2026) is commercial privileges -- which is where the day-VFR-50nm restriction lives for a commercial pilot without an instrument rating. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/193?at=2026) and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026) are CFI privileges and limitations -- relevant to me because [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026) draws the line between when I'm being paid as a pilot ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026) governs) versus as a CFI ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026) governs)."

Demonstrates that the cert-subpart anatomy parallels (privileges + limitations sections in each subpart) and shows familiarity with where common Chief Counsel interpretations land.

### Stage 7: Routing a question through Part 61

> "Given a Part 61 question, I route it: first to a subpart -- which cert level or population? -- then to the section anatomy. Eligibility / aero-experience / privileges-and-limitations is the pattern in every cert-level subpart. So 'can a 200 hr commercial pilot carry passengers for hire across the country?' routes to subpart F (commercial), then to privileges and limitations -- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/133?at=2026) -- and the answer is no, day VFR within 50 nm if the pilot doesn't have an instrument rating. The structure tells me where to look before I look."

Demonstrates the routing skill that the rest of the course depends on.

## Compressed answer (under 90 seconds)

> "Part 61 is the pilot subchapter. Subparts E, F, G are private, commercial, ATP -- they rhyme. Each has eligibility, aero-experience ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/109?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/129?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/159?at=2026)), and privileges and limitations sections. The high-yield sections are [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026) (logging), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) (flight review = currency), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) (recency), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/58?at=2026) (PIC proficiency check for large/turbojet), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026) (private privileges). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026) is endorsements. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) is the dictionary. Currency is the regulatory window ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/58?at=2026)). Recency is the rolling-window for specific operations ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)). Proficiency is the ceiling -- not regulatory, but the test the CFI's *advice* should meet."

## What goes wrong (failure modes)

| Failure mode                                                              | Diagnosis |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------- |
| Recites the subparts as a list with no anatomy                            | Misses the rhyme; suggests memorization without comprehension |
| Cites only the total-hour number for a cert level                         | Misses that the sub-requirements bind first; gives bad advice |
| Says "currency" when meaning "recency"                                    | Loses the analytical separation that makes Part 61 navigable |
| Forgets the night recency uses *full-stop* landings                       | High-yield checkride trap -- the regulation is specific |
| Says cross-country always means 50 nm                                     | Different cross-country definitions for instrument vs. other ratings; [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) has multiple |
| Confuses the IFR 6-month currency window with the 6-month grace           | Two different windows. Currency = first 6. Grace = next 6 with safety pilot. After grace = IPC. |
| Recites the AC 61-65 endorsement language as if it's a regulation         | AC 61-65 is guidance. The endorsement requirement is [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026). |
| Treats the §61.58 PIC proficiency check as universal                      | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/58?at=2026) is for large or turbojet airplanes only. Most GA pilots don't touch it. |
| Says "the regulations make a pilot proficient"                            | Currency and recency are *legal* tests. Proficiency is a *practical* test. The CFI's job is to advise to the proficiency ceiling, not the legal floor. |
| Doesn't know the R-ATP carve-out                                          | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/160?at=2026) is high-yield for any pilot career conversation -- 1000 / 1250 / 1500 |
| Says "[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026) lets me cost-share so anyone can pay for the flight" | Wrong. Pro rata + common purpose. Mangiamele (2009) is the canonical interpretation. |

## Variant prompts

| Variant                                                                          | What changes |
| -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------ |
| "Walk me through Part 61 from a brand-new student pilot's perspective"           | Lead with subpart C (student pilots), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/87?at=2026) solo limits and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/93?at=2026) solo cross-country; lighten on commercial/ATP |
| "Walk me through Part 61 from a CFI candidate's perspective"                     | Same as model answer; subpart H privileges and limitations are the centerpiece |
| "Walk me through Part 61 from a returning pilot's perspective (lapsed currency)" | Lead with [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) flight review and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) recency; explain the proficiency ceiling and why the regs alone are insufficient |
| "How does Part 61 relate to Part 91?"                                            | Part 61 = the pilot. Part 91 = the flight. They stack -- both apply to every flight. The pilot question is "can this person fly?" The flight question is "is this flight legal?" |
| "How does Part 61 relate to Part 141?"                                           | Part 61 sets the *pilot* requirements. Part 141 sets *the school* requirements. A 141 student is still subject to Part 61 minimums (the 141 syllabus mins can be lower for some hour requirements, but the cert eligibility under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/103?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/123?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/151?at=2026) still binds). |
