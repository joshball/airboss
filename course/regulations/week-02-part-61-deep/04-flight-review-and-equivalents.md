# Flight review and equivalents

The flight review is the most-tracked, most-missed currency item in general aviation. Every working pilot owes one every 24 calendar months, but the regulation is short, the equivalents are wide, and the operational practice varies more than most pilots realize.

This lesson is the deep-dive promised in [03-currency-vs-recency-vs-proficiency.md](03-currency-vs-recency-vs-proficiency.md). We unpack §61.56 paragraph-by-paragraph and walk every equivalent in §61.56(d) through §61.56(g).

## What you'll be able to do

- State the §61.56 requirement cold, including the 24-calendar-month math and what an "authorized instructor" means here
- Recognize each equivalent in §61.56(d)/(e)/(f)/(g) and predict which one applies to a given pilot
- Apply AC 61-98 guidance on what a flight review *should* contain without confusing guidance with regulation
- Counsel a returning pilot through the difference between satisfying §61.56 and being proficient

## Why this matters

A pilot who lapses on the flight review is *not legally PIC*. Not "rusty," not "not current for passengers," not "needs an IPC." Past 24 calendar months from the last flight review (or equivalent), the pilot may not act as PIC of any aircraft, period. This is one of the few cases where a clean audit trail in the logbook is the only thing standing between a pilot and a regulatory violation that ends with a 91.13 charge.

CFIs are the only people who can sign a flight review for most aircraft, which means CFIs need to know §61.56 cold -- not because checkride examiners ask about it but because every renewing pilot in the airport will, sooner or later, ask for one.

## The discovery question

A pilot you've never met walks up to you at the FBO and says: "I'm thinking of buying a Bonanza. Last logbook entry from anyone signing off currency was October 2023. Today is April 2026. Can I rent a 172 today and go fly?"

Pause.

October 2023 -> April 2026 is 30 calendar months. The pilot is 6 months past their flight review. They cannot legally act as PIC. They need:

1. A flight review under §61.56 (or equivalent under (d)/(e)/(f)/(g)) before exercising any PIC privilege.
2. Three takeoffs and landings under §61.57(a) within the preceding 90 days before carrying passengers.
3. Three night full-stop landings under §61.57(b) if they want to carry passengers at night.
4. Six-six-HIT under §61.57(c) within the preceding 6 calendar months if they want to fly IFR -- and they're well past that window already too. Likely needs an IPC.

The flight review is the *first* of those gates. Without it, none of the others matter.

This is not a hypothetical. Returning pilots show up at the airport about every other week. The pilot in this story is the typical case, not the edge case.

## What §61.56 actually says

§61.56 is short. Read it directly; here is the structural paraphrase to anchor the reading.

```text
§61.56  Flight review.

(a) Except as provided in paragraphs (b) and (f) of this section,
    a flight review consists of a minimum of 1 hour of flight training
    and 1 hour of ground training. The review must include --

    (1) A review of the current general operating and flight rules
        of part 91; and

    (2) A review of those maneuvers and procedures that, at the
        discretion of the person giving the review, are necessary for
        the pilot to demonstrate the safe exercise of the privileges
        of the pilot certificate.

(b) (glider exception, balloon exception)

(c) Except as provided in paragraphs (d), (e), (f), and (g) of this
    section, no person may act as pilot in command of an aircraft unless,
    since the beginning of the 24th calendar month before the month in
    which that pilot acts as pilot in command of an aircraft, that
    person has --

    (1) Accomplished a flight review given in an aircraft for which
        that pilot is rated by an authorized instructor; and

    (2) A logbook endorsement from an authorized instructor who gave
        the review certifying that the person has satisfactorily
        completed the review.

(d) (practical test equivalent)
(e) (sport-pilot proficiency check equivalent)
(f) (Wings phase equivalent -- 3 phases substitute)
(g) (military competency check equivalent)

(h) (rotorcraft autorotation requirement, since 2018)

Note: paraphrase of §61.56. Read the actual reg before referencing in
exam.
```

Five things to extract from the literal text:

1. **One hour ground + one hour flight.** That's the floor. The reg doesn't cap the upper bound -- "minimum." A pilot who hasn't flown in three years takes a *much* longer flight review in practice; the regulation just says one hour minimum.
2. **The flight portion has to happen "in an aircraft for which that pilot is rated."** A glider-only pilot does the flight portion in a glider. An ASEL pilot can do it in any ASEL airplane. A pilot who holds both ASEL and AMEL can choose. The trap: a CFI giving an instrument-airplane check ride wouldn't count for an ASEL flight review unless the IFR check was conducted in an airplane the pilot is rated for *and* the CFI specifically signs a flight review endorsement (which would be redundant with the IPC endorsement).
3. **"Authorized instructor."** Defined in §61.1(b) as a person who holds a current flight instructor certificate, ground instructor certificate (for the ground portion only and only specific cases), or specific other authorities. For the typical airplane flight review, the signing party is a CFI-A.
4. **"Logbook endorsement."** AC 61-65 publishes the canonical wording (Endorsement A.1 in current revisions). The endorsement specifies the date, the CFI's name and certificate number, the certificate-and-rating it covers, and a statement that the review was satisfactorily completed.
5. **Reset event.** Successfully completing the review (with the endorsement in the book) starts the new 24-calendar-month clock. The 24 months count from the *first day of the month after* the review, so a flight review on March 15, 2026 is good through March 31, 2028 -- the back-end calendar month is free.

## What it actually means -- AC 61-98 and the working-CFI flight review

§61.56's text is one hour and one hour, period. AC 61-98 (Currency Requirements and Guidance for the Flight Review and Instrument Proficiency Check) supplies the *recommended content* and is treated by FSDOs as the de facto standard.

AC 61-98D (current as of 2026) recommends a flight review structure that aligns with the [WINGS Pilot Proficiency Program](https://www.faasafety.gov/wings) phases. Concretely:

| Element                          | Recommended (AC 61-98)                                                              |
| -------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Pre-review pilot self-assessment | A short questionnaire identifying weak areas the pilot wants to focus on            |
| Ground portion                   | Part 91 review, weather, performance, airspace updates, accident-trend conversation |
| Flight portion (preflight)       | Risk management briefing, go/no-go discussion                                       |
| Flight portion (basic maneuvers) | Slow flight, stalls, steep turns, ground reference                                  |
| Flight portion (emergency)       | Engine-out, lost-comm, system failure                                               |
| Flight portion (instrument)      | If the pilot is instrument-rated, partial-panel and unusual attitude recovery       |
| Post-flight                      | Debrief, areas for continued study, the endorsement                                 |
| Recommended duration             | 2-3 hours total when AC 61-98 elements are honored                                  |

AC 61-98 is *guidance*. The regulation requires only 1+1. A CFI who signs a 1+1 review meeting only the §61.56(a) elements is technically compliant. A CFI who signs a 1+1 review for a 30-month-lapsed pilot in a Cirrus they've never flown before is technically compliant *and* irresponsible. The AC tells the working CFI what compliance plus competence looks like.

### The "satisfactorily completed" gate

§61.56(c)(2) requires the endorsement state "satisfactorily completed." If the CFI is not satisfied with the review, they do not sign. The pilot continues the review until the CFI is satisfied (potentially over multiple sessions on different days), or the pilot finds a different CFI willing to sign.

The CFI's signature is a professional judgment. The Hicks (2010) Chief Counsel letter on §61.51(e) clarifies that a CFI's signature certifies the review was satisfactory in the CFI's judgment; if the CFI later determines the pilot was not actually proficient, the CFI's only remedy is to refuse to sign future endorsements -- not to retroactively withdraw the prior one.

A CFI who signs a flight review endorsement for a pilot who is dangerous can become exposed to FAA enforcement under §61.197 (CFI privileges) for failing to ensure the pilot can safely exercise privileges. This is the *correct* pressure point: the CFI is the gatekeeper.

## Where it sits

§61.56 lives in subpart B (Aircraft Ratings and Pilot Authorizations), between §61.51 (logging) and §61.57 (recency). The subpart B order is:


Sitting next to §61.51 (logging) makes the logbook-endorsement requirement structural: the flight review is the second of the "what you've done counts as what" sections, and the §61.51(b) entry requirements feed directly into the §61.56(c)(2) endorsement. The AC 61-65 endorsement language for the flight review (Endorsement A.1) is the recommended boilerplate.

## The equivalents -- §61.56(d), (e), (f), (g)

Most pilots know the 24-month rule. Fewer know that §61.56 has *four* equivalents, any of which substitutes for the 1+1 review and resets the 24-month clock.

### §61.56(d) -- Practical test in same category and class

> A person who has, within the period specified in paragraph (c) of this
> section, passed a pilot proficiency check conducted by an examiner, an
> approved pilot check airman, or a U.S. Armed Force, for a pilot
> certificate, rating, or operating privilege need not accomplish the
> flight review required by this section.

In practice, this means: any FAA practical test (ASEL add-on, instrument rating, commercial, ATP, type rating) in the same category and class as the cert the pilot is exercising counts as a flight review. A pilot who earns their instrument rating on March 1, 2025 is good on the flight review until March 31, 2027 -- the practical test counted as the flight review for the underlying ASEL.

The trap: the practical test must be in the *same category and class* the pilot wants to exercise. A pilot who earns their AMEL add-on does not refresh the ASEL flight review by virtue of the AMEL practical -- different class. The reverse is also true.

A practical test that *fails* does not count. §61.56(d) requires a pilot who "passed."

### §61.56(e) -- Sport-pilot proficiency check

A sport-pilot proficiency check under §61.309 is the equivalent for sport pilots. Niche; mentioned for completeness.

### §61.56(f) -- WINGS phase

> A person who has, within the period specified in paragraph (c) of this
> section, accomplished one or more phases of an FAA-sponsored pilot
> proficiency award program need not accomplish the flight review
> required by this section.

The FAA's Pilot Proficiency Program (WINGS), administered through faasafety.gov, awards "phases" for completing combinations of online courses (knowledge credits) and flight activities with a CFI (flight credits). Three different credit levels: Basic, Advanced, Master. Completing one phase at any level satisfies §61.56(f) for the next 24 calendar months.

The mechanics:

1. Pilot logs in at faasafety.gov, picks topics, completes courses online (each credit certificate is dated).
2. Pilot logs flight activities (specific to phase requirements -- typically each level requires three flight activities like takeoff/landing currency, IFR procedures, etc.) with a CFI who validates them in WINGS.
3. When the pilot has both knowledge and flight credits for a phase, the system awards the phase, and the pilot can print the WINGS phase certificate.
4. The phase award date starts the 24-calendar-month clock.

The AC 61-91 series documents the WINGS program. AC 61-91J (2017) is the most recent comprehensive version.

The advantage of WINGS over a traditional flight review: the pilot self-paces, can spread training across multiple CFIs, and accumulates a documented training record beyond a single endorsement.

The trap: WINGS requires *both* knowledge credits and flight credits to award a phase. A pilot who finishes the online courses but never books the flight activities is not done. A pilot who books the flight activities but skips the online courses is also not done. Each phase is a complete unit.

### §61.56(g) -- Military competency check

> A person who has, within the period specified in paragraph (c) of this
> section, completed a military competency flight check ... need not
> accomplish the flight review required by this section.

For active-duty military pilots, the annual military proficiency check counts as a flight review. Civilian-track ATP pilots flying for the airlines do *not* automatically benefit -- airline proficiency checks are conducted under Part 121, not military authority. (Part 121 PCs do, however, count as §61.58 PIC proficiency checks for the type-rated airplane.)

## Calendar-month math, in detail

§61.56(c) uses "the 24th calendar month before the month in which that pilot acts as pilot in command." Translated:

- A flight review accomplished any time in October 2025 is good through October 31, 2027.
- A flight review accomplished any time in March 2026 is good through March 31, 2028.

The pilot gets the back-end calendar month for free. A pilot who completes their review on March 31, 2026 has the same expiration date as a pilot who completes it on March 1, 2026.

This back-end-month gift is consistent across the calendar-month timers in Part 61: §61.57(c) (IFR currency) uses the same "preceding 6 calendar months" math, §61.58 (PIC proficiency check) uses "preceding 12 calendar months," and so on.

The other math trap: a pilot whose previous review was March 1, 2024 and who flies the new review on April 5, 2026 (35 days late, technically lapsed for 35 days) gets *the new clock starting from April 2026*, not from March 2024. A late review still resets the clock; the lapse is what creates the §61.56-violation window between the expiration date and the new review date.

If the pilot acted as PIC during the lapse window, that PIC time is a §61.56(c) violation (and likely a §91.13 catch-all violation). The clock-reset on the new review doesn't backdate the violation.

## §61.56(h) -- the autorotation rule (for rotorcraft)

Added in 2018. Helicopter pilots performing a flight review must include at least one practice autorotation, regardless of the CFI's discretion under §61.56(a)(2). The reg recognizes that the helicopter community had been signing 1+1 reviews that omitted autorotations, leading to skill atrophy on a maneuver that is occasionally life-saving.

For airplane pilots, the analog reflection is: nothing in §61.56 *requires* a stall practice or an emergency descent on the flight review. The CFI's discretion governs. Working CFIs include both anyway. AC 61-98 recommends them.

## Common misreadings

- **"A flight review is just one hour of dual."** Wrong. §61.56(a) requires *both* one hour of flight training *and* one hour of ground training. A pilot who flew 1.5 hours with a CFI but received no formal ground portion has not received a §61.56-compliant review.
- **"Anyone who can sign endorsements can sign a flight review."** Mostly right, with a wrinkle. The regulation says "authorized instructor" (§61.56(c)(1)). For airplane reviews, this is a CFI-A (or a CFI-A who also holds the rating being reviewed -- a glider review needs a CFI-G, etc.). A ground instructor cannot sign a flight review (no flight portion authority). A CFI whose own renewal has lapsed cannot sign endorsements -- check the CFI's expiration date before flying with them for a review.
- **"A practical test always counts as a flight review."** Wrong. Only practical tests in the same category and class as the cert being exercised. An AMEL practical does not refresh the ASEL flight review.
- **"The WINGS program is just optional fun."** Wrong. §61.56(f) gives WINGS phase completion the same regulatory force as a traditional flight review. Many CFIs prefer to log students through WINGS because the knowledge credits are documented and the structure encourages broader topic coverage.
- **"The 24-month clock starts on the day of the review."** Half-true. The 24-calendar-month math means the clock starts the *first day of the month after* the review, and the review is good through the last day of the *24th month after*. Pilots and CFIs commonly miscompute by 30 days in either direction.
- **"AC 61-98 is regulation."** Wrong. AC 61-98 is guidance. It tells you what the FAA *recommends*. The regulation is §61.56. A 1+1 review meeting §61.56(a) is compliant even if it omits everything in AC 61-98. (Whether such a review is *responsible* is the CFI's professional judgment.)
- **"A pilot can act as PIC during a lapsed flight review window if no passengers are aboard."** Wrong. §61.56(c) prohibits acting as PIC of an aircraft, full stop, regardless of passenger status. Solo flying past the 24-month deadline is still a §61.56 violation.

### Apply the rules

> A pilot's last flight review is dated October 12, 2024. The pilot took an instrument rating practical test on June 5, 2025 (passed). Today is November 8, 2026. Is the pilot current on the flight review?

Answer: Yes. The instrument rating practical test on June 5, 2025 counted as a flight review under §61.56(d) (same category and class, ASEL). New 24-calendar-month clock from June 5, 2025; good through June 30, 2027. As of November 8, 2026 the pilot is well within the window.

> A pilot completed two WINGS Basic-level phases in 2024 and one Advanced phase on March 15, 2026. Are they current on the flight review as of August 2026?

Answer: Yes. §61.56(f) treats *any* phase award as equivalent to a flight review. The Advanced phase on March 15, 2026 starts a new 24-calendar-month clock; good through March 31, 2028.

> A pilot's last flight review was on April 1, 2024. They have not flown since. They want to rent and fly today, December 1, 2026. Walk through the regulatory steps before they can act as PIC.

Answer: The April 1, 2024 review was good through April 30, 2026. The pilot is 7 months lapsed. Required:

1. §61.56 flight review (or equivalent). The pilot needs 1+1 minimum with a CFI in an aircraft they're rated for. The CFI signs the endorsement; the new 24-month clock starts.
2. §61.57(a) three takeoffs and landings within 90 days. Likely accomplished during the flight review itself if the CFI plans the review to include three full-stop landings.
3. §61.57(b) night currency if the pilot wants to carry passengers at night. Three night full-stop landings within 90 days.
4. §61.57(c) IFR currency review separately if the pilot is instrument-rated and wants to fly IFR. They are also more than 12 calendar months past their IFR currency, so they need an IPC. AC 61-98 covers the IPC content; the IPC and the flight review can be combined into a single training event in many cases.

> A pilot's CFI signed a flight review endorsement on June 1, 2025. The CFI's CFI certificate had expired on April 30, 2025 (the CFI was 32 days lapsed when the endorsement was signed). Is the pilot's flight review valid?

Answer: No. §61.56(c) requires "an authorized instructor." A CFI whose certificate has lapsed is not currently an authorized instructor. The endorsement is invalid. The pilot is *not* current. They need to either re-fly the review with a current CFI or find another route to a valid endorsement (a practical test or a WINGS phase), and the actual lapse window for the pilot is from when the prior valid endorsement expired through whenever a valid one is obtained. CFIs occasionally renew on time but sign endorsements during the gap before the new certificate arrives in the mail; the §61.197(b) "valid for 24 calendar months from the month of issue" rule means the endorsement signed during the gap is invalid even if the renewal practical test happened the same week. CFIs check their own expiration before signing, and pilots check the CFI's certificate before flying.
