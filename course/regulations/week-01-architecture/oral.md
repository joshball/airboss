# Week 1 oral -- "Walk me through Title 14"

## The question

> "I'm new to flight training. Walk me through Title 14 like you're showing it to me from the outside. What are the parts I'll care about, what does each one do, and how would I find a regulation if I had a question?"

This is a foundation oral. It tests whether the structural map is in your head -- nothing more, nothing less. No specific regulation needs to be recalled. The skill being tested is *the navigation framework*.

## What this is testing

- Whether you can name Title 14's structure without reciting the eCFR's table of contents
- Whether you have the three-subject framing internalized ("the pilot / the flight / the operation")
- Whether you can route a question to the right Part in seconds
- Whether you know the major non-Title-14 documents you'll still need (NTSB Part 830, AIM, ACs)

## Model answer (full walkthrough)

A defensible answer takes about 8-12 minutes spoken. The structure:

### Stage 1: The frame (one sentence)

> "Title 14 is the FAA's section of the Code of Federal Regulations. Pilots live mostly in Subchapters D, F, and H -- the airmen, the operating rules, and the schools. Three subjects organize everything: the pilot, the flight, and the operation."

This opening earns trust. You've named the structural reality, the subset that matters to pilots, and the framing that organizes the rest.

### Stage 2: The pilot (Part 61, with Part 67 / Part 68 nearby)

> "The pilot lives in Part 61. That's certificates, ratings, currency, endorsements -- anything about who can fly. Medical lives next door in Part 67, with BasicMed in Part 68 plus 49 USC 44703 as the underlying authority. Part 61 has subparts organized roughly by certificate level: A through D are general, E is private, F is commercial, G is ATP, H is the CFI subpart -- the one I'm becoming -- and I, J, K cover light-sport, foreign, and other special cases."

Hits Part 61, Part 67, BasicMed, the structural pattern of subparts, and identifies the CFI subpart as your home.

### Stage 3: The flight (Part 91, the floor of everything)

> "The flight lives in Part 91. This is the part that applies to *every* civil flight in U.S. airspace, always. Subpart A is general -- 91.3 is PIC authority, 91.13 is the careless or reckless catch-all. Subpart B is flight rules: 91.103 preflight, 91.155 VFR weather minimums, 91.167 / 91.169 / 91.171 IFR fuel and alternates, 91.211 oxygen. Subpart C is equipment -- 91.205 is the ATOMATOFLAMES list and the cumulative VFR-night and IFR additions, 91.213 is the no-MEL pathway when something is broken. Subpart D is special operations: aerobatic, parachute, towing. Subpart E is maintenance and inspection: 91.405 PIC airworthiness responsibility, 91.409 annual and 100-hour inspections, 91.411 altimeter / static IFR, 91.413 transponder. Subparts F through N get progressively more specialized -- large turbine, RVSM, foreign aircraft, fractional. I name them; I don't memorize them."

The "I name them; I don't memorize them" line is deliberate. It tells the examiner you know about the existence of subparts F-N without claiming false depth.

### Stage 4: The operation (Parts 119, 121, 125, 135, 141, 142, 145)

> "The operation lives in the commercial-operating Parts. Part 119 is the certification framework that air carriers and commercial operators hang off of. Part 121 is airline operations -- domestic, flag, supplemental. Part 125 is for airplanes 6,000 pounds and up not in the airline regime, niche. Part 135 is on-demand commercial -- charter, air taxi -- and is what most of my future commercial students will move into. Part 141 is structured pilot schools, with appendices A through N spelling out the syllabi for each cert level. Part 142 is training centers -- type rating shops. Part 145 is repair stations, where overhauls happen. The crucial thing about all these: they *add* requirements on top of Part 91, they don't replace it. A 135 charter still complies with 91.103 *and* 135.213. A 141 student still complies with 91.155 *and* 141 syllabus mins. The stricter rule wins when both apply."

This stage demonstrates the cumulative-stack mental model -- which is where most pilots stumble. Stating it clearly earns extra credit.

### Stage 5: The companion documents

> "Title 14 isn't the whole regulatory ecosystem. AIM is FAA-published expected practice -- not regulation, but enforceable indirectly via 91.13 if deviation creates a hazard. Advisory Circulars are FAA-published guidance, numbered to mirror CFR Parts: AC 61-65 covers endorsements, AC 91-92 covers preflight briefing, and so on. Chief Counsel letters interpret regulations when meaning is contested -- Mangiamele on 61.113 cost sharing, Hicks on PIC logging, Walker on 91.103's 'all available information.' NTSB Board orders are precedent that binds the FAA in enforcement. NTSB Part 830 -- the accident and incident reporting rule -- lives in *Title 49*, not Title 14, because the NTSB is institutionally separate from the FAA. And 49 CFR 1552 is TSA's flight-training rule for foreign students."

Names the document categories with examples; flags the titles for the NTSB and TSA rules.

### Stage 6: Routing a question

> "If you give me a question, I route it through three subjects first: is this about the pilot, the flight, or the operation? That tells me which Part to consult. 'Can this person fly?' is a pilot question -- 61. 'Is this airplane equipped right?' is a flight question -- 91 subpart C. 'Does the school have to be 141?' is an operation question. Most non-trivial questions hit two or three subjects. For example, 'I'm flying tomorrow IFR at night with a passenger' -- I walk pilot first to confirm currency in 61.57, then flight to walk preflight, equipment, fuel, alternates, inspections, oxygen across 91. Pilot first, flight second. If anything's bad on the pilot side, I don't bother with the rest until I've fixed it."

Demonstrates the routing skill that the rest of the course depends on.

## Compressed answer (under 90 seconds)

For an examiner who wants the integration without the deep dive:

> "Title 14 is the FAA's section of the CFR. Pilots live in **Part 61** for certification and currency, **Part 67 / Part 68** for medical and BasicMed, **Part 91** for every flight's operating rules and equipment and inspections, and the commercial-operating Parts -- **141** for schools, **135** for charters, **121** for airlines, **119** as their certification framework, **145** for repair stations. The framing: **the pilot** (61), **the flight** (91), **the operation** (141 / 135 / 121). Part 91 is the floor; commercial Parts stack on top. Companion documents: the **AIM** is expected practice, **ACs** are guidance, **Chief Counsel letters** interpret, **NTSB Board orders** are precedent. **49 CFR 830** -- accident reporting -- lives outside Title 14 because the NTSB is a separate agency. To find any rule: route the question to one of the three subjects, then go to the matching Part."

## What goes wrong (failure modes)

| Failure mode                                     | Diagnosis |
| ------------------------------------------------ | --------- |
| Recites Title 14 chapter and subchapter list     | Comes across as memorized table-of-contents; doesn't show navigation skill |
| Says "Part 91 is for non-commercial flying"      | Misses the cumulative-stack mental model. Part 91 is the floor for *all* civil flight. |
| Cites only the AIM for ATC procedures            | Misses that AIM is non-regulatory; can't articulate the 91.13 enforcement path |
| Forgets BasicMed lives in 49 USC, not 14 CFR     | Common gap. Part 68 is in the CFR but the underlying authority is 49 USC 44703. |
| Locates NTSB rules in Title 14                   | The accident-reporting rule lives at 49 CFR 830. Mistaking the title is mistaking the agency. |
| Names parts without describing them              | "61, 91, 141, 135, 121" with no scope -- looks like memorization without comprehension |
| Routes "I'm flying IFR tonight" to a single part | Cross-cutting questions need a sequence. Pilot first, flight second. Walking only Part 91 misses the currency check in 61. |
| Confuses "Subchapter F" (Part 91 lives here) with "Subpart F" of Part 91 | Different levels of granularity. Be precise. |

## Variant prompts

| Variant                                                                          | What changes |
| -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------ |
| "Walk me through Title 14 from the perspective of a brand-new student pilot."     | Drop CFI subpart H emphasis, add Part 61 subpart C (student pilot rules), emphasize Part 67 medical because they're getting their first one |
| "Walk me through Title 14 from the perspective of a CFI candidate."               | Same as model answer; the CFI subpart H is the centerpiece |
| "Walk me through Title 14 like you're explaining it to your check airman who's been an airline pilot for 30 years and isn't familiar with GA regulations." | Emphasize Part 91 as the GA floor, contrast with Part 121 they know, and point out that 91 subpart D special ops are unfamiliar to airline pilots |
| "I'm a former Part 121 pilot returning to GA. What's the structural difference?"  | Same content, but emphasize Part 91 vs Part 121 contrast: 91 has no chief pilot, no ops specs, no MEL by default, but 91.13 and 91.103 still apply |
| "Show me Title 14 like you'd show it to a non-pilot who wants to understand how aviation is regulated." | Drop the technical vocabulary, lead with "the FAA writes and enforces these rules; the NTSB investigates after something goes wrong; airlines and charters operate under stricter rules than private pilots." |
