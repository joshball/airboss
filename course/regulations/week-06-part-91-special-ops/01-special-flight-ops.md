# Special flight ops -- aerobatic, parachute, towing, formation

Most flights are governed by subparts A and B of Part 91. The general operating rules and the flight rules. Once a flight stops being "an airplane going from A to B" and starts being aerobatics, dropping parachutists, towing a glider or a banner, or flying in formation, subpart D adds rules that supersede or augment the general rules.

Subpart D is short. It is also where pilots get tripped up because the rules read like exceptions to subpart B but actually function as overlays. A formation flight is *not* exempt from right-of-way rules; it has its own. An aerobatic flight is not exempt from minimum altitude rules; it has tighter ones. Knowing where each special-op rule lives, and knowing what it modifies, is the substance of this lesson.

## What you'll be able to do

- State the four restrictions on aerobatic flight under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026) cold (location, altitude, visibility, persons aboard)
- Cite [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026) parachute rules including the parachute-pack-and-inspection cycle
- Distinguish [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/309?at=2026) (towing gliders / unpowered ultralight vehicles) from [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/311?at=2026) (towing anything else, which requires a Letter of Authorization)
- Apply [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026) formation flight rules and recognize the consent-of-PIC requirement

## Why this matters

Aerobatic, parachute, and towing operations carry the highest accident rates per flight hour in non-commercial general aviation. The regulations are tight because the operations are unforgiving. A pilot who flies aerobatics under the minimum altitude has no recovery margin if anything goes wrong; a pilot who tows without the right tow-pilot endorsement and a Letter of Authorization for non-glider tows is operating illegally and uninsured; a parachute jump from an unrigged or out-of-pack-cycle rig is a life-safety violation, not just a paperwork one.

A working CFI does not necessarily fly any of these missions. A working CFI does, however, get asked about them. "Can I do aerobatics over my house?" "Can my buddy throw out a parachute on his birthday?" "Can I tow my friend's banner?" These are the questions, and the answers are in subpart D.

## The discovery question

Imagine four pilots calling you the same week:

1. A 600-hour ASEL pilot wants to do "a little aerobatics" 1,000 feet over an open field on a clear day. No passengers. Legal?
2. A skydiving club asks you to fly the jump plane this Saturday over an uncontrolled airport. The owner says "the parachutes are fine, we've used them all season."
3. A friend asks you to tow their glider. You hold a CFI-A and have 1,500 hours. They have a current glider rating.
4. Two friends with airplanes want to fly to lunch in formation, 200 feet apart, lead and wing.

Pause and predict. What is the answer in each case, and which section of Part 91 owns it?

The answers walk through the subpart-D structure:

1. Aerobatics 1,000 feet AGL is *below* the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026) minimum altitude (1,500 feet AGL above any congested area or open-air assembly is not the right number; the actual rule is no aerobatic flight below 1,500 feet AGL anywhere, period). 1,000 AGL is illegal regardless of who is around. Plus the visibility and location restrictions.
2. Parachute operations are governed by [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026), but the *equipment* rules (pack cycle, the type of parachute, the rigger seal) live there too. A pilot flying jumpers is responsible under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/3?at=2026) for the airworthiness of the operation; if the parachutes are out of pack cycle, the pilot has a direct exposure even if they didn't pack them.
3. Towing a glider is [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/309?at=2026). A CFI-A with 1,500 hours doesn't automatically have tow-pilot privileges; the pilot needs a logbook endorsement under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/309?at=2026)(b)(2)(ii) and the experience listed in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/309?at=2026)(b)(2)(i) (100 hours PIC in airplanes, 3 actual or simulated tows under supervision, etc.).
4. Formation flight requires *prior arrangement* under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(b). Two airplanes flying together informally without that prior arrangement are not in formation; they are two airplanes flying near each other, and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(a) prohibits operating "so close to another aircraft as to create a collision hazard."

Subpart D and the formation rule in subpart B are where these answers live.

## §91.303 -- aerobatic flight

The text:

```text
§91.303  Aerobatic flight.

No person may operate an aircraft in aerobatic flight --

(a) Over any congested area of a city, town, or settlement;

(b) Over an open air assembly of persons;

(c) Within the lateral boundaries of the surface areas of Class B,
    Class C, Class D, or Class E airspace designated for an airport;

(d) Within 4 nautical miles of the center line of any Federal airway;

(e) Below an altitude of 1,500 feet above the surface; or

(f) When flight visibility is less than 3 statute miles.

For the purposes of this section, aerobatic flight means an
intentional maneuver involving an abrupt change in an aircraft's
attitude, an abnormal attitude, or abnormal acceleration, not
necessary for normal flight.
```

Six restrictions. They are conjunctive: a violation of any one is a violation of [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026). The framework is "where can I do aerobatics legally?" rather than "what's banned." Legal aerobatic flight is:

- Not over a city, town, or settlement
- Not over a crowd
- Not in the controlled-airspace surface area at an airport (Class B, C, D, or the surface E)
- Not within 4 nm of a Federal airway centerline
- At or above 1,500 feet AGL
- In 3 statute miles or better flight visibility

The 4-nm-from-airway rule is the one that catches most pilots. Federal airways (Victor airways and the jet routes) are 8 nm wide centerline-to-edge, but the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026)(d) buffer is *4 nm from the centerline*, which means an additional clear margin outside the airway itself. Aerobatic boxes (the FAA-issued waivered aerobatic practice areas) are typically located deliberately away from airways for this reason.

### What §91.303 does not cover

Parachutes. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026) doesn't say "wear a parachute." That rule is in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)(c) -- a pilot performing aerobatics with another person aboard must have *each occupant* wearing a parachute, and the parachute must meet the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026) pack-cycle requirements. The combination of the two sections is what makes "aerobatics with a passenger" a working operation.

Solo aerobatics (no other person aboard) does not require a parachute under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)(c)(2)(ii). The pilot is free to choose. Most aerobatic pilots wear one anyway.

### Aerobatic Competency Cards and waivers

A pilot performing aerobatics at airshows or below the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026) altitude floor needs a low-altitude waiver issued by the FAA, typically administered through the International Council of Air Shows (ICAS) Aerobatic Competency Evaluation (ACE) program. The waiver authorizes specific lower altitudes by performer level (200, 250, 500, 800, 1,500 feet AGL surface). The card lives outside the regulation but is what makes the regulation operational for the airshow community.

## §91.307 -- parachutes and parachuting

The text addresses two distinct populations: pilots wearing parachutes and pilots flying parachutists.

### Parachute pack cycle

Any parachute used to comply with [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026) must have been packed by an FAA-certificated and appropriately rated parachute rigger:

- **Within the preceding 180 days** if the canopy is composed of nylon, rayon, or other similar synthetic fiber. Nearly all modern parachutes.
- **Within the preceding 60 days** if the canopy is composed of natural fibers (silk, linen, etc.). Vintage rigs only.

The pack cycle is the rigger's responsibility but the *user's* exposure. A pilot who straps on a rig and flies aerobatics with a passenger is responsible for verifying the rigger seal and date. The trap: pilots assume the rig is current because someone else owns it. The regulation places the burden on the operator.

### Parachute use during aerobatic flight

When more than one person is aboard during aerobatic flight, *each occupant* must wear an approved parachute that meets the pack-cycle requirements. If only the pilot is aboard, the pilot may but is not required to wear a parachute.

The definition of "aerobatic flight" here cross-references [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026): an intentional maneuver involving an abrupt change in attitude, abnormal attitude, or abnormal acceleration, not necessary for normal flight. The trap: bank angles greater than 60 degrees or pitch attitudes greater than 30 degrees nose up or nose down trigger the parachute requirement under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)(c)(1) when carrying passengers, even when the maneuver isn't aerobatic in the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026) sense. Steep turn instruction therefore lives near this line and is excluded by [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)(d) only when the maneuver is *required for certification or rating* and conducted by a CFI with a person receiving instruction.

### Parachute operations (jump plane ops)

The regulation refers to FAA Order 8900.1 / AC 105-2 and 14 CFR Part 105 (Parachute Operations) for the substantive jump rules. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026) is short on the operational side. The pilot of the jump plane is responsible for [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/3?at=2026) PIC obligations (airworthiness, weight, weather), the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/103?at=2026) preflight, and any specific Part 105 NOTAM and ATC coordination requirements.

Part 105 governs the jumper side: where jumps may be conducted, parachute operations near airports, jumps into Class B / C / D / E airspace, jumps over congested areas. Working CFIs flying jump planes need both [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026) and Part 105 in their head.

## §91.309 -- towing gliders and unpowered ultralight vehicles

Pilots towing gliders need:

- A current pilot certificate other than student pilot
- A logbook endorsement from an authorized instructor certifying tow-pilot proficiency in the make and model of the tow airplane
- 100 hours PIC in airplanes if towing for compensation, or 200 hours otherwise (paragraph references vary; verify current text)
- 3 actual or simulated tows in the preceding 12 months under supervision of an authorized tow pilot

The 12-month currency requirement under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/309?at=2026)(b)(2)(iii) is the tow-pilot equivalent of the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(a) passenger recency rule. A tow pilot who has not towed in 12 months is no longer current to tow even if the original endorsement is in the logbook.

The towed-aircraft side is silent in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/309?at=2026); the glider's airworthiness, the glider pilot's currency, and the tow-rope strength requirements live in adjacent rules and the glider's type certificate data sheet.

## §91.311 -- towing other than under §91.309

Banner-towing, advertising sleeves, target sleeves, anything that isn't a glider or unpowered ultralight: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/311?at=2026) requires a Letter of Authorization (LOA) from the FAA Administrator. The LOA specifies what may be towed, what equipment is required, and what altitude, area, and operational restrictions apply. Without the LOA the operation is illegal even if the airplane is otherwise capable.

A common misreading is that a CFI's tow endorsement under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/309?at=2026) covers banner towing. It does not. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/311?at=2026) is a separate authority and a separate paperwork stack.

## §91.111 -- formation and proximity

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026) is in subpart B (general flight rules), not subpart D, but it belongs in any treatment of special operations because formation flight is functionally a special operation. The text:

```text
§91.111  Operating near other aircraft.

(a) No person may operate an aircraft so close to another aircraft as
    to create a collision hazard.

(b) No person may operate an aircraft in formation flight except by
    arrangement with the pilot in command of each aircraft in the
    formation.

(c) No person may operate an aircraft, carrying passengers for hire,
    in formation flight.
```

Three paragraphs. Each does work.

- **(a)** is the catch-all that applies to *any* aircraft proximity, formation or not. A close pass in cruise flight, even briefly, can be a [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(a) violation.
- **(b)** is the consent rule. Formation flight is legal only when every PIC in the formation has agreed to it. "By arrangement" means a brief, an agreed plan, communications, and a defined formation. Two airplanes that happen to be flying close together are not in formation; they are in violation of [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(a).
- **(c)** prohibits formation flight when *any* aircraft in the formation carries passengers for hire. A Part 135 charter cannot be flown in formation under any circumstances.

The trap on (b): "by arrangement" is not codified beyond consent. The Formation Flight Inc. (FFI) and Red Star Pilots Association have widely-used briefing standards and signoff cards (the "FAST card") that the formation community treats as the de facto operating standard. The FAA does not issue formation-flight certifications directly; the FAST card is the industry-self-regulated equivalent. CFIs teaching formation flight typically teach to the FFI standard.

## Common misreadings

- **"Aerobatics under 1,500 AGL is fine if I'm over open country."** Wrong. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026)(e) prohibits aerobatic flight below 1,500 feet AGL *anywhere*, regardless of what's underneath. The "open country" is necessary but not sufficient. The waivered low-altitude work at airshows is legal because of the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026) waiver, not because of [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026).
- **"My CFI tow endorsement lets me tow banners."** Wrong. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/309?at=2026) covers gliders and unpowered ultralight vehicles only. Banner towing falls under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/311?at=2026), which requires a separate Letter of Authorization.
- **"Two airplanes flying together to lunch are in formation."** Conditionally. They are in formation only if each PIC has agreed to fly in formation with the other ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(b)). Without arrangement they are two airplanes operating "near other aircraft" and bound by [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(a) only -- which prohibits operations creating a collision hazard.
- **"Solo aerobatics requires a parachute."** Wrong. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)(c) requires parachutes only when more than one person is aboard. A solo pilot is free to choose; many wear them anyway.
- **"Steep turn training requires parachutes."** Almost. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)(c)(1) triggers the parachute requirement at bank angles greater than 60 degrees or pitch attitudes greater than 30 degrees nose up or down. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)(d) carves out an exception when the maneuver is required for a certificate or rating and is being given by a CFI to a person receiving instruction. Standard 45-degree-bank steep turns don't trigger the rule; 60-degree-bank training does, but the (d) carve-out usually applies in dual instruction.
- **"The 4-nm-from-airway rule is from the airway edge."** Wrong. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026)(d) measures *from the centerline* of the airway, not from the airway boundary. Federal airways are 8 nm wide (4 each side of centerline); the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026)(d) buffer adds another 4 nm beyond the centerline, which means the aerobatic operation must be at least 4 nm from where the airway is even drawn.
- **"Parachute pack cycle is 120 days."** Wrong. The synthetic-fiber pack cycle was 60 days under the old rule and was extended to **180 days** in 2008. Many older training materials still cite 120 days; check the current text.
- **"Carrying passengers for hire in formation is allowed if the formation lead briefs everyone."** Wrong. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(c) bans formation flight when *any* aircraft in the formation is carrying passengers for hire. No briefing exception.

### Apply the rules

> A pilot wants to do a 5-minute aerobatic sequence at 2,000 feet AGL, 5 nm from the centerline of V25, 4 SM visibility, no passengers, in Class G airspace. Legal?

Answer: Yes. Above 1,500 AGL, more than 4 nm from airway centerline, visibility above 3 SM, not in any controlled-airspace surface area, not over a congested area or open-air assembly. Solo so no parachute requirement. Each [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026) restriction is satisfied.

> The same pilot wants to bring a friend along to "see what aerobatics is like." Same conditions. What changes?

Answer: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)(c) now applies. Both pilot and passenger must wear approved parachutes within the 180-day pack cycle. The pilot must verify the rigger's seal date.

> A pilot with a CFI-A and 1,200 hours wants to start towing gliders this weekend. They have not towed before. What's required?

Answer: Under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/309?at=2026), the pilot needs a logbook endorsement from an authorized instructor certifying tow-pilot proficiency in the specific tow airplane, and they need to log 3 actual or simulated tows under supervision of an authorized tow pilot before towing solo. The 100-hour PIC airplane minimum is met (1,200 > 100). The CFI-A doesn't grant tow privileges automatically; the endorsement does.

> Two pilots brief a formation flight to a fly-in. The lead's airplane is rented from an FBO; the wingman's is owned. Is this legal?

Answer: Yes, assuming each PIC has agreed under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(b). Neither airplane is carrying passengers for hire, so [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(c) doesn't apply. Both pilots are bound by [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(a) -- no operating so close as to create a collision hazard -- which is what the formation brief addresses.
