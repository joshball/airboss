# Subparts H, J, K, L and the SFAR overlay model

The last four subparts of Part 91 are the ones a working CFI almost never opens. They cover foreign aircraft and U.S. ops abroad (subpart H), waivers (subpart J -- not to be confused with subpart H foreign-ops topic in [03-large-turbine-and-rvsm.md](03-large-turbine-and-rvsm.md)), fractional ownership (subpart K), and continued airworthiness / safety improvements (subpart L). Beyond these subparts, the FAA layers Special Federal Aviation Regulations (SFARs) -- temporary or experimental rules that modify Part 91 (and other parts) for specific aircraft, regions, or operations.

We name and locate. The pedagogical bar matches lesson 03: locate-first, recite-not. Plus we close the loop on the *integration question* of how SFARs modify Part 91, which is the conceptual piece a CFI candidate has to carry into the rest of the course.

## What you'll be able to do

- State what subparts H, J, K, L cover and when each applies
- Locate [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026) (international operations of U.S. aircraft), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026) (waivers), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1001?at=2026) (fractional applicability)
- Recognize the SFAR overlay model: SFARs sit alongside Part 91 and modify or supplement specific rules for defined aircraft, regions, or operations
- Cite at least one currently-active SFAR by number and effect (SFAR 73 -- R22 / R44; SFAR 108 -- Mitsubishi MU-2B; etc.)
- Articulate when a CFI's daily flying might cross an SFAR boundary

## Why this matters

A CFI who teaches in an R22 is operating under SFAR 73. A pilot transitioning to an MU-2B is operating under SFAR 108. A pilot ferrying an experimental aircraft internationally crosses subparts H and (potentially) the operating-limitations document. A fractional pilot at NetJets, Flexjet, or PlaneSense operates under subpart K. None of these are theoretical -- each is a real population of pilots and aircraft.

The integration question -- "where does the rule for X live, given that X has multiple regulatory homes?" -- is the conceptual skill a CFI candidate has to demonstrate. SFARs are the most-confusing version of this question because they sit *outside* Part 91 numerically but *modify* Part 91 substantively. The answer is "look at the SFAR list before you trust the Part 91 text."

## The discovery question

Five scenarios to route:

1. A CFI you know is hired to teach in a Robinson R22. The chief instructor says "make sure you've signed off the SFAR 73 awareness training before you start." What is SFAR 73 and where does it modify Part 91?
2. A pilot ferries a Cirrus SR22 from the U.S. to Germany for delivery. What Part 91 sections govern the international segments?
3. A pilot interviewing at NetJets is asked to articulate "the difference between Part 91 subpart F and subpart K." What do they say?
4. A flight school holds a noise waiver to overfly a national park boundary at lower altitudes during specific months. Which subpart authorizes the waiver?
5. A pilot flying an MU-2B in their owner-operated Part 91 corporate role. What SFAR governs?

Pause and route.

1. SFAR 73 modifies Part 91 for the R22 and R44. Specific awareness training and recurrent ground / flight training are required for pilots and CFIs operating those airframes; the modifications include awareness of mast-bumping risk and minimum hour thresholds before solo and CFI privileges.
2. Subpart H governs operations of U.S.-registered aircraft outside the U.S. ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026)). The pilot complies with U.S. regulations when in conflict with ICAO, except in specific cases where the host country's rule controls. In foreign airspace, foreign-airspace rules govern unless they conflict with U.S. rules and the pilot is operating under U.S. authority.
3. Subpart F covers some fractional applicability anchors and large/turbine ops generally; subpart K covers the substantive fractional-ownership program rules (training, ops manual, operational control, drug and alcohol testing). NetJets pilots routinely operate under both.
4. Subpart J -- waivers ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026)). The FAA can authorize specific deviations from Part 91 when the operation can be conducted safely. The flight school's noise waiver is a [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026) action.
5. SFAR 108 modifies Part 91 for the MU-2B series. Specific training, currency, and pilot proficiency standards apply.

These scenarios are why subpart-naming and SFAR-awareness matter even for daily-light-GA CFIs.

## Subpart H -- foreign aircraft and international ops

The full title: "Foreign Aircraft Operations and Operations of U.S.-Registered Civil Aircraft Outside of the United States; and Rules Governing Persons On Board Such Aircraft."

Sections [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/701?at=2026) through [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/715?at=2026). The shape:

```text
Subpart H -- Foreign Aircraft Operations and Operations of U.S.-
             Registered Civil Aircraft Outside of the United States

§91.701  Applicability
§91.702  Persons on board
§91.703  Operations of civil aircraft of U.S. registry outside of
          the United States
§91.705  Operations within airspace designated as MNPS airspace
§91.706  Operations within airspace designated as RVSM airspace
§91.707  Flights between Mexico or Canada and the United States
§91.709  Operations to Cuba
§91.711  Special rules for foreign civil aircraft
§91.713  Operation of civil aircraft of Cuban registry
§91.715  Special flight authorizations for foreign civil aircraft
```

When does it apply?

- A U.S.-registered aircraft operating *outside* the U.S. is subject to [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026): U.S. rules continue to apply where they don't conflict with the host country's rules. ICAO Annex 2 (Rules of the Air) provides the international framework; [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026)(a)(2) effectively incorporates ICAO rules for U.S. aircraft when over high seas.
- A *foreign-registered* aircraft operating in U.S. airspace is subject to [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/711?at=2026), which lays out additional restrictions on top of general Part 91.

The substantive piece a CFI candidate should know: U.S. operating rules continue across the border; pilots don't suddenly drop everything and follow only ICAO. The high-seas exception ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026)(a)(2)) applies in international airspace.

## Subpart J -- waivers

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/901?at=2026) and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026) cover the waiver mechanism:

```text
Subpart J -- Waivers

§91.901  (Reserved)
§91.903  Policy and procedures
§91.905  List of rules subject to waivers
```

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026) authorizes the Administrator to waive any rule listed in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/905?at=2026) when the proposed operation can be conducted safely under the terms and conditions of the waiver. Common waivers:

- **Aerobatic-flight low-altitude waivers** -- modify [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026) for airshow performers (200, 250, 500, 800 ft AGL surface)
- **Banner-towing authorizations** -- the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/311?at=2026) Letter of Authorization is functionally a waiver mechanism
- **Special-event airspace** -- TFR-adjacent waivers for fly-overs, parachute demos, military training
- **Aerial-photography clearances** -- specific [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/119?at=2026) waivers for low-altitude work

The locate skill: a question about "can the FAA authorize me to do X that's normally prohibited?" routes to subpart J before anywhere else.

## Subpart K -- fractional ownership operations

Sections [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1001?at=2026) onward (numbered in the 1000s and 1400s; the FAA structured this as a separate subpart with its own numbering).

```text
Subpart K -- Fractional Ownership Operations

§91.1001  Applicability
§91.1011  Operational control
§91.1013  Operational control briefing
§91.1015  Management specifications
§91.1017  Drug and alcohol testing
§91.1057  Flight, duty, and rest time requirements
§91.1109  Aircraft maintenance: Inspection program
§91.1411  Continuous airworthiness maintenance program
... (many more, totaling > 100 sections)
```

When does it apply? [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1001?at=2026) defines a "fractional ownership program" as a program that provides:

- A fractional ownership interest in a program aircraft
- A "dry" lease aircraft exchange arrangement among fractional owners
- A multi-year program agreement covering the operation of the aircraft

NetJets, Flexjet, PlaneSense, and similar operators fall under subpart K. A solo airplane owner who hires a corporate pilot does not. The dividing line is the fractional-program structure.

What does it cover? The substantive piece is **operational control** ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1011?at=2026)) -- the program manager and the fractional owner each have specific operational-control responsibilities. The owner has authority over decisions about the trip; the manager has the operational-control infrastructure. This is parallel to the operational-control concept in Part 135 and Part 121, ported into Part 91.

Other heavyweight subpart K sections:

- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1057?at=2026) -- Flight, duty, and rest time.** Pilots in a fractional program have FAR-mandated duty / rest cycles, mirroring (but distinct from) Part 135.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1411?at=2026) -- Continuous airworthiness maintenance program (CAMP).** Subpart K aircraft can opt into a CAMP rather than the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/409?at=2026) annual / 100-hour cycle.

Working CFI candidates rarely live in subpart K. They should be able to locate it.

## Subpart L -- continued airworthiness and safety improvements

Sections [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1501?at=2026) onward. Niche subpart covering FAA-mandated safety improvements (life-limited parts tracking, specific airframe directives) for certain aging-aircraft populations. Locate; do not memorize.

## SFARs -- the overlay model

A Special Federal Aviation Regulation (SFAR) is a temporary or limited-population rule that modifies one or more existing FARs. SFARs are numbered, dated, and structurally separate from the Part they modify. They are typically codified at the front of 14 CFR (in the "Special Federal Aviation Regulations" section that precedes Part 21) and may sunset or remain in effect indefinitely.

The integration question: an SFAR modifies the regulations *outside* the regular Part 91 text. A pilot reading [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) currency rules and stopping there can miss an SFAR that adds aircraft-specific requirements.

### High-yield SFARs for a working CFI

- **SFAR 73 -- Robinson R22 / R44.** Adds awareness training, minimum solo hour thresholds, recurrent training requirements. A CFI signing off an R22 student is bound by SFAR 73 as well as Part 61 and Part 91.
- **SFAR 108 -- Mitsubishi MU-2B.** The MU-2B has a higher accident rate than peers; SFAR 108 imposes specific training and currency requirements. Any pilot in an MU-2B Part 91 op is bound by it.
- **SFAR 23 -- Lear Jet, Cessna Citation, Falcon 20, Hawker 125, etc.** Older SFAR addressing specific type-rating training requirements.
- **SFAR 13 -- Mitsubishi twin-engine multi-engine aircraft.** Older.
- **SFAR 100 -- Special purpose flights (e.g., during certain military operations) or specific airspace zones (Washington DC SFRA).** The DC SFRA is governed by SFAR-style rules; check current text before flying near DC.

Currently-active SFARs are listed at the front of 14 CFR. The list changes; CFI candidates should know how to find them rather than memorize the numbers.

### How to integrate SFARs into the Part 91 read

For any operation, the read order is:

1. **Part 91 itself** -- subparts A, B, C, D, E for the universal rules; F, G, H for the population-specific overlays.
2. **The aircraft's airworthiness certificate** -- standard, restricted, limited, experimental, or special light-sport. The certificate dictates which subpart D operating limits apply.
3. **SFARs that touch the aircraft type or the operation** -- check by aircraft type (R22, MU-2B, Citation, etc.) and by operation type (formation, parachute, agricultural).
4. **Any Letter of Authorization, waiver, or operating-limitations document** -- the per-operator paper that further modifies the applicable rules.

A pilot who stops at step 1 misses the substance of steps 2-4. The CFI's job is to teach the read order, not just the rule.

## Common misreadings

- **"Subpart H is just RVSM."** Wrong. Subpart H covers foreign aircraft, U.S. ops abroad, MNPS, RVSM, and special bilateral arrangements (Mexico, Canada, Cuba). RVSM is one section ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026)) within it.
- **"Subpart K is for any private operation flying for hire."** Wrong. Subpart K applies specifically to fractional-ownership programs as defined in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1001?at=2026). A solo aircraft owner with a contract pilot is not in subpart K.
- **"SFARs are old rules I don't need to worry about."** Wrong. Many SFARs are active and binding. SFAR 73 is the most-encountered for daily CFI work in helicopters; SFAR 108 is a meaningful corporate-ops constraint.
- **"Waivers under subpart J replace the rule."** Wrong. A waiver under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026) authorizes a specific deviation under specific terms. The base rule continues to apply outside the waiver's scope.
- **"International flight just means following ICAO."** Half-true. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026) keeps U.S. rules in force for U.S. aircraft except where they conflict with the host country's rules; over high seas, ICAO Annex 2 applies.
- **"The DC SFRA is in Part 93."** Half-true. Part 93 codifies many special air-traffic rules including the Washington DC SFRA, but the SFRA structure was originally established via SFAR 94 / SFAR 100-style instruments. Today the DC SFRA rules are in Part 93 subpart V; the SFAR mechanism was the historical pathway.
- **"Fractional owners are governed by Part 135."** No. Subpart K is the regulatory home for fractional. The owner is operating under Part 91 (the aircraft is in their control), but with subpart K's program-management rules.

### Apply the rules

> A CFI takes a job at a flight school that operates R22s primarily for primary training. What needs to be in their first-day briefing folder beyond Part 91 and Part 61?

Answer: SFAR 73 (R22 / R44 awareness training requirement, minimum hour thresholds for solo, recurrent training). The CFI needs to be SFAR-73-compliant before flying with R22 students.

> A pilot flying a Cirrus SR22 ferries the airplane from KSGJ to EDDS Stuttgart. Walk through the regulatory layers.

Answer: U.S. departure under Part 91 subparts A-E. Once over the Atlantic, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026) keeps U.S. rules in force where they don't conflict with ICAO; ICAO Annex 2 governs high-seas operations. RVSM ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026)) applies if filed in the FL290-FL410 band. MNPS ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/705?at=2026)) applies in North Atlantic Track airspace. Once over Germany, German air-law and EASA rules govern (with U.S. rules continuing where applicable). On arrival, the airplane is subject to the local-airport rules.

> A NetJets pilot flying a Citation Sovereign on a fractional-owner trip. Which Part 91 sections govern?

Answer: Subparts A, B, C, D, E (universal). Subpart F for large/turbine ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/501?at=2026) onward). Subpart G for transport-category equipment ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/601?at=2026) onward) if the airplane is transport-category. Subpart K for fractional program rules ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1001?at=2026) onward, especially [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1011?at=2026) operational control). RVSM ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026)) at altitude.
