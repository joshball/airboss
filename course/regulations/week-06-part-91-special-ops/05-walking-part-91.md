# Walking Part 91 -- the whole part in 90 minutes

This lesson is the integration walk. The mental model: a CFI candidate with this lesson in their head can sit across from an examiner, walk Part 91 from the front cover to the back, and at every section produce one or two sentences on what the section does, why it's there, and when it applies. No memorization of the full text; full coverage of the structural map.

The walk is the connective tissue between the deep lessons in Weeks 4 and 5 (subparts A, B, C, D, E) and the locate-only lessons earlier in this week (subparts F, G, H, J, K, L plus SFARs). After this walk you have the whole part as a single navigable map.

The walk takes about 90 minutes spoken at oral-exam pace. We'll move briskly. Where a section is covered deeply elsewhere in the course, we point at the deep lesson and move on.

## What you'll be able to do

- Walk Part 91 from subpart A through subpart L plus the SFAR layer
- Predict which subpart owns the answer to any Part 91 question in under 5 seconds
- Articulate the cumulative-stack model: subparts A-E apply to every flight; subparts F-L stack on for the populations they cover; SFARs further modify
- Cite the most-commonly-tested sections by number without scanning a table of contents

## Why this matters

By Week 6 the learner has seen Part 91 in pieces: PIC authority (Week 4), flight rules (Week 4), equipment (Week 5), maintenance (Week 5), special operations (Week 6 lessons 1-4). The pieces don't add up to navigation skill until they're walked end-to-end. Examiners will rarely ask "tell me [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/213?at=2026)(d) verbatim." They will ask "walk me through Part 91" or "given this scenario, where do you look first?" The walk is what builds the navigation reflex.

Working CFIs use Part 91 every day. The structure is the muscle. This lesson installs it.

## The discovery question

Sit back from the page. Without looking, name the subparts of Part 91 in order. A through L. One sentence per subpart on what it covers.

Pause.

Likely you got A (general), B (flight rules), C (equipment), D (special ops), E (maintenance) cleanly. F got murky. G, H, J, K, L blurred. SFARs felt like a footnote.

That blurring is exactly what this lesson fixes. By the end you'll have all twelve subparts (A through L, skipping I if the learner doesn't fly noise-restricted ops -- subpart I exists but is rarely encountered) plus the SFAR layer, with one sentence per subpart and a short list of high-yield sections in each.

## The shape of Part 91 -- the headline

```text
Part 91 -- General Operating and Flight Rules

  Subpart A -- General                                   §91.1 - §91.25
    The big picture: applicability, definitions, PIC authority,
    careless or reckless. Every flight in U.S. airspace passes
    through subpart A.

  Subpart B -- Flight Rules                              §91.101 - §91.199
    The flight rules: VFR, IFR, weather, fuel, right-of-way,
    altitude, communications. The longest subpart by section count.

  Subpart C -- Equipment, Instrument, and Certificate    §91.201 - §91.225
                Requirements
    Equipment cumulative requirements (91.205), inop equipment
    (91.213), inspections triggered by equipment (transponder,
    ELT). Reads as a checklist.

  Subpart D -- Special Flight Operations                 §91.301 - §91.327
    Aerobatic, parachute, towing, restricted/limited/experimental
    /light-sport operating rules. The "non-normal flight" subpart.

  Subpart E -- Maintenance, Preventive Maintenance,      §91.401 - §91.421
                and Alterations
    Annual, 100-hour, altimeter/static, transponder, ELT, AD
    compliance. The "is the airplane current?" subpart.

  Subpart F -- Large and Turbine-Powered Multiengine     §91.501 - §91.535
                Airplanes and Fractional Ownership
                Program Aircraft
    Population-specific overlay for large/turbine and fractional.
    A working CFI rarely flies these but should know they exist.

  Subpart G -- Additional Equipment and Operating        §91.601 - §91.615
                Requirements for Large and Transport
                Category Aircraft
    Additional layer on subpart F for transport-category aircraft
    (Part 25 type cert). Speed warning, weight limits, FDR/CVR.

  Subpart H -- Foreign Aircraft Operations and           §91.701 - §91.715
                Operations of U.S.-Registered Civil
                Aircraft Outside of the United States
    International ops: U.S. aircraft abroad, foreign aircraft in
    U.S. airspace, MNPS, RVSM, special bilateral.

  Subpart I -- Operating Noise Limits                    §91.801 - §91.881
    Stage 1/2/3/4/5 noise limits, supersonic ops. Niche.

  Subpart J -- Waivers                                   §91.901 - §91.905
    Where the FAA can authorize deviations from specific Part 91
    rules. Aerobatic low-altitude, banner, special events.

  Subpart K -- Fractional Ownership Operations           §91.1001 - §91.1443
    NetJets, Flexjet, PlaneSense, etc. Operational control,
    duty/rest, ops manuals, training, drug testing. Most
    substantive of the F-K stack.

  Subpart L -- Continued Airworthiness and               §91.1501 - onward
                Safety Improvements
    FAA-mandated safety programs and life-limited-parts tracking
    for specific aircraft populations. Niche.

SFAR overlay (numbered separately, modifying Part 91 substantively)
  SFAR 73 (R22/R44), SFAR 108 (MU-2B), SFAR 23 (older transports),
  Part 93 SFRAs (DC), SFAR 50 (Grand Canyon), and others.
```

The structural pattern: A-E are universal. F-K stack on for specific populations. L is niche. SFARs sit outside but modify in.

Now we walk it.

## Subpart A -- General (§91.1 - §91.25)

Three sections do the heavy lifting:

### §91.1 -- Applicability

Part 91 prescribes rules governing the operation of aircraft within the United States. Other Parts (61, 91, 121, 135 etc.) layer on top of this floor.

### §91.3 -- Responsibility and authority of the pilot in command

The PIC is directly responsible for, and is the final authority as to, the operation of the aircraft. In an in-flight emergency, the PIC may deviate from any rule of Part 91 to the extent required to meet that emergency. The deviation must be reported to the Administrator on request.

The trap: paragraph (b) covers in-flight emergencies; paragraph (c) requires a written report on request, not always proactively.

### §91.13 -- Careless or reckless operation

The catch-all. No person may operate an aircraft in a careless or reckless manner so as to endanger the life or property of another. Violations of [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/13?at=2026) frequently accompany other violations as the *resulting* harm.

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/15?at=2026) (dropping objects), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/17?at=2026) (alcohol or drugs), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/19?at=2026) (controlled substances) round out subpart A. Locate; the substance is in [Week 4 lesson 01](../week-04-part-91-general-and-flight-rules/01-pic-authority-and-careless-reckless.md).

## Subpart B -- Flight Rules (§91.101 - §91.199)

The longest subpart. Walk by chunks.

### Preflight and operating obligations (§91.103 - §91.111)

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/103?at=2026) preflight action -- "all available information." [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/105?at=2026) crew-position requirements during takeoff and landing. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/107?at=2026) passenger seatbelt and shoulder-harness use. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/109?at=2026) flight instruction equipment (dual controls). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026) operating near other aircraft (formation; lesson 01).

### Right-of-way and altitudes (§91.113 - §91.119)

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/113?at=2026) right-of-way (other than water). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/115?at=2026) right-of-way (water). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/117?at=2026) speed limits below 10,000 MSL (250 KIAS), within Class B underlying (200), within 4 nm of Class C/D (200). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/119?at=2026) minimum safe altitudes (1000 over congested, 500 over uncongested with 500-foot horizontal buffer to people / vessels / structures, sectional-specific over open water).

### ATC and IFR ops (§91.121 - §91.144)

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/121?at=2026) altimeter settings (29.92 above 18,000 MSL). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/123?at=2026) compliance with ATC clearances. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/125?at=2026) ATC light signals. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/126?at=2026) - [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/135?at=2026) airspace ops (G, E, B, C, D, A). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/137?at=2026) - [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/144?at=2026) TFRs and special-use airspace.

### VFR weather, fuel, altitudes (§91.151 - §91.159)

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/151?at=2026) fuel reserves (30 minutes day VFR, 45 minutes night VFR, after planned destination at normal cruise). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/155?at=2026) cloud clearances by airspace. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/157?at=2026) special VFR (1 SM, clear of clouds, by ATC clearance, only some Class B/C/D/E surface areas). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/159?at=2026) VFR cruising altitudes above 3,000 AGL (odd-thousand plus 500 east, even-thousand plus 500 west, by magnetic course). Deep-dive in [Week 4 lesson 04](../week-04-part-91-general-and-flight-rules/04-vfr-weather-and-altitudes.md).

### IFR ops (§91.167 - §91.193)

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/167?at=2026) IFR fuel (destination + alternate + 45 minutes at normal cruise). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/169?at=2026) IFR alternate selection (the 1-2-3 rule for required alternate, the 1500/2 / 800/2 / 600/2 alternate-mins boilerplate, the operations-specs-substitute paragraph). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/171?at=2026) VOR equipment check. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/173?at=2026) ATC clearance and flight plan required. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/175?at=2026) takeoff and landing under IFR (DH/MDA, contact / visual approach, the 91.175(c) "look down" runway-environment items). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/177?at=2026) - [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/183?at=2026) IFR cruising rules. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/185?at=2026) lost-comm procedures (the AVEF / MEA route / AVEF altitude rule). ## Subpart C -- Equipment, Instrument, and Certificate Requirements (§91.201 - §91.225)

### Documents (§91.203)

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/203?at=2026) -- AROW. Airworthiness certificate, Registration, Operating limitations (POH/AFM placards), Weight and balance. The legal documents-aboard checklist.

### Equipment lists (§91.205)

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/205?at=2026) -- the cumulative equipment list. VFR day = ATOMATOFLAMES (or your favorite mnemonic). VFR night = adds FLAPS (Fuses, Landing light if for hire, Anticollision, Position lights, Source of electrical power). IFR = adds GRABCARDD or your variant. The cumulative-stack model: each more demanding op adds equipment to the prior set. Deep-dive in Week 5.

### Required equipment by operation (§91.207 - §91.227)

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/207?at=2026) ELT. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/209?at=2026) airplane lights (sunset to sunrise). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/211?at=2026) supplemental oxygen (12,500 to 14,000 cabin pressure altitude required for flight crew if more than 30 min; over 14,000 always for flight crew; over 15,000 must be available for passengers). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/213?at=2026) inoperative instruments and equipment (the no-MEL pathway). [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/215?at=2026) ATC transponder requirements and Mode C. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/217?at=2026) - [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/223?at=2026) niche transponder, TCAS, autopilot rules. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/225?at=2026) ADS-B Out (the airspace where required, the equipment standards).

## Subpart E -- Maintenance, Preventive Maintenance, and Alterations (§91.401 - §91.421)

The maintenance subpart. Walk-rules:

## SFAR overlay

Outside Part 91 numerically; modifying Part 91 substantively. Currently active SFARs include:

- **SFAR 73** -- Robinson R22 / R44 (training, currency, mast-bumping awareness)
- **SFAR 108** -- Mitsubishi MU-2B (training, currency)
- **SFAR 23** -- specific older transport-category aircraft (training)
- **Part 93 subpart V** -- Washington DC SFRA (codified in Part 93 but functionally an SFAR-style overlay)
- Older / aircraft-specific SFARs (numbered in 14 CFR's prefatory section)

The read order: Part 91 first, then check the SFAR list for the aircraft and operation. A pilot who reads only Part 91 and stops there can miss substantial requirements.

## The cumulative-stack model

The integration model. Every U.S. civil flight operates under subparts A, B, C, D (if applicable), E. Specific populations stack additional subparts on top:

```text
                  +------------+
                  | SFAR overlay|   for specific aircraft / regions
                  +------------+
              +--------+
              | F + G  |   for large, turbine multi, transport
              +--------+
          +---+---+---+---+
          | H | I | K | L |   foreign, noise, fractional, continued-airworthiness
          +---+---+---+---+
   +-------------------------+
   | A + B + C + D + E       |   universal, every U.S. civil flight
   +-------------------------+
```

A King Air 350 in fractional service operates under A, B, C, D, E (universal) plus F (large/turbine) plus K (fractional) plus possibly H (RVSM) plus possibly I (noise). Five subparts for one flight.

A Cessna 172 doing local flight training under Part 91 operates under A, B, C, D (only the bits that apply -- e.g., not aerobatic), E. Four subparts. Most of Part 91 is invisible because it doesn't apply to that population.

## How to use the walk

When a question hits, route by:

1. **Subject** -- pilot, flight, or operation? (See [Week 1 lesson 04](../week-01-architecture/04-the-pilot-the-flight-the-operation.md).)
2. **If flight** -- subpart? Universal A-E, or population-specific F-L? Check the aircraft.
3. **Section** -- which section in the subpart? Use the deep-lesson references.
4. **SFAR check** -- does an SFAR modify the answer for this aircraft / region / operation?

The walk gives you steps 1-3. The SFAR check is step 4 and it's habit, not reflex; build it in.

## Common misreadings

- **"Subpart A is just definitions."** Wrong. Subpart A includes [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/3?at=2026) PIC authority and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/13?at=2026) careless or reckless -- two of the most-cited regulations in Part 91 enforcement.
- **"Subpart B is VFR flight rules."** Half-true. Subpart B covers both VFR and IFR flight rules. The IFR ops sections ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/167?at=2026) - [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/193?at=2026)) are subpart B, not a separate subpart.
- **"Equipment requirements are in subpart C only."** Half-true. Subpart C is the universal-aircraft equipment baseline. Subpart F adds for large/turbine. Subpart G adds for transport. Each layer adds.
- **"Maintenance is one subpart -- E."** Subpart E covers Part 91 maintenance currency. Part 43 covers the maintenance work itself. Subpart K introduces the fractional-program CAMP path. The maintenance world has multiple regulatory homes.
- **"Subpart H is the international subpart only."** Subpart H mixes foreign-aircraft-in-U.S. ops, U.S.-aircraft-abroad ops, and the RVSM rule. RVSM is a U.S.-airspace operation; it's not "international" in the colloquial sense, but it lives in subpart H.
- **"SFARs are obscure historical rules."** Some SFARs are obscure; SFAR 73 and SFAR 108 are routine in their respective communities and binding daily.
- **"If a Part doesn't have a specific rule, the operation is unregulated."** Wrong. The catch-all from [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/13?at=2026) (careless or reckless) and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/3?at=2026) (PIC authority) cover most of the gaps. The FAA can also act under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026) waivers or aircraft-specific operating-limitations documents.
- **"Part 91 doesn't apply to commercial operations."** Wrong, exactly the opposite. Part 91 is the floor for *every* civil flight in U.S. airspace including commercial. Parts 121, 135 layer on top of Part 91 for the commercial-operations they cover. Removing Part 91 from a Part 135 charter would be removing the floor.

### Apply the integration model

> A pilot in a NetJets Citation X files IFR FL410 from Chicago to London. Walk the regulatory layers.

Answer: Universal -- subpart A (PIC, careless), subpart B (flight rules; IFR ops sections), subpart C (equipment), subpart D as applicable, subpart E (maintenance currency). Population overlays -- subpart F (large turbojet multi-engine), subpart G (transport-category equipment if applicable), subpart H (international ops [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026), MNPS [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/705?at=2026), RVSM [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026)), subpart K (fractional ownership program). No SFAR for the Citation X specifically. Five subparts plus subpart H's three sections.

> A Robinson R22 student doing primary training at a flight school. Walk the layers.

Answer: Universal -- A, B, C, D (D rarely applicable for primary training), E. Population overlays -- none of F-K apply. SFAR overlay -- SFAR 73 applies and modifies Part 61 / Part 91 for R22 / R44. So three subparts plus an SFAR.

> A pilot ferries an experimental amateur-built RV-7 from KCXO to KOSH for the Oshkosh fly-in. Walk the layers.

Answer: Universal -- A, B, C, D ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) experimental operating limitations), E (maintenance under condition-inspection cycle). The RV-7's operating-limitations document layers in (specific authorizations for night, IFR, formation if applicable). No subpart F-L applies. Subpart J ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026) waivers) might apply if the Oshkosh airspace TFR has special procedures requiring a waiver. The integration: regulation + operating-limitations document + airshow-procedures NOTAMs.
