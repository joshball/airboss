# Week 6 oral -- "Walk me through this owner's flying year"

## The question

> "I want to walk through a pilot's full flying year and ask you, at each stop, what regulatory layers apply. The pilot owns three airplanes -- a 172 they trained their wife in this year, an experimental amateur-built RV-7 they finished last spring, and a fractional share in a Citation Sovereign for business travel. They also fly an aerobatic Pitts every other weekend at the local box. They picked up a tailwheel endorsement last summer in their Champ. Walk me through Part 91, where it touches each of these missions, where Part 61 layers on, what SFARs apply, and where the integration breaks if I'm not careful."

This is the integration oral. Cumulative across Weeks 1-6. Pilot, flight, operation -- the framing from Week 1 -- cross with the Part 61 currency machinery from Weeks 2-3 and the Part 91 stack from Weeks 4-6. The CFI candidate has to keep three planes in the air mentally and route each operation correctly.

## What this is testing

- Routing each operation through the pilot/flight/operation framing
- Recognizing which Part 91 subparts apply per airplane and per mission
- Cross-referencing Part 61 currency / recency / endorsement requirements per operation
- Spotting the SFAR overlay layer (any of the airplanes / operations a candidate)
- Articulating what "integration" means when one pilot wears multiple regulatory hats

## Model answer (full walkthrough)

A defensible answer takes 18-25 minutes spoken.

### Stage 1: The frame (one minute)

> "Right. Three airplanes plus a Pitts, plus a tailwheel signoff, plus fractional access. The framing is the same for each -- pilot first, flight second, operation third. The pilot is the same person across all of them, but their *currency footprint* is different per airplane and per operation. The flight rules are universal Part 91 plus population-specific overlays per airplane. The operation is mostly Part 91 personal flight, with one piece -- the fractional Citation -- pulling in subpart K. Let me walk through each in turn."

### Stage 2: The 172 (training the wife)

> "172 is normal-category, standard airworthiness. The owner trained his wife to private pilot in it this year, so I have to ask: was he the CFI, or did they hire one? If he's not a CFI, the answer is they hired one, and the CFI is bound by [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/193?at=2026). The wife is a student pilot, then private. Her aero-experience builds toward [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/109?at=2026) -- 40 hours total, 20 dual, 10 solo. Her medical is [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/23?at=2026) third-class. Her endorsements track AC 61-65.
>
> The 172 itself: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/103?at=2026) preflight every flight, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/203?at=2026) AROW documents aboard, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/205?at=2026) equipment per operation. If he's renting it out for instruction, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/409?at=2026)(b) 100-hour inspection cycle in addition to the annual. If it's an owner-only airplane the 100-hour doesn't apply. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/411?at=2026) altimeter/static every 24 calendar months for IFR ops. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/413?at=2026) transponder every 24 calendar months. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/207?at=2026) ELT every 12 months for the inspection, with battery replacement intervals.
>
> Subparts that apply: A, B, C, E. No D unless they did spin training (which most CFIs avoid in 172s anyway). No F, G, H, I, J, K, L. No SFAR. The simplest case."

### Stage 3: The RV-7 (experimental amateur-built)

> "Now the RV-7. Experimental amateur-built. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) is the operating-rule home, plus the operating-limitations document the FAA issued with the airworthiness certificate.
>
> The pilot side: same private pilot certificate, same currency. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(a) for passengers. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) flight review. The cert covers the airplane (single-engine land), so no new aero-experience required.
>
> The flight side -- this is where it changes. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(d)(2): VFR day only by default. The operating-limitations document may authorize night and IFR; the pilot has to read it. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(b): no operations over densely populated areas or congested airways unless authorized in the operating limitations. Phase I (the test phase) is now done -- I'd assume he flew the 40-hour test program last spring before the airplane went to Phase II. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(d)(1): every passenger must be advised of the experimental nature. He briefs his wife each time. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(d)(3): tower notification at towered airports.
>
> Maintenance: condition inspection annually rather than the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/409?at=2026) annual. The repairman certificate the builder earned for the RV-7 specifically authorizes the condition inspection.
>
> Subparts: A, B, C, D ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)), E. No SFAR for the RV-7. The integration trap here is forgetting the operating-limitations document is part of the regulatory stack -- if the document doesn't authorize night IFR and the pilot files an IFR flight plan at night anyway, that's a violation regardless of how well-equipped the airplane is."

### Stage 4: The Pitts at the aerobatic box

> "The Pitts is for aerobatics. Same pilot, same private cert, plus aerobatic training experience.
>
> Pilot side: no specific aerobatic endorsement under Part 61. The pilot's aerobatic competence is tracked by experience, currency, and any specialty training (IAC, ICAS) -- but [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026) does not include 'aerobatic' as a required endorsement.
>
> Flight side: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026) governs. The aerobatic box is a designated practice area away from controlled airspace, away from congested areas, away from airways. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026) requires:
>
> - Above 1,500 AGL ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026)(e)) -- the box has standard floor of 1,500 AGL unless waivered.
> - 3 SM visibility ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026)(f)).
> - Outside Class B/C/D/surface E ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026)(c)).
> - More than 4 nm from airway centerline ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026)(d)).
> - Not over a congested area or open-air assembly ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026)(a), (b)).
>
> Parachute: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)(c). Solo aerobatics in the Pitts -- no parachute required. The pilot wears one anyway because aerobatic culture and good practice. If the pilot ever takes another person -- both wear approved parachutes within the 180-day pack cycle.
>
> If he ever wants to fly aerobatics below 1,500 AGL (airshow demos), that's a subpart J waiver under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026), typically administered through ICAS / ACE.
>
> Subparts: A, B (formation if applicable, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)), C, D ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)), E. No SFAR. No subpart F-L."

### Stage 5: The Champ (tailwheel)

> "The tailwheel signoff in the Champ is the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026)(i) endorsement. One-time training, logbook entry. AC 61-65 has the canonical wording. After the endorsement the pilot is good in any tailwheel airplane they're rated for (single-engine land); the endorsement isn't make-and-model-specific.
>
> The Champ itself: standard airworthiness, normal category, light. Subparts A, B, C, E. Most likely no IFR ops (vintage Champ probably isn't IFR-equipped), so [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/411?at=2026) altimeter/static doesn't apply. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/413?at=2026) transponder applies if the airplane is transponder-equipped.
>
> No real surprises here. The integration point is just that the tailwheel endorsement, once earned, applies broadly -- so when the pilot picks up a Cub or a Stearman to ride somewhere, the same logbook entry covers them too."

### Stage 6: The fractional Citation Sovereign

> "Now the interesting one. Fractional share in a Citation Sovereign. The pilot here is *not* the operating pilot most of the time -- a fractional program manager (NetJets, Flexjet) hires the pilots. So the regulatory layers split:
>
> The owner side: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1001?at=2026) defines the fractional ownership program. The owner has operational control responsibilities under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1011?at=2026) -- they're the legal operator of the aircraft for their fractional flights. The program manager handles the operational infrastructure (scheduling, dispatch, training, maintenance) but operational control sits with the owner per the program agreement.
>
> The pilot side: NetJets pilots hold ATPs, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/58?at=2026) PIC proficiency checks (annually for type), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) flight reviews (often satisfied by [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1057?at=2026) or company training programs).
>
> The flight side: subparts A, B, C, D, E (universal). Subpart F ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/501?at=2026) -- turbojet multi-engine). Subpart G ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/601?at=2026) -- transport-category if the Sovereign is type-certificated transport-cat; the Citation Sovereign is technically Part 25, so subpart G applies). Subpart K ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1001?at=2026) onward -- fractional program rules). At altitude FL370+ -- subpart H [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026) RVSM. International segments -- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026).
>
> No SFAR for the Sovereign specifically. The integration is heaviest here -- five-plus subparts plus per-aircraft type rating plus the fractional ownership program agreement. The owner's job in the operational-control conversation is to know they have it; the pilots' job is to follow the company manuals and the regulations the manuals are built around."

### Stage 7: The integration question -- where does it break?

> "The integration breaks where the pilot wears one hat in their head but a different hat regulatorily. Three traps:
>
> One. He flies his RV-7 for business -- to a client meeting, say. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(a)(2) prohibits carriage for compensation in an experimental amateur-built. If the trip is reimbursed business travel and IRS treats it as compensation, he might have a [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(a)(2) issue. The 2021 LODA framework addresses CFI compensation but the personal-business-flight question is different.
>
> Two. He pays a friend to fly the 172 with his wife as 'safety pilot' or 'second-set-of-eyes' for cross-country instruction. If the friend is a CFI -- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026) and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026) compensation rules. If the friend is a private pilot getting paid -- that's [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026)(a) violation territory. The Mangiamele letter on cost-sharing applies.
>
> Three. He flies the Pitts with a friend along -- both wear parachutes per [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026)(c). But the parachute pack-cycle. He owns the rigs; he checks the rigger seal. If the seals are 200 days old, the rigs are out of cycle and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026) is being violated.
>
> Each of these is a concrete failure mode that comes from forgetting to integrate. The framing -- pilot, flight, operation -- combined with the cumulative-stack model and the SFAR overlay check is what catches them."

### Stage 8: Cleanup and SFAR check

> "And the final SFAR check. Across all five airplanes -- 172, RV-7, Pitts, Champ, Citation Sovereign -- which SFARs apply?
>
> 172: none.
> RV-7: none specific to the airframe; the operating-limitations document acts like an SFAR per-airplane.
> Pitts: none specific. If he flies aerobatics in airshow waivers, those are subpart J [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/903?at=2026) instruments, not SFARs.
> Champ: none.
> Citation Sovereign: no SFAR for the Sovereign type specifically. (SFAR 23 covers some older Citations and Falcons; the Sovereign isn't on the list.)
>
> So this pilot is SFAR-free, which is unusual for a five-airplane portfolio -- but it's because none of his types are R22, R44, MU-2B, or the older transport-category aircraft that have SFARs. If he ever picks up a helicopter, that calculus changes."

## Compressed answer (under 90 seconds)

> "Five airplanes, one pilot. Frame each as pilot / flight / operation. Pilot side stays the same -- private cert, current third-class, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) flight review, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) recency. Flight side stacks: 172 is universal A-E, no overlay. RV-7 adds [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) experimental rules and the operating-limitations document. Pitts at the box adds [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/303?at=2026) aerobatic and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/307?at=2026) parachute. Champ adds the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026)(i) tailwheel endorsement (one-time, broadly applicable). Citation Sovereign adds subparts F, G, H, K plus pro pilots' [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/58?at=2026) PIC proficiency checks. SFAR check: none of these airframes have an SFAR. The integration traps are [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(a)(2) compensation in the experimental, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026) compensation cases generally, and the parachute pack cycle in the Pitts."

## What goes wrong (failure modes)

| Failure mode                                                                          | Diagnosis |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------- |
| Lists Part 91 subparts but doesn't connect them to specific airplanes                 | Misses the integration -- the test isn't naming subparts, it's routing by airplane |
| Forgets the operating-limitations document is part of the experimental regulatory stack | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) plus the document; the document is binding |
| Treats fractional like Part 135                                                       | Fractional is Part 91 subpart K; the operational-control concept ports from 135 / 121 but the home is 91 |
| Says aerobatic flight requires a [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026) endorsement | No such endorsement. Aerobatic competence is experiential, tracked by training records but not codified as a Part 61 endorsement |
| Conflates [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/309?at=2026) and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/311?at=2026) | 309 = gliders / unpowered ultralight; 311 = banner / target / advertising (LOA required) |
| Forgets [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(a)(2) compensation prohibition | Carriage for compensation in experimental is prohibited; LODA framework addresses CFI instruction but personal business travel is its own analysis |
| Says RVSM is automatic above FL290                                                     | RVSM requires authorization under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026) -- aircraft, pilot training, operator |
| Forgets the parachute pack-cycle responsibility falls on the operator                  | Pilot's responsibility to check the seal date even if they didn't pack it themselves |
| Skips the SFAR check                                                                   | Reflexive habit; not all aircraft have SFARs but the check is cheap and cheap-skipped is expensive when it bites |
| Confuses the two formation rules ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/111?at=2026)(a) vs (b)) | (a) = collision hazard catch-all; (b) = formation requires arrangement; (c) = no formation with passengers for hire |
| Treats subpart F applicability as "any turbine"                                        | F applies to large airplanes (> 12,500 lb), turbojet multi-engine, or fractional. A single-engine turboprop under 12,500 isn't subpart F. |
| Doesn't separate the CFI's regulatory role from the pilot's regulatory role            | When the pilot is also a CFI, two sets of rules ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/197?at=2026)) layer onto the pilot's operating rules ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/3?at=2026)). |

## Variant prompts

| Variant                                                                            | What changes |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------ |
| "Walk me through this pilot's year, but the experimental is an MU-2B."             | Add SFAR 108. Substantial training and currency on top of Part 91. |
| "Walk through the year, but the helicopter is an R44 not the Pitts."               | Add SFAR 73. Mast-bumping awareness, recurrent training. |
| "What changes if he flies the Citation Sovereign internationally to Mexico?"        | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/707?at=2026), foreign airspace rules, customs/eAPIS, the bilateral NAFTA-style provisions for Mexican operations. |
| "What changes if his wife is a student pilot doing solo cross-country in the 172?" | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/87?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/89?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/93?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/95?at=2026); CFI-mediated currency endorsements; the per-flight cross-country review and weather check by the CFI. |
| "What changes if he's the CFI giving instruction in any of these airplanes?"        | Layer in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/193?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/197?at=2026); the LODA framework for the experimental; the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) flight-review-as-CFI process. |
