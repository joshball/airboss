# Subpart F and subpart G -- name and locate

The first half of Part 91 (subparts A, B, C, D, E) covers the rules a working CFI applies daily: PIC authority, flight rules, equipment, maintenance, special operations. The second half (subparts F, G, H, I, J, K, L) covers the rules a working CFI rarely *applies* but regularly encounters in conversation, in the cockpit jumpseat of someone else's airplane, or when a question crosses from light GA into the corporate / fractional / international world.

This lesson covers subparts F and G. We don't memorize their interiors. We name them, locate them, and articulate when each applies. The pedagogical bar is "if a question lands in subpart F or G territory, can the CFI candidate find the answer?" -- not "can they recite it cold."

## What you'll be able to do

- State what subpart F applies to and when (large airplanes, turbojet, fractional ownership)
- State what subpart G applies to and when (RVSM operations, certain foreign-aircraft ops)
- Recognize a few high-yield sections in each subpart (locate, do not memorize)
- Articulate why a CFI candidate should know subparts F and G exist even though their daily flying never touches them

## Why this matters

The bar for "I should know this" tightens as a pilot's career advances. A CFI candidate flying for the first time in a King Air 350 needs subpart F. A pilot transitioning to a fractional program needs subpart F's [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/501?at=2026) and the dedicated subpart K (covered in [04-foreign-and-fractional.md](04-foreign-and-fractional.md)). A pilot riding the jumpseat on an RVSM-eligible flight needs to know what RVSM is and where the rule lives.

A CFI who cannot locate subpart F when asked "what governs this King Air operation under Part 91?" loses the student. The locate-first skill matters here as much as anywhere in the course.

## The discovery question

Three scenarios:

1. A student of yours is hired to fly right seat in a corporate Citation 560XL. The chief pilot tells the new hire "you'll need to know subpart F for the company orientation."
2. A pilot you mentor flies a TBM 940 (single-engine turboprop, six seats, max takeoff 7,394 lb). They wonder which subpart of Part 91 governs their operation -- subpart F or the general subpart B.
3. A friend is flying a leased Falcon 7X across the Atlantic at FL410. Their dispatcher mentions RVSM authorization and a specific subpart G section.

Pause. Where is each scenario governed?

1. Citation 560XL: large turbine multi-engine, type-rated. Subpart F applies (specifically [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/501?at=2026) on applicability, plus the relevant operating sections).
2. TBM 940: single-engine turboprop, under 12,500 lb max gross. Subpart F's applicability section ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/501?at=2026)) does *not* cover this airplane in most paragraphs. The TBM operates under the general Part 91 subparts (A, B, C, E). The TBM 850, however, may reach the 12,500 lb threshold depending on the variant.
3. Falcon 7X at FL410: RVSM (Reduced Vertical Separation Minimum) airspace from FL290 to FL410. Subpart G section [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026) governs RVSM operations. The dispatcher and pilot need RVSM-authorized aircraft and pilot training.

The skill is recognizing which subpart applies in each scenario, not reciting the substance.

## Subpart F -- large and turbine-powered multi-engine airplanes; fractional

The full title: "Large and Turbine-Powered Multiengine Airplanes and Fractional Ownership Program Aircraft."

Sections roughly [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/501?at=2026) through [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/535?at=2026). The shape:

```text
Subpart F -- Large and Turbine-Powered Multiengine Airplanes and
             Fractional Ownership Program Aircraft

§91.501  Applicability
§91.503  Flying equipment and operating information (the "kit")
§91.505  Familiarity with operating limitations and emergency procedures
§91.507  Equipment requirements: Over-the-top or night VFR ops
§91.509  Survival equipment for overwater operations
§91.511  Communication and navigation equipment for overwater operations
§91.513  Emergency equipment
§91.515  Flight altitude rules
§91.517  Passenger information signs
§91.519  Passenger briefing
§91.521  Shoulder harness
§91.523  Carry-on baggage
§91.525  Carriage of cargo
§91.527  Operating in icing conditions
§91.529  Flight engineer requirements
§91.531  Second in command requirements
§91.533  Flight attendant requirements
§91.535  Stowage of food, beverage, and passenger service equipment
            during aircraft movement on the surface, takeoff, and landing
```

### When does subpart F apply?

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/501?at=2026)(a) and (b) define applicability. Most paragraphs apply to:

- Large airplanes (over 12,500 lb max certificated takeoff weight)
- Turbojet-powered multi-engine airplanes (any weight)
- Fractional-ownership program aircraft (any weight, in fractional service)

Some paragraphs apply more broadly; others narrower. The trap on subpart F applicability: a single-engine turbine (TBM, PC-12, Caravan) is *not* subpart F by virtue of being turbine -- subpart F's turbine threshold requires multi-engine *and* turbojet. A King Air 350 (multi-engine turboprop) is over 12,500 lb so falls under the "large" prong; a Citation Mustang (multi-engine turbojet) falls under the "turbojet" prong; a TBM 940 (single-engine turboprop, under 12,500) falls under neither and operates under the general Part 91 subparts.

### Sections worth knowing exist

- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/503?at=2026) -- Flying equipment and operating information.** The "kit" rule. Required publications, manuals, and equipment to be aboard for the flight. Cockpit-organization adjacent.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/505?at=2026) -- Familiarity with operating limitations and emergency procedures.** Each crewmember must be familiar with the aircraft's V-speeds, system limitations, and emergency procedures. The training-record adjacent rule.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/513?at=2026) -- Emergency equipment.** Megaphone, life rafts (overwater), first aid, fire extinguishers. The "what's in the cabin" stack.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/519?at=2026) -- Passenger briefing.** What every passenger must be told before takeoff (smoking, seatbelts, exits, life jackets, oxygen). Adjacent to the smaller [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/107?at=2026) briefing in subpart B.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/527?at=2026) -- Operating in icing conditions.** When icing equipment is required, when departure or flight in icing is prohibited.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/531?at=2026) -- Second in command requirements.** When an SIC is required by Part 91 (typically when the type certificate requires two pilots, or when the operation is fractional).
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/533?at=2026) -- Flight attendant requirements.** Cabin crew thresholds (typically 19+ passenger seats).

### Fractional ownership and subpart K

Subpart F has historically governed fractional ownership in the same regulatory home as large/turbine. As of recent FAA reorganization, fractional gets its own subpart K ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1001?at=2026) onward). Subpart K is a more substantive operational set covering fractional-ownership program management, training, ops, manuals, drug and alcohol testing, and operational control. Most fractional rules now live in subpart K rather than subpart F. We touch subpart K in [04-foreign-and-fractional.md](04-foreign-and-fractional.md).

The locate skill: a question about a fractional ops manual is subpart K. A question about the airplane's operating equipment requirement is subpart F.

## Subpart G -- additional equipment and operating requirements for large and transport-category aircraft

The full title: "Additional Equipment and Operating Requirements for Large and Transport Category Aircraft."

Sections roughly [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/601?at=2026) through [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/613?at=2026). The shape:

```text
Subpart G -- Additional Equipment and Operating Requirements for
             Large and Transport Category Aircraft

§91.601  Applicability
§91.603  Aural speed warning device
§91.605  Transport category civil airplane weight limitations
§91.607  Emergency exits for airplanes carrying passengers for hire
§91.609  Flight data recorders and cockpit voice recorders
§91.611  Authorization for ferry flights with one engine inoperative
§91.613  Materials for compartment interiors
§91.615  (Reserved)
```

### When does subpart G apply?

Subpart G adds to subpart F for *transport-category* aircraft and large airplanes carrying passengers for hire. The transport-category designation is from 14 CFR Part 25 (the certification standards for transport-category airplanes -- airliners and large business jets).

If the airplane is transport-category (Part 25 type certificate), subpart G applies in addition to subpart F. The "additional" in the subpart title is literal: F first, then G adds.

### Sections worth knowing exist

- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/603?at=2026) -- Aural speed warning device.** Required for turbojet aircraft; the alarm if the airplane exceeds Vmo or Mmo.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/605?at=2026) -- Transport category civil airplane weight limitations.** Where the takeoff/landing weight limits adjusted for runway length, climb gradient, and obstacle clearance live.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/609?at=2026) -- Flight data recorders and cockpit voice recorders.** Which aircraft require FDR / CVR, the recording duration, the post-incident preservation requirements.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/611?at=2026) -- Authorization for ferry flights with one engine inoperative.** A multi-engine airplane can be ferried (without passengers) with one engine inoperative under specific authorization. Maintenance ferry flight territory.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/613?at=2026) -- Materials for compartment interiors.** Cabin-material burn requirements; transport-category specific.

## Subpart G international addendum -- foreign aircraft and RVSM

A second piece of subpart G that gets confused with the first: rules for foreign aircraft and U.S. aircraft operating internationally. These appear in subpart H (Foreign Aircraft Operations and Operations of U.S.-Registered Civil Aircraft Outside of the United States; and Rules Governing Persons On Board Such Aircraft) which is sections [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/701?at=2026) through [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/715?at=2026). The most-cited:

- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/701?at=2026) -- Applicability.** What subpart H covers (foreign air carriers in U.S. airspace, U.S. operators outside the U.S., persons aboard either).
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026) -- Operations of civil aircraft of U.S. registry outside of the United States.** When ICAO rules apply, when U.S. rules continue, what conflicts mean.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/705?at=2026) -- Operations within airspace designated as Minimum Navigation Performance Specifications (MNPS) airspace.** North Atlantic operations.
- **[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026) -- Operations within airspace designed as Reduced Vertical Separation Minimum (RVSM) airspace.** RVSM is from FL290 to FL410 with 1,000-foot vertical separation (instead of 2,000); requires authorized aircraft, training, and procedures. High-yield for any pilot transitioning to turbojet operations because RVSM authorization is a per-aircraft, per-operator package.

## Where these subparts sit

Subparts F and G represent a step up in operational complexity from the general subparts:

```text
Part 91 -- General Operating and Flight Rules

  Subpart A -- General                                  (everyone)
  Subpart B -- Flight Rules                             (every flight)
  Subpart C -- Equipment, Instrument, Certificate Reqs  (every aircraft)
  Subpart D -- Special Flight Operations                (some flights)
  Subpart E -- Maintenance, Preventive Maint, Alts      (every aircraft)

  Subpart F -- Large and Turbine                        (some aircraft)
  Subpart G -- Additional for Transport / Large         (subset of F)
  Subpart H -- Foreign / U.S. International / RVSM      (some flights)
  Subpart I -- Operating Noise Limits                   (some aircraft)
  Subpart J -- Waivers                                  (per-application)
  Subpart K -- Fractional Ownership                     (fractional)
  Subpart L -- Continued Airworthiness / Safety Improvements
```

Subparts F, G, H, I, J, K, L are stacked: each *adds* to the universal A-E baseline for the population it applies to. A King Air 350 in fractional service operates under subparts A, B, C, D, E (universal) plus F (large and turbine) plus K (fractional) plus possibly H (if international) plus possibly I (noise).

## Common misreadings

- **"Subpart F applies to any turbine airplane."** Wrong. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/501?at=2026) limits applicability to large airplanes (over 12,500 lb), turbojet multi-engine, or fractional. A single-engine turboprop (TBM, PC-12) under 12,500 lb is not subpart F; it's general Part 91.
- **"Subpart G is the international subpart."** Half-true. The "G" in this lesson covers two unrelated topics that share the letter: G itself (additional equipment for transport/large) and the RVSM and international rules in subpart H ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/701?at=2026) onward). The RVSM rule lives in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026), which is *subpart H*, not subpart G.
- **"Fractional rules are in subpart F."** Historically yes; currently the substantive fractional rules live in subpart K ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/1001?at=2026) onward). Subpart F retains some fractional applicability anchors.
- **"RVSM is automatic above FL290."** Wrong. RVSM airspace exists from FL290 to FL410 in many parts of the world, but operating in it requires *RVSM authorization* under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026) -- aircraft equipment, pilot training, operator authorization. A non-RVSM-authorized aircraft cannot legally operate in RVSM airspace.
- **"Subpart F passenger briefing replaces the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/107?at=2026) briefing."** No. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/519?at=2026) (subpart F passenger briefing) adds requirements to [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/107?at=2026) for large/turbine ops. Both apply when an airplane falls under subpart F.
- **"Transport-category airplanes are airliners."** Roughly. Transport category is the Part 25 type-certification category. Most airliners are transport-category, but not all transport-category airplanes are airliners (some large business jets are also transport-category).
- **"FDR and CVR are required on every Part 91 turbojet."** Wrong. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/609?at=2026) sets specific thresholds (passenger seat counts, certification dates). Many small turbojets in pure Part 91 ops are exempt. The thresholds tighten under Part 135 and Part 121.

### Apply the rules

> A flight school instructor moves to a corporate department flying a Citation 560XL on Part 91 trips. They ask which subpart F sections matter most for daily flying.

Answer: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/503?at=2026) (operating-info "kit"), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/505?at=2026) (familiarity with V-speeds and emergency procedures), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/513?at=2026) (emergency equipment), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/519?at=2026) (passenger briefing -- expanded from [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/107?at=2026)), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/527?at=2026) (icing). The CVR/FDR rule from subpart G ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/609?at=2026)) and RVSM rule from subpart H ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026)) likely also apply because of the airplane's certification basis and typical altitude band.

> A pilot files IFR FL370 over the North Atlantic. Which Part 91 sections govern?

Answer: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/703?at=2026) (operations of U.S. aircraft outside the U.S.), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/705?at=2026) (MNPS), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/706?at=2026) (RVSM since FL370 is in the FL290-FL410 RVSM band). ICAO rules apply where U.S. rules don't conflict.

> A new TBM 850 owner asks "what does subpart F say about my airplane?"

Answer: Subpart F's main applicability tests don't catch a single-engine turboprop under 12,500 lb. The TBM operates under the general Part 91 subparts (A, B, C, D, E). Most subpart F provisions are inapplicable. (The TBM owner manual will reference Part 91 generally; subpart F isn't the home subpart for their aircraft.)
