# Restricted, limited, and experimental category operating rules

Most aircraft a working CFI flies hold a *standard* airworthiness certificate. The airplane was designed, certified, and is being operated under the same rules. Subparts D rules from [01-special-flight-ops.md](01-special-flight-ops.md) (aerobatics, parachute, towing) sit on top of normal-category operations.

A second class of aircraft holds a *special* airworthiness certificate. These are airplanes the FAA has decided don't fit the normal certification box -- restricted (purpose-built for ag, fire, survey), limited (post-WWII surplus military), provisional, experimental (homebuilts, R&D, exhibition), primary, or light-sport. Each gets a special certificate with operating restrictions baked in.

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/313?at=2026) through [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) (and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/327?at=2026) for light-sport) are the operating rules that go with each special-airworthiness category. A working CFI rarely flies any of these for hire, but does encounter them: a homebuilt at the airport, a former L-39 in a private collection, a restored Stearman doing rides. The questions are "who can fly it?" and "who can ride in it?"

## What you'll be able to do

- Distinguish restricted, limited, and experimental airworthiness categories at first glance
- State the passenger restriction common to [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/313?at=2026) (restricted) and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) (experimental): no person may be carried unless essential to the purpose of the flight
- Apply the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(b) over-densely-populated-area restriction for experimentals
- Cite the operating-limitations document each special-airworthiness aircraft carries, and recognize it as the binding restriction (not the regulation alone)
- Recognize light-sport airworthiness ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/327?at=2026)) and how it differs from experimental light-sport

## Why this matters

A pilot who climbs into an experimental amateur-built and takes a non-essential passenger is in a [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) violation regardless of whether the airplane is well-built and the pilot is highly qualified. A flight school that buys a former military trainer in restricted category cannot use it for primary training. A homebuilder who logs a Phase I flight test over a populated area is operating in violation of [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(b). These aren't paperwork violations; they're consequence-bearing operational restrictions, and the operating-limitations document on the aircraft is the binding contract.

CFIs encounter these aircraft regularly because owners want training in them. The CFI's first question should not be "can I instruct in this?" but "what airworthiness category is the airplane?" The answer routes the rest of the conversation.

## The discovery question

Imagine four phone calls:

1. A homebuilder finishing his RV-7 wants you to do his Phase I flight testing. He'll log the hours, you'll be PIC. He's offering to pay you.
2. An owner of a restored P-51 Mustang (restricted category) wants you to give his nephew a ride at his airport's open house.
3. A flight school director wants to know if their restricted-category Air Tractor (used for crop dusting Mon-Fri) can be used for tailwheel transition training on weekends.
4. The owner of a Cessna 172 light-sport (LSA) wants you to instruct her in it for a sport-pilot certificate. The airplane has a special light-sport airworthiness certificate, not standard.

Pause. What's the answer in each case?

1. The Phase I program is governed by [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(b) and the airplane's operating-limitations document. Phase I prohibits carrying persons not essential to the flight. You can be PIC if the operating limitations name an authorized test pilot, but the homebuilder can't be aboard until Phase I is complete (typically 25-40 hours flown without incident). Compensation is also restricted under most experimental amateur-built operating limitations.
2. The P-51 in restricted category cannot carry passengers under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/313?at=2026) unless the person is essential to the purpose of the flight. A nephew at an open house is not essential. The ride is illegal.
3. Restricted-category Air Tractors are airworthiness-restricted to the *special purpose* on the airworthiness certificate (agricultural). Tailwheel transition training is not "agricultural"; it would require a separate authorization or a different airframe.
4. Light-sport airworthiness ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/327?at=2026)) is its own animal. A Cessna 172 with a special light-sport certificate is unusual but possible (a 1958-1963 C-172 conversion); the operating rules differ from a standard-airworthiness 172.

These four scenarios are why this lesson exists.

## §91.313 -- restricted category civil aircraft

The restricted-category aircraft has a special airworthiness certificate issued for a specific *special purpose* under 14 CFR 21.25. The special purposes include agriculture, forestry, fish wildlife conservation, aerial surveying, patrolling pipelines and powerlines, weather control, aerial advertising, and "other operations specified by the Administrator."

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/313?at=2026) tightens what these aircraft can do:

```text
§91.313  Restricted category civil aircraft: Operating limitations.

(a) No person may operate a restricted category civil aircraft --

  (1) For other than the special purpose for which it is certificated;
      or

  (2) In an operation other than one necessary to accomplish the work
      activity directly associated with that special purpose.

(b) For the purpose of paragraph (a) of this section, in addition to
    activities required by law, operations of the following types are
    considered necessary to accomplish the work activity directly
    associated with the special purpose for which a restricted category
    aircraft is certificated:

  (1) Flights for training in the necessary special skills required
      for that special purpose; and

  (2) Flights for the purpose of conducting nonpassenger-carrying
      airlift missions ... [federal/state/local governmental purposes]

(c) No person may operate a restricted category civil aircraft
    carrying persons or property for compensation or hire.

(d) No person may be carried in a restricted category civil aircraft
    unless that person --

  (1) Is a flight crewmember;

  (2) Is a flight crewmember trainee;

  (3) Performs an essential function in connection with the special
      purpose for which the aircraft is certificated; or

  (4) Is necessary to accomplish the work activity directly associated
      with that special purpose.

(e) (over densely populated area / congested airway / near busy
    airport restrictions, with carve-outs)
```

Five things to extract:

1. **Restricted category aircraft fly only for the certificated special purpose.** A crop-dusting Air Tractor cannot fly tailwheel transition training. A surveying aircraft cannot fly to lunch.
2. **Training in the special-purpose skill is allowed.** A new ag pilot can train in the Air Tractor for ag operations specifically.
3. **No carriage for compensation or hire.** A restricted-category aircraft cannot be a charter or air tour aircraft.
4. **Only essential persons may be aboard.** Crew, crew trainees, persons performing essential function, persons necessary to accomplish the special purpose. The "nephew at the open house" is not on this list.
5. **Restrictions on populated areas, airways, and busy airports.** The restricted-category aircraft is held away from the parts of the system the FAA designed for normal-category traffic.

## §91.315 -- limited category civil aircraft

Limited-category aircraft are former military aircraft (typically WWII-era) that were certified to a special "limited" airworthiness standard during the post-war demilitarization wave. The category is largely vestigial today; few aircraft remain in limited category. The operating restriction:

```text
§91.315  Limited category civil aircraft: Operating limitations.

No person may operate a limited category civil aircraft carrying
persons or property for compensation or hire.
```

That's the entire section. One sentence. The carriage-for-hire prohibition is the only [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/315?at=2026) operating limitation. The aircraft's operating-limitations document carries everything else.

## §91.317 -- provisionally certificated civil aircraft

A "provisional" airworthiness certificate is issued during the type-certification process for a new aircraft -- the manufacturer wants to begin limited operations before final type certification. Niche; the operating restrictions live in the provisional certificate itself and largely mirror restricted-category limits ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/317?at=2026) lists special-purpose operations and prohibits carriage for hire and persons not essential to the flight). Locate; do not memorize.

## §91.319 -- aircraft having experimental certificates

The biggest population in this lesson by far. "Experimental" is the umbrella for everything outside the normal certification box: amateur-built (homebuilt), exhibition, racing, R&D, market survey, crew training, kit-built, and experimental light-sport.

```text
§91.319  Aircraft having experimental certificates: Operating
limitations.

(a) No person may operate an aircraft that has an experimental
    certificate --

  (1) For other than the purpose for which the certificate was issued;
      or

  (2) Carrying persons or property for compensation or hire.

(b) No person may operate an aircraft that has an experimental
    certificate over a densely populated area or in a congested airway.
    The Administrator may, however, authorize operations over a densely
    populated area or in a congested airway, in accordance with terms
    and conditions specified in the airworthiness certificate.

(c) Unless authorized by the Administrator in accordance with §21.195
    of this chapter, no person may conduct flight tests of an aircraft
    that has an experimental certificate carrying persons or property
    for compensation or hire.

(d) Each person operating an aircraft that has an experimental
    certificate shall --

  (1) Advise each person carried of the experimental nature of the
      aircraft;

  (2) Operate under VFR, day only, unless otherwise specifically
      authorized by the Administrator; and

  (3) Notify the control tower of the experimental nature of the
      aircraft when operating into or out of airports with operating
      control towers.

(e) No person may operate an aircraft that is issued an experimental
    certificate ... after January 31, 1981, in a flight to operate
    above 18,000 feet MSL ... [exception path]
```

Six things to extract:

1. **Operate only for the purpose for which the experimental certificate was issued.** A homebuilt certificated for "amateur-built" cannot be used for racing without a re-issued certificate.
2. **No carriage for hire.** Same prohibition as restricted and limited.
3. **No flight over densely populated areas or in congested airways unless specifically authorized.** This is the famous "Phase I" restriction: the operating limitations on a new amateur-built typically prohibit flight over densely populated areas during the test-flight phase.
4. **Each person aboard must be told the airplane is experimental.** The brief is non-optional. The phrase "experimental airplane" is included in the standard pre-flight passenger briefing for any experimental.
5. **VFR day only, unless specifically authorized.** Many experimental aircraft are authorized for night and IFR via their operating-limitations document, but the *default* under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(d)(2) is VFR day only.
6. **Tower notification required.** When operating to or from a towered airport, the pilot must advise the tower of the experimental nature of the aircraft. (Tower will sometimes assign a specific procedure or pattern.)

The (e) paragraph adds an FL180+ restriction that has its own carve-out path. Niche; mention but do not memorize.

### The operating-limitations document

The substantive restrictions on an experimental aircraft live in the *operating-limitations document* the FAA issues with the airworthiness certificate. The document specifies:

- Purpose for which the certificate is issued (amateur-built, exhibition, R&D, etc.)
- Geographic flight test area for Phase I
- Required flight-test hours before Phase II (passenger-carrying allowed)
- Specific authorizations for night, IFR, IMC, formation, aerobatics
- Conditions for showing the airplane at airshows or fly-ins
- Inspection requirements (condition inspection annually for amateur-built; not the same as a standard "annual inspection" under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/409?at=2026))

The pilot is bound by both [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) *and* the operating-limitations document. The document typically references and incorporates [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) directly. A pilot who knows [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) but hasn't read the operating limitations is not flying legally.

### Phase I and Phase II

Amateur-built and most experimental aircraft begin life in **Phase I** -- the flight-test phase. Under the standard operating limitations, Phase I requires:

- 25-40 hours of test flight (typically 40 for FAA-certificated engine, 25 for certificated engine *and* propeller)
- Solo pilot only -- no person aboard who isn't essential to the test flight
- Within the geographic flight test area specified
- No flight over densely populated areas or in congested airways
- Specific airframe and engine performance verified before signoff

When Phase I is complete and the airframe is signed off, the airplane transitions to **Phase II** -- normal experimental operations. Passengers may now be carried (after the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(d)(1) experimental briefing). Geographic restrictions lift. The airplane operates anywhere, subject to the rest of [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) and the operating limitations.

## §91.327 -- light-sport category aircraft

Special light-sport airworthiness is its own thing. A factory-built airplane meeting the ASTM consensus standards holds a *special light-sport* airworthiness certificate. The operating rules:

- VFR day-only by default, with night allowed if equipped and authorized
- Specific aircraft category (airplane, weight-shift, glider, gyroplane, lighter-than-air, powered parachute) determined by the certificate
- Maintenance under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/327?at=2026) and the manufacturer-published maintenance program (the "LSRM" repairman cert authorizes it)

Note the distinction between *special light-sport* (factory-built, ASTM-compliant, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/327?at=2026) governs) and *experimental light-sport* (kit-built or modified, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) governs with light-sport-specific operating limitations). The distinction matters operationally: factory-built LSAs can be used for instruction and rental; experimental light-sport often has flight-instruction restrictions identical to other experimentals.

## Common misreadings

- **"Experimental amateur-built can fly anywhere after Phase I."** Almost. Even after Phase I, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(b) prohibits flight over densely populated areas and in congested airways absent specific authorization in the operating limitations. Most modern operating limitations grant this authorization for Phase II, but the regulation default is restrictive.
- **"Restricted-category aircraft can fly any commercial mission."** Wrong. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/313?at=2026)(c) prohibits carriage for hire, and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/313?at=2026)(a) limits ops to the certificated special purpose. A restricted Air Tractor flies only ag missions; period.
- **"Experimental aircraft can fly IFR if equipped."** Conditional. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(d)(2) defaults to VFR day only. IFR and night require specific authorization in the operating-limitations document. A panel-equipped experimental without that authorization is still VFR day only legally.
- **"The Phase I solo restriction is in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)."** Partially. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) prohibits carrying persons not essential to the flight. The specific Phase I geography and hours come from the operating-limitations document; the *no-passenger* rule comes from the regulation; the *no-densely-populated-area* rule comes from the regulation; the *flight test area boundary* is the operating limitations.
- **"Light-sport and experimental are interchangeable."** Wrong. Special light-sport (factory-built, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/327?at=2026)) and experimental light-sport (kit, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)) are different airworthiness categories with different operating rules, different maintenance authorities, and different rental and instruction permissions.
- **"Anyone can fly an experimental."** With a current pilot certificate of the appropriate category and class, yes. But the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(d)(1) experimental briefing is mandatory for *every* passenger every flight. A CFI giving a flight in an experimental must brief the student that the airplane is experimental.
- **"A restricted-category aircraft can carry the owner."** Only if the owner is essential to the special-purpose mission ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/313?at=2026)(d)). Owner-along-for-the-ride does not satisfy "essential to the purpose."
- **"Carrying a CFI for instruction in an experimental requires special authorization."** Half-true. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(a)(2) prohibits carriage for compensation. Compensated CFI instruction in an experimental amateur-built is governed by the LODA (Letter of Deviation Authority) framework that emerged after the FAA tightened its position in 2021. Without a LODA the CFI's compensation creates a [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(a)(2) issue.

### Apply the rules

> A homebuilt RV-7 is at hour 18 of its Phase I 40-hour test program. The owner asks you to fly with him to log time. Legal?

Answer: No. Phase I prohibits persons not essential to the test flight ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026) plus the operating-limitations document). You cannot be aboard until Phase I is complete and signed off (typically by the builder logging the required hours and entering the condition-inspection sign-off in the airframe logbook).

> A flight school owns a restricted-category former-military Stearman used for "exhibition" displays at airshows. They want to use it for tailwheel transition training Monday-Friday. Legal?

Answer: No. The Stearman in restricted category is restricted to the certificated special purpose ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/313?at=2026)(a)). Tailwheel transition is not "exhibition." [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/313?at=2026)(b)(1) carves out training in the *special-purpose skills*, but tailwheel transition is general primary instruction, not exhibition-specific skill training.

> A CFI accepts compensation to give dual in a Cessna 172 with a special light-sport airworthiness certificate. Legal?

Answer: Yes. Special light-sport ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/327?at=2026)) factory-built aircraft can be used for instruction and rental. The CFI's pilot certificate must include the appropriate sport-pilot or higher rating, and the airplane category is bound by the special light-sport definition.

> Same scenario but the airplane is an experimental amateur-built that the student owns. The CFI is being paid for instruction. Legal?

Answer: Conditional. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(a)(2) prohibits carriage for compensation. The 2021 FAA position required a LODA (Letter of Deviation Authority) for compensated CFI instruction in an experimental amateur-built. With the LODA, instruction is legal; without it, the CFI's compensation creates a [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/319?at=2026)(a)(2) issue.
