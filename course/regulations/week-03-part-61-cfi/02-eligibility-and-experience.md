# CFI eligibility, aeronautical experience, and instructor records

61.183, 61.187, and 61.189 are the three sections that take a commercial pilot from "I want to teach" to "I am authorized to teach, and here is the record I keep while I do it." They are the gate, the test, and the file cabinet.

You walked the shape of subpart H in [01-subpart-h-walk.md](01-subpart-h-walk.md). This lesson zooms into the three sections that govern getting in and staying organized. 61.185 (the knowledge subjects list) is referenced here as the syllabus that 61.183(e)/(f) test against; we don't deep-dive 61.185 because its content is "the topics on the FOI and FIA written tests" and you'll see it again as the table of contents for your test prep.

## What you'll be able to do

- Recite the eight CFI eligibility requirements in 61.183 from memory, and predict which ones a typical commercial-rated candidate has already satisfied
- State what 61.187 actually requires (areas of operation, right-seat, training-from-an-authorized-instructor) and what it doesn't require (no minimum hour count beyond the underlying commercial)
- Run the 61.189 record obligation correctly: the student-side endorsement AND the instructor-side ledger, kept for at least 3 years
- Diagnose why most CFI applicants who fail the records portion of an FSDO inspection fail it the same way

## Why this matters

These three sections are operationally invisible to a pilot who has never been a CFI, and operationally constant to one who is. Every endorsement you sign for the rest of your career runs through 61.189. Every initial-CFI applicant in the country goes through 61.183. The aeronautical experience requirements in 61.187 are what your CFI training plan has to satisfy, in addition to whatever your instructor's syllabus says.

The three reasons working CFIs get in trouble are: (1) signing an endorsement they weren't authorized to sign (a 61.193/61.195 failure, covered in lesson 03), (2) keeping incomplete or absent records (a 61.189 failure), and (3) letting the certificate lapse (a 61.197 failure, covered in lesson 04). Two of the three are this lesson.

## The discovery question

You finish the CFI initial practical test on Tuesday morning. Your DPE hands you the temporary certificate at 11:00. By Tuesday afternoon, the local flight school has scheduled your first lesson with a primary student, Wednesday at 8:00 AM. The student is going to do solo work in three weeks.

What records does the regulation require you to keep, starting tomorrow morning? Where do you keep them? For how long? And which records do you keep that the student never sees?

Pause and think.

The answer is in 61.189(b). You owe the student a logbook entry every time you fly with them or give ground (61.189(a)). Separately, you owe yourself a ledger that records every solo endorsement, every knowledge-test endorsement, and every practical-test endorsement, with names, dates, and results, kept for 3 years (61.189(b) and (c)). This second record is the one most new CFIs forget because nobody tells them about it: the ledger never shows up in the student's logbook, but it's the document the FSDO inspector asks for.

If you skip ahead and only sign the student's logbook, you're 50% compliant with 61.189. The student-side documentation is real; the instructor-side ledger is also real and statutorily required.

## What 61.183 actually says

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/183?at=2026) gives the eight initial-CFI eligibility requirements. The exact text:

```text
14 CFR 61.183  Eligibility requirements.

To be eligible for a flight instructor certificate or rating
a person must:

(a) Be at least 18 years of age;

(b) Be able to read, speak, write, and understand the English
    language. ...;

(c) Hold either a commercial pilot certificate or airline transport
    pilot certificate with:
    (1) An aircraft category and class rating that is appropriate
        to the flight instructor rating sought; and
    (2) An instrument rating, or privileges on that person's pilot
        certificate that are appropriate to the flight instructor
        rating sought, if applying for --
        (i) A flight instructor certificate with an airplane category
            and single-engine class rating;
        (ii) A flight instructor certificate with an airplane category
             and multiengine class rating;
        (iii) A flight instructor certificate with a powered-lift
              category rating; or
        (iv) A flight instructor certificate with an instrument rating;

(d) Receive a logbook endorsement from an authorized instructor on the
    fundamentals of instructing listed in 61.185(a)(1) ...;

(e) Pass a knowledge test on the:
    (1) Fundamentals of instructing listed in 61.185(a)(1); and
    (2) Aeronautical knowledge areas listed in 61.185(a)(2)
        appropriate to the flight instructor rating sought;

(f) Pass a knowledge test on the areas of operation listed in
    61.187(b) appropriate to the flight instructor rating sought;

(g) Receive a logbook endorsement from an authorized instructor
    on the areas of operation listed in 61.187(b) ...;

(h) Pass the required practical test ...;

(i) Have received a logbook endorsement from an authorized
    instructor for the fundamentals of instructing required ...
    in fact, in (i): a logbook endorsement that the applicant has
    received flight training and is prepared to demonstrate
    instructional proficiency on stall awareness, spin entry, spins,
    and spin recovery procedures, in an aircraft of the appropriate
    category that is certificated for spins. ...

Note: structural paraphrase. Read the actual reg.
```

Eight items. Each one is a separate gate.

## What it actually means -- by paragraph

### (a) -- Age 18

This is the same minimum as commercial. By the time you hold a commercial certificate (which (c) requires), you are already at least 18. The age gate is rarely the bind.

### (b) -- English

Written into the certificate at the commercial level. Not re-tested at the CFI level unless the prior English determination was provisional or restricted. For most candidates this is a non-event.

### (c) -- Commercial or ATP, plus instrument rating where applicable

This is where the eligibility floor lives. The CFI rating "rides on" an underlying pilot certificate. You cannot CFI airplane single-engine without holding a commercial (or ATP) airplane single-engine. If you intend to instruct in instrument operations or in airplanes where instrument is operationally required (multi, powered-lift), you also need the instrument rating on your pilot certificate.

The trap: a commercial pilot with single-engine privileges who wants to add a CFII (instrument-airplane instructor) needs *the instrument rating on their commercial pilot certificate*. The CFII rides on the IR, not on the CFI alone.

### (d) -- FOI endorsement from an authorized instructor

The Fundamentals of Instructing -- the teaching-theory material in 61.185(a)(1) -- has to be signed off by an instructor before you can take the FOI knowledge test. The FOI material is universal across all CFI ratings (it's about teaching, not flying), so you only need to receive this endorsement once in your CFI career. AC 61-65 publishes the recommended language for the FOI endorsement (paragraph A.36 in current revisions).

If you already have a CFI rating in any aircraft category and are adding another (for example, CFI airplane adding CFI helicopter), the FOI endorsement and the FOI knowledge test under (e)(1) are *not* re-required. The FOI is one-time-forever.

### (e) -- Two-part knowledge test

The "FOI" written test (subpart 1 -- the teaching subjects) and the aeronautical-knowledge written test (subpart 2 -- the aviation subjects from the underlying commercial knowledge bank, plus the topics in 61.51, 61.187, 61.189). At one point these were administered as separate tests; in current practice the FAA combines them into the FIA (Flight Instructor Airplane) test for airplane CFI candidates. Read the section: it specifies *two* knowledge tests. PSI and the FAA's contracted test centers will sometimes call this the "FOI plus FIA" combo. The regulation says two; the implementation can deliver them in one sitting.

The FOI is the test commercial-rated candidates routinely underestimate. The subjects (the learning process, methods of instruction, evaluating student performance, planning instructional activity) are not aviation subjects, and a candidate who reads only the FAA-S-ACS-25 expecting flight content will be unprepared. AC 60-14 (Aviation Instructor's Handbook) and FAA-H-8083-9 (Aviation Instructor's Handbook) are the source documents.

### (f) -- Knowledge test on the areas of operation in 61.187(b)

This is the FIA -- the aviation-subject test scoped to instructor-applicable areas of operation. It overlaps significantly with the commercial-pilot knowledge content but adds the teaching framing.

### (g) -- Practical-test prep endorsement

A separate endorsement from your CFI training instructor stating you've received training and are prepared for the practical. AC 61-65 paragraph A.37 publishes the recommended language.

### (h) -- Practical test

The CFI practical, given by a designated examiner against the FAA-S-ACS-25 (CFI Airplane Single-Engine Airman Certification Standards) or the equivalent ACS for whatever rating is being sought. Includes a ground portion (which is much heavier than commercial -- the candidate has to teach, not just demonstrate knowledge), and a flight portion *from the right seat*. Right-seat-rated proficiency is where many candidates lose form on the day; the same maneuvers a 250-hour commercial pilot has flown 100 times from the left seat get re-evaluated from the other side of the airplane.

### (i) -- The spin endorsement

Famous, tested, and a separate logbook endorsement from an authorized instructor stating you've received training in stall awareness, spin entry, spins, and spin recovery, and that you can teach those subjects. The endorsement is a *one-time* requirement at the CFI initial. We deep-dive in [05-endorsements.md](05-endorsements.md) (the spin endorsement is one of the canonical 61.183(i) endorsements that AC 61-65 publishes recommended language for, paragraph A.43).

The spin endorsement applies to airplane CFI initials regardless of single/multi: a CFI multi-engine candidate who takes the practical in a twin still needs the spin endorsement, signed in a spin-certified airplane (typical: a Citabria, Decathlon, Pitts).

## What 61.187 actually says

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/187?at=2026) -- "Aeronautical experience" and "areas of operation":

```text
14 CFR 61.187  Flight proficiency.

(a) General. A person who is applying for a flight instructor
    certificate must receive and log flight and ground training
    from an authorized instructor on the appropriate aeronautical
    knowledge areas of 61.185 and the areas of operation listed
    in this section. ...

(b) Areas of operation:
    (1) Fundamentals of instructing;
    (2) Technical subject areas;
    (3) Preflight preparation;
    (4) Preflight lesson on a maneuver to be performed in flight;
    (5) Preflight procedures;
    (6) Airport, seaplane base, and water operations, as appropriate;
    (7) Takeoffs, landings, and go-arounds;
    (8) Fundamentals of flight;
    (9) Performance maneuvers;
    (10) Ground reference maneuvers;
    (11) Slow flight, stalls, and spins;
    (12) Basic instrument maneuvers;
    (13) Emergency operations;
    (14) Postflight procedures.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg.
```

Read against the underlying-cert sections (61.109 private, 61.129 commercial), 61.187 has a wrinkle: it does not specify minimum hour counts. It specifies *areas of operation* and requires that training in those areas be received and logged. The hour bind is set by your underlying commercial pilot certificate (which you must already hold per 61.183(c)) and by whatever the school's syllabus or your CFI instructor's training plan demands.

In practical terms, the typical CFI initial candidate logs 25 to 40 hours of CFI-specific dual training: right-seat work, fundamentals-of-flight teaching practice, spin training, and the maneuvers from 61.187(b). The hours are a school-syllabus or CFI-syllabus matter; they're not in the regulation as a minimum.

The right-seat language is in 61.187 implicitly via the practical-test ACS -- the FAA-S-ACS-25 specifies the practical is conducted from the right seat. The regulation directs you to the ACS; the ACS sets the seat.

## What 61.189 actually says

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/189?at=2026) -- "Flight instructor records":

```text
14 CFR 61.189  Flight instructor records.

(a) A flight instructor must sign the logbook of each person to
    whom that instructor has given flight training or ground training.

(b) A flight instructor must maintain a record in a logbook or a
    separate document that contains the following:
    (1) The name of each person whose logbook or student pilot
        certificate that instructor has endorsed for solo flight
        privileges, and the date of the endorsement; and
    (2) The name of each person that instructor has endorsed for
        a knowledge test or practical test, and the record shall
        also indicate the kind of test, the date, and the results.

(c) Each flight instructor must retain the records required by this
    section for at least 3 years.
```

Three paragraphs. The first is what the *student* sees; the second and third are what the *instructor* keeps. Read paragraph by paragraph.

### (a) -- The student-side logbook

When you fly with a student or give them ground, you sign their logbook. This is what most pilots think of as "the CFI's record" because it's the part of the record they own.

The regulatory phrasing is "must sign the logbook of each person to whom that instructor has given ... training." Three implications:

1. The signature is mandatory, not optional. A CFI who declines to sign because the student didn't pay is not in compliance.
2. Both flight training and ground training generate signatures. A 30-minute ground session at the kitchen table gets signed.
3. The signature is in the *student's* logbook (or training record). It is not the instructor's record.

### (b) -- The instructor-side ledger

This is the obligation most new CFIs miss. Separate from the student's logbook, you maintain a record (in your own logbook or in a separate document) of:

- Every solo endorsement: name + date
- Every knowledge-test endorsement: name + date + which test + result
- Every practical-test endorsement: name + date + which test + result

The "results" piece is what makes this useful to the FAA. A CFI's pass-rate on practical tests is calculable from this record, which is what 61.197(a)(3) (the 80%-recommendation-rate renewal pathway) requires you to produce. It's also what the FSDO will inspect during oversight.

A typical new-CFI mistake: keeping no separate record at all, and pointing to the student's logbook ("I signed it in their book") when asked. The student's logbook is paragraph (a), not (b). They are different obligations.

### (c) -- Three years

The record obligation runs for three years from each entry. A CFI who has been instructing for ten years is legally required to retain *only* the most recent three years of solo / knowledge-test / practical-test endorsements -- but in practice, most CFIs keep them indefinitely because the records support 61.197 renewal calculations, build the CFI's professional history, and protect the CFI in any post-incident review.

The retention is on the instructor, not on the school the instructor works for. If you change schools, your records go with you. If you stop instructing, you keep the records for three years past your last endorsement.

## Where this sits

61.183 and 61.187 sit at the front of subpart H -- they are the *getting-in* sections. 61.189 sits in the middle, between (61.187 proficiency and 61.193 privileges); the placement is structural -- the records obligation activates the moment you become a CFI and runs forever.

```text
14 CFR Part 61  Subpart H  -- Flight Instructors Other Than Sport Pilot

  61.181  Applicability               (which CFIs this subpart governs)
  61.183  Eligibility requirements    <- THIS LESSON
  61.185  Aeronautical knowledge      (subjects on FOI / FIA tests)
  61.187  Flight proficiency          <- THIS LESSON
  61.189  Flight instructor records   <- THIS LESSON
  61.191  Additional flight instructor ratings  (adding categories/classes)
  61.193  Flight instructor privileges    (lesson 03)
  61.195  Flight instructor limitations   (lesson 03)
  61.197  Renewal                         (lesson 04)
  61.199  Expired CFI certificates
  61.201  [Reserved]
```

The eligibility/proficiency/records grouping is the same anatomy you saw in subparts E (private) and F (commercial): cert eligibility -> aero experience -> records / privileges / limitations. The difference at the H level: the records obligation is its own section because the CFI is the only pilot certificate where the holder generates records about *other people*, not just themselves.

## Common misreadings

- **"61.183 says I need 200 hours of dual given before I can apply."** Wrong. There is no such requirement in 61.183 (or anywhere in subpart H). The 200-hour-dual-given threshold is in 135.243(b) for an SIC-equivalent role at certain charter operators, not in CFI eligibility. A commercial-rated pilot with zero hours of dual given is eligible for the CFI initial as soon as they meet 61.183(a)-(i).
- **"The FOI knowledge test gets re-taken at every CFI renewal."** Wrong. The FOI knowledge test is one-time-forever (passed once, valid forever for CFI purposes). 61.183(e)(1) does not appear in the renewal section 61.197. The renewal options test recurrent material (FIRC content, practical test areas of operation), not the FOI subjects.
- **"I sign the student's logbook and that satisfies my record obligation."** Wrong per 61.189(b). The student-side logbook satisfies 61.189(a). The instructor-side ledger of solos / knowledge tests / practical tests is a separate, statutory obligation under 61.189(b) and must be retained 3 years per 61.189(c).
- **"61.187 sets the hour minimum for the CFI initial."** Wrong. 61.187 specifies *areas of operation* (a list of 14 subject blocks). It does not specify hours. The hour bind comes from your school's syllabus or your CFI instructor's training plan. A flight school can require 30 hours of right-seat training, but the regulation does not.
- **"The spin endorsement is required for every CFI rating."** Mostly true, with a caveat. 61.183(i) requires the spin endorsement for *initial* CFI airplane and powered-lift candidates. CFI helicopter and CFI glider candidates do not need the spin endorsement (rotorcraft and gliders have different aeronautical demands; the spin endorsement is airplane-specific). Adding a CFI rating to an existing CFI cert does not require a new spin endorsement -- it's one-time-forever.
- **"If I let my CFI lapse, my records are no longer required."** Wrong. The 3-year retention under 61.189(c) survives certificate lapse. A CFI whose certificate expires in 2026 still owes the FAA the records of solo / knowledge-test / practical-test endorsements signed between 2023 and 2026 if requested.
- **"The instructor-side ledger has to be paper."** Wrong. 61.189(b) says "in a logbook or a separate document." A spreadsheet, a structured database, the school's flight-training-management system -- all qualify, as long as the data elements (name, date, kind of test, result) are present and retainable for 3 years. AC 61-65 doesn't dictate the medium. FAA Order 8900.1 Volume 5 Chapter 1 (the airman certification surveillance order) describes what an inspector will ask for -- the data, not the format.

### Apply the rules

> A 235-hour commercial pilot with single-engine and instrument ratings wants to start CFI initial training next week. They've never given dual before. What's their eligibility status?

Answer: They meet 61.183(a) (assumed 18+), (b) (English at commercial level), and (c) (commercial with appropriate cat/class and IR). They still need (d) FOI endorsement, (e) and (f) knowledge tests, (g) practical-test prep endorsement, (h) practical test, and (i) spin endorsement. There is no minimum hours-of-dual-given requirement in 61.183. They are *eligible to begin CFI training* immediately.

> A new CFI gives a primary student a 30-minute pre-flight ground briefing on Tuesday morning, then takes them flying for 1.0. What records does the CFI owe afterward?

Answer: 61.189(a) -- sign the student's logbook entry showing 1.0 dual given (and any associated training topics). The 30-minute ground portion may be combined with the flight entry or recorded as a separate ground entry. There is no 61.189(b) entry yet because no solo, knowledge-test, or practical-test endorsement has been signed. The instructor-side ledger does not get an entry from a routine flight lesson.

> Same scenario as above, but the next day the CFI signs the student's pre-solo knowledge test endorsement and the student takes the test the same afternoon, passes. What records does the CFI owe?

Answer: Now the 61.189(b) ledger gets an entry: student name, date of endorsement, "pre-solo knowledge test" (per 61.87(b)), and the test result (passed). The student's logbook also gets the 61.87 endorsement signed in. The CFI must retain the (b) ledger entry for at least 3 years.

> An ATP-rated, 5000-hour airline first officer wants to add a CFI initial rating. They want to skip the FOI test because they "already know how to teach." Are they exempt?

Answer: No. 61.183(e)(1) requires the FOI knowledge test for any initial CFI applicant who does not already hold a CFI certificate. The test is one-time-forever, but they have to take it once. The 5000 hours of airline experience is not relevant to 61.183 eligibility -- airline experience is governed by Part 121 and 61.159, neither of which exempt anyone from CFI initial eligibility under 61.183.

### Trap detector

| Statement                                                            | Trap and correction |
| -------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| "I need 200 hours of dual given before I can apply for the CFI."     | Wrong. No such requirement in 61.183. The hour bind is the underlying commercial cert (250 hr per 61.129) and whatever syllabus governs. |
| "Signing the student's logbook covers all my CFI record obligations." | Wrong. 61.189(a) covers the student-side. 61.189(b) requires a separate instructor-side ledger of solos / knowledge tests / practical tests, retained 3 years. |
| "The spin endorsement is required for every CFI add-on rating."      | Wrong. 61.183(i) is for initial airplane and powered-lift. Subsequent ratings (CFII, MEI) do not re-require the spin endorsement. |
| "I have to use a paper logbook for the 61.189(b) record."            | Wrong. 61.189(b) accepts "a logbook or a separate document." Spreadsheet, database, or training-management software -- as long as the data elements are present and retainable. |
| "If my CFI cert expires, my records obligation ends."                | Wrong. 3-year retention under 61.189(c) survives the certificate lapse. |
| "I can take the CFI practical test from the left seat."              | Wrong. FAA-S-ACS-25 specifies the practical is conducted from the right seat. 61.187 directs you to the ACS. |
| "61.187 has minimum hour requirements for CFI training."             | Wrong. 61.187 specifies areas of operation, not hours. The hours come from the syllabus or training plan, not from the regulation. |
