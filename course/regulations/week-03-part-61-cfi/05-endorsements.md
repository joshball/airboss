# Endorsements -- AC 61-65 and the daily-CFI signature set

Endorsements are the operational currency of the CFI's pen. Every authority you exercise as a CFI lands as a logbook signature. The regulation specifies *when* you may sign; AC 61-65 specifies *what to write*. Knowing both -- and the difference between them -- is the working CFI's daily-life skill.

This lesson is the endorsement reference. We organize by category, point at the regulation that creates the requirement, and point at the AC 61-65 paragraph that publishes the recommended language.

## What you'll be able to do

- Find any common endorsement in AC 61-65 by category (solo / cross-country / 61.31 / checkride-prep / flight review / spin / FOI)
- State which regulation creates each endorsement requirement and which AC 61-65 paragraph publishes the recommended language
- Distinguish what the regulation requires (what content the endorsement must communicate) from what AC 61-65 recommends (the words used)
- Apply the spin endorsement (61.183(i)) and the 61.31 endorsements (HP, complex, tailwheel, high-altitude) correctly to candidate scenarios

## Why this matters

Endorsements are how a CFI's authority leaves the CFI and becomes the pilot's documentation. A primary student cannot solo without 61.87 endorsements. A complex airplane cannot be flown by a non-endorsed pilot. A checkride applicant cannot be tested without 61.39 recommendation. A flight review is invalid without 61.56(c)(2) endorsement.

The trap most CFIs fall into: treating AC 61-65 as a "recipe book" that they copy from. AC 61-65 is *recommended language*, not regulation. The regulatory authority comes from the underlying section (61.87 for solo, 61.31 for HP, etc.). A CFI who has the regulatory facts right but uses non-AC-61-65 language has technically signed a valid endorsement. But FSDOs and DPEs expect the AC 61-65 phrasing, and deviation creates friction. The professional norm: use AC 61-65 language unless there's a specific reason not to.

The other trap: signing endorsements outside the CFI's authority. A CFI airplane single-engine without a CFII cannot sign an instrument-rating-prerequisite endorsement under 61.39 -- the authority isn't there even if the words are right.

## The discovery question

A primary student has finished the pre-solo training. They can land the airplane consistently, they understand the airspace and weather constraints for their solo, and the CFI judges them ready. Tomorrow morning, the CFI plans to send them solo for the first time.

What endorsements does the CFI need to have signed in the student's logbook *before* the student walks to the airplane tomorrow?

Pause and think.

The pre-solo endorsement set is in 61.87. There are at least three:

1. **Pre-solo aeronautical knowledge test endorsement** (61.87(b)) -- the CFI signs that the test was administered and reviewed; AC 61-65 paragraph A.6.
2. **Pre-solo flight maneuvers and procedures endorsement** (61.87(c)) -- the CFI signs that the student has demonstrated proficiency in the listed maneuvers; AC 61-65 paragraph A.7.
3. **Solo flight endorsement** (61.87(n)) -- the CFI signs that the student is authorized to conduct solo flight under specified conditions (date range, location, aircraft, weather minimums); AC 61-65 paragraph A.8.

Without all three, the student is not authorized to solo. The endorsements are a layered structure: knowledge first, maneuvers second, solo authorization third. They are also time-windowed: 61.87(n) requires the solo endorsement to be reviewed and re-signed every 90 days for it to remain valid.

This is the daily-CFI work. Six other endorsement types operate similarly: solo cross-country (61.93), 61.31 endorsements, knowledge-test recommendations, practical-test recommendations, flight reviews, and IPCs.

## Pre-solo endorsements (61.87)

61.87 governs student pilot solo flight. To send a student solo for the first time, the CFI must have signed:

### 61.87(b) -- pre-solo aeronautical knowledge test (AC 61-65 paragraph A.6)

> "I certify that [name] has satisfactorily completed the pre-solo knowledge test of 61.87(b) for the [make and model] aircraft."

The test itself is administered by the CFI and reviewed before signing. 61.87(b) requires the test to cover the FARs (the rules applicable to student pilot solo), airspace, and the AFM/POH for the airplane. The CFI grades the test, identifies and discusses incorrect answers, and signs the endorsement once the discussion is complete.

### 61.87(c) -- pre-solo flight maneuvers and procedures (AC 61-65 paragraph A.7)

> "I certify that [name] has received and logged pre-solo flight training for the maneuvers and procedures that are appropriate to the [make and model] aircraft. I have determined that [he/she] has demonstrated satisfactory proficiency and safety on the maneuvers and procedures required by 61.87 in this or a similar make and model aircraft to be flown."

61.87(c) lists 15+ specific maneuvers (proper flight preparation procedures, taxiing, takeoffs and landings, slow flight, stalls, approaches, emergency procedures, etc.). The CFI must have given training in each and judged the student proficient.

### 61.87(n) -- solo flight authorization (AC 61-65 paragraph A.8)

> "I certify that [name] has received the required training to qualify for solo flying. I have determined that [he/she] is prepared to conduct a solo flight on [date] under the following conditions: [aircraft, location, weather minimums, expiration]."

This is the operational endorsement. It specifies the date range (typically 90 days), the aircraft, the location (which airport), the weather minimums (the CFI's judgment about what conditions the student can handle), and other constraints. 61.87(n) requires the endorsement to be reviewed every 90 days for the student to continue exercising solo privileges.

The trap: a 61.87(n) endorsement that lapses (more than 90 days since the last review) does not authorize solo flight, and the student is back to dual-instruction-only until the CFI re-signs.

## Solo cross-country endorsements (61.93)

61.93 governs student pilot solo cross-country. To authorize a student for solo cross-country, the CFI must sign:

### 61.93(c)(1) -- solo cross-country flight (AC 61-65 paragraph A.9)

> "I certify that [name] has received the required solo cross-country training. I find [he/she] has met the applicable requirements of 61.93, and is authorized to make solo cross-country flights."

This is the general solo-XC authorization. It gives the student the framework authority. To actually make a specific cross-country, additional endorsements are required:

### 61.93(c)(2) -- specific solo cross-country flight (AC 61-65 paragraph A.10)

> "I have reviewed the cross-country planning of [name]. I find the planning and preparation to be correct to make the solo flight from [origin] to [destination] via [route] on [date] in a [make and model] aircraft. ..."

The CFI reviews the *specific* planned cross-country, including weather, NOTAMs, route, performance, and the student's preparation. The endorsement is single-flight-specific.

### 61.93(c)(3) -- solo flight in Class B airspace, etc. (AC 61-65 paragraphs A.11 - A.13)

Additional endorsements for solo flight to or through Class B airspace, takeoffs and landings at an airport in Class B (per 91.131), and other special-airspace operations.

## 61.31 operational endorsements

61.31 creates one-time operational endorsements that travel with the *pilot*, not with any specific aircraft. These are the daily-life endorsements primary, recreational, and private pilots receive over the course of their flying.

### 61.31(e) -- complex airplane (AC 61-65 paragraph A.16)

A "complex" airplane has retractable landing gear, a controllable-pitch propeller, and (with post-2018 wrinkles) flaps. The CFI gives ground and flight training in the systems and operating procedures of a complex airplane and signs:

> "I certify that [name] has received the required training of 61.31(e). I have determined that [he/she] is proficient in the operation of a complex airplane."

One-time, not aircraft-specific (the same endorsement covers any complex airplane the pilot subsequently flies).

### 61.31(f) -- high-performance airplane (AC 61-65 paragraph A.15)

A "high-performance" airplane has more than 200 horsepower. Training and endorsement procedurally identical to 61.31(e):

> "I certify that [name] has received the required training of 61.31(f). I have determined that [he/she] is proficient in the operation of a high-performance airplane."

### 61.31(g) -- pressurized aircraft / high altitude (AC 61-65 paragraph A.17)

For aircraft that have a service ceiling or maximum operating altitude above FL250. The training covers high-altitude physiology, hypoxia, decompression procedures, and pressurization systems:

> "I certify that [name] has received the required training of 61.31(g). I have determined that [he/she] is proficient in the operation of a pressurized aircraft."

### 61.31(i) -- tailwheel airplane (AC 61-65 paragraph A.18)

For tailwheel-configuration airplanes. Training covers ground handling, takeoffs, landings, and crosswind technique:

> "I certify that [name] has received the required training of 61.31(i). I have determined that [he/she] is proficient in the operation of a tailwheel airplane."

### 61.31 -- type-specific (e.g., MU-2, Mitsubishi MU-2B Special Federal Aviation Regulation 108)

The MU-2 has its own special training rule under SFAR 108. Niche; mentioned for completeness.

## Recommendation-for-test endorsements (61.39)

61.39 governs the prerequisites for a practical test. The CFI's recommendation is one of those prerequisites. AC 61-65 publishes recommended language by certificate level:

### 61.39 -- private pilot recommendation (AC 61-65 paragraph A.19)

> "I certify that [name] has received the required training in accordance with 61.107 and 61.109. I have determined [he/she] is prepared for the [private pilot ASEL/AMEL/etc.] practical test."

The endorsement covers two regulations: 61.107 (the flight proficiency areas of operation) and 61.109 (the aero-experience minimums). The CFI is certifying both: training in the areas, and the documented hours satisfying the minimums.

### 61.39 -- commercial pilot recommendation (AC 61-65 paragraph A.27 typical)

> "I certify that [name] has received the required training in accordance with 61.123 [eligibility], 61.125 [aero knowledge], 61.127 [areas of operation], and 61.129 [aero experience]. I have determined [he/she] is prepared for the commercial pilot [ASEL/AMEL/etc.] practical test."

### 61.39 -- instrument rating recommendation (AC 61-65 paragraph A.20 typical)

> "I certify that [name] has received the required training in accordance with 61.65. I have determined [he/she] is prepared for the instrument-airplane practical test."

The CFII (instrument-instructor rating) is required to sign the instrument-rating recommendation per 61.195(c). A CFI without the CFII cannot sign this endorsement.

### 61.39 -- knowledge test (AC 61-65 paragraph A.2, A.3, A.33, etc.)

> "I certify that [name] has received the training required by 61.[XX]. I have determined that [he/she] is prepared for the [knowledge test name]."

A separate endorsement from the practical-test recommendation. The knowledge test is taken before the practical; the recommendation endorsements are required prerequisites for each.

## Flight review endorsement (61.56)

### 61.56(c)(2) -- flight review (AC 61-65 paragraph A.1)

> "I certify that [name] has satisfactorily completed a flight review as required by 61.56 on [date]."

One-line endorsement. The CFI's signature certifies the review was satisfactory in their judgment. Per the Hicks (2010) Chief Counsel letter, the CFI cannot retroactively withdraw the endorsement -- the only remedy if the CFI later judges the pilot unsafe is to refuse future endorsements.

The flight review endorsement is the *most-signed* endorsement in general aviation. Most CFIs sign more flight reviews than any other type of endorsement over their careers.

## Instrument proficiency check (61.57(d))

### 61.57(d) -- IPC (AC 61-65 paragraph A.4)

> "I certify that [name] has satisfactorily completed an instrument proficiency check as required by 61.57(d) on [date] in a [make and model] aircraft."

The IPC restores currency for an instrument-rated pilot whose 6-6-HIT recency under 61.57(c) has lapsed beyond the 6-month grace. AC 61-98 specifies the recommended content (which mirrors the practical test). A CFII is required.

## CFI-initial-specific endorsements

### 61.183(d) -- FOI training (AC 61-65 paragraph A.36)

> "I certify that [name] has received the required training in the fundamentals of instructing of 61.185(a)(1)."

One-time-forever for the receiving CFI candidate.

### 61.183(g) -- CFI practical test prep (AC 61-65 paragraph A.37)

> "I certify that [name] has received the required training in accordance with 61.187(b). I have determined [he/she] is prepared for the [CFI airplane single-engine, etc.] practical test."

Specific to the rating sought; renewed each time a new CFI rating is added.

### 61.183(i) -- spin training endorsement (AC 61-65 paragraph A.43)

> "I certify that [name] has received the training required by 61.183(i) for stall awareness, spin entry, spins, and spin recovery procedures, and I have determined [he/she] is prepared to demonstrate instructional proficiency on this material."

The spin endorsement. One-time-forever for the receiving CFI candidate; required for initial CFI airplane and powered-lift candidates per 61.183(i). The signing CFI must hold the appropriate CFI rating in a spin-certified aircraft (typical: a Citabria, Decathlon, Pitts) and must have given the actual training (ground discussion plus flight demonstration of spin entry, fully developed spin, and recovery).

The spin endorsement is famous because it's tested. DPEs ask CFI candidates "show me your spin endorsement" and ask follow-up questions about the training -- which spin-certified aircraft was used, who signed, what the training covered. A poorly-prepared candidate who can't articulate what they actually did in spin training has a problem.

## Subpart I -- light-sport CFIs (name and locate)

Subpart K of Part 61 (sections 61.401 through 61.429) covers "Flight Instructors With a Sport Pilot Rating" -- the CFI-S certificate. Parallel to the CFI-A you're working toward, but limited to light-sport aircraft and with reduced aero-experience requirements:

- 61.405 -- CFI-S eligibility (parallel to 61.183 but with sport-pilot underlying cert minimum)
- 61.407 -- CFI-S aeronautical knowledge subjects
- 61.409 -- CFI-S flight proficiency areas of operation
- 61.411 -- CFI-S records (parallel to 61.189)
- 61.415 -- CFI-S privileges
- 61.417 -- CFI-S limitations
- 61.421 -- CFI-S renewal (parallel to 61.197)

The CFI-S can sign sport-pilot-specific endorsements but cannot sign endorsements toward higher-than-sport pilot certificates. Their signatures live in light-sport regulation lanes only. Subpart K is not a deep-dive item for the CFI airplane initial; we name and locate.

(Subpart I -- "Ground Instructors" -- governs the BGI/AGI/IGI ground-instructor certificates, sections 61.211-61.219. A ground instructor is *not* a flight instructor and has no flight-endorsement authority. Mentioned in lesson 01; named again here for completeness.)

## Common misreadings

- **"AC 61-65 is regulation."** Wrong. AC 61-65 publishes *recommended language*. The regulation is in the underlying section (61.87, 61.31, 61.39, 61.56, 61.183, etc.). A CFI who uses different wording but accurately states the regulatory facts has signed a valid endorsement. FSDOs and DPEs expect AC 61-65 phrasing, but deviation alone is not a regulatory violation.
- **"The 61.87(n) solo endorsement is good for the rest of the student's training."** Wrong. 61.87(n) requires re-review every 90 days. A student whose endorsement is more than 90 days old is back to dual-instruction-only.
- **"The complex / HP / tailwheel endorsements are aircraft-specific."** Wrong. 61.31(e), (f), (g), (i) endorsements travel with the pilot. The same endorsement is good in any complex / high-performance / pressurized / tailwheel airplane the pilot subsequently flies.
- **"A CFI without a CFII can sign an IPC if they're flying with the student."** Wrong. 61.57(d) requires the IPC be given by an "authorized instructor" who holds the CFII. The CFI without the CFII is not authorized.
- **"Spin endorsement is only for CFI airplane multiengine."** Wrong. 61.183(i) applies to both CFI airplane single-engine and CFI airplane multiengine initial candidates. Either category requires the spin endorsement before practical test.
- **"I need to sign the flight review endorsement on the practical-test prep paperwork too."** Wrong. The flight review (61.56) and the practical-test recommendation (61.39) are separate endorsements with separate purposes. A practical test counts as a flight review under 61.56(d) automatically -- you don't need both endorsements; the practical-test recommendation is sufficient.
- **"AC 61-65 paragraph A.1 is the right wording for an IPC."** Wrong. A.1 is the flight review endorsement. The IPC endorsement is paragraph A.4 (in AC 61-65H). Different content, different paragraph; using A.1 for an IPC misrepresents the operation.
- **"I can sign a 61.31(g) endorsement based on the student's reading the POH."** Wrong. 61.31(g) requires *training* (ground and flight). The CFI must give the training, not just verify the student read material.
- **"CFI-S endorsements are valid for higher-than-sport-pilot training."** Wrong. The CFI-S can only sign endorsements within sport-pilot regulation lanes (per Subpart K). A CFI-S who signs a private-pilot 61.39 recommendation has signed an invalid endorsement.

### Trap detector

| Statement                                                              | Trap and correction |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| "AC 61-65 wording is regulation."                                      | Wrong. AC 61-65 is recommended language. The regulatory authority lives in 61.31, 61.39, 61.56, 61.87, 61.183, etc. |
| "I can use AC 61-65 paragraph A.1 wording for the IPC endorsement."    | Wrong. A.1 is the flight review. IPC is A.4. Different operation, different paragraph. |
| "Once I've signed a 61.87(n) solo endorsement, the student can solo for the next 6 months." | Wrong. 61.87(n) requires re-review every 90 days. |
| "The complex endorsement is aircraft-specific."                        | Wrong. 61.31(e) endorsement travels with the pilot, not with a specific airplane. |
| "I'm a CFI airplane single-engine. I can sign the instrument-rating recommendation since I have the IR on my pilot cert." | Wrong. 61.195(c) requires the CFII for instrument-rating endorsements. CFI-A alone is not enough. |
| "The spin endorsement is required only if the candidate hasn't done spin training in their commercial." | Wrong. 61.183(i) requires the spin endorsement specifically for the CFI airplane initial, regardless of prior spin exposure. |
| "Tailwheel and complex are the same endorsement."                      | Wrong. Different sections (61.31(e) and (i)) and different AC 61-65 paragraphs (A.16 and A.18). Same airplane could need both (a complex tailwheel like a Pitts S-2 -- actually has flaps and is tailwheel). |
| "I can sign my friend's flight review endorsement at the end of a recreational flight where I gave them some pointers." | Wrong. 61.56 requires *training* and *endorsement* in a 1-hour-flight + 1-hour-ground format. Recreational pointers don't satisfy 61.56(a). |
