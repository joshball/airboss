# CFI renewal

The CFI certificate expires every 24 calendar months. There is no equivalent for pilot certificates -- a private, commercial, or ATP cert does not expire. The CFI cert does. 61.197 specifies the renewal pathways.

You walked the structure of subpart H in [01-subpart-h-walk.md](01-subpart-h-walk.md). You understand the records obligation in [02-eligibility-and-experience.md](02-eligibility-and-experience.md) and the privileges/limitations in [03-privileges-and-limitations.md](03-privileges-and-limitations.md). This lesson handles the renewal cycle that touches every CFI every 24 months for the rest of their career.

## What you'll be able to do

- State the 24-calendar-month rule for CFI renewal cold, including the back-end calendar-month math
- Recite each of the five renewal pathways under 61.197(a) and predict which one applies to a given working CFI
- Describe how an FIRC works mechanically (length, content, completion record, FAA submission)
- Compute whether a CFI qualifies for the 80%-pass-rate / 5-student renewal pathway from their 61.189(b) records
- Distinguish a *renewal* (under 61.197) from a *reinstatement* (under 61.199 -- expired CFIs)

## Why this matters

If your CFI cert expires and you sign an endorsement during the lapse, the endorsement is invalid. The pilot you signed for is not authorized for whatever the endorsement was supposed to bless. Beyond the regulatory exposure to you, the *student* who acted on the invalid endorsement is also exposed: a flight review signed by an expired CFI does not satisfy 61.56, and the pilot is technically not legally PIC.

The renewal cycle is also where most working CFIs drift. The 24-calendar-month clock is precise, but most CFIs experience it as "about every two years," and most lapse occur because the CFI did the FIRC two months too late, or scheduled the practical-test renewal for a date that was already past expiration.

The professional norm: schedule your renewal a calendar month or more *before* expiration. The regulation gives you a 3-calendar-month FIRC window before expiration; using it well preserves your working window.

## The discovery question

Your CFI certificate was issued April 14, 2024. Today is March 1, 2026. You have a primary student who is scheduled to take their pre-solo knowledge test endorsement next Tuesday. You're planning a flight review for a returning pilot Wednesday. Your CFI renewal practical test is scheduled for May 15, 2026.

When does your CFI cert expire? When do you have to complete renewal by? Are next week's endorsements valid?

Pause and think.

The CFI cert is valid through April 30, 2026 (24 calendar months from the month of issue: April 2024 -> April 2026, and the back-end calendar month is free). On May 1, 2026, if you have not renewed, the cert is expired. On May 15, 2026 -- the date of your scheduled renewal practical -- you would be 15 days into expiration, which means you have 15 days where 61.199 (expired CFI) is the governing section, not 61.197.

Reading 61.197 vs 61.199:

- 61.197 (renewal) applies to a CFI whose cert is *not yet expired*. The pathways available include FIRC, practical test, designated examiner, military, and the 80%-record path.
- 61.199 (expired) applies once the cert has lapsed. The pathways narrow: practical test or designated examiner. FIRC is *not* a 61.199 reinstatement option.

The May 15 practical works mechanically (you can take the practical to renew under 61.197(a)(1) right up to the day before expiration; or under 61.199 to reinstate after expiration). But scheduling it past expiration means you cannot teach between May 1 and May 15, and any endorsements you sign in that window are invalid.

The endorsements next Tuesday and Wednesday (March, well before expiration) are valid. The plan needs a fix: move the practical test up to before April 30, or accept a two-week non-teaching window.

## What 61.197 actually says

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/197?at=2026) -- "Renewal requirements for flight instructor certification":

```text
14 CFR 61.197  Renewal requirements for flight instructor
                certification.

(a) A person who holds a flight instructor certificate that has
    not expired may renew that flight instructor certificate
    by --

    (1) Passing a practical test for --
        (i) One of the ratings listed on the current flight
            instructor certificate; or
        (ii) An additional flight instructor rating;

    (2) Submitting a completed and signed application with the
        FAA and presenting a record of training students showing
        that, during the preceding 24 calendar months, the flight
        instructor has endorsed at least 5 students for a
        practical test for a certificate or rating and at least
        80 percent of those students passed that test on the
        first attempt;

    (3) Submitting a completed and signed application with the
        FAA and presenting a record showing that, within the
        preceding 24 calendar months, the flight instructor has
        served as a company check pilot, chief flight instructor,
        company check airman, or flight instructor in a Part 121
        or Part 135 program ...;

    (4) Graduating from an approved flight instructor refresher
        course (FIRC) consisting of ground training or flight
        training, or a combination of both, within the preceding
        3 calendar months prior to the expiration month of the
        flight instructor's current certificate; or

    (5) Passing an official U.S. Armed Forces military instructor
        pilot or pilot examiner proficiency check ...;

(b) The expiration month of a renewed flight instructor
    certificate shall be 24 calendar months from --

    (1) The month the renewal requirements ... were accomplished;
        or

    (2) If renewed within the 3 calendar months preceding the
        expiration month, then 24 calendar months from the
        expiration month.

Note: structural paraphrase. Read the actual reg.
```

Five pathways under (a). The 24-calendar-month clock under (b), with the back-end-month gift if you renew within the 3-month preceding window.

## What it actually means -- the five pathways

### Path 1: Practical test (61.197(a)(1))

Take a practical test for one of the ratings already on your CFI cert (a *renewal* practical) or for an additional rating (which counts as both adding a rating *and* renewing the existing ones). The practical is given by a designated examiner against the appropriate ACS. Cost: $500 to $1500 depending on the DPE and locale. Effort: a half-day or more. Result: a brand-new 24-month clock from the month of the practical (or from the prior expiration month if within the 3-month window).

This is the most expensive and time-consuming pathway, but it's also the most rigorous. CFIs who haven't been actively teaching find the practical-test path useful because it forces a re-grounding in the ACS; CFIs who have been teaching daily often prefer the FIRC because it's lower-friction.

### Path 2: 80% pass-rate / 5-student record (61.197(a)(2))

Submit a record showing that in the preceding 24 calendar months you have:

- Endorsed *at least 5* students for a practical test for a certificate or rating
- *At least 80%* of those students passed on the first attempt

The 80% threshold is computed on first-attempt pass rate; a student who busted the practical, retrained, and passed on the second attempt does *not* count toward the 80% (they had a fail on first attempt). A student who you recommended but never took the test does not count toward the 5.

The math: with 5 students, 80% means 4 of 5 passed first attempt. With 6 students, 80% means 5 of 6 (83.3%, rounds up). With 10 students, 80% means 8 of 10. The threshold is computed at the time of submission.

This pathway is the working-CFI's gift. If you're actively teaching primary, instrument, commercial, or CFI candidates, and you're recommending consistently and your students are passing, you renew without paying for an FIRC or a practical. The 61.189(b) records are exactly what the FAA wants to see -- this is why those records matter.

The trap: many CFIs assume "I taught 5 students this period." But 61.197(a)(2) requires *recommended for a practical test*. A student you taught but never recommended (because they switched CFIs, or stopped flying, or never reached checkride-ready) does not count.

### Path 3: 121/135 instructor or check pilot (61.197(a)(3))

If you're working as a check pilot, chief flight instructor, or company instructor in a Part 121 or Part 135 program, you renew via that role. The submission shows your continuous service in the recurrent training role, which the FAA accepts as functional equivalent to renewal training. Niche to airline/charter operations, but valuable to ATP-CFI candidates who go that direction.

### Path 4: Flight Instructor Refresher Course (FIRC) (61.197(a)(4))

The most common pathway for working CFIs in general aviation. An FIRC is an FAA-approved course of ground training (sometimes combined with flight training) totaling at least 16 hours, completed within the *3 calendar months preceding* the CFI cert's expiration month.

The 16-hour minimum has been the historical floor; current FAA-approved FIRCs (delivered by providers like AOPA, ASA, Gleim, and Sporty's) deliver the content as 16 to 24 hours of online study with periodic checkpoints, plus a graduation exam. Completion is recorded by the provider, who notifies the FAA via the IACRA or AFS-630 systems on your behalf.

The mechanics:

1. CFI registers for an FAA-approved FIRC with a provider.
2. CFI completes the course content within the 3-calendar-month window preceding expiration.
3. Provider submits the completion to the FAA.
4. FAA issues a renewed CFI cert, mailed to the CFI's address of record.
5. The new 24-month clock starts from the month of expiration (per 61.197(b)(2)) -- the back-end-month gift.

Cost: $150 to $300 typical, depending on provider. Effort: 16-24 hours of online time, spread over weeks if desired.

The trap: FIRCs are *only* a renewal mechanism. 61.197(a)(4) says "within the preceding 3 calendar months prior to the expiration month." Translation: if your cert expires April 30, 2026, you may complete the FIRC in January, February, March, or April 2026 to satisfy this pathway. Completing the FIRC before January 2026 (more than 3 calendar months ahead of expiration) does not count -- the renewal didn't take place in the renewal window.

The other trap: the FIRC works only on a non-expired cert. If your cert expires before you complete the FIRC, 61.197 no longer applies; you're under 61.199 and FIRCs are not a 61.199 reinstatement option. Plan FIRC completion at least 30 days before expiration to give yourself a buffer for processing time.

### Path 5: Military proficiency check (61.197(a)(5))

For active-duty military pilots, the annual military instructor-pilot or examiner proficiency check counts as renewal. Submit the documentation. Niche to dual-status (military + civilian-CFI) pilots.

## The 24-calendar-month math

61.197(b) sets the renewal clock:

- A CFI cert renewed *outside* the 3-month preceding window starts the new clock at the month of renewal completion.
  Example: cert expires April 30, 2026. CFI takes a practical test on March 5, 2026 (which is within the 3-month window). New cert valid through April 30, 2028. The cert renews to start of the *next* expiration month relative to the prior, not from the test date.
- A CFI cert renewed during the 3-calendar-month preceding window also gets the back-end gift.
  Example: cert expires April 30, 2026. CFI completes FIRC on February 15, 2026 (within the 3-month window). New cert valid through April 30, 2028. (Same answer as the practical test in the same window.)

Why is this important? The 3-month window protects against the cliff. A CFI who finishes their renewal early in the 3-month window doesn't lose the back-end calendar months they otherwise would have had. Without the (b)(2) carve-out, an early-FIRC CFI would lose up to 3 months of total instructional life over a long career.

A CFI who completes renewal *outside* the 3-month preceding window (more than 3 months before expiration) does *not* get the back-end gift. The new clock starts the month of completion. This is rare but possible if a CFI takes a renewal practical 6 months before expiration; their next expiration would be 24 months from the test month, not from the original expiration month.

The clock-shift incentive: complete renewal in the 3-month preceding window. Don't drift outside it on either side.

## What 61.199 actually says (briefly)

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/199?at=2026) -- "Expired flight instructor certificates and ratings":

```text
14 CFR 61.199  Expired flight instructor certificates and ratings.

(a) A person who holds an expired flight instructor certificate
    or rating may exchange that certificate for a flight
    instructor certificate or rating by passing the practical
    test prescribed in 61.183(h) ...

(b) ... [provisions for ratings on expired certificates]

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg.
```

If the cert is expired, the only renewal pathway is a practical test. FIRC, the 80% record, the military check -- none of them apply once 61.199 is the governing section. The FOI knowledge test result remains valid (one-time-forever per 61.183(e)) and the spin endorsement remains valid (61.183(i) is one-time-forever), so a 61.199 reinstatement applicant takes only the practical test.

The cost of a lapse: you can no longer teach during the lapse window, you cannot use any of the cheaper renewal pathways, and you must take a practical test (with associated DPE fees and time). Lapse is expensive in three different ways.

## Common misreadings

- **"My CFI cert is just like my pilot cert."** Wrong. Pilot certs (private, commercial, ATP) do not expire. CFI certs expire every 24 calendar months. 61.197 governs renewal; 61.199 governs the post-expiration reinstatement.
- **"FIRCs are valid up to 24 months before expiration."** Wrong per 61.197(a)(4). The FIRC must be completed *within the 3 calendar months preceding the expiration month*. Completing earlier doesn't count for that renewal cycle.
- **"I can use a FIRC to reinstate an expired CFI cert."** Wrong. 61.199 governs expired CFIs and requires a practical test (or designated examiner check). The FIRC is a 61.197 tool; once expired, you're out of 61.197.
- **"The 80%-record path needs only that 80% of all my students passed."** Wrong. 61.197(a)(2) requires *at least 5 students recommended* AND 80% pass *on the first attempt*. A 4-student-100%-pass cohort doesn't meet the 5-student floor. A 10-student cohort with 7 first-attempt passes (70%) doesn't meet the 80% bar.
- **"If I renew early in the 3-month window, my new clock starts from that date."** Wrong per 61.197(b)(2). Renewal during the 3-month preceding window gives you the back-end calendar month -- the new clock starts at the prior expiration month, not the test/FIRC completion date.
- **"My students who failed the practical and then re-took and passed count as first-attempt passes."** Wrong. 61.197(a)(2) requires "at least 80 percent of those students passed that test on the first attempt." Second-attempt passes don't count toward the 80%.
- **"I just need to do *some* CFI activity in the 24 months to renew automatically."** Wrong. There is no automatic renewal. You must affirmatively take one of the (a)(1)-(a)(5) actions.
- **"After I complete the FIRC, I can immediately resume teaching."** Mostly right, with a wrinkle. The FIRC graduation date is not the renewal date until the FAA processes the paperwork; until your new temporary or permanent cert is in hand (or IACRA shows the renewal posted), you do not have evidence of renewal. CFIs who finish the FIRC the day before expiration and then sign an endorsement before the renewal posts have created a documentation gap. The professional norm: complete the FIRC at least 30 days before expiration.

### Apply the rules

> A CFI cert was issued April 14, 2024. The CFI completes an FIRC on February 15, 2026. When does the new cert expire?

Answer: April 30, 2028. The original cert expires April 30, 2026 (24 calendar months from April 2024). The FIRC is completed within the 3-calendar-month window preceding April. Per 61.197(b)(2), the new clock runs from April 2026, so the new expiration is April 30, 2028.

> A CFI cert expires April 30, 2026. The CFI completes an FIRC on November 15, 2025 (more than 3 months early). What's the renewal status?

Answer: The November FIRC is *outside* the 3-calendar-month preceding window. It does not count for the April 2026 renewal. The CFI must complete another renewal action (FIRC, practical test, or 80%-record path) within the 3-month window preceding April 30, 2026 (i.e., between January 1 and April 30, 2026), or the cert lapses.

> Same CFI as above, but they realize the November FIRC won't work and they have no time to do another. They've recommended 6 students for practical tests in the past 24 months; 5 passed first attempt. Are they eligible for the 80% pathway?

Answer: Yes. 5 of 6 = 83.3%, which exceeds 80%. The 5-student floor is met. They submit the 61.189(b) records and the application; renewal is granted under 61.197(a)(2).

> A CFI cert expired May 30, 2026 (4 days ago, today is June 3). The CFI wants to teach a student today. Can they?

Answer: No. The cert is expired. 61.199 governs. Even if the CFI completes a practical test today (June 3), the practical-test result becomes the new issuance date; teaching prior to test completion is not authorized. The CFI must complete the 61.199 reinstatement first.

> A CFI completed a renewal practical test on April 5, 2026. The original expiration was April 30, 2026. When does the new cert expire?

Answer: April 30, 2028. The April 5 test is within the 3-month preceding window, so 61.197(b)(2) gives the back-end gift: new clock from April 2026 = April 2028.

### Trap detector

| Statement                                                              | Trap and correction |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| "I have 24 months from my last renewal to do my next FIRC."            | Wrong. 61.197(a)(4) requires the FIRC be in the 3 calendar months preceding expiration. Earlier doesn't count for that cycle. |
| "I let my cert lapse for 2 weeks; I can FIRC it back."                 | Wrong. 61.199 governs expired CFIs and excludes the FIRC pathway. Practical test or DPE check are the only options. |
| "I recommended 4 students who all passed first time. I qualify for the 80% pathway." | Wrong. 61.197(a)(2) requires *at least 5* students recommended. 4 doesn't reach the floor regardless of pass rate. |
| "I had 10 students recommended; 6 passed first try, 2 failed first then passed second try, 2 are still pending. My pass rate is 80%." | Wrong calculation. 6 first-attempt passes / 8 students who took the test = 75%. The 2 still pending don't count yet. The 80% bar isn't met. |
| "FIRCs are 8 hours of online study."                                   | Wrong. FIRCs are 16-24 hours typical, and FAA-approved courses meet a 16-hour floor. The "8 hour" number sometimes confuses with 61.195(a)'s daily training cap. |
| "If I renew via FIRC, my next renewal can be by FIRC again."           | Right, with no FAA-imposed limit on consecutive FIRCs. The same CFI can FIRC every 24 months for their entire career. The professional question is whether the FIRC keeps a CFI sharp -- some FIRCs are heavier on content than others. |
| "An expired CFI can sign a flight review."                             | Wrong. The expired CFI is not "an authorized instructor" under 61.1(b). Any endorsement signed during the lapse is invalid. The pilot relying on it is not legally PIC for flight-review purposes (61.56(c)). |
