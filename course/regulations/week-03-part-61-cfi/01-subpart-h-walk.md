# Walking Part 61 subpart H, front to back

This is the front-to-back commentary on Part 61 subpart H. Read it slowly. Subpart H is short, but every section in it is operationally load-bearing for a working CFI. The shape of the subpart matches the cert-level subparts you walked in Week 2 (E for private, F for commercial, G for ATP), with one important wrinkle: the CFI subpart has a renewal section. Pilot certificates don't expire; instructor certificates do.

We walk subpart H section by section. Subparts I (ground instructors), J (sport pilots), and K (sport pilot CFIs) are named and located at the end; we do not deep-dive them.

## What you'll be able to do

- Recite the sections of subpart H with their topic and a one-sentence purpose
- Predict, given a CFI question, which section owns the answer
- Recognize the parallel between this subpart and the cert-level subparts you walked in Week 2
- Locate the most-cited sections (61.193 privileges, 61.195 limitations, 61.197 renewal) without scanning a table of contents

## Why this matters

Pilots study Part 61 subparts E, F, G the way they study a recipe -- ingredients in order, measure twice, follow the steps. They don't usually study subpart H that way, because they're not yet inside it. The result is a CFI candidate who shows up to a checkride with strong recall on private and commercial subparts and a vague sense of "I'll figure out the CFI sections in initial training."

That's a mistake. Subpart H is your *home subpart* for the next several years. Everything you do as a CFI -- what you can sign, what you can't, how you log dual given, how you renew, what training records you keep -- lives here. Internalizing the structural map now means you spend zero cognitive load on "where does this rule live" during the most important years of your aviation career.

The discovery question for this lesson:

> You're a brand-new CFI. A friend asks you to give them a flight review. Walk me through which Part 61 sections you touch, in order, from "yes, I'll do it" to "I sign your logbook."

Pause and think. Done well, the answer routes through 61.193 (do I have the privilege?), 61.195 (am I limited from doing it?), 61.51 (how do I log it?), 61.189 (what record do I keep?), 61.56 (what does a flight review actually require, by section?), and back to 61.193 / 61.56(c) (what specifically do I sign in the logbook?). Six sections, four of them in subpart H. The structural map of subpart H *is* the map of how you do your daily job.

## The shape of subpart H

```text
Subpart H -- Flight Instructors Other Than Flight Instructors With a Sport Pilot Rating

  Section 61.181 -- Applicability
    Tells you which CFIs this subpart governs. The "other than sport-pilot" carve-out
    points you at subpart K for the sport-pilot CFI cert.

  Section 61.183 -- Eligibility requirements
    The floor. Age, English, certificates held, knowledge tests, practical test,
    spin training endorsement, FOI knowledge.

  Section 61.185 -- Aeronautical knowledge
    The topics the FOI knowledge test covers. The "what does a CFI need to know
    about teaching?" subject list.

  Section 61.187 -- Flight proficiency
    The maneuvers and skills the CFI practical test will exercise. Includes the
    "from the right seat" requirement that catches a lot of candidates flat-footed.

  Section 61.189 -- Flight instructor records
    The logbook-and-record obligation that tracks every endorsement you sign,
    every student you train, every recommendation you make.

  Section 61.191 -- Additional flight instructor ratings
    How you add a category, class, or instrument-instructor rating to an existing
    CFI cert.

  Section 61.193 -- Flight instructor privileges
    The "what you may do" section. Eight privileges. Memorize them.

  Section 61.195 -- Flight instructor limitations and qualifications
    The "what you may NOT do" section. Plus what you must hold to do certain things
    (instrument instruction requires CFII; etc.).

  Section 61.197 -- Renewal requirements for flight instructor certification
    Five paths: practical test, FIRC, designated examiner, military, 80%-pass-rate
    activity record. Heavy section. This is the deep dive.

  Section 61.199 -- Expired flight instructor certificates and ratings
    What happens if you let it lapse. Reinstatement mechanics.

  Section 61.201 -- [Reserved]
    Placeholder. Don't worry about it; it's where future content might land.
```

The pattern: applicability, eligibility, knowledge / proficiency requirements, training records, privileges and limitations, renewal mechanics. This rhymes with subparts E, F, G but adds the renewal layer that pilot certs don't have.

## Section 61.181 -- Applicability

Tells you the subpart applies to all CFIs *other than* sport-pilot CFIs (those go to subpart K). Brisk. The trap is reading this as boilerplate and missing that the carve-out is real: a sport-pilot CFI has a parallel set of privileges and limitations in subpart K, and the rules are not identical.

## Section 61.183 -- Eligibility requirements

The eligibility floor. To be eligible for an initial CFI cert you must:

- (a) Be at least 18 years old
- (b) Be able to read, speak, write, and understand English
- (c) Hold either a commercial pilot certificate or an airline transport pilot certificate, with the appropriate aircraft category and class ratings (and instrument rating, if seeking the instrument-instructor rating)
- (d) Receive a logbook endorsement from an authorized instructor on the fundamentals of instructing (FOI) -- the endorsement must specifically address the topics named in 61.185(a)(1)
- (e) Pass a knowledge test on the FOI subjects of 61.185(a)(1) and the aeronautical knowledge subjects of 61.185(a)(2)
- (f) Pass a knowledge test on the areas of operation listed in 61.187(b)
- (g) Receive flight training and a logbook endorsement to take the practical test
- (h) Pass a practical test
- (i) Have received a logbook endorsement to demonstrate spin entry, spin, and spin recovery procedures (covered in lesson 5)

The two knowledge tests in (e) and (f) are commonly known as the FOI and the FIA (Flight Instructor Airplane). Some candidates conflate them; they're separate tests on separate subjects. The FOI is the one that surprises commercial pilots -- it's a teaching-theory test, not a flying test.

The spin endorsement under (i) is the famous one. We deep-dive in lesson 5. Note: the spin endorsement is required for CFI initial regardless of category-and-class; you cannot avoid it by adding the CFI rating later.

## Section 61.185 -- Aeronautical knowledge

The subject list for the FOI and FIA knowledge tests:

- (a)(1) FOI subjects: the learning process, methods of instruction, human behavior and effective communication, evaluating student performance, planning instructional activity, course development, lesson plans
- (a)(2) Aeronautical knowledge subjects: the same list 61.105 (private), 61.125 (commercial), 61.155 (ATP) cover, plus the rules unique to flight instruction (61.51, 61.187, 61.189)

The takeaway: a CFI knowledge test exercises both teaching and flying knowledge. A commercial pilot studying for the CFI initial often underprepares the FOI side because the subjects feel non-aviation. They're testable and they bind.

## Section 61.187 -- Flight proficiency

The areas of operation the practical test exercises. Per the current Airman Certification Standards (FAA-S-ACS-25 for the CFI airplane single-engine), these include fundamentals of flight instruction, technical subject areas (PTS knowledge from the right seat), preflight preparation, preflight procedures, airport operations, takeoffs and landings, fundamentals of flight, performance maneuvers, ground reference maneuvers, slow flight and stalls, basic instrument maneuvers, emergency operations, and post-flight procedures.

The wrinkle most candidates feel: the practical test is *from the right seat*. The same maneuvers a commercial-rated pilot has flown 100 times from the left seat get re-evaluated from the other seat. Many candidates do not budget enough right-seat time during their CFI training and surprise themselves.

## Section 61.189 -- Flight instructor records

The training-record obligation:

> (a) A flight instructor must sign the logbook of each person to whom that instructor has given flight training or ground training.

Plus:

> (b) A flight instructor must maintain a record in a logbook or a separate document that contains the following:
>
> (1) The name of each person whose logbook or student pilot certificate that instructor has endorsed for solo flight privileges, and the date of the endorsement; and
>
> (2) The name of each person that instructor has endorsed for a knowledge test or practical test, and the record shall also indicate the kind of test, the date, and the results.

> (c) Each flight instructor must retain the records required by this section for at least 3 years.

Read carefully. There are two record obligations: the *student's* logbook (the endorsement) and the *instructor's* record (a separate ledger of every solo, knowledge-test, and practical-test endorsement). Many CFIs keep only the student-side record and miss the 3-year instructor-side ledger entirely.

The trap: the instructor-side ledger is the document a FAA inspector or DPE will ask to see during an oversight visit. "I signed it in the student's book and they took the book with them" is not compliance with 61.189(b).

## Section 61.191 -- Additional flight instructor ratings

How you add another category, class, or the instrument-instructor rating to an existing CFI cert. The mechanics: meet the eligibility for the new rating, take the knowledge test for the new rating's areas of operation, pass a practical test in the appropriate category and class for the new rating. The FOI is *not* re-required because you've already passed it.

## Section 61.193 -- Flight instructor privileges

The eight things a CFI may do. Worth memorizing:

- (a)(1) Flight training
- (a)(2) Ground training
- (a)(3) Endorsement for solo flight, solo cross-country flight
- (a)(4) Endorsement for a knowledge test or practical test
- (a)(5) Endorsement for a flight review or instrument proficiency check (61.56, 61.57(d))
- (a)(6) Endorsement for the additional training requirements of 61.31 (HP, complex, tailwheel, high-altitude)
- (a)(7) Endorsement of the U.S. pilot certificate of a foreign pilot under 61.75
- (a)(8) Endorsement for the recreational pilot to act as PIC under 61.101 in additional categories or classes

Lesson 2 unpacks each of these and the limits that hide inside them. The shape to remember now: training, endorsement-for-solo, endorsement-for-tests, endorsement-for-currency, endorsement-for-ratings, foreign-pilot bridge, recreational-pilot bridge. Six of the eight are endorsements; only two ((a)(1) and (a)(2)) are training itself.

## Section 61.195 -- Flight instructor limitations and qualifications

The mirror-image of 61.193. Includes:

- (a) Hours of training: 8 hours in any 24-hour period
- (b) Aircraft ratings: a CFI may give training only in aircraft for which they hold the appropriate category, class, and type rating
- (c) Instrument rating limits: a CFI may give instrument training only if they hold an instrument-airplane (or instrument-helicopter, etc.) instructor rating
- (d) Limitations on endorsing for an additional category or class of aircraft
- (e) Training in multiengine, helicopters, gliders, etc. -- specific currency requirements before endorsing
- (f) Training for an initial pilot cert -- the CFI must have logged at least 5 hours of PIC time in that make and model
- (g) Training for an instrument rating -- the CFI must have at least 15 hours of PIC instrument time
- (h) Lighter-than-air specifics

Many of these are quiet limitations that catch CFIs by surprise. A CFI who accepts a student in a make-and-model the CFI has flown only twice as PIC has not met (f). A CFI who endorses a student for a flight in IMC without having logged 15 hours instrument PIC has not met (g).

## Section 61.197 -- Renewal requirements

The CFI cert is good for 24 calendar months. Renewal pathways under 61.197:

- (a)(1) Pass a practical test for one of the ratings on the certificate
- (a)(2) Pass a practical test for an additional rating
- (a)(3) Submit to the FAA a record of training students that shows at least an 80 percent pass rate on the practical test, and at least 5 students recommended for that test
- (a)(4) Complete an FAA-approved flight instructor refresher course (FIRC) within 3 calendar months preceding expiration
- (a)(5) Pass a designated examiner check
- (a)(6) Be a participating member of an air carrier's pilot training program under Part 121 or Part 135 with relevant duties

Most working CFIs renew via FIRC. The 80 percent record path is for high-volume CFIs whose actual practice satisfies the requirement. We deep-dive in lesson 3.

## Section 61.199 -- Expired flight instructor certificates and ratings

If you let your CFI cert expire, you can reinstate by passing a practical test in the rating you held, plus you must meet the 61.183 requirements that apply (the FOI test result is permanent and does not need re-taking; the spin endorsement is permanent). You do not need to recomplete FOI training.

## Subparts I, J, K -- name and locate

- Subpart I (Ground Instructors) -- 61.211 to 61.219. The non-flying instructor cert. Three flavors: basic ground (BGI), advanced ground (AGI), instrument ground (IGI). Skim. The CFI you'll become can give ground instruction without an additional ground-instructor cert; the ground-instructor cert is for non-flying instructors.

- Subpart J (Sport Pilots) -- 61.301 to 61.327. Sport pilot privileges and limitations. Not relevant to your CFI initial unless you're going sport-pilot CFI (different subpart, K).

- Subpart K (Flight Instructors with a Sport Pilot Rating) -- 61.401 to 61.429. The CFI-S cert, parallel to your CFI but limited to light-sport aircraft. Skim. Different aero-experience requirement, different endorsement scope, but the same pedagogical philosophy (FOI, training records, renewal cycle).

## How to use the walk

Subpart H is short enough that the whole shape fits in your head after one careful read. The high-yield sections are 61.193 (privileges) and 61.195 (limitations) -- they bound the daily decisions you'll make. The next-most-cited is 61.197 (renewal) because it touches you every 24 months for the rest of your career. The other sections are eligibility, training records, and reinstatement -- structurally important, less frequently consulted.

Route a CFI question through the shape:

- "Can I sign this?" -> 61.193 (privileges) for what's covered, 61.195 (limitations) for what's blocked
- "Do I need to keep a record of this?" -> 61.189
- "How do I renew?" -> 61.197
- "I let it lapse, now what?" -> 61.199
- "Can I add multi-engine instructor to my cert?" -> 61.191
- "What does the practical test cover?" -> 61.187 + ACS

## Common misreadings

- **"61.193 lists everything I can do as a CFI."** Wrong, technically. 61.193 lists the eight categorical privileges, but the *scope* of those privileges depends on what categories, classes, and ratings appear on your cert. A CFI airplane single-engine cannot sign a multiengine flight review under 61.193(a)(5) -- the privilege exists, but the scope doesn't.
- **"My CFI cert is just like my pilot cert."** Wrong. Your pilot cert (private, commercial, ATP) does not expire. Your CFI cert does -- 24 calendar months. 61.199 is what happens if you forget.
- **"I only have to keep records of solo endorsements."** Wrong per 61.189. The record obligation covers solo endorsements, knowledge-test endorsements, AND practical-test endorsements. Three categories. Three years. Many CFIs keep only the first.
- **"FOI is one-time forever."** Mostly true: the FOI knowledge test result does not need retaking, and the spin endorsement is permanent. But the *FOI subjects* show up in renewal FIRC content; you don't get to forget them.
- **"If I let my CFI lapse I can renew with a FIRC."** Wrong. 61.199 governs expired CFIs and requires a practical test (or designated examiner check). The FIRC is a *pre-expiration* tool, not a post-expiration one.
- **"61.195(b) says I can teach in any airplane I'm rated in."** Wrong. 61.195(b) says you can teach in any airplane you hold the *appropriate* category, class, AND TYPE rating for. A CFI rated airplane single-engine land cannot teach in a King Air without the type rating, and the right-seat type rating is structurally complicated.
