# CFI privileges and limitations

61.193 and 61.195 are the two sections you will consult most often as a working CFI. Together they answer every "can I sign this?" question, every "is this within my authority?" question, and every "do I need a different rating before I can do this?" question.

You walked the shape of subpart H in [01-subpart-h-walk.md](01-subpart-h-walk.md). You learned the gate (61.183) and the records obligation (61.189) in [02-eligibility-and-experience.md](02-eligibility-and-experience.md). This lesson handles the daily-life sections.

## What you'll be able to do

- Recite the eight categorical privileges of 61.193(a) without referring to a table of contents
- Apply the major limitations in 61.195 to a real CFI in a real situation: hours-per-day, aircraft ratings, instrument-instruction, make-and-model PIC time, instrument PIC time
- Diagnose where 61.193 ends and 61.113 begins -- the compensation boundary that catches CFIs flying as pilots
- Predict, given a sign-off request, whether a particular CFI is authorized to sign it

## Why this matters

Every endorsement you sign as a CFI is an exercise of a 61.193 privilege constrained by 61.195 limitations. Sign outside your privilege and you've created an invalid endorsement (the student is not actually authorized to do whatever you tried to bless), and you've exposed yourself to certificate action.

The harder version of this concept is the compensation boundary. A CFI who flies a friend's airplane to dinner and accepts gas money is functioning as a *pilot* receiving compensation, not as a *CFI* receiving compensation. The same person, the same airplane, the same flight -- but a different pilot certificate governs. 61.113 (private privileges) is the binding limitation here, not 61.195. Most CFIs do not internalize that 61.195 doesn't authorize them to do anything 61.113 forbids.

## The discovery question

A friend of yours, a private pilot rated single-engine, asks if you'll come along on a flight to a destination 200 nm away for a Saturday lunch with their family. They offer to pay for the gas. You hold a CFI certificate.

The flight is in their airplane. They will be PIC. You will be in the right seat.

Pause. What questions do you ask before saying yes? Specifically:

1. Is this a CFI flight? If so, what privilege of 61.193 covers it?
2. If not, what regulation governs you accepting the gas money?
3. If you accept the gas money, are you flying as a CFI or as a private pilot, for purposes of 61.113 and 61.195?
4. Does the answer change if your friend asks you to "give me some pointers" during the flight?
5. Does the answer change if you're a commercial-rated pilot in addition to being a CFI?

Most of these answers come from 61.113 and 61.195, not from 61.193. The discovery is that the CFI privileges section is a small island in a much bigger ocean of pilot-compensation rules. You can be a CFI on the certificate and still be governed by 61.113 on the day, depending on what you're doing.

## What 61.193 actually says

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/193?at=2026) -- "Flight instructor privileges":

```text
14 CFR 61.193  Flight instructor privileges.

(a) A person who holds a flight instructor certificate is
    authorized within the limitations of that person's flight
    instructor certificate and ratings to give training and
    endorsements that are required for, and relate to:

    (1) A student pilot certificate;
    (2) A pilot certificate;
    (3) A flight instructor certificate;
    (4) A ground instructor certificate;
    (5) An aircraft rating;
    (6) An instrument rating;
    (7) A flight review, operating privilege, or recency of
        experience requirement of this part;
    (8) A practical test; and
    (9) A knowledge test.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg.
```

Nine privilege categories. (1) through (4) are the certificate-related privileges. (5) and (6) are the rating-related privileges. (7) covers everything currency- and recency-related (flight reviews, IPCs, the operating privileges of 61.31). (8) and (9) cover the recommendation-for-test endorsements.

The structural takeaway: a CFI is authorized to give *training and endorsements* across the full pilot-certification ladder. Read narrowly, 61.193(a) is a list of nine bullets. Read structurally, it says: a CFI is the FAA's primary authority-extender into the field. Almost everything that puts a pilot in the air for the first time, or recertifies them periodically, runs through a CFI's signature.

The two most-cited subsections in daily practice:

- **61.193(a)(7)** is the flight review and IPC authority. Every airplane flight review in the country is signed by a CFI under this provision.
- **61.193(a)(8)** is the practical-test recommendation authority. Every checkride applicant gets here through a CFI's recommendation.

## What 61.195 actually says

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026) -- "Flight instructor limitations and qualifications":

```text
14 CFR 61.195  Flight instructor limitations and qualifications.

A person who holds a flight instructor certificate is subject to
the following limitations:

(a) Hours of training. In any 24-consecutive-hour period, a flight
    instructor may not conduct more than 8 hours of flight training.

(b) Aircraft ratings. A flight instructor may not conduct flight
    training in any aircraft for which the flight instructor does
    not hold:
    (1) A pilot certificate and flight instructor certificate with
        the applicable category and class rating; and
    (2) If appropriate, a type rating ...

(c) Instrument rating. A flight instructor who provides instrument
    training for the issuance of an instrument rating, a type rating
    not limited to VFR, or the instrument training required for
    commercial pilot and airline transport pilot certificates must
    hold an instrument rating on his or her flight instructor
    certificate and pilot certificate that is appropriate to the
    category and class of aircraft used.

(d) Limitations on endorsements. A flight instructor may not endorse
    a:
    (1) Student pilot's pre-solo flight maneuvers and procedures
        endorsement (61.87(c)) unless that flight instructor has:
        (i) Given that student the flight training required by
            61.87(c)(1) and (2); and
        (ii) Determined that the student is prepared to conduct the
             flight safely under known circumstances ...;
    (2) ... [pre-solo cross-country, solo cross-country, additional
        91.205, etc.];

(e) Training in multiengine airplane, helicopter, glider, etc. A
    flight instructor may not give training required for the
    issuance of a certificate or rating in a multiengine airplane,
    helicopter, or powered-lift unless that flight instructor has
    at least 5 hours of pilot in command time in the specific make
    and model ...

(f) Training for an additional aircraft rating. A flight instructor
    may not give training to a person for an additional aircraft
    rating ... unless that flight instructor has, in the specific
    make and model ... at least 5 hours of pilot-in-command flight
    time.

(g) Position in aircraft and required pilot stations for providing
    flight training. A flight instructor must perform all training
    from in an aircraft that complies with the requirements of
    91.109 of this chapter.

(h) Aerobatic flight instruction. A flight instructor must hold an
    aerobatic flight instructor rating ... [niche, if applicable].

Note: structural paraphrase. Read the actual reg.
```

The major limitations, plain reading:

### (a) -- 8 hours per 24-hour period

You may not conduct more than 8 hours of flight training in any 24-consecutive-hour period. Note: the limit is on *flight training*, not on total time in the airplane. A 9-hour ferry flight where you are PIC and not training a student is not a 61.195(a) issue (although it may be a different fatigue / 91.13 issue). This caps the daily teaching load. In practice, 8 hours of flight teaching on a single day is exhausting -- the regulation rarely binds before the CFI's energy does.

### (b) -- Hold the rating you teach in

You cannot teach in an aircraft you don't hold the appropriate category, class, and (if required) type rating for. A CFI airplane single-engine cannot teach in a King Air without a type rating, even if they happen to be commercial-rated for the King Air via 61.31(g) and the high-altitude endorsement. The CFI rating itself is what binds.

The trap: a commercial-rated pilot who is *not* a CFI in multiengine cannot give multi-engine instruction even if they have abundant multi PIC time. They need a CFI multiengine to teach multi-engine. The pilot certificate alone is not enough.

### (c) -- Instrument instruction requires CFII

To give instrument instruction toward an instrument rating, or instrument-rating-required portions of commercial / ATP training, the CFI must hold the instrument-airplane (or instrument-helicopter, etc.) instructor rating -- the CFII. A CFI who lacks the CFII can teach VFR portions of commercial training but cannot teach the 10 hours of instrument time the commercial 61.129(a)(3)(i) requires.

The wrinkle: the CFI's underlying *pilot* certificate must also have the instrument rating. A CFI who lost their instrument rating in some unusual scenario but kept the CFII would still be unable to instruct under (c) until the IR is back on the pilot certificate.

### (e) -- 5 hours in multi/heli/powered-lift make and model

To give training "required for the issuance of a certificate or rating" in a multi-engine airplane, helicopter, or powered-lift, the CFI must have at least 5 hours of PIC in the *specific make and model*. A CFI multi-engine who has 200 hours in a Seminole but only 2 hours in an Aztec cannot give a student the rating-issuance training in the Aztec. They would need to log 3 more hours of PIC in the Aztec first.

### (f) -- 5 hours in any rating's specific make and model

Same 5-hour rule applies for any aircraft for which the training will lead to an additional rating. This catches CFIs who have abundant ASEL time but step into an unusual category or class for the first time -- a CFI airplane single-engine who tries to give a student tailwheel-endorsement training in an aircraft the CFI has only 2 hours of PIC in is in 61.195(f) trouble.

### (g) -- 91.109-compliant flight stations

The CFI must perform training from a flight station that complies with 91.109 (the dual-control rule). Most flight-school airplanes are factory-equipped with dual controls. Aircraft that lack a dual yoke or dual rudder pedals at the right seat may not be used for flight instruction without a 91.109 waiver or a configuration change.

### (i) Implicit -- the spin endorsement currency

Not in 61.195 itself but cross-referenced via 61.183(i): a CFI who has never received the spin endorsement cannot give *initial* CFI candidates the spin training they need for their own 61.183(i) endorsement. (The spin endorsement is one-time-forever for both the receiving CFI candidate and for the giving CFI to be authorized to teach spins on a regular basis.)

## The 61.193 / 61.113 boundary -- the compensation problem

This is the single most important integration concept in subpart H. It is what catches CFIs who think their CFI rating insulates them from private-pilot compensation rules.

### The frame

A CFI is also a pilot. You hold (per 61.183(c)) a commercial or ATP certificate underneath the CFI cert. When you fly, you are governed by:

- The privileges and limitations of *your underlying pilot certificate* (61.113 if you are exercising private privileges, 61.133 if commercial)
- Plus, *if you are giving instruction*, the privileges and limitations of your CFI certificate (61.193 / 61.195)

The 61.193 / 61.195 layer activates only when you are *giving instruction*. The rest of the time, you are operating under your pilot certificate.

### What "compensation" looks like in each lane

| Activity                                                                                             | Governing reg                                                      | What's allowed                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| You give a primary student a 1.0 lesson                                                              | 61.193(a)(2) + 61.195                                              | You may charge for the instruction.                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| You sign a flight review for a private pilot                                                         | 61.193(a)(7) + 61.195                                              | You may charge for the review.                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| You ferry a friend's airplane home for them, no instruction given                                    | 61.113 (you are exercising private privileges)                     | No compensation; cost-sharing under 61.113(c) only with pro-rata + common purpose.                                                                                                                                     |
| You take a commercial rated friend's airplane to dinner, friend pays gas, you fly                    | 61.113 (private privileges, because no instruction is being given) | Cost-sharing under 61.113(c). The CFI rating gives you no extra compensation right.                                                                                                                                    |
| You "give some pointers" to your friend during the flight                                            | Probably 61.193(a) (instruction is being given)                    | The compensation question shifts: if you're being paid as a CFI, the CFI lane applies. But "I'll teach you a thing or two" is not a CFI relationship; the FAA looks at the substance of the agreement, not the labels. |
| You hold commercial and CFI; you're paid to fly your friend's airplane somewhere with no instruction | 61.133 (commercial privileges -- pilot for hire)                   | The CFI rating is irrelevant. Commercial pilot rules govern.                                                                                                                                                           |

### The Mangiamele framing

The Mangiamele (2009) Chief Counsel letter on 61.113 is not about CFIs specifically, but it sets the test the FAA uses for "compensation" generally: a benefit received in exchange for the flight is compensation, regardless of whether money changes hands. A CFI who flies a friend's airplane to a destination the *friend* wanted to go to, and accepts gas money, is in 61.113(c) cost-sharing territory -- the friend's destination is the common purpose -- and the CFI must satisfy the pro-rata cost-sharing requirements.

The trap: many CFIs assume "I'm a CFI, I can fly anywhere with anyone and accept gas money." Wrong. The CFI rating gives no additional compensation rights for non-instructional flight. 61.113(c) governs.

### The "I gave them some pointers" trap

A CFI who flies recreationally with a friend and casually offers some pointers during the flight has potentially crossed into 61.193 territory. The Walker (2011) Chief Counsel letter and others have framed this: instruction is what makes a relationship instruction. Not labels, not invoices. If the substance of what happens in the cockpit is teaching, the FAA views it as instruction. Combined with compensation, this creates a CFI flight subject to 61.193 / 61.195 -- not a private-pilot recreational flight subject to 61.113.

The defensive posture: if you are flying recreationally with another pilot and you're not being paid to instruct, do not "give pointers." Either log the flight as instruction (and trigger 61.193 / 61.195 / 61.189 obligations and an endorsement), or don't teach during the flight. The middle ground is where enforcement actions live.

## Common misreadings

- **"61.193 lets me sign anything for any pilot."** Wrong. 61.193 lists nine privilege categories, but each is "within the limitations of that person's flight instructor certificate and ratings." A CFI airplane single-engine cannot sign a multiengine flight review under 61.193(a)(7). The privilege exists; the scope (governed by 61.195(b) and (e)) doesn't.
- **"61.195 only matters if I'm doing something unusual."** Wrong. 61.195 binds every flight-instruction encounter. The 8-hour-per-24 rule, the rating-match rule, the make-and-model PIC requirement, the position-in-aircraft rule -- they all apply to ordinary teaching, every day.
- **"My CFI rating lets me accept gas money for flights with friends."** Wrong. The CFI rating gives no additional compensation rights for non-instructional flight. 61.113 (private) or 61.133 (commercial) governs the underlying pilot certificate; the CFI rating only kicks in when you are *giving instruction*.
- **"I can teach in any airplane I'm a commercial pilot in."** Wrong. 61.195(b) requires the CFI rating to match the category, class, and (if applicable) type. Commercial-rated in a King Air without a CFI multi-engine and a King Air type rating? Not a CFI multi-engine, can't teach there.
- **"I can teach instrument as long as I have an instrument rating on my pilot certificate."** Wrong. 61.195(c) requires the *CFI* certificate to have the instrument-instructor rating (CFII). The IR on the pilot cert is necessary but not sufficient.
- **"I can give multi-engine training as soon as I have my CFI multi-engine."** Subtly wrong. 61.195(e) and (f) require 5 hours of PIC in the *specific make and model* before you can give rating-issuance training in that make and model. A new CFI multi-engine with 0 hours in an Aztec cannot teach an Aztec student until they've logged 5 hours of PIC in the Aztec themselves.
- **"61.195(a)'s 8-hour limit means I can fly at most 8 hours per day."** Wrong. The 8-hour cap is on *flight training*. A CFI who flies 4 hours of training plus a 3-hour ferry flight is at 4 hours of flight training that day, not 7.
- **"If I sign a flight review, I'm done; the pilot is current for everything."** Wrong. The flight review (61.56) addresses one currency item. Recency-of-experience requirements for passenger carriage (61.57(a) day, (b) night) and IFR currency (61.57(c)) are separate timers; the review does not refresh them.
- **"AC 61-65 is regulation."** Wrong. AC 61-65 is guidance. It publishes recommended endorsement language. The regulatory authority to give endorsements lives in 61.193; the regulatory limitations on endorsements live in 61.195. AC 61-65 tells you the words; the regulations tell you when the words are valid.

### Apply the rules

> A CFI airplane single-engine, instrument-rated, holds a CFII. They have 200 hours of PIC in a Cessna 172 and 0 hours of PIC in a Diamond DA40. A primary student wants to take their checkride in the DA40. Can the CFI provide the rating-issuance training?

Answer: Not yet. 61.195(f) requires 5 hours of PIC in the specific make and model before giving rating-issuance training. The CFI must log 5 hours of PIC in the DA40 first. Once logged, they may begin training the student.

> A CFI airplane single-engine, no instrument rating on their pilot certificate, holds the CFI airplane single-engine rating but no CFII. A commercial student needs the 10 hours of instrument training required by 61.129(a)(3)(i). Can the CFI provide it?

Answer: No. 61.195(c) requires the instrument-instructor rating (CFII), and that requires the IR on the underlying pilot cert. The CFI must add the IR to their pilot cert and the CFII to their CFI cert before instructing in any of the commercial-required instrument time.

> A CFI gives 4 hours of flight training in the morning, then ferries an airplane 5 hours that afternoon (no student, just repositioning). The CFI is at 4 hours of flight training that day. Are they at 9 hours and out?

Answer: No. 61.195(a) caps *flight training* at 8 hours in 24, not total flight time. The 5-hour ferry is not flight training. The CFI is at 4 of 8 in the daily flight-training cap. (Other fatigue concerns and 91.13 may apply, but 61.195(a) is not the bind.)

> A friend asks a CFI to fly the friend's airplane to a destination 200 nm away to drop the friend off. The CFI's expected compensation: free use of the airplane on the return leg. Is this a CFI flight or a private flight?

Answer: This is a private flight. No instruction is contemplated. 61.113 governs. The "free use of the airplane on the return" is potential compensation; if it doesn't satisfy 61.113(c) cost-sharing (pro rata + common purpose), and isn't covered by another 61.113 carve-out, the CFI is exercising private privileges in a way that may violate 61.113. The CFI rating is irrelevant here.

> Same friend, same trip, but during the outbound leg the CFI gives the friend a 1.0 hour of CFI-supervised hood time toward the friend's instrument rating. Now what?

Answer: Now there's an instructional component. 61.193(a)(2) covers the training. 61.195 limitations apply: CFI must hold the CFII (61.195(c)), the airplane must comply with 91.109 (61.195(g)), records under 61.189 must be kept (logbook entry), and the CFI must hold the appropriate CAT/class rating in the aircraft (61.195(b)). The CFI may charge for the 1.0 instruction. The remaining flight time outside the instruction is private flying under 61.113.

### Trap detector

| Statement                                                                 | Trap and correction                                                                                                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| "61.193 says I can sign anything as a CFI."                               | Wrong. 61.193 lists categories; 61.195(b) constrains them to the category/class/type rating you hold on the CFI cert.                                                                      |
| "I'm a commercial pilot, so I can teach commercial students."             | Wrong. Commercial pilot is a *pilot* rating. Teaching requires a CFI cert with appropriate CFI rating per 61.195(b), and CFII for the instrument portions per 61.195(c).                   |
| "I can teach in any aircraft I have a complex endorsement for."           | Wrong. 61.31 endorsements are operational privileges for *your* flying. Teaching requires 61.195(b) match: CFI cert with appropriate cat/class/type. The 61.31 endorsement is independent. |
| "Once I'm a CFI, I can fly any flight with any compensation arrangement." | Wrong. The CFI rating layer activates only for instruction. 61.113 (private) or 61.133 (commercial) governs your non-instructional flying.                                                 |
| "61.195(a)'s 8-hour cap is on total time in the aircraft."                | Wrong. The cap is on flight *training*. Ferry flights, personal flying, and other non-instructional time are not counted against the 8-hour limit.                                         |
| "If I have the CFII, I can teach instrument in a multiengine."            | Wrong without further details. 61.195(b) requires the CFI multiengine rating *and* CFII for instrument instruction in a multiengine. CFII alone is not enough.                             |
| "AC 61-65 grants me the authority to sign endorsements."                  | Wrong. AC 61-65 publishes recommended language. Authority to sign comes from 61.193; limitations from 61.195.                                                                              |
