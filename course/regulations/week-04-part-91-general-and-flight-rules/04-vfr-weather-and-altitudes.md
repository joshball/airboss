# VFR fuel, weather, special VFR, and cruising altitudes

This lesson covers the four VFR rules pilots quote constantly and misapply about as often. §91.151 sets fuel reserves. §91.155 sets the basic VFR weather minimums (visibility and cloud clearance). §91.157 carves out special VFR. §91.159 sets the hemispheric cruising altitude rule. Together these four sections define the legal envelope of a VFR flight.

§91.155 is the most-recited regulation on the private-pilot oral. The "3 SM, 1,000 above, 500 below, 2,000 horizontal" cadence is repeated by every CFI candidate. The trap is that the cadence applies *only* to a specific airspace and altitude band; the table is layered, not flat. §91.157 special VFR is layered on top of §91.155, not a separate path. §91.151 fuel reserves are absolute floors; §91.159 cruising altitudes are absolute requirements above 3,000 AGL on a magnetic course.

## What you'll be able to do

- State §91.151 fuel reserves cold (day VFR, night VFR, IFR) and apply them to flight planning
- Recite the §91.155 visibility and cloud-clearance table by airspace and altitude
- Apply §91.157 special VFR criteria, including the night-currency-and-instrument-rated requirement
- State §91.159 cruising altitudes (east/west hemispheric rule, magnetic course, above 3,000 AGL)
- Distinguish "VFR weather minimums" from "good weather to fly today" -- the regulation is a floor, not a recommendation

## Why this matters

The VFR envelope rules are tested on every checkride and applied on every flight. Pilots get them wrong in three predictable ways:

1. They quote the §91.155 "3-1-1-1-2-2-2" pattern as if it applies everywhere, when the actual table varies by airspace and altitude.
2. They treat §91.157 special VFR as a free lower-than-VFR exit; it requires ATC clearance, day/night and instrument-rated conditions, and applies only in controlled airspace surrounding airports.
3. They plan with §91.151 fuel reserves as targets instead of floors, then arrive with bingo fuel and a §91.13 careless charge after a precautionary landing.

The four sections are also building blocks for higher-altitude flying, IFR alternates, and the integration scenarios in Week 6. Pilots who skim them in private-pilot training pay for it on the commercial and the CFI checkride.

## The discovery question

You file a VFR flight from KPAO to KSAC tomorrow morning. Forecast: 2,500 broken at KPAO, scattered clouds at 4,500 along the route, clear at KSAC. Visibility 6 statute miles at KPAO clearing to 10+ at KSAC. You plan 5,500 feet for the cruise leg, magnetic course 015 (north). Fuel: tabs full, ~36 gallons usable in a 172, planning a 1.4 hour flight at ~7.5 gph cruise.

Pause.

Three checks before you launch. First, are the §91.155 weather minimums met for your departure, en route, and arrival? Second, is your altitude legal under §91.159? Third, is your fuel legal under §91.151?

The §91.155 check routes through the airspace and altitude. KPAO is in Class D surface to 2,500 feet. With a forecast of 2,500 broken, the ceiling at the field is right at 2,500 -- below the §91.155(c) Class D minimum of 1,000 feet ceiling for VFR pattern operations. You cannot legally depart VFR with a ceiling at 2,500 if the §91.155(c)(2) "VFR ceiling" definition applies. (You may be able to depart with special VFR; see below.)

The §91.159 check routes through "magnetic course." 015 magnetic is "0 to 179" -- the eastbound hemisphere. §91.159 requires odd-thousands-plus-500: 3,500, 5,500, 7,500. 5,500 is legal eastbound. (West/south would be 4,500, 6,500, 8,500.) The check applies above 3,000 AGL; below that you're not bound by §91.159.

The §91.151 check routes through day VFR. Day VFR requires 30 minutes reserve at normal cruise consumption. 30 minutes at 7.5 gph is 3.75 gallons. Your planned flight uses 10.5 gallons; with 36 usable, you have ~25 gallons reserve. Fine.

But: the §91.155(c) Class D ceiling-and-visibility check fails. The trap is that pilots focus on the en route and arrival weather and forget that the *departure* must meet §91.155 too. With a 2,500 broken ceiling at a Class D field, you're either grounded, or you're requesting special VFR (§91.157) under day, into-airspace conditions.

This is the typical Part 91 VFR-weather trap. Pilots think VFR planning means "is the destination clear?" The regulation requires meeting the minimums at every point.

## §91.151 fuel reserves

```text
§91.151  Fuel requirements for flight in VFR conditions.

(a) No person may begin a flight in an airplane under VFR conditions
    unless (considering wind and forecast weather conditions) there is
    enough fuel to fly to the first point of intended landing and,
    assuming normal cruising speed--

    (1) During the day, to fly after that for at least 30 minutes; or
    (2) At night, to fly after that for at least 45 minutes.

(b) No person may begin a flight in a rotorcraft under VFR conditions
    unless (considering wind and forecast weather conditions) there is
    enough fuel to fly to the first point of intended landing and,
    assuming normal cruising speed, to fly after that for at least 20
    minutes.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The structural points:

- **"Begin a flight."** The check is at takeoff. You may not depart legally with insufficient fuel for §91.151. You may consume your reserve in flight if conditions change; the §91.13 careless rule attaches if you knew at takeoff you would.
- **"First point of intended landing."** Not the destination of the flight plan. The next planned landing point.
- **"Considering wind and forecast weather."** Plan with actual headwinds and forecast deviations. A pilot who plans no-wind on a 30-knot headwind day is in §91.151 territory before departure.
- **"Normal cruising speed."** The number is set by the AFM/POH cruise figure, not the optimistic best-economy number on the iPad.

| Condition       | Reserve required (airplane) | Reserve required (rotorcraft) |
| --------------- | --------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Day VFR         | 30 minutes                  | 20 minutes                    |
| Night VFR       | 45 minutes                  | 20 minutes                    |
| IFR (§91.167)   | 45 minutes (different rule) | --                            |

§91.151 is an absolute floor, not a target. Plan with margin.

## §91.155 VFR weather minimums

§91.155 sets visibility and cloud clearance by airspace and altitude. The table is layered:

```text
§91.155(a) -- General airspace minimums (table by airspace)
§91.155(b) -- Class G night exception
§91.155(c) -- Airport (Class B/C/D/E surface) minimum ceilings for VFR ops
```

The §91.155(a) table:

| Airspace           | Altitude band              | Visibility (SM) | Cloud clearance        |
| ------------------ | -------------------------- | --------------- | ---------------------- |
| Class B            | All                        | 3               | Clear of clouds        |
| Class C, D, E      | < 10,000 MSL               | 3               | 500 below / 1,000 above / 2,000 horizontal |
| Class C, D, E      | >= 10,000 MSL              | 5               | 1,000 below / 1,000 above / 1 SM horizontal |
| Class G day        | <= 1,200 AGL               | 1               | Clear of clouds        |
| Class G day        | > 1,200 AGL, < 10,000 MSL  | 1               | 500 below / 1,000 above / 2,000 horizontal |
| Class G            | > 1,200 AGL, >= 10,000 MSL | 5               | 1,000 below / 1,000 above / 1 SM horizontal |
| Class G night      | <= 1,200 AGL               | 3               | 500 below / 1,000 above / 2,000 horizontal |

Two structural patterns to memorize once and apply forever:

1. **Class G is the exception band.** Class G below 1,200 AGL during day is the "1 mile, clear of clouds" zone -- this is where pilots are surprised the regulation lets you fly. The rest of the airspace requires 3 SM and the 500/1,000/2,000 (or 5/1,000/1,000/1 SM at high altitude) clearances.
2. **The 10,000 MSL break is an altitude rule, not an airspace rule.** Above 10,000 MSL the visibility increases to 5 SM and the horizontal clearance to 1 SM, regardless of airspace (other than B). The reason: at high altitude you're closing on traffic faster, you need more sight distance.

§91.155(c) layers a *ceiling and visibility* check on top of (a) for operations in Class B/C/D/E surface area underneath an airport's controlled airspace. The relevant numbers: 1,000 foot ceiling and 3 SM visibility for VFR pattern operations. Without a 1,000 foot ceiling, you can't legally do VFR pattern work even if §91.155(a) cloud clearance is met.

## §91.157 special VFR

```text
§91.157  Special VFR weather minimums.

(a) Except as provided in appendix D, section 3, of this part, special
    VFR operations may be conducted under the weather minimums and
    requirements of this section, instead of those contained in §91.155...

(b) Special VFR operations may only be conducted--
    (1) With an ATC clearance;
    (2) Clear of clouds;
    (3) Except for helicopters, when flight visibility is at least 1
        statute mile;
    (4) Except for helicopters, between sunrise and sunset (or in Alaska,
        when the sun is 6 degrees or more below the horizon) unless--
        (i) The person being granted the ATC clearance meets the applicable
            requirements for instrument flight under part 61 of this chapter; and
        (ii) The aircraft is equipped as required in §91.205(d).

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

Special VFR is a clearance, not a self-issued exception. Three operative gates:

1. **Clearance required.** Request from ATC. ATC may deny or hold.
2. **Day-only by default.** Sunrise to sunset. Night special VFR requires the pilot to be instrument-rated and current under §61 and the airplane to be equipped per §91.205(d) (IFR equipment). The exception exists because instrument-rated pilots in IFR-equipped airplanes can transition to instruments if the special VFR conditions further deteriorate.
3. **1 SM and clear of clouds.** Ceiling and visibility on the ground; flight visibility 1 SM in the air.

Special VFR applies inside the Class B/C/D/E surface area of an airport. It does not authorize special VFR in en-route Class E airspace. The carve-out is for getting in or out of an airport when basic VFR can't be met, not for cross-country flight in marginal weather.

Appendix D, section 3 lists airports where special VFR is prohibited. These are major airports (KORD, KLAX, KJFK, etc.). At those airports, special VFR is unavailable and the only legal exit/entry below VFR minimums is IFR.

## §91.159 VFR cruising altitudes

```text
§91.159  VFR cruising altitude or flight level.

Except while holding in a holding pattern of 2 minutes or less, or while
turning, each person operating an aircraft under VFR in level cruising
flight more than 3,000 feet above the surface shall maintain the
appropriate altitude or flight level prescribed below, unless otherwise
authorized by ATC:

(a) When operating below 18,000 feet MSL and--
    (1) On a magnetic course of zero degrees through 179 degrees, any
        odd thousand foot MSL altitude + 500 feet (such as 3,500, 5,500,
        or 7,500); or
    (2) On a magnetic course of 180 degrees through 359 degrees, any
        even thousand foot MSL altitude + 500 feet (such as 4,500, 6,500,
        or 8,500).

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The pattern: above 3,000 AGL, maintain hemispheric VFR altitudes. East (000-179): odd thousands + 500. West (180-359): even thousands + 500.

| Magnetic course | Altitudes (below 18,000 MSL) |
| --------------- | ---------------------------- |
| 0-179 (east)    | 3,500, 5,500, 7,500, 9,500   |
| 180-359 (west)  | 4,500, 6,500, 8,500, 10,500  |

Three operative points:

1. **Above 3,000 AGL.** Below 3,000 AGL the rule does not apply. Pattern work, low cross-country, traffic-pattern transitions are exempt.
2. **Magnetic course, not magnetic heading.** The course is the line drawn on the chart between two points; the heading is the airplane's nose direction. Pilots flying with a strong crosswind sometimes use heading by mistake. The regulation is course.
3. **"Unless otherwise authorized by ATC."** ATC can assign any altitude. A flight following 5,500 westbound is legal because ATC authorized it; the violation is the pilot self-selecting an inappropriate altitude without authorization.

## Common misreadings

**§91.155 "3-1-1-1-2-2-2" everywhere.** The pattern applies to Class C/D/E below 10,000 MSL. Above 10,000 MSL the rule changes to 5/1,000/1,000/1 SM. Class B is "3 and clear of clouds." Class G below 1,200 AGL during day is "1 and clear of clouds." Pilots who recite the pattern as universal flunk the application question.

**Special VFR as a self-clearance.** Pilots remember the 1 SM and clear-of-clouds and forget the ATC clearance and day/night-instrument-rated requirement. Operating "special VFR" without the clearance is a violation.

**§91.151 day vs. night.** The fuel reserve doubles at night. Pilots planning a flight that crosses sunset routinely forget the rule changes mid-flight. The §91.151 standard at takeoff is the standard for the *flight*; if the flight will end at night, plan with night reserves.

**§91.159 below 3,000 AGL.** Pilots cite the rule for pattern work or short flights. The rule does not apply below 3,000 AGL.

**§91.155 ceiling vs. cloud clearance.** A "ceiling" is the lowest broken or overcast layer. A "cloud clearance" is the minimum vertical or horizontal distance from any cloud. They are different concepts. A 2,500 broken layer is a 2,500 ceiling; the cloud clearance below it is 500 feet, which puts you no higher than 2,000 MSL legally. Pilots conflate the two.

## Where these sit in the regulatory map

§91.151 lives in subpart B (Flight Rules) under the §91.103 preflight planning umbrella. §91.155 / §91.157 / §91.159 live in subpart B under the visual flight rules section. The four sections together set the legal envelope of any VFR flight. Each layers on top of the others; you must meet all four to be legal.

The §91.13 catch-all attaches to violations of any of these sections. A §91.151 fuel-reserve violation is a §91.13 careless violation. A §91.155 cloud-clearance violation is a §91.13 careless violation. A §91.159 wrong-altitude violation is a §91.13 careless violation if it produces a near-miss.
