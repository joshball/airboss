# Week 4 oral

## The scenario

You're the CFI. A 130-hour private pilot you have not flown with before walks into the school at 9 AM Saturday and says they want to fly KPAO -> KMRY -> KPAO today, three friends in the airplane, a 172. Plan: depart KPAO 1100 local, fuel at KMRY, return KPAO by 1700.

Conditions:

- KPAO 1100z forecast: 2,500 broken, 6 SM, wind 270 at 8
- KMRY 1300 forecast: 4,000 scattered, 8 SM, wind 290 at 12
- KPAO 1700 forecast: 1,800 broken, 4 SM, wind 250 at 10 (deteriorating PM)
- En route: scattered to broken layer at 5,000-6,000 MSL, visibility 6-8 SM
- Sigmet: none
- AIRMET: occasional moderate turbulence below 8,000 along coast

The pilot's logbook:

- Last flight review: 18 months ago, in a 172
- Last 3 takeoffs and landings: 6 weeks ago, day, in a 172
- Last night flight: 4 months ago
- Total time: 130
- Time in 172: 95
- Last 90 days: 8 hours

Walk me through the regulatory checks before you decide whether to fly with them, ride along, or send them solo. Identify every rule, the section number, and the live answer.

This oral pulls cumulatively from Weeks 1-4 and connects to Week 9 (the regulatory consequences if something goes wrong).

## Expected discussion arcs

### Arc 1: The pilot is current, but is the flight legal?

Pilot currency: §61.56 flight review at 18 months -- lapsed (24-month limit). Cannot legally act as PIC. **First gate fails.**

If the flight review were current: §61.57(a) day passenger-carrying recency -- 3 takeoffs and landings within 90 days. The pilot's 6-week-ago count is fine. Legal for daytime passenger carrying.

§61.57(b) night recency: not directly relevant for a planned 1100-1700 flight, but if weather delays push the return past sunset, the night recency check (3 takeoffs and landings to a full stop within 90 days) becomes operative. The pilot's 4-month-ago last night flight fails. If the return slips past sunset, the pilot cannot legally carry passengers.

The CFI question to ask: is this pilot fit to fly under §61.56 today? If the answer is "their flight review lapsed," the CFI either declines or offers to *do* the flight review on this flight (which changes the structure of the trip).

Expected: candidate identifies §61.56 as the immediate disqualifier, and surfaces the §61.57(b) night-passenger trap if the return slips past sunset.

### Arc 2: §91.7 and §91.103 -- preflight

The pilot has the airplane reserved. Has the airplane been preflighted? The CFI checks:

§91.7(a) airworthiness. Is the airplane in airworthy condition? Annual inspection current (within 12 calendar months)? 100-hour inspection if applicable (rented for instruction = 100-hour applies)? VOR check within 30 days if filing IFR (not relevant for VFR but the airplane should be current)? ELT inspection (last 12 calendar months for ELT, battery within published interval)? Transponder (24 calendar months; §91.413).

§91.7(b) is the PIC's determination for *this flight*. The PIC, not the mechanic, decides the airplane is fit. The CFI's job is to verify the pilot is asking the right questions.

§91.103 preflight action. The pilot must have considered "all available information." This is the broad preflight clause. The CFI walks the pilot through:

- Weather: KPAO and KMRY METARs and TAFs; en-route AIRMETs and SIGMETs (the moderate turbulence AIRMET applies); winds aloft.
- NOTAMs: KPAO, KMRY, en-route VORs, and any TFRs (always check TFRs; pilots forget).
- Runway lengths and weights at KMRY for the 172 and the loading.
- Fuel: enough at KPAO for departure, fuel availability at KMRY confirmed (not just assumed -- some airports have fuel pump issues).
- Alternates: not legally required for VFR but the §91.103 "all available information" includes thinking about diversion options if KMRY weather drops below VFR (they're below VFR by 1700 forecast at KPAO).
- Performance data: actual takeoff distance and landing distance for KPAO/KMRY in the 172 with the planned loading.
- Weight and balance: four POB, fuel for the trip, baggage. Compute. The 172 has limited useful load with four adults and full fuel.

The Walker (1991) Chief Counsel letter: "all available information" is broader than the NWKRAFT mnemonic and includes information the pilot does not yet have but reasonably could obtain. The candidate should reference Walker if pressed on what "all available information" really means.

Expected: candidate names §91.7 and §91.103, then walks the preflight checklist with attention to what the pilot might miss (TFRs, KMRY fuel availability, the deteriorating PM forecast).

### Arc 3: §91.151, §91.155, §91.159 -- the VFR envelope

Fuel: §91.151 day VFR requires 30 minutes reserve at normal cruise. The 172 burns ~7.5 gph at cruise; 30 minutes = 3.75 gallons. Plan KPAO -> KMRY 50 min + KMRY -> KPAO 50 min + 30 min reserve = ~17 gallons fuel. With 36 usable, comfortable margin. (If the return is delayed and the pilot has to divert, recompute.)

Weather: §91.155 -- KPAO is Class D surface. KPAO forecast 2,500 broken at departure. §91.155(c)(1) requires 1,000 ceiling and 3 SM visibility for VFR pattern operations in Class D surface. 2,500 ceiling exceeds 1,000; 6 SM exceeds 3. **VFR departure legal at KPAO.**

En route: scattered to broken layer at 5,000-6,000 MSL. §91.155(a) Class C/D/E below 10,000 MSL: 3 SM visibility, 500 below / 1,000 above / 2,000 horizontal. The pilot must plan an altitude that maintains these clearances. 4,500 MSL westbound (KPAO -> KMRY is roughly 170 magnetic, so eastbound 3,500 or 5,500). 3,500 MSL puts the pilot below the broken layer with comfortable cloud clearance. **En-route VFR legal.**

Cruise altitude: §91.159 magnetic course 170 (eastbound, 0-179) -> odd thousands + 500 -> 3,500 or 5,500 are legal eastbound. Westbound return (course ~350) -> even thousands + 500 -> 4,500 or 6,500. Above 3,000 AGL only.

KMRY arrival: forecast 4,000 scattered, 8 SM. KMRY is Class C. §91.155(c) Class C requires 1,000-3 (same as Class D for surface area). Forecast 4,000 scattered -> no broken/overcast ceiling at 1,000 -> ceiling check passes (or is "ceiling unlimited" if scattered is the only layer). 8 SM > 3. **VFR arrival legal.**

The trap: the 1700 KPAO forecast is 1,800 broken, 4 SM. If the return slips past 1700, the destination ceiling drops to 1,800 -- below VFR Class D minimum if the broken layer is at the airport. §91.155(c) ceiling fails. The pilot would need special VFR (§91.157), which requires day-only by default. Sunset is around 1700-1800 in this season.

If sunset has passed when the pilot returns and the ceiling has dropped to 1,800: §91.157 special VFR night requires the pilot to be instrument-rated and current under §61, with the airplane equipped per §91.205(d). If the pilot is private with no instrument rating: special VFR is not available. The pilot is grounded at KMRY or has to divert to an alternate with VFR conditions.

Expected: candidate computes the §91.151 fuel, walks §91.155 by airspace and altitude, identifies the §91.159 cruising altitude, and flags the deteriorating PM forecast as a §91.157 / §61 trap.

### Arc 4: §91.107, §91.119, §91.211, §91.215 -- the in-flight rules

Passengers: §91.107(a) requires every person on board to be briefed on seatbelt and shoulder harness use, and to be restrained during taxi, takeoff, and landing. The four POB are all adults; no §91.107(a)(3) lap-child issue. The CFI confirms the pilot will brief.

Minimum altitudes: §91.119(c) en route over the bay and coastal terrain -- 500 feet above the surface, with the 500-foot horizontal rule from any person, vessel, vehicle, or structure over water or sparsely populated. The route may bring the airplane near Highway 1 and beach areas. Pilot must plan a route that respects §91.119 throughout.

§91.211 oxygen: planned altitudes 3,500 / 4,500 / 5,500 -- well below the 12,500 trigger. Not operative for this flight.

§91.215 transponder: KPAO is in the 30 NM Mode C veil of KSFO Class B (KSFO is the Class B primary). Transponder + Mode C required at KPAO and en route within the veil. Above 10,000 MSL is not a factor at planned altitudes.

Expected: candidate identifies which altitude/equipment rules attach and which do not.

### Arc 5: contingency -- §91.3 and §91.13

If en route the pilot encounters the AIRMET turbulence and conditions worsen: §91.3 PIC authority allows the pilot to deviate (descend, divert, return) as required. If the pilot deviates from a clearance under §91.3(b), they may be required to file a written report under §91.3(c).

If the pilot makes an avoidable bad call (continues into deteriorating weather, descends below §91.119 to "stay under the cloud"), §91.13 careless attaches. The §91.13 catch-all is the FAA's enforcement utility; even a lucky outcome can become a §91.13 case if the bad decision can be reconstructed from radar tracks and pilot statements.

Expected: candidate frames the §91.3 / §91.13 dynamic as the safety-net pair: §91.3 protects the necessary deviation; §91.13 punishes the avoidable bad decision.

## Compressed answer (if the candidate is on the clock)

The candidate should be able to deliver this in under 90 seconds:

> "First, §61.56 -- flight review is lapsed at 18 months. Cannot act as PIC. That's the gate. If we re-do the flight review on this flight, we can fly with three observers, not three passengers carried for hire or with passenger benefit. §61.57 day recency is current (3 takeoffs/landings within 90 days), but night recency is lapsed; if the return slips past sunset, no passenger carrying. §91.7 airworthiness check on the airplane. §91.103 preflight: weather, TAFs, AIRMETs, NOTAMs, TFRs, KMRY fuel availability, performance, weight and balance. §91.151 fuel: 30 min reserve, plenty with 36 gallons. §91.155 VFR weather: KPAO and KMRY surface areas need 1,000-3 (Class D / C surface); 2,500-6 at KPAO and 4,000-8 at KMRY both qualify. The 1700 forecast is 1,800-4 -- if we slip return, special VFR (§91.157) is needed and is day-only unless the pilot is instrument-rated; private pilot can't get night SVFR. §91.159 cruising altitude eastbound 3,500 or 5,500. §91.119 minimum altitudes en route, 500 horizontal from highway and beach. §91.107 passenger briefings. §91.215 transponder Mode C in the 30 NM Mode C veil of KSFO. §91.3 / §91.13 frame the deviation/decision risk."

## Failure modes (what the candidate gets wrong)

1. **Skips §61.56.** Goes straight to weather analysis without checking the pilot's currency. The flight review check is the first gate. Failing to check it means the candidate could legally allow an unqualified PIC to fly.
2. **Treats §91.155 as universal "3-1-1-1-2-2-2."** Misses the Class D / C surface-area ceiling and visibility requirements (§91.155(c)) and the night Class G change.
3. **Forgets the §91.157 day-only default.** Cites special VFR as a return option without checking whether the return is past sunset and whether the pilot is instrument-rated.
4. **Confuses §91.169 (filing IFR alternate) with §91.151 (VFR fuel).** Different sections, different flights.
5. **Misses §91.215 Mode C veil at KPAO.** KPAO is inside the 30 NM ring of KSFO Class B; Mode C veil applies. Candidates from outside the SF Bay area may miss this.
6. **Assumes weight and balance is fine without computing.** Four adults + full fuel in a 172 is rarely legal at gross. The candidate must surface this as a §91.103 / §91.9 issue.
7. **States §91.13 as a separate decision rather than a catch-all.** §91.13 is not a planning rule; it's the FAA's enforcement pickup. The candidate should know it attaches automatically to any avoidable bad decision.

## Variant prompts

If the candidate handles the main scenario fluidly, escalate with one of these variants:

1. **Three friends become two friends and one toddler.** Now §91.107(a)(3) lap-child rule applies. Walk the parent-restraint requirement, the carrier-approved CRS preference, and the §91.107 briefing duty.
2. **Pilot wants to file IFR for the return.** Now §91.167 / §91.169 / §91.171 / §91.175 / §91.185 are operative. Walk fuel (destination + alternate + 45), the 1-2-3 rule (KPAO 1700 forecast 1,800-4; ceiling 1,800 -> 1-2-3 fails -> alternate required), VOR check, expected approach minimums, and lost-comm contingency.
3. **Pilot mentions wanting to take a friend up to Lake Tahoe (KTVL) afterward at 12,500 MSL.** Now §91.211 oxygen attaches at the trigger altitude. Walk the 30-minute grace and what it means for a 1-hour leg at 13,000.
4. **Engine roughness en route, pilot wants to divert below clouds at 1,200 MSL over a populated area.** Now §91.3(b) deviation, §91.13 careless test, and §91.119(b) congested area collide. Walk the priority: the §91.3(b) deviation is authority to break the cloud-clearance rule, but it does not authorize deliberately operating below the §91.119 floor when alternatives exist.
5. **The pilot's iPad app shows a TFR that hasn't appeared on the FAA NOTAM Search yet (a celebrity is in town).** Walk the §91.103 "all available information" duty -- TFR briefings, the apps that pull from FAA, and what to do when sources disagree.
