# Occupant rules, right-of-way, and minimum altitudes

This lesson covers three clusters that pilots routinely conflate: who must be seated and restrained (§91.105 / §91.107), who has the right-of-way when traffic conflicts (§91.111 / §91.113 / §91.115), and how low you may legally fly (§91.119). Each cluster has a structural pattern that is easy to remember once seen and a trap that bites pilots who skim.

§91.105 governs flight crewmembers at their stations. §91.107 governs other occupants and their restraints. The two sections sound similar; they are not. They apply to different people, in different phases of flight, with different exceptions. Confusing one for the other is the most common Part 91 occupant-rule misreading.

§91.111 / §91.113 / §91.115 are the right-of-way trio. §91.111 is "operating near other aircraft" -- the formation rule. §91.113 is the right-of-way rule itself, organized by the kind of distress and the kind of operation. §91.115 is the special right-of-way rule for water operations. The trio is an ordered sequence in the regulation, not three independent rules.

§91.119 sets minimum safe altitudes. The "congested area" trap is the most-cited misreading in Part 91. Pilots assume the altitude rule lets them fly low when "no one is around"; the regulation requires a specific altitude floor over any congested area, defined by the FAA case-by-case, not by the pilot's eye.

## What you'll be able to do

- State who §91.105 applies to and why it differs from §91.107
- Apply §91.107 to passengers, including the small-child exception and the parachute-jump exception
- Recite the §91.113 right-of-way order: distress / aircraft towing or refueling / balloon / glider / airship / engine-driven, and head-on rule
- Apply §91.119 minimums to congested areas, sparsely populated areas, and water/open terrain
- Distinguish the §91.119(a) emergency-landing-without-undue-hazard floor from the §91.3 emergency-deviation clause

## Why this matters

These three clusters cover most of the day-to-day scenarios a CFI will brief: who needs the seatbelt, what to do when another airplane appears, how low can you fly the river. Pilots get the answers wrong in three predictable ways:

1. They apply §91.105 (crewmember-station) to passengers because the language overlaps. It does not.
2. They invoke "right-of-way" as the answer to a near-miss without remembering the actual order in §91.113.
3. They fly low over a road or a beach treating it as "sparsely populated" because they cannot see houses, when the FAA's enforcement record treats roads with cars and beaches with people as congested by definition.

Each misreading produces a §91.13 catch-all charge alongside the underlying rule violation. The pattern of these three sections is the structural map you carry into every flight that involves passengers, traffic, or low altitude.

## The discovery question

You are flying a four-seat Cherokee from KSQL to KMRY along the coastline. Three friends in the back. Two adults, one toddler held in a parent's lap. You are descending from 4,500 to 2,500 to give them a closer look at Big Sur. The toddler is asleep on the parent's chest, no restraint. ATC is silent. No traffic on TIS-B. Your friend in the front seat is thumbing through the iPad pulling up Bixby Bridge photos.

Pause.

Three questions before you let down. First, is the toddler legal? Second, is your altitude (you are about to descend to 1,500 feet over the cliffs) legal? Third, your friend in the front seat -- does §91.105 apply to them?

The third question routes through §91.105's scope. The section applies to *flight crewmembers* required for the operation. A passenger in the right seat is not a flight crewmember. §91.105 does not apply to them. §91.107 does -- they need a seatbelt fastened during taxi, takeoff, and landing, and a shoulder harness if installed and the airplane is post-1978 manufacture.

The toddler routes through §91.107(a)(3). A child under two years old may be held by an adult provided the airplane meets the rest of the section, including the carrier-approved child-restraint exception (post-2007 AC 120-87). The toddler is legal in the configuration described. But the rule is narrower than pilots remember. "Held" means held; the parent must be awake and physically restraining the child.

The altitude question routes through §91.119(c). Sparsely populated and over water: 500 feet from any person, vessel, vehicle, or structure. A coastline with cliffs is not sparsely populated by FAA enforcement standards. The pattern of cars on Highway 1, hikers on the bluffs, boats in the cove, all count toward "person, vessel, vehicle, or structure." 1,500 feet may keep you legal if you stay 500 feet horizontally from the highway and the bluffs. The descent to "give them a closer look" is the trap; pilots pulled into showing the scenery underestimate how easily 500 feet horizontal becomes 100 feet horizontal in a turn.

This is the typical Part 91 cluster trap: three sections, each easy to recite, applied together in a real scenario where the answers depend on each other.

## §91.105 vs. §91.107: the scope split

```text
§91.105  Flight crewmembers at stations.

(a) During takeoff and landing, and while en route, each required
    flight crewmember shall--
    (1) Be at the crewmember station unless the absence is necessary
        to perform duties in connection with the operation of the aircraft
        or in connection with physiological needs; and
    (2) Keep the safety belt fastened while at the crewmember station.

(b) Each required flight crewmember of a U.S.-registered civil aircraft
    shall, during takeoff and landing, keep the shoulder harness fastened
    while at the crewmember station.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

§91.105 applies only to *required* flight crewmembers. In a single-pilot Part 91 operation, that's the PIC. In a two-pilot operation requiring a second-in-command (a Part 91K fractional, certain large/turbine ops), it's both pilots. A passenger in the right seat is not a crewmember by virtue of sitting in the right seat.

```text
§91.107  Use of safety belts, shoulder harnesses, and child
restraint systems.

(a) Unless otherwise authorized by the Administrator--
    (1) No pilot may take off a U.S.-registered civil aircraft
        unless the pilot in command of that aircraft ensures that
        each person on board is briefed on how to fasten and unfasten
        that person's safety belt and, if installed, shoulder harness.
    (2) No pilot may cause to be moved on the surface, take off,
        or land a U.S.-registered civil aircraft unless the pilot
        in command of that aircraft ensures that each person on
        board has been notified to fasten his or her safety belt
        and, if installed, shoulder harness.
    (3) Except as provided in this paragraph, each person on board
        a U.S.-registered civil aircraft must occupy an approved
        seat or berth with a safety belt and, if installed, shoulder
        harness, properly secured about him or her during movement
        on the surface, takeoff, and landing.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

§91.107 applies to *every person on board*, not just crewmembers. It applies during taxi, takeoff, and landing. It does not apply en route in cruise. The PIC has the duty to brief and to ensure compliance.

The split that bites pilots:

| Question                               | §91.105                             | §91.107                                          |
| -------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Who is covered?                        | Required flight crewmembers only    | Every person on board                            |
| When does it apply?                    | Takeoff, landing, en route          | Movement on surface, takeoff, landing            |
| Seatbelt required at the station/seat? | Yes, while at station               | Yes, during covered phases                       |
| Shoulder harness required?             | Yes, takeoff and landing, post-1978 | Yes, takeoff and landing, if installed           |
| Brief the person?                      | Not the section's concern           | PIC must brief                                   |
| Lap child under two?                   | Not addressed (passenger rule)      | Held by adult OK; carrier-approved CRS preferred |

The §91.107(a)(3) lap-child carve-out is the second-most-common misreading after the §91.105/§91.107 confusion. The exception is for children under two, held by an adult. There is no general "small child" exception. A four-year-old needs an approved seat and a safety belt.

## §91.111 / §91.113 / §91.115: the right-of-way trio

§91.111 is the formation rule. The structural point: you may not fly so close to another aircraft as to create a collision hazard, and you may not fly in formation except by arrangement with the PICs of all aircraft in the formation. Passengers may not be carried in a formation flight for hire.

```text
§91.113  Right-of-way rules: Except water operations.

(b) General. When weather conditions permit, regardless of whether
    an operation is conducted under instrument flight rules or visual
    flight rules, vigilance shall be maintained by each person operating
    an aircraft so as to see and avoid other aircraft.

(c) In distress. An aircraft in distress has the right-of-way over
    all other air traffic.

(d) Converging. When aircraft of the same category are converging
    at approximately the same altitude (except head-on, or nearly so),
    the aircraft to the other's right has the right-of-way.

    [Different-category order: balloon yields to nothing, glider yields
    to balloon, airship yields to balloon and glider, aircraft towing
    or refueling other aircraft yields to all of the above, engine-driven
    aircraft yields to all of the above.]

(e) Approaching head-on. When aircraft are approaching each other
    head-on, or nearly so, each pilot of each aircraft shall alter
    course to the right.

(f) Overtaking. Each aircraft that is being overtaken has the
    right-of-way and each pilot of an overtaking aircraft shall
    alter course to the right to pass well clear.

(g) Landing. Aircraft, while on final approach to land or while landing,
    have the right-of-way over other aircraft in flight or operating
    on the surface...

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The order of priority in §91.113 is fixed and tested:

1. **In distress** -- everyone yields
2. **Balloon** -- yields to nothing else
3. **Glider** -- yields to balloon
4. **Airship** -- yields to balloon and glider
5. **Aircraft towing or refueling** -- yields to all above
6. **Engine-driven (powered)** -- yields to all above

Within the same category, the converging rule (right-of-way to the aircraft on the other's right) and the head-on rule (both turn right) apply. Overtaking and landing rules sit on top of the category order.

§91.115 is the same logic for water operations: vessel-of-the-day priorities (the maritime equivalent of distress / sail / power), with seaplanes integrated.

The §91.111 / §91.113 / §91.115 sequence in Part 91 is structurally similar to the order of operations in math: read it once, internalize the order, then apply.

## §91.119 minimum safe altitudes

```text
§91.119  Minimum safe altitudes: General.

Except when necessary for takeoff or landing, no person may operate
an aircraft below the following altitudes:

(a) Anywhere. An altitude allowing, if a power unit fails, an emergency
    landing without undue hazard to persons or property on the surface.

(b) Over any congested area of a city, town, or settlement, or over any
    open air assembly of persons. An altitude of 1,000 feet above the
    highest obstacle within a horizontal radius of 2,000 feet of the
    aircraft.

(c) Over other than congested areas. An altitude of 500 feet above the
    surface, except over open water or sparsely populated areas. In
    those cases, the aircraft may not be operated closer than 500 feet
    to any person, vessel, vehicle, or structure.

(d) Helicopters, powered parachutes, and weight-shift-control aircraft.
    [Lower altitudes permitted with conditions.]

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

The structural pattern:

- **§91.119(a) is the floor everywhere.** You must always be high enough that an engine failure produces a landing without undue hazard. The altitude is not a number; it depends on terrain, traffic, and the airplane.
- **§91.119(b) is the congested-area floor.** 1,000 feet above the highest obstacle within 2,000 feet horizontal. The "highest obstacle" includes terrain, towers, buildings.
- **§91.119(c) is the non-congested floor.** 500 feet above the surface as a baseline. Over water or sparsely populated, the rule pivots to a horizontal 500-foot rule from any person, vessel, vehicle, or structure.

The "congested area" trap. The FAA does not define "congested area" with a list. Enforcement cases have treated as congested: a single road with traffic on it, a beach with people, a freeway, a small-town main street, a parking lot of a big-box store, a county fair, a parade route. The rule is that the FAA sets the bar high; pilots routinely set it too low. If you can see people, vehicles, or structures in numbers, treat it as congested and apply (b).

The §91.119(a) "without undue hazard" floor is not bypassed by §91.3(b). §91.3(b) lets you deviate when the emergency requires it. §91.119(a) sets the floor *before* the emergency: you must be high enough that an emergency does not endanger people on the ground.

## Common misreadings

**§91.105 applied to passengers.** The right-seat passenger doing iPad navigation is not a flight crewmember. §91.105 does not apply to them. §91.107 applies. This is the most-common Part 91 misreading; pilots brief "everyone has to keep their belt on en route" when the rule is "everyone fastens during taxi, takeoff, landing; crewmembers stay belted en route at the station."

**§91.107 lap-child rule overstated.** "Children under two can sit in a parent's lap" is correct only with conditions. The parent must be awake and physically restraining the child. The carrier-approved child restraint system is preferred and is the FAA's recommendation. There is no extension to children three or four years old.

**§91.113(d) recited as "the aircraft to the right has right-of-way" without category context.** The category order trumps the right-side rule. A glider converging from the left of a powered airplane has right-of-way; the powered airplane yields, regardless of the right-side rule. Pilots forget the category order.

**§91.119(c) "I see no houses, so I'm sparsely populated."** Roads with vehicles and beaches with people count. Pilots get violation notices for low passes over highways and beaches based on this misreading. Treat anything with people, traffic, or structures as triggering the 500-foot horizontal rule even outside towns.

**§91.119(a) confused with §91.3(b).** §91.119(a) is the altitude floor *for normal operations*. §91.3(b) is the deviation authority *for an in-progress emergency*. They are not the same and they do not substitute for each other. Operating at an altitude below §91.119(a) is a violation regardless of pilot skill.

## Where these sit in the regulatory map

§91.105 / §91.107 live in subpart B (Flight Rules), occupant section. §91.111 / §91.113 / §91.115 live in subpart B as right-of-way. §91.119 lives in subpart B as minimum altitudes. All three clusters are subpart B; they are pre-takeoff, in-flight, and post-takeoff rules that the PIC must apply continuously.

The §91.13 catch-all attaches to violations of any of the four sections. A §91.119 violation (low altitude over a beach) is also a §91.13 careless violation. A §91.107 violation (unrestrained child not under two) is a §91.13 careless violation. The catch-all means the FAA is rarely without a charging tool when these clusters are misapplied.
