# PIC authority and careless or reckless

§91.3 and §91.13 are the two most-cited sections in Part 91. They show up at the top of nearly every certificate-action case, every NTSB Board order, and every Chief Counsel interpretation that involves a Part 91 operator. They are also the two shortest sections in the part. Their power is in their breadth, not their length.

§91.3 grants the PIC the final authority over the flight, including the authority to deviate from any other regulation in an emergency. §91.13 prohibits operating an aircraft in a careless or reckless manner. Together they are the bookends: the PIC has the most authority of anyone on the airplane, and that authority comes with a corresponding duty to operate carefully. Pilots who deviate from a rule under §91.3 still answer to §91.13 if the deviation was avoidable.

## What you'll be able to do

- State §91.3 paragraphs (a), (b), and (c) cold and explain when each applies
- Recognize the §91.3(b) emergency-deviation clause as authority, not absolution
- State §91.13(a) and (b) and explain why §91.13 attaches to nearly every enforcement case
- Distinguish a §91.13 careless violation (failure to use ordinary care) from a §91.13 reckless violation (conscious disregard)
- Predict when an FAA enforcement case will charge §91.13 alongside another section, and why

## Why this matters

If you fly long enough, you will face a situation where a regulation written for a normal flight does not fit the moment in front of you. §91.3(b) is the regulation that covers you in those moments. It is also the regulation pilots cite incorrectly more often than any other in Part 91, usually as a defense after the fact for a violation they could have avoided.

§91.13 is the FAA's enforcement utility. Almost any time the agency wants to pursue a certificate action, they charge the specific operating rule that was violated *and* §91.13 as a tag-along. The §91.13 charge is what gives the FAA the leverage to suspend or revoke even when the underlying rule violation was minor. Working pilots who understand §91.13 understand why the FAA's enforcement power is broader than any single regulation suggests.

These two sections frame everything that follows in Part 91. Read them slowly.

## The discovery question

You are 30 minutes into a VFR cross-country in a 172. You enter an unforecast band of moderate icing at 6,500 feet, the only altitude where you have terrain clearance and decent visibility under a lowering ceiling. The 172 is not certified for flight in known icing. You can climb to 8,500 (above the freezing level, possibly above cloud tops) but that requires a steeper-than-normal climb that puts you in the cloud for ~3 minutes. You can descend to 4,500 but that puts you 500 feet above terrain in a valley with deteriorating visibility. The nearest airport is 22 miles behind you. ATC is asking your intentions.

Pause.

What does the regulation let you do? What does the regulation *require* you to do? And what happens after, when the FSDO asks for your account?

The answer routes through §91.3. Paragraph (a) says you are PIC and are directly responsible for the operation. Paragraph (b) says in an in-flight emergency requiring immediate action, you may deviate from any rule in this part to the extent required to meet the emergency. Paragraph (c) says if you deviate under (b), you may be required to send a written report to the Administrator.

The deviation clause is what gives you the freedom to climb through cloud in a non-IFR-certified airplane, or to break a §91.155 cloud clearance, or to land off-airport, or to overfly an airport without communications, when the alternative is worse. The deviation must be required by the emergency. It does not authorize convenience deviations or preference deviations. A deviation that the pilot could reasonably have avoided is exposed to a §91.13 careless charge, not protected by §91.3(b).

This is the typical Part 91 trap. §91.3(b) is broad but precise: an actual emergency, requiring immediate action, deviation only to the extent required. Pilots cite it as a general "I'm the PIC" authority and lose certificate cases over the misreading.

## What §91.3 actually says

```text
§91.3  Responsibility and authority of the pilot in command.

(a) The pilot in command of an aircraft is directly responsible for,
    and is the final authority as to, the operation of that aircraft.

(b) In an in-flight emergency requiring immediate action, the pilot
    in command may deviate from any rule of this part to the extent
    required to meet that emergency.

(c) Each pilot in command who deviates from a rule under paragraph (b)
    of this section shall, upon the request of the Administrator,
    send a written report of that deviation to the Administrator.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

Five things to extract:

1. **"Directly responsible."** Paragraph (a) is not a privilege grant; it is a responsibility assignment. The PIC owns the operation. Other crew, dispatchers, ATC, and operators have inputs; the PIC has the decision. This is the language the FAA uses to attach responsibility for a violation to the PIC even when other parties contributed (a mechanic who signed a defective return-to-service, a dispatcher who released the flight with bad weather, etc.). The PIC is on the hook.
2. **"Final authority."** Paragraph (a) gives the PIC the last word. ATC clearances are *clearances to do something the PIC has decided is acceptable*, not commands. The PIC can refuse a clearance ("unable") and is not in violation for refusing. The PIC's final authority does not extend over the airplane's certification, the aircraft's airworthiness, or the rules in Part 91 themselves; it extends over operational decisions inside the rules.
3. **"In an in-flight emergency requiring immediate action."** Paragraph (b) has three gates. *In-flight* (an emergency on the ground does not engage §91.3(b); it engages other authorities). *Emergency* (a real one, not a convenience). *Requiring immediate action* (no time to land and assess; the situation must demand response now). All three must be present.
4. **"To the extent required."** Paragraph (b) does not authorize indefinite or maximalist deviation. It authorizes only the deviation needed to meet the emergency. A pilot who deviates further than the emergency required is exposed to §91.13.
5. **"May be required to send a written report."** Paragraph (c) is the audit trail. The Administrator (the FAA, in practice the FSDO) may request a written report of the deviation. The report goes to the FSDO; it is not a public document. The fact that a report is required does not, by itself, indicate the FAA intends enforcement; it is information-gathering. Pilots and CFIs read paragraph (c) as ominous; in practice many §91.3(b) deviations result in no enforcement action when the report shows the deviation was required and proportionate.

### "Final authority" -- the operational meaning

The FAA reads §91.3(a) consistently across decades of enforcement: the PIC owns the safety of the operation. This authority is not transferable to ATC, the operator, the chief pilot, or anyone else. A PIC who follows an ATC instruction that puts the airplane in unsafe terrain or weather conditions is still responsible if the result is a violation or accident. ATC issues clearances; the PIC accepts or refuses them.

Two famous illustrations. In the *Eastern 401* (1972) accident, the crew followed standard procedure but lost altitude awareness; the NTSB's findings reaffirmed the PIC's final authority over the airplane's safe operation regardless of crew workflow. In the more recent *Asiana 214* (2013) accident at SFO, the captain's authority over the approach was emphasized; the PIC could not transfer responsibility for monitoring airspeed to automation or to other crew.

"Final authority" cuts both ways. It is the PIC's protection (you can refuse an unsafe clearance) and the PIC's burden (you cannot offload responsibility for the safe operation).

### The §91.3(b) emergency deviation -- when it works

§91.3(b) is the FAA's acknowledgment that regulations cannot anticipate every situation. The classic uses of the deviation:

- **Engine failure on takeoff.** A pilot who lands straight ahead off-airport when the airplane cannot return to the runway is operating under §91.3(b) to the extent the deviation (off-airport landing, possibly disregarding §91.119 minimum altitudes) is required.
- **Inadvertent IMC.** A VFR-only pilot who enters cloud and must climb or descend to terrain-clearance altitudes that would otherwise violate VFR cloud clearances.
- **Medical emergency on board.** A pilot who declares an emergency and accepts ATC direct routing through restricted airspace, or lands at the nearest field regardless of intended destination.
- **Mechanical emergency.** Engine trouble, pressurization failure, smoke or fumes, fuel exhaustion -- any of these may require deviation from cruising altitude rules, fuel-reserve rules, or class-of-airspace requirements.

In each case the deviation must be (1) required by the emergency, (2) proportionate to the emergency, (3) terminated when the emergency is resolved. A pilot who declares an emergency and uses the declaration to skip a fuel stop they could have made is exposed.

### The §91.3(b) emergency deviation -- when it does not work

Common §91.3(b) misuses:

- **The "schedule emergency."** A pilot tight on time who needs to make a scheduled meeting decides the time pressure is an emergency. Not an emergency under §91.3(b). Time pressure created by the pilot is not an emergency the regulation contemplates.
- **The "fuel-low-but-not-exhausted" call.** A pilot 30 minutes from destination with 35 minutes of fuel calls "minimum fuel" or "emergency" to get a direct routing. If the fuel state was the result of the pilot's planning (or lack of), §91.3(b) may not protect; §91.13 may attach for careless planning.
- **The "weather got worse than I expected" descent through a cloud layer.** If the pilot had the option to land short or to climb to better conditions and chose to descend through cloud instead, §91.3(b) does not authorize the choice. The deviation has to be required, not preferred.

The FAA's read is consistent: §91.3(b) protects the pilot who deviates because the alternative was worse. It does not protect the pilot who deviated because it was easier.

### The §91.3(c) written report

The written report under §91.3(c) is requested by the Administrator. In practice, the FSDO requests it after a controller reports an unusual event ("the pilot at FL080 deviated to FL100 without clearance and reported he was avoiding ice"). The report is the pilot's narrative.

A defensible §91.3(c) report names:

- The flight (date, route, registration)
- The emergency (what happened, when, how the pilot recognized it)
- The deviation (what rule was deviated from, when, for how long)
- The justification (why the deviation was required to meet the emergency)
- The resolution (when the deviation ended, when normal operation resumed)
- The notification (whether ATC was advised, if so when)

Pilots and CFIs occasionally treat the §91.3(c) report as an admission of guilt. It is not. It is the pilot's narrative of an authorized deviation. A well-written report frequently closes the case.

## What §91.13 actually says

```text
§91.13  Careless or reckless operation.

(a) Aircraft operations for the purpose of air navigation. No person
    may operate an aircraft in a careless or reckless manner so as to
    endanger the life or property of another.

(b) Aircraft operations other than for the purpose of air navigation.
    No person may operate an aircraft, other than for the purpose of
    air navigation, on any part of the surface of an airport used by
    aircraft for air navigation, in a careless or reckless manner so
    as to endanger the life or property of another.

Note: paraphrase. Read the actual reg before referencing in exam.
```

Two operative concepts:

1. **"Careless or reckless."** Two different mental states. Careless is failure to exercise the care a reasonable pilot would. Reckless is conscious disregard for the safety risk. The FAA charges the appropriate level based on the facts; the sanctions under careless are typically lighter than under reckless.
2. **"So as to endanger the life or property of another."** The endangerment element. The FAA does not have to prove an actual harm occurred; it has to prove the operation *could have* endangered. Near-misses, low passes over populated areas, and unsafe maneuvers near other aircraft are all chargeable even when no harm resulted.

Paragraph (b) covers ground operations that are not for the purpose of air navigation -- taxiing for maintenance, towing, repositioning. It exists because the agency wanted authority over careless ground operations on airport surfaces even when the operator was not technically "navigating."

### Why §91.13 attaches to nearly every enforcement case

The FAA's *Order 2150.3C* (the Compliance and Enforcement Program) lays out how the agency charges. When a pilot violates a specific operating rule, the FAA usually charges:

1. The specific rule violation (e.g., §91.155 cloud clearance, §91.103 preflight, §91.119 minimum altitudes).
2. §91.13(a) as a separate count, alleging the underlying conduct was careless.

The §91.13 charge does several things for the FAA. It captures the underlying behavior (rather than just the technical rule break). It supports a higher sanction (because §91.13 contemplates the actual safety risk). And it gives the agency leverage in settlement -- a pilot can plead to one charge while the other is dropped.

NTSB Board orders are filled with §91.13 cases. A representative pattern: pilot busts Class B without clearance ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/131?at=2026)), FAA charges the §91.131 violation *and* §91.13. The Board often suspends the certificate for 30-90 days based on both charges.

### Careless vs. reckless -- the standard

The NTSB has consistently read "careless" as failure to use the degree of care a reasonable pilot would in the same circumstances. This is an objective standard. The pilot's intent doesn't matter; the reasonable-pilot standard does.

"Reckless" is a higher bar. It requires a conscious disregard for known risk. Buzzing a friend's house at 50 feet is reckless. Failing to remove tie-down ropes during preflight and taxiing into the wing of another airplane is careless. The difference matters because the sanctions differ.

The FAA's burden of proof is by a preponderance of the evidence (more likely than not), not beyond a reasonable doubt. This is a civil standard; criminal-law concepts of intent do not apply.

### *Administrator v. Lobeiko* -- the canonical case

*Administrator v. Lobeiko*, NTSB Order EA-4877 (1998) is the case CFI candidates learn for §91.13. The pilot lost engine power on departure, attempted to return to the airport, and crashed short. The FAA charged §91.13 alleging the decision to return rather than land straight ahead constituted careless operation.

The NTSB found the pilot careless but not reckless. The Board's analysis:

- The pilot was the PIC and had final authority (§91.3(a))
- The pilot's decision was not reckless (no conscious disregard) but was unreasonable under the circumstances (a reasonable pilot, faced with engine failure on departure at low altitude, lands straight ahead absent clear ability to return)
- The §91.13 violation stands; certificate suspension imposed

*Lobeiko* is famous because it shows §91.13 attaching even to a decision the pilot believed was correct. Subjective good faith does not defeat a careless charge; the test is what the reasonable pilot would have done.

## Where §91.3 and §91.13 sit

Subpart A of Part 91 covers "General":

```text
§91.1   Applicability
§91.3   Responsibility and authority of the pilot in command
§91.5   Pilot in command of aircraft requiring more than one required pilot
§91.7   Civil aircraft airworthiness
§91.9   Civil aircraft flight manual, marking, and placard requirements
§91.11  Prohibition on interference with crewmembers
§91.13  Careless or reckless operation
§91.15  Dropping objects
§91.17  Alcohol or drugs
§91.19  Carriage of narcotic drugs, marihuana, and depressant or stimulant drugs or substances
§91.21  Portable electronic devices
§91.25  Aviation Safety Reporting Program: Prohibition against use of reports for enforcement purposes
```

The shape: §91.1 is applicability. §91.3 is the PIC responsibility-and-authority anchor. §91.5 is the second-pilot rule (mostly relevant to two-crew operations). §91.7 is airworthiness. §91.9 is the flight manual requirement. §91.11 is the interference rule (relevant to alcohol/drugs and to passengers). §91.13 is the catch-all. §91.15-§91.21 are specific prohibitions. §91.25 is the ASRS protection (covered in Week 9).

§91.3 and §91.13 are the bookends of subpart A: the responsibility/authority anchor and the catch-all. The middle sections add specific rules (airworthiness, flight manual, interference, drugs) that deserve their own treatment but always sit inside the §91.3/§91.13 frame.

## Common misreadings

- **"§91.3(b) lets me deviate from any rule whenever I think I need to."** Wrong. §91.3(b) requires an in-flight emergency requiring immediate action, and authorizes deviation only to the extent required. Convenience deviations are exposed to §91.13.
- **"If ATC tells me to do something, I have to do it."** Wrong. §91.3(a) gives the PIC final authority. ATC issues clearances; the PIC can refuse with "unable." A PIC who follows an unsafe clearance is still responsible for the unsafe operation.
- **"§91.3(c) means I'm in trouble if I deviate."** Wrong. §91.3(c) is a reporting mechanism, not a sanction. The Administrator may request a written report; the report alone does not trigger enforcement. A defensible report often closes the inquiry.
- **"§91.13 is just a catch-all the FAA throws in for show."** Wrong. §91.13 carries independent sanction weight. The NTSB regularly imposes 30-90 day suspensions on §91.13 charges alongside the underlying violation.
- **"Careless under §91.13 requires intent."** Wrong. The standard is objective: what a reasonable pilot would have done. The pilot's subjective belief is not the test.
- **"If no one was actually endangered, §91.13 doesn't attach."** Wrong. The endangerment element requires only that the operation *could have* endangered life or property. Actual harm is not required.
- **"Reckless and careless are the same."** Wrong. Reckless requires conscious disregard for known risk; careless requires only failure to exercise reasonable care. The sanctions differ.
- **"The PIC is final authority over airworthiness."** Wrong. §91.3(a) gives the PIC final authority over the *operation* of the aircraft. Airworthiness lives in §91.7 (PIC determination of airworthiness) and Part 39 (airworthiness directives) and Part 43 (maintenance). The PIC determines whether to fly a given aircraft on a given day; the airworthiness standard itself is set by the regs.
- **"§91.3(b) deviation means I don't need to file a NASA ASRS report."** Wrong. The §91.3(c) FAA report and the NASA ASRS report are independent. ASRS provides limited enforcement immunity under §91.25 for inadvertent violations, conditional on filing within 10 days. Pilots routinely file both.

### Apply the rules

> A VFR pilot at 6,500 feet in deteriorating visibility encounters cloud they did not expect. They climb to 8,500 to stay above cloud, accepting a brief 30-second pass through cloud during the climb. They report the deviation to ATC and continue. When the FSDO calls the next day asking for a written report, what do they write?

Answer: The pilot reports an in-flight emergency under §91.3(b) -- inadvertent IMC. The deviation was from §91.155 (VFR cloud clearance / visibility) and possibly §91.159 (VFR cruising altitude, if 8,500 was not appropriate for direction of flight). The deviation was required (the alternative was continued VFR in deteriorating conditions, with worse exposure). The deviation was proportionate (climbed to clear conditions, leveled off). The pilot notified ATC. The deviation ended when VFR was restored. A clean §91.3(c) report; rarely results in enforcement.

> A pilot is 15 minutes late for a meeting. To make up time they fly through a Class C airspace shelf without communications, justifying the deviation as "I needed to get there." Are they protected by §91.3(b)?

Answer: No. There is no in-flight emergency. Time pressure is not an emergency under §91.3(b). The pilot has violated §91.130 (Class C operations) and is exposed to §91.13(a) for careless operation. Schedule pressure does not engage the deviation clause.

> A pilot busts the floor of Class B by 200 feet for 90 seconds while maneuvering to avoid traffic ATC pointed out. They had communications, did not have a Class B clearance. Charges?

Answer: §91.131 (Class B operations). Possible §91.13(a) careless. Possible §91.123 (ATC compliance) depending on whether ATC instructions were involved. The pilot can argue §91.3(b) (avoiding traffic in close proximity is an in-flight emergency requiring immediate action) but the case will turn on whether the maneuver was proportionate to the actual collision risk and whether the pilot could have requested a Class B clearance instead. The §91.3(b) defense is strongest when the pilot can show no other option.

> A CFI conducting a flight review with a student practices a power-off 180 over an unpopulated area. The pilot lands at 50 knots at the threshold. The FSDO receives a complaint from a person on the ground who saw the low altitude pass and felt endangered. Charges?

Answer: §91.13(a) potentially. The endangerment element (was life or property potentially endangered?) is the question. If the maneuver was conducted with appropriate altitude margins and the airplane was at no point at risk of impact, the §91.13 charge is weak. If the airplane was below traffic-pattern altitude over populated structures, §91.13 attaches; possibly also §91.119 (minimum safe altitudes). Power-off 180s are PTS / ACS-required maneuvers and are taught regularly; the question is execution, not concept.

> A pilot loses an engine on takeoff at 200 feet AGL in a single-engine airplane. The pilot turns 180 to attempt return to the runway. The airplane stalls in the turn and crashes short of the runway. Pilot survives. Charges?

Answer: §91.13(a) careless, per *Lobeiko* (1998) reasoning. The pilot's decision to turn back at low altitude is the type of decision the NTSB has consistently found careless; reasonable pilots in the same circumstances are trained to land straight ahead. The pilot's subjective belief in their ability to return is not the test. Possibly also §91.103 (preflight planning if route did not contemplate the engine-failure case) and §91.119 (minimum altitudes) depending on the impact zone.

> A passenger asks the pilot to "show off" by flying low over their house. The pilot descends to 200 feet AGL over a suburban neighborhood, makes one pass, and climbs back. Charges?

Answer: §91.13(a) reckless (not just careless). Conscious disregard for known risk -- the pilot knew Part 91 prohibits low passes over populated areas, did it anyway. Also §91.119(b) (minimum 1,000 feet AGL over the highest obstacle within 2,000 feet of the aircraft, in congested areas). Sanction typically more severe than the careless variant -- 90-180 day suspensions are common.
