# Pilot/Controller Glossary

> The shared dictionary that pilots and controllers use so the same word means the same thing on both ends of the radio. Advisory, but it is the definitions everything else relies on.

## What it is

The Pilot/Controller Glossary is the FAA's authoritative dictionary of the terms and
phrases used in air traffic control communication and in FAA flight publications. It
exists to solve one problem: a word said by a controller and heard by a pilot has to mean
exactly the same thing to both. The P/CG defines terms such as "cleared as filed,"
"resume own navigation," "traffic in sight," and "line up and wait" precisely, so that
phraseology is unambiguous. It is published as a companion to the AIM and to the
controller handbook Order JO 7110.65, and the three are designed to use a single shared
vocabulary. The P/CG is not a how-to and not a rulebook; it is the definitions layer
underneath both.

## Binding or advisory

Advisory. The P/CG is a glossary, not a regulation, and it does not impose obligations of
its own. Its force is indirect but real: the AIM, the controller handbook, and ATC
phraseology all draw their precise meanings from it, so a pilot who misunderstands a P/CG
term will misexecute a procedure or a clearance that *is* operationally binding. The
glossary itself does not compel you. What it does is fix the meaning of the words in the
documents and clearances that do.

## How it is identified

The P/CG is not cited by a chapter-section-paragraph number the way the AIM is. Entries
are referenced by the defined term itself, alphabetically. A reference looks like
"P/CG term: CLEARED AS FILED" or simply names the term and notes that the P/CG defines it.
Each entry gives the term in upper case, the definition, and often a cross-reference to
the AIM paragraph or 14 CFR section where the term is used in context. The glossary also
carries a short table of standard abbreviations and acronyms. Because the access key is
the word, not a number, the way to "read a P/CG citation" is simply to look up the term
alphabetically in the current edition.

## Revisions and currency

The P/CG is reissued on the same cycle as the AIM, and the front of each edition lists the
terms added, modified, or deleted since the prior edition. New phraseology enters the
glossary when ATC procedures change; obsolete terms are removed. To confirm currency, use
the online P/CG at faa.gov, which serves the current edition, and check the change summary
at the front. A definition in an old printed copy can lag a phraseology change.

## Where to find it

The official online Pilot/Controller Glossary is at
`faa.gov/air_traffic/publications/atpubs/pcg_html`. It is also bound into the back of the
printed AIM. The air traffic publications hub at `faa.gov/air_traffic/publications` carries
the P/CG alongside the AIM and the controller handbook so the three companion documents
can be opened together.

## How it relates to the others

The P/CG is the definitions layer that two larger documents sit on top of:

- The **AIM** uses P/CG terms throughout. When the AIM says a phrase, the P/CG is where
  that phrase's precise meaning is fixed.
- The controller handbook, **Order JO 7110.65**, uses the same glossary, which is what
  makes pilot and controller language match.
- Where a P/CG term is defined more formally for legal purposes, the entry often cross-
  references the **14 CFR** definition (Part 1 is the regulatory definitions part).

The P/CG does not stand alone in practice; it is the dictionary you keep open beside the
AIM.

## Common gotchas

- The P/CG is a dictionary, not a procedures manual. It tells you what a term means, not
  how to perform a procedure. The AIM does the how.
- Some terms have a casual hangar meaning and a precise P/CG meaning that differ. The
  P/CG meaning is the one ATC is using.
- A few terms are defined in both 14 CFR Part 1 and the P/CG. The CFR definition is the
  regulatory one; the P/CG entry usually points to it.
- An old printed glossary can carry retired phraseology. Confirm against the current
  online edition for anything radio-current.
