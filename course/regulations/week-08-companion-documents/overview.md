# Week 8 -- Companion documents

The CFR is not the whole regulatory ecosystem. Title 14 sits inside a larger network of guidance, expected practice, and binding interpretation. Working pilots who only read the CFR lose oral exams. Pilots who read summaries (ASA test prep, the *Pilot's Handbook*) without ever opening the AIM, an AC, or a Chief Counsel letter develop confident wrong beliefs.

Week 1's lesson [03-companion-documents.md](../week-01-architecture/03-companion-documents.md) introduced the document types. This week we go deeper: how to actually find the binding answer, how to read an AC for what it grants, how a Chief Counsel letter overrides a plain reading of a regulation, and where pilot-relevant rules live *outside* Title 14 entirely.

## Goal

The learner knows the regulatory ecosystem outside Title 14 and can triage a regulatory question to the document that actually settles it.

## What you'll be able to do by end of week

- Locate the right AIM chapter for an airspace, ATC, weather, or emergency question
- Decode an AC number to predict its CFR-Part subject and find the current revision
- Recognize a Chief Counsel letter by recipient name (Mangiamele, Hicks, Murphy) and state what each one settled
- Distinguish FAR (binding), AIM (expected, enforceable via 91.13), AC (one acceptable means), Chief Counsel (binding interpretation), and FAA Order (internal procedure)
- Locate 49 CFR 1552 (TSA flight training) and 49 CFR Part 830 (NTSB reporting) and explain why they don't live in Title 14
- Walk a regulatory question up the source hierarchy: FAR -> CFR cross-reference -> AIM -> AC -> Chief Counsel letter -> FAA Order

## Why this week is required

Every checkride examiner asks at least one question whose answer doesn't live in the FAR being cited. The classic pattern: examiner reads §91.103 ("become familiar with all available information"), and the actual standard for compliance is in the Walker letter (2017) plus AC 91-92 plus AIM 5-1. A pilot who can only quote the CFR misses the question. A pilot who can route the question to its actual source nails it.

The same pattern repeats with cost-sharing (61.113 -> Mangiamele), CFI logging (61.51(e) -> Hicks, Murphy), endorsements (61.31 -> AC 61-65 wording), non-towered ops (91.126 + AIM 4-3 + AC 90-66), runway operations (AC 91-79), and SOPs in Part 121/135 worlds (AC 120-71). The companion documents *are* the answer for many of the questions a working pilot or CFI fields every week.

## Front-to-back reading

This week's reading assignments live in the lessons:

- AIM Chapter 1 (Air Navigation) and Chapter 4 (Air Traffic Procedures) -- read end-to-end. These are the chapters most likely to be cited at an examiner's first question.
- Three ACs in detail: AC 00-6 (weather), AC 90-66 (non-towered ops), AC 91-79 (runway overrun prevention). The first is reference material every flight; the second and third are policy you'll be asked to defend on a checkride.
- Two Chief Counsel letters in full: Mangiamele (2009, on §61.113 cost-sharing) and Hicks (2009, on §61.51 logging by an instructor).
