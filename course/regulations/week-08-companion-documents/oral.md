# Week 8 oral -- "Walk me through finding the authoritative interpretation"

## The question

> "A regulation is unclear to you. Walk me through how you find the authoritative interpretation. Use a real example. Pick a regulation that pilots commonly misread, walk me through the FAR, the procedural guidance, the AC, and the Chief Counsel letter, and tell me what the actual binding answer is."

This oral integrates all of Week 8 plus the regulatory architecture from Weeks 1-7. It tests whether the source-triage discipline is in your head -- whether you can route a question to the right document layer, walk up the source hierarchy, and synthesize a defensible answer.

## What this is testing

- Whether you can name the document layers and articulate the regulatory force of each: FAR (binding), 49 CFR (cross-agency, also binding), AIM (expected, enforceable through §91.13), AC (one acceptable means), Chief Counsel (binding interpretation), FAA Order (agency procedure)
- Whether you can pick an apt example of a regulation that's actually misread and walk through the actual ambiguity
- Whether you cite the famous Chief Counsel letters by recipient name and year (Mangiamele, Hicks, Murphy, Walker)
- Whether you understand "satisfied" answers integrate the legal frame (FAR), the operational guidance (AIM/AC), and the binding interpretation (CC letter) when applicable
- Whether you preserve the analytical separation between the layers, especially when the plain reading and the controlling interpretation differ
- Whether you can articulate the limits of each layer (a CC letter does not amend a regulation; the AIM is not regulation; an AC is one means among several)

## Model answer (full walkthrough)

A defensible answer takes about 15-18 minutes spoken. The example I'll work through is §91.103 ("all available information") because it's the cleanest example of a regulation that lives across all five layers and where the practical answer is in the Chief Counsel letter, not the FAR.

### Stage 1: The frame -- the document layers and what each is (90 seconds)

> "When a regulation is unclear, the actual answer almost always lives somewhere other than the FAR itself. The regulatory ecosystem has five layers I route through:
>
> Layer 1 -- 14 CFR. The binding regulation. The legal floor. Everything else builds on what the FAR says.
>
> Layer 2 -- 49 CFR. Same regulatory force as Layer 1, different agency. NTSB Part 830, TSA Part 1552. When pilot rules cross agencies, Title 14 isn't the home.
>
> Layer 3 -- the AIM. Not regulation. The FAA's official statement of expected procedure. Enforceable indirectly through [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/13?at=2026) when deviation creates a hazard.
>
> Layer 4 -- Advisory Circulars. Guidance on what compliance looks like. One acceptable means; alternative means are also legal if they meet the underlying regulation.
>
> Layer 5 -- Chief Counsel interpretive letters. Binding on FAA enforcement within the scope of the question presented. NTSB Board orders sit alongside as case law.
>
> FAA Orders are the agency-procedure layer -- 2150.3 enforcement, 8900.1 FSDO operations, 8000.373 compliance philosophy. Pilots reach for those when the question is about how the FAA itself operates."

This earns trust. You named the layers, you articulated the regulatory force of each, and you placed Orders correctly as a parallel reference rather than confusing them with binding regulation.

### Stage 2: Pick the example and read the FAR (90 seconds)

> "I'll use [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/103?at=2026) -- preflight action. The text reads: 'Each pilot in command shall, before beginning a flight, become familiar with all available information concerning that flight.' It then lists specific items: weather reports and forecasts, fuel requirements, alternates, traffic delays.
>
> The phrase 'all available information' is what makes this interesting. The FAR also says 'shall ... become familiar.' Read literally, that's a passive obligation -- become familiar with what's available. A pilot could argue: 'I had a weather briefing thrust upon me by the briefer; I'm familiar with it; that's compliance.' Or a pilot could argue: 'I was busy; the information wasn't pushed at me; I didn't see it; therefore I didn't have to be familiar with it.' Both readings are plausible from the FAR text alone. The FAR doesn't settle the question."

Demonstrates that you've read the FAR carefully and surfaced the actual ambiguity. The examiner now knows you understand the regulation enough to identify *what* is unclear.

### Stage 3: Read the FAR's neighborhood (60 seconds)

> "I check the neighborhood. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/13?at=2026) (careless or reckless) overlays anything in 91 -- a pilot who deviates from 91.103 in a way that creates a hazard is exposed to a 91.13 charge in addition. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/155?at=2026) (VFR weather minimums) is the regulatory floor on visibility -- 91.103 doesn't relax it. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/167?at=2026) and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/169?at=2026) (fuel and IFR alternates) are specifically referenced in 91.103's listed items.
>
> The neighborhood doesn't resolve the 'all available information' phrase. It clarifies the surrounding rules but doesn't settle what 'all available' demands."

### Stage 4: Check the AIM (90 seconds)

> "AIM Chapter 5 covers Air Traffic Procedures, with Section 1 specifically on Preflight. AIM 5-1 walks through the briefing -- weather, NOTAMs, fuel, performance, alternates, runway lengths -- and describes the canonical self-briefing structure. AIM 7-1 covers weather information sources and the FAA's recommended briefing tools.
>
> The AIM tells me what a complete briefing looks like procedurally. But the AIM is procedural, not regulatory. It tells me 'here's what compliance looks like,' it doesn't tell me 'this is what the FAR demands.'"

### Stage 5: Check the AC (60 seconds)

> "AC 91-92 is 'Pilot's Guide to a Preflight Briefing.' This is the AC that operationalizes 91.103. The AC describes acceptable means: a pilot self-briefs, checks NOTAMs, evaluates weather, considers alternates, computes fuel, reviews chart updates. AC 91-92 doesn't expand the regulation; it tells me what self-briefing should cover for the FAA to consider me compliant.
>
> The AC is one acceptable means. If I do all of AC 91-92, I'm safe. If I do something different that meets the underlying regulation, I'm also legal -- but I'd have to defend the alternative. The AC is the well-trodden path."

### Stage 6: Check the Chief Counsel letter (2 minutes)

> "Now the contested layer. the Walker letter -- the Walker letter (2017). This is the FAA Chief Counsel's interpretation of 'all available information' in 91.103. The letter holds that the regulation imposes an *active-effort* obligation, not a passive-receipt obligation. The pilot must affirmatively seek out information -- weather, NOTAMs, fuel, alternates -- not just process whatever happens to land on their desk.
>
> The Walker letter is the authoritative answer to the ambiguity I identified in Stage 2. The plain reading suggested 'become familiar' could be passive. The Walker reading is that 91.103 obligates the pilot to *seek* information, including from sources the pilot might not have thought to consult. The pilot who didn't check a NOTAM that was readily available has not satisfied 91.103 even if they sincerely 'didn't know' about it.
>
> So Walker resolves what the FAR alone left unclear. The FAA enforces against the Walker reading. A pilot who relies on the plain-reading-passive interpretation has a position the FAA will reject. Walker is binding within the scope of the question presented."

### Stage 7: Check the case law (45 seconds)

> "There's also NTSB case law. *Administrator v. Pirsch* and related Board orders interpret 91.103 in specific accident contexts. The NTSB has historically deferred to the FAA's interpretation -- including the Walker reading -- which means Walker is reinforced by case law as well as binding administratively.
>
> If a pilot disputes the Walker reading and the case goes to the NTSB, the pilot is likely to lose. The NTSB has a pattern of upholding the Walker-style active-effort interpretation."

### Stage 8: Check 49 CFR / Orders (30 seconds)

> "49 CFR isn't relevant here -- 91.103 is purely a 14 CFR question, not a security or reporting issue. FAA Orders also aren't relevant -- the question is about pilot compliance, not agency procedure.
>
> If this had been a question about TSA approval for a foreign student or NTSB reporting after an incident, I'd route to 49 CFR. If it had been 'what does an inspector check during a ramp inspection,' I'd route to Order 8900.1. Neither applies."

### Stage 9: Synthesize the binding answer (90 seconds)

> "Here's the synthesis. The legal frame is [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/103?at=2026), which says the pilot must become familiar with all available information. The procedural detail is in AIM 5-1 and AC 91-92, which describe what self-briefing should cover. The binding interpretation -- the part that actually settles the ambiguity -- is the Walker letter: the obligation is active-effort, not passive-receipt.
>
> A pilot who self-briefs by actively checking weather, NOTAMs, fuel, alternates, runway lengths, and TFRs is compliant. A pilot who 'didn't know' about an available NOTAM that should have been checked is in violation, even if they didn't see the NOTAM, because they should have looked. The Walker reading is the binding standard.
>
> If a checkride examiner asks me 'what does 91.103 require?', I cite the FAR text first, then AC 91-92 for the briefing scope, then Walker for the active-effort standard. That's the complete defensible answer."

### Stage 10: The limits (60 seconds)

> "Two limits worth flagging.
>
> First, the Walker letter is binding within the scope of the question presented. If I face a fact pattern Walker didn't analyze, I'd need to read the letter carefully to see how broadly the holding extends. Letters bind cases like the one analyzed; whether they extend to a different case is a question of analogy.
>
> Second, the Walker letter is the most recent FAA interpretation of 91.103 'all available information.' The FAA can issue a later letter that supersedes Walker. I'd check the chief-counsel-letters page periodically to make sure Walker hasn't been modified. The 2017 letter is the controlling one as of today, but that's a date-stamped fact -- not a permanent one."

## Compressed answer (under 2 minutes)

> "When a regulation is unclear, I walk five layers. Layer 1 is the FAR. Layer 2 is 49 CFR for cross-agency rules. Layer 3 is the AIM for procedural guidance. Layer 4 is the matching Advisory Circular for what compliance looks like. Layer 5 is Chief Counsel letters for the binding interpretation of contested cases.
>
> Take [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/103?at=2026) -- 'become familiar with all available information.' Plain reading is ambiguous: passive or active? AIM 5-1 and AC 91-92 describe self-briefing scope. The Walker letter (2017) settles the ambiguity: it's an active-effort obligation. The pilot must affirmatively seek out information; passive receipt isn't enough. So my answer integrates the FAR (the legal frame), AC 91-92 (the briefing scope), and Walker (the binding standard). That's how I'd defend a 91.103 question."

## What goes wrong (failure modes)

| Failure mode                                                 | Diagnosis                                                                                                      |
| ------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Cites only the FAR and stops                                 | Misses Layers 3-5; gives a half-answer that the examiner reads as shallow                                      |
| Names the AIM as regulation                                  | The AIM is expected practice, not regulation. Saying "the AIM requires..." is technically wrong                |
| Names an AC as regulation                                    | An AC is one acceptable means, not the only legal path. Compliance is the regulation                           |
| Cites a Chief Counsel letter as if it amended the regulation | The letter binds enforcement; rulemaking changes the regulation. Different processes                           |
| Names a letter without recipient name and year               | "There's a letter on this" isn't enough. Examiners expect "Walker (2017)" or "Mangiamele (2009)"               |
| Forgets the 49 CFR layer                                     | TSA AFSP and NTSB reporting live in Title 49. Pilots who only know Title 14 miss these                         |
| Treats FAA Orders as binding on pilots                       | Orders govern the FAA. Pilots reference them; they don't comply with them as regulation                        |
| Cites a stale revision of an AC or AIM                       | Current revision controls. Stale citations are a red flag of stale knowledge                                   |
| Cites the Hicks letter to support every CFI logging question | Hicks/Murphy are narrow. Not every logging question is settled by Hicks. Read carefully before citing          |
| Says "I'd just look it up" instead of describing the routing | The exam tests routing skill. Demonstrating you don't have one fails the question                              |
| Conflates AIM enforcement with regulatory enforcement        | AIM deviations are enforceable indirectly through §91.13. AIM is not directly enforceable                      |
| Names the wrong AC for a topic                               | High-yield ACs (61-65, 61-98, 91-92, 91-79, 90-66, 120-71) are memorized by topic                              |
| Treats a CC letter as universally binding                    | Letters bind FAA enforcement within scope. NTSB and federal courts generally defer but technically can decline |
