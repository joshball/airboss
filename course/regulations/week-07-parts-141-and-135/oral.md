# Week 7 oral -- "Your student is leaving you for a 135 operator. What changes?"

## The question

> "One of your commercial students just signed an offer with a Part 135 operator. They start initial training in three weeks. Walk me through what changes for them. Medical, currency, training, ops constraints. Tell me what their first 90 days will look like."

This is a cumulative oral. It pulls from Weeks 1-7. The student should land everything from "what part is this?" all the way through "what's a competency check?", and they should be able to do it from a CFI's perspective, advising the transitioning student.

## What this is testing

- Whether you can hold the operations frame from Week 1 ("the pilot, the flight, the operation") and apply it to the 91 -> 135 transition
- Whether you know the medical step ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/23?at=2026)) and can articulate why the second-class is required
- Whether you have the 135 PIC minimums ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/243?at=2026)) and SIC currency ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/247?at=2026)) close at hand
- Whether you understand the structure of operator training and the 12-month competency check ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/293?at=2026))
- Whether you can name what's *layered* (Part 91 always applies; 135 layers on top; Part 119 issues the certificate) without confusing the layers
- Whether you can talk a student through the operational shifts (duty / rest, drug/alcohol testing, ops specs constraints) without being preachy

## Model answer (full walkthrough)

A defensible answer takes about 12-15 minutes spoken.

### Stage 1: The frame (one sentence)

> "First, the framing. Their flight as a private pilot was Part 91. Their training as a commercial student was Part 91 plus Part 61. Their job tomorrow is Part 91 plus Part 119 plus Part 135. The 91 floor never goes away -- everything they learned about preflight, weather, equipment, airspace, fuel, alternates -- that all still applies. What's *new* is the 119 certificate the operator holds, and the 135 operating rules that hang off it."

This frames the conversation. The student isn't replacing Part 91 with Part 135; they're adding 119 + 135 on top. Get this right or the rest doesn't land.

### Stage 2: Medical

> "Medical changes immediately. As a Part 61 commercial student they were on a second-class for the practical test under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/23?at=2026), and a third-class would have covered most of their training flying. As a 135 PIC they need a *current* second-class. The duration matters: under 40, the second-class is good for one calendar year for commercial-PIC privileges; over 40, it's also one calendar year. They should plan a medical visit before initial training because the operator will want a current second-class on file for the day they sign on. Their third-class privileges drop to private-pilot privileges if they let the second-class lapse. They should also know about the timing of the FAA medical processing -- it's not always same-day."

Names the section, the durations, the practical timing problem.

### Stage 3: Hour minimums and ATP question

> "[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/243?at=2026) is the PIC requirements section. The big numbers: for IFR PIC under 135, they need 1,200 hours total time, of which 500 cross-country, 100 night, 75 instrument actual or simulated. For VFR PIC, the minimums are lower -- 500 hours total, 100 cross-country, 25 night. They're 250 hours total today. Most 135 operators will hire them as a *second in command* (SIC) or as a freight first-officer to build hours toward 1,200. Some single-pilot 135 operations they could PIC at lower hours under specific subparts -- but most charter 135 IFR work requires the 1,200.
>
> The ATP question is downstream. Part 135 doesn't require an ATP for PIC. Part 121 does ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/153?at=2026)). If their target is 121 regional, the ATP is the next milestone after they hit 1,500 hours -- or 1,250 if they have a four-year aviation degree from a Part 141 program (R-ATP under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/160?at=2026))."

Hour numbers cold. ATP positioned correctly as a 121 gate, not a 135 gate.

### Stage 4: Currency on top of currency

> "Currency layers. They keep all their Part 61 currency: [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) flight review every 24 calendar months, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) recency for passenger and IFR ops. *On top of that*, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/247?at=2026) adds 135-specific recency requirements -- 3 takeoffs and landings in the operator's aircraft type within the preceding 90 days, similar shape to 61.57 but specific to the type they'll fly under 135.
>
> And then [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/293?at=2026) requires a *competency check* every 12 calendar months for each aircraft they fly under 135. The competency check is a checkride-like event administered by a check airman or the operator's chief pilot. The flight review under 61.56 is replaced by the 135.293 check for 135 ops -- 61.56(d) recognizes the equivalent. So they don't need a separate flight review while they're on the 135 cycle.
>
> Also -- the 6-month line check ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/299?at=2026) for IFR PIC). Some operators do it more often by ops specs, but every 12 months minimum, and the 6-month interval is required for IFR PIC duties."

Lays out the three currency layers (61, 135.247 recency, 135.293/.299 checks) and the equivalencing of the flight review.

### Stage 5: Training

> "Initial training is everything. The operator runs an FAA-approved training program under 135 subpart H. It typically includes ground school, systems training for the specific aircraft, normal and emergency procedures, simulator or aircraft checkout, and an Initial Operating Experience (IOE) -- supervised line flying with a check airman before they're released to fly the line themselves.
>
> Then they're in *recurrent* training every 12 months for the rest of their 135 career. Same shape, lighter cadence -- ground refresh, systems update, simulator session, competency check. They will never not be in training while they're flying 135."

Names the structure. Avoids overwhelming detail; this is a CFI's overview, not a 135 instructor's deep dive.

### Stage 6: The operation around them

> "Operationally, several things tighten up:
>
> **Weather minimums.** Subpart D of 135 sets weather minimums that are tighter than Part 91 in many cases. Takeoff minimums under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/225?at=2026), alternate requirements under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/223?at=2026) -- alternates are required more often than under Part 91 [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/169?at=2026).
>
> **Equipment.** Subpart C of 135 requires more equipment than 91.205 alone. Some IFR ops require autopilot or two pilots. Some EMS ops require specific HEMS equipment.
>
> **Ops specs.** This is the one most pilots don't expect. The operator's *operations specifications* are a contract between the operator and the FAA that specify which aircraft, which airports, which kinds of operations, which weather minimums, which crew requirements. The pilot operates inside the ops specs. They can't, for example, divert to an airport that isn't in the operator's airport-authorization list without specific exception.
>
> **Duty / rest.** Subpart F of 135 sets duty period limits and required rest. Single-pilot day-VFR has different limits from multi-pilot IFR. The pilot doesn't get to override these even if they feel fine -- the operator can't schedule them outside the limits.
>
> **Drug and alcohol testing.** They're now subject to mandatory random testing under 14 CFR Part 120 / 49 CFR Part 40. Pre-employment, random, post-incident, return-to-duty -- all of it. Most 135 jobs require a passing pre-employment test before the first flight."

Names each operational shift, names the subpart, lands the practical implication.

### Stage 7: Recordkeeping and what doesn't change

> "What *doesn't* change: their pilot certificate, their ratings, the airplane logbook structure, the basic aerodynamics, the basic weather skills. They're still a pilot. They're still the PIC of every flight they fly as PIC. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/3?at=2026) authority is theirs, not the operator's.
>
> What *does* change: their training records now live in the operator's training file (135 subpart H). Their flight time logging changes -- they'll log a lot of SIC time before they log PIC time. Their operational decisions are now subject to the operator's procedures and the dispatcher / flight-follower they may have to coordinate with. They learn to live with operational control that's shared between captain, dispatcher (or flight follower), and the operator's chief pilot."

Reassures the student where their existing skills hold; flags where their habits will need to change.

### Stage 8: The 90-day picture

> "Realistic first 90 days at a 135 operator:
>
> - Week 1-2: pre-employment medical, drug test, paperwork, ID, system access.
> - Week 3-4: ground school. Systems, ops manual, ops specs, emergency procedures, security training.
> - Week 5-6: simulator or aircraft training. Type-specific procedures, normals, emergencies.
> - Week 7: competency check ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/293?at=2026)) administered by a check airman.
> - Week 8-12: Initial Operating Experience -- supervised line flying as SIC or single-pilot under check-airman observation.
> - End of 90: signed off, on the line, flying revenue trips.
>
> They will not log many hours in their first 90 days. They'll spend most of those 90 days in classrooms, simulators, and on jumpseat. The operator is investing in them to a regulatory standard before letting them fly the line."

Concrete picture of what the transition actually looks like, with the regulatory milestones embedded.

## Compressed answer (under 90 seconds)

> "Their flight goes from 91-only to 91 + 119 + 135. Medical: second class required, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/23?at=2026). Hours: 1,200 IFR PIC under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/243?at=2026); they're at 250 so they'll start as SIC. Currency: keeps Part 61, adds [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/247?at=2026) recency and a [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/293?at=2026) competency check every 12 months -- the 135 check substitutes for the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) flight review under 61.56(d). Training: initial then recurrent forever, under 135 subpart H. Ops shift: tighter weather minimums, equipment requirements in subpart C, duty/rest in subpart F, mandatory drug/alcohol testing. They operate inside the operator's ops specs. ATP isn't required for 135 -- that's the next gate, when they target 121."

## What goes wrong (failure modes)

| Failure mode                                                                           | Diagnosis                                                                                            |
| -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Says "they need an ATP for 135"                                                        | Wrong. ATP is the 121 gate ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/153?at=2026)). 135 PIC is 1,200 / 500 hours under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/243?at=2026). |
| Forgets the second-class medical                                                       | High-yield. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/23?at=2026) is straightforward; missing it loses trust. |
| Treats 135 as replacing Part 91                                                        | 135 *layers on* 91. The 91 rules never go away. Misses the structural insight from Week 1.            |
| Doesn't know the 12-month competency check                                             | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/293?at=2026). Plus the 6-month line check for IFR. Pilots who haven't flown 135 confuse these. |
| Says "they keep their flight review"                                                   | Half-true. [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026)(d) recognizes the 135 competency check as an equivalent, so a separate flight review isn't needed during the 135 cycle. |
| Forgets ops specs                                                                      | The pilot's daily life is governed more by the operator's ops specs than by the regulations themselves. Missing this misses the operational reality. |
| Doesn't mention drug / alcohol testing                                                 | A pre-employment test is a real-world barrier to starting; pilots who don't know about it can be surprised. |
| Says "Part 119 doesn't matter to the pilot"                                            | The pilot doesn't *hold* the 119 certificate, but their entire job exists because the operator does. The chief pilot ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/119/69?at=2026)) is the pilot's regulatory chain. |
| Conflates 135 with 121                                                                 | 135 = on-demand / charter / smaller scheduled. 121 = scheduled airline. The pilot is going to one, not the other. |
| Frames the conversation as "good news / bad news"                                      | This isn't a values conversation. It's a regulatory orientation. Stay in the regulations and the operational changes; don't moralize about 135 vs 91. |
| Recommends they take a 135 charter "informally" before training is complete            | Career-ender. The operator cannot schedule them outside the training program. The pilot cannot fly 135 outside the training program. Both would be violations. |

## Variant prompts

| Variant                                                                                          | What changes                                                                                            |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Your student is leaving you for a 121 regional carrier"                                         | Add ATP requirement ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/153?at=2026)); FAR 117 rest rules ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/117?at=2026)); two-pilot crew always; dispatcher in operational control. |
| "Your student is leaving you for a corporate Part 91 flight department"                          | Less changes -- still Part 91 (with subpart F large-aircraft rules if applicable). Still need a second-class for compensation. No 119 certificate, no ops specs. |
| "Your student is leaving you for a fractional operator (NetJets / Wheels Up)"                    | Part 91K (fractional) plus often 135. Different management structure, different training cycles. Some 121-style discipline applied to 91K ops. |
| "Your student is leaving you for an EMS (HEMS) operator"                                         | 135 with HEMS-specific subparts. Higher minimums on weather, IIMC procedures, NVG ops. Single-pilot many ops. Different fatigue regime. |
| "Your student is staying at the school but moving from 61 to 141 instructor"                     | Recordkeeping discipline (Lesson 02). Stage check authority. Chief structure. Zero change in airman privileges. |
| "What if the 135 operator wants my student to instruct under their 141 cert as a side gig?"      | The 141 school side and 135 operator side are *separate certificates* even if owned by the same parent. Each has its own chief, its own TCO / ops specs, its own paperwork. The CFI works under both, but their work under each is governed by that side's rules. |
