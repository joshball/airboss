# Parts 135 and 119 -- on-demand commercial and the certification of operators

Part 135 is the part your future commercial students keep asking about. They've heard it pays better than instructing. They've heard it requires "more hours" and "an IPC every 6 months." They've also heard it's the gateway to a 121 career. They want to know what changes when they cross from Part 91 / Part 61 into Part 135.

This is the lesson that tells them.

Along the way it tells them about Part 119, the part that *issues* the operator certificate that 135 hangs off. Most pilots have heard of 135. Almost none have heard of 119. Both apply to the same flight.

## What you'll be able to do

- Distinguish a Part 91 flight from a Part 135 flight using operational facts (who's flying, who's paying, what's being moved)
- State why Part 119 exists and how it relates to Part 135
- Locate the Part 135 minimums for pilot experience, equipment, and currency
- Recognize the "Part 91 in disguise" pattern: a flight that looks recreational but legally requires Part 135 conduct
- Counsel a commercial-rated pilot on what changes when they take a 135 job

## Why this matters

Two reasons. First, the compensation-or-hire trap is real. A private pilot who flies their boss to a meeting "for free" while the boss pays for the airplane has, depending on facts, just operated a Part 135 flight without a 119 certificate. This is a career-ender. CFIs hear the phrase "I have a friend who needs a ride to Reno, can I just take them in the club plane?" every other month. The CFI needs to know what to say.

Second, your student probably will go to 135 next. The transition from a 91 / 61 life to a 135 life is one of the largest regulatory step-ups in a pilot career. The minimums change, the currency rules change, the medical changes, the training changes. Ground them now in what to expect.

## The discovery question

A commercial pilot, fresh certificate. They're approached by a wealthy family friend who owns a Cessna 414. The friend says: "I'll pay you $300 to fly me and my wife to Vegas this weekend. I'll pay for the airplane separately. We're old friends -- this is just a favor."

Pause. What part is this flight conducted under?

Several questions to surface:

1. Is anyone *holding out* (offering air transportation to the public)? -- maybe. The pilot is being paid to provide transportation. The friend is paying. There's no general advertisement, but the pilot is being compensated for the flight.
2. Is the pilot a "common carrier"? -- the four-element test is *holding out + transportation + persons or property + for compensation or hire*. The pilot is conducting transportation of persons for compensation. Not advertising publicly might exclude *common* carriage but doesn't exclude *private* carriage, which still requires Part 135.
3. Is there a 119 certificate? -- almost certainly not. Neither the pilot nor the friend is a certificated operator.

The conclusion: this is a flight that requires Part 135 conduct under the authority of a Part 119 certificate. Neither party has the certificate. Therefore the flight is *illegal*. The compensation makes the flight commercial; without a 119 certificate, no part of 14 CFR allows the flight to occur.

The pilot says no. The pilot tells the friend they need to charter from a 135 operator who has the certificate, the ops specs, and the qualified pilot. The friend can pay the 135 operator; the 135 operator can pay the pilot.

This is the "Part 91 in disguise" trap. Hold it. We'll come back.

## What Part 119 is

Part 119 is the *certification* part. It does not contain operating rules. It contains the rules for *issuing* an operator certificate.

```text
Part 119 -- Certification: Air Carriers and Commercial Operators

Subpart A -- General                          (119.1 to 119.5)
  Applicability, definitions, who needs a cert, who doesn't.

Subpart B -- Applicability of Operating Requirements
                                              (119.21 to 119.25)
  When you have a 119 cert, which operating rules apply -- 121 vs 135.

Subpart C -- Certification, Operations Specifications,
              and Certain Other Requirements   (119.33 to 119.73)
  How to get a cert, what's in your operations specifications,
  the management positions required (chief pilot, director of
  operations, director of maintenance, etc.).
```

The two sections to know cold:

- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/119/1?at=2026) -- applicability. Tells you whether you need a 119 cert.
- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/119/21?at=2026) -- which operating rules. Tells you whether your 119 cert puts you in 121 or 135.

A 119 certificate by itself authorizes nothing. The certificate carries *operations specifications* (the "ops specs") that say what the operator may do: the kinds of operations, the aircraft, the airports, the maintenance program. The ops specs are a contract between the operator and the FAA. The ops specs are what makes a real commercial operation possible.

[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/119/69?at=2026) requires the management positions: every 119 certificate holder must employ a *director of operations*, a *chief pilot*, and (for most operators) a *director of maintenance*. These are not honorary titles. The DO and chief pilot are responsible to the FAA for the operator's compliance. They sign things; their names appear on the ops specs; if the operation goes sideways, they're the ones in front of the administrator.

## What Part 135 is

Part 135 is the *operating rules* for on-demand commercial operations conducted by a 119 certificate holder whose ops specs put them under 135.

The "on-demand" part matters. Part 135 covers:

- Air taxi (passenger charter, including light-jet fractional)
- Cargo charter
- Air ambulance / EMS
- Single-pilot air taxi (a real category, with its own ops specs limits)

The rough boundary with Part 121: 121 is *scheduled* service in larger aircraft. 135 is *unscheduled* (charter, on-demand) or smaller-aircraft scheduled service. The exact boundary is in [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/119/21?at=2026); for a CFI, "scheduled airline = 121, charter = 135" is the right frame.

The shape of Part 135:

```text
Part 135 -- Operating Requirements: Commuter and On-Demand Operations

Subpart A -- General                          (135.1 to 135.45)
  Applicability, applicability of part 91, where 135 layers on top.

Subpart B -- Flight Operations                (135.61 to 135.121)
  How flights are conducted -- pilot duties, weather minimums,
  fuel, alternates, etc.

Subpart C -- Aircraft and Equipment           (135.141 to 135.185)
  What aircraft can be used, what equipment must be installed.

Subpart D -- VFR/IFR Operating Limitations
              and Weather Requirements        (135.201 to 135.227)
  The day-to-day weather and route minimums.

Subpart E -- Flight Crewmember Requirements   (135.241 to 135.247)
  Pilot eligibility, recency, currency.

Subpart F -- Crewmember Flight Time and Duty Period Limitations
              and Rest Requirements           (135.261 to 135.273)
  Duty time and rest. The reason 135 pilots talk about
  "duty rigs" and "rest minimums."

Subpart G -- Crewmember Testing Requirements  (135.291 to 135.301)
  Initial, recurrent, and competency checks.

Subpart H -- Training                         (135.321 to 135.353)
  Training programs, who delivers training, recurrent training
  schedules, ground-school requirements.

Subpart I -- Airworthiness                    (135.411 to 135.443)
  Continuing airworthiness program, inspections, maintenance.

Subpart J -- Maintenance, Preventive Maintenance, and Alterations
                                              (135.501 to 135.621)
  Maintenance program, work performed, recordkeeping.
```

For a CFI sending a student to 135, the high-yield subparts are:

- **Subpart E** -- the pilot rules ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/243?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/247?at=2026))
- **Subpart F** -- duty / rest
- **Subpart G** -- competency checks ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/293?at=2026))
- **Subpart H** -- training

## What changes for a pilot moving to 135

A commercial-rated pilot leaving a 91 / 61 instructing job for a 135 job sees several large shifts:

| Area              | Part 91 / 61                     | Part 135                                                                                |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Medical           | Third class fine for many ops    | Second class required as a 135 pilot ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/23?at=2026))   |
| Pilot minimums    | Whatever the cert says           | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/243?at=2026): 1,200 hr for IFR PIC, 500 hr for VFR PIC (typical) |
| Currency          | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) recency | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/247?at=2026) recency, ON TOP of 61.57 |
| Competency check  | Flight review every 24 months    | Competency check every 12 months ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/293?at=2026)), plus 6-month line check |
| Training          | None required                    | Initial training, recurrent training, ground school -- per the operator's training program (subpart H) |
| Duty / rest       | None                             | Duty period limits, mandatory rest periods (subpart F)                                  |
| Drug / alcohol    | None for self-employed           | Mandatory random testing under 14 CFR Part 120 / 49 CFR Part 40                         |
| Aircraft equipment | Whatever 91.205 / 91.213 require | Higher equipment minimums in subpart C; some IFR ops require two pilots / autopilot     |
| Weather minimums  | Part 91 minimums                 | [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/225?at=2026) and neighbors -- alternate required more often, higher takeoff/landing mins |

The big takeaways for the conversation with a transitioning student:

1. **You may need a second-class medical.** Third-class won't cover 135 PIC duties.
2. **You'll be in recurrent training forever.** This is not optional, not waivable, not "I just did one." Initial, then every 12 months, plus a 6-month line check.
3. **The duty rules will rule your life.** A 135 pilot can't just "fly one more leg." The operator's duty schedule is enforced.
4. **You'll be under random drug and alcohol testing.** Not the same regime as a Part 91 owner-operator.

## The compensation-or-hire trap

This is the trap that trips up new commercial pilots and the CFIs who advise them. The pattern:

A pilot has a friend, family member, or business connection who needs a flight. The pilot is rated and capable. The other party offers to pay for the airplane, or pay the pilot, or both. Everyone agrees this is "just a favor." No 119 certificate is involved.

The legal test (see Advisory Circular AC 120-12, "Private Carriage Versus Common Carriage of Persons or Property," and the line of Chief Counsel letters interpreting it):

1. **Is there compensation or hire?** Almost always yes if anyone is paying anything that benefits the pilot or covers the flight beyond pure cost-sharing under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026).
2. **Is the pilot acting as the operator?** If yes, the pilot needs a 119 cert.
3. **Is someone else acting as the operator?** If yes, *they* need a 119 cert.
4. **Is this exempt under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/119/1?at=2026)?** A short list of exemptions exists -- ferry flights for the owner under specific conditions, certain student instruction, sightseeing under §91.146/§91.147, banner tow under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/313?at=2026), etc. Most "favor flights" don't qualify.

If the answer is "compensation, no 119 cert, no exemption," the flight is illegal. The pilot can lose their certificate. The aircraft owner can be cited. The Chief Counsel has been clear and consistent on this for two decades.

The student-facing version: **if money is changing hands and it's not strict pro-rata cost sharing under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/113?at=2026)(c), assume Part 135 applies.** Then check the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/119/1?at=2026) exemptions. If none apply, the flight is illegal without a 119 certificate.

## Common misreadings

- **"Part 135 is for big airplanes."** Wrong. Part 135 covers everything from a single-pilot Cessna 208 charter to a Gulfstream V on-demand operation. Aircraft size doesn't determine 135 vs 121; *scheduled vs unscheduled* mostly does, with size limits at the boundaries.
- **"If I have a commercial cert, I can fly Part 135 the next day."** Wrong. The commercial cert is necessary but not sufficient. The pilot must meet [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/135/243?at=2026) hour minimums, complete the operator's initial training, pass a competency check, and be added to the operator's authorized-pilot list under their ops specs.
- **"As long as I don't 'hold out' to the public, I can fly someone for compensation."** Wrong. The four-element test requires holding out *for common* carriage. *Private* carriage (a contract with one or a few specific customers) still requires a 119 cert under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/119/1?at=2026). The "no holding out" defense fails because private carriage is also regulated.
- **"Part 119 is just paperwork."** Half-true. Part 119 is the certification machinery, not operating rules. But the operations specifications issued under [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/119/49?at=2026) are operationally enormous: they specify aircraft, airports, maintenance programs, training programs. Without ops specs, no operation. Pilots who haven't worked at a 135 operator underestimate how much daily operational latitude lives in ops specs versus in the regulation itself.
- **"My student will fly Part 135 the way I taught them to fly Part 91."** Wrong. The operating rules are different. Alternates required more often, higher weather minimums for takeoff and landing in some configs, equipment requirements, recordkeeping requirements. The student needs to learn the operator's procedures (which the operator will train).
