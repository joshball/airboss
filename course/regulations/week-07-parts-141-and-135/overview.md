# Week 7 -- Parts 141 and 135

This is the operation week. After six weeks living inside Part 61 (the pilot) and Part 91 (the flight), we step outside the cockpit and look at the certificated *organizations* that pilots and aircraft work for: training schools, on-demand commercial operators, airlines, and repair stations.

The treatment is intentionally light. A working CFI almost never *operates* under Part 121 or Part 145. A working CFI may or may not work at a Part 141 school; if they do, parts of 141 govern their daily life, and if they don't, it never matters. Part 135 is the part their commercial students keep asking about, but they themselves probably don't fly under it. Goal: literacy. Know the shape, know when each part applies, know where to look when a question hits.

## What you'll be able to do by end of week

- Distinguish a Part 91, Part 135, Part 141, and Part 121 operation from the operational facts: who's flying, what's being moved, who's paying, what aircraft, what crew.
- Locate the Part 141 appendices A through N and name what each one is the syllabus for.
- Name the Part 135 chief structure (chief pilot, director of operations, director of maintenance) and where in 119 they're required.
- Recognize a "Part 91" flight that's actually a Part 135 flight in disguise (the compensation-or-hire trap) and locate the rule that governs the line.
- Counsel a student transitioning to a 135 operator: what changes for them in medical, currency, training, recordkeeping, and operational constraints.
- Locate Part 145 (repair stations) and Part 121 (airline) without confusing the two.

## Front-to-back reading

For this literacy week the reading is lighter than the deep weeks:

- Part 141 subpart A (the general / applicability section). Read carefully.
- Part 141 appendices A through N -- skim. Notice that each appendix is a syllabus for one cert or rating level.
- Part 135 -- skim. Notice subpart structure: A (general), B (flight ops), C (aircraft), D (VFR limits), E (IFR limits), F (crewmember requirements), G (testing), H (training), I (airworthiness), J (maintenance).
- Part 119 -- skim. This is the certification-of-operators part. 135 hangs off it.
- Part 121 and Part 145 -- name and locate. Open the table of contents, see the shape, close it.

## The framing for the week

The same operational facts can be governed by different parts depending on who's flying, what's being moved, and who's paying:

| Operational fact                                               | Part                                                                |
| -------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Private pilot taking friends on a hamburger run                | 91                                                                  |
| Private pilot training student during a lesson at a 61 school  | 91 + 61                                                             |
| Private pilot training student during a lesson at a 141 school | 91 + 61 + 141                                                       |
| Charter pilot flying paying passengers in a King Air           | 91 + 119 + 135                                                      |
| Airline pilot flying paying passengers in a 737                | 91 + 119 + 121                                                      |
| Mechanic returning an aircraft to service after a 100 hr       | 91 + 43 + (145 if at a repair station, otherwise individual A&P/IA) |

Part 91 always applies. The other parts *layer on top*. A 135 flight is also a 91 flight -- 135 just adds requirements. Same for 121, 141, 145. This is the load-bearing insight for the week and for the rest of your career.

## Common confusion to head off now

The single most common mistake students (and some CFIs) make is treating "Part 135" as a kind of *aircraft* or a kind of *pilot*. It is neither. Part 135 is a kind of *operation*: an on-demand commercial flight conducted by a certificate holder under 119. The same airframe can fly Part 91 in the morning (the owner repositions it) and Part 135 in the afternoon (the same owner-pilot flies a paying customer). What changed is not the airplane and not the pilot; what changed is the flight's purpose and the certificate it's flown under.

Hold that distinction. We'll return to it in [03-part-135-and-119.md](03-part-135-and-119.md).
