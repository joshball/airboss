# The four canonical scenarios

## What you'll be able to do

- Name each of the four sibling capstone orals and explain in one sentence what each tests
- Pick the scenario most likely to expose your weakest subpart, and run it first
- Recognize when an examiner is asking a variant of a scenario you have rehearsed

## Why this matters

Four scenarios cover the full Title 14 surface that a CFI candidate is expected to integrate. Each scenario is a different *mode* of integration, not a different *subset* of regulations. A candidate who can deliver all four cleanly has been pressure-tested across pre-flight, post-incident, CFI-side authority, and qualifications counting. Most CFI initial practical tests pull questions that map to one or more of these four modes; rehearsing the four covers the surface area of what gets asked.

## The four scenarios

Each scenario lives in its own file under [orals/](../orals/). The filename is the scenario name; the file follows the structure in [DESIGN.md](../DESIGN.md). Read each file in full before the partner exam.

### Scenario 1: Night IFR with a passenger

> "You're flying tomorrow at night, IFR, with a passenger. Walk me through every regulatory check."

[Source: orals/night-ifr-passenger.md](../orals/night-ifr-passenger.md)

This is the **pre-flight integration** scenario. A single question pulls preflight ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/103?at=2026)), pilot currency ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)), aircraft inspections ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/409?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/411?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/413?at=2026)), aircraft documents ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/203?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/7?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/213?at=2026)), equipment ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/205?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/215?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/225?at=2026)), oxygen ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/211?at=2026)), fuel and alternates ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/167?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/169?at=2026)), and the non-Title-14 ecosystem (49 CFR 1552, NTSB Part 830). Eight stages, 13+ regulations, capable of being delivered in 25 minutes long-form or 90 seconds compressed.

What it tests: whether the structural map of Title 14 is in your head and whether you can route a single fact pattern through every layer of the stack without dropping a layer. The most common failure is dropping a stage -- forgetting [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/213?at=2026) (the no-MEL pathway) or [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/171?at=2026) (the VOR check) -- because the stage is buried inside another part of the answer.

What it builds toward: the working CFI's daily mental check before any flight. A pilot who can run this oral in their head before each launch is a pilot who has internalized the regulations as a habit, not a memory exercise.

### Scenario 2: Gear-up landing on a night IFR cross-country

> "You're the CFI. Your student lands gear-up on a night IFR cross-country. Walk me through your regulatory obligations from the second of impact to the FAA ramp check the next morning."

[Source: orals/gear-up-night-ifr.md](../orals/gear-up-night-ifr.md)

This is the **post-incident integration** scenario. The regulations span two titles -- 14 CFR for emergency authority ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/3?at=2026)), airworthiness ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/7?at=2026)), maintenance and return-to-service ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/405?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/407?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/417?at=2026)), and CFI privileges ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/193?at=2026)) -- and 49 CFR for NTSB notification (49 CFR 830.5), the substantial-damage definition (830.2), and wreckage preservation (830.10). On top of that, the FAA enforcement progression layer: Letter of Investigation, Pilot's Bill of Rights 2, FAA Order 8000.373 compliance program, and ASRS.

What it tests: whether you know which obligations are immediate vs. follow-up, whether you distinguish FAA enforcement from NTSB reporting, whether you exercise PBR2 protections deliberately, and whether you keep your CFI candor without compounding the original event with new statements. The most common failure is calling the FSDO immediately to "explain" -- self-incrimination disguised as cooperation.

What it builds toward: the working CFI's response when an incident actually happens. Most CFIs go an entire career without an incident, but the cost of being unprepared the one time it happens is high enough that walking the scenario once now is the right investment.

### Scenario 3: A friend asks you to give them their flight review

> "A friend of yours -- current PPL, current Class 3 medical, current renter at your school -- texts you Tuesday and asks if you'd give them their flight review next Saturday. Walk me through every regulatory check, every conversation, every signature, and every thing you absolutely do not do."

[Source: orals/friend-flight-review.md](../orals/friend-flight-review.md)

This is the **CFI-side authority** scenario. The regulation chain is shorter than the first two ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/59?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/193?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/197?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/189?at=2026), AC 61-65, AC 61-98) but the social-pressure dimension is unique: each plot twist tests whether the candidate refuses cleanly or negotiates a compromise that is also a violation. The backdating twist is the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/59?at=2026) trap; the medical-lapse twist is the Hicks-letter trap; the complex-aircraft twist is the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/31?at=2026) bundling trap.

What it tests: whether the CFI cert is treated as a regulated authority, whether [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/189?at=2026) instructor records are recognized as part of the CFI's burden, whether AC 61-65 endorsement language is treated as the verbatim canonical text, and whether the candidate can say "no" plainly when the regulation requires no. The most common failure is soft-deflecting on the backdate request ("I can't, but maybe ...") instead of refusing outright.

What it builds toward: every CFI's career involves at least one moment where a pilot asks for something the regulation prohibits and the social cost of refusing is real. Rehearsing the refusal once with a friend in a scenario makes the refusal easier when it happens for real.

### Scenario 4: A 250-hour PPL applies for an instrument rating

> "A current private pilot, 250 hours total, current Class 3 medical, no recent IFR training of any kind, walks into your school Monday and says 'I want my instrument rating.' Walk me through every regulatory check, every counting question, every endorsement, and every twist of [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) they're going to try to talk you into bending."

[Source: orals/ppl-applies-for-ir.md](../orals/ppl-applies-for-ir.md)

This is the **qualifications-counting** scenario. The regulatory chain centers on [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) -- specifically the five nested aero-experience buckets in (d), the simulator substitution rules in (g) and (h), and the AC 61-65 A.31 (written prep) and A.32 (practical prep) endorsements. The chain extends to [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026) (logging), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) (recency), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) (the instrument-rating XC definition), and [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/189?at=2026) (instructor records).

What it tests: whether the candidate can count buckets, distinguish device classes (FFS / FTD / AATD / BATD), recognize when prior time is creditable but not in the bucket the candidate is assuming, and write endorsements only when the standard is met. The most common failure is over-counting -- accepting the candidate's logbook entries at face value without verifying that the entries match the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) definitions.

What it builds toward: the day-one conversation with every IR student. The candidate who walks in expecting to "knock out the IR in three months" is the candidate who needs the long version of [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026) explained patiently and accurately. A CFII who can run this oral can answer any IR-counting question that comes up over the next year of training.

## Reading the four together

The four scenarios are designed to be *non-overlapping in mode* but *overlapping in content*. A regulation cited in one scenario can recur in another. For example:

- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) appears in scenarios 1, 3, and 4
- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026) appears in scenarios 3 and 4
- [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/3?at=2026) appears in scenario 2 and is implicit in scenario 1
- AC 61-65 appears in scenarios 3 and 4

This is intentional. A learner who repeatedly meets the same regulation in different scenarios builds a more durable recall than a learner who memorizes one regulation in one context. By the time the four scenarios are rehearsed, the high-yield sections have been reinforced from multiple angles.

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating one scenario as "the easy one."** Every scenario has a depth that becomes apparent only after the first delivery. A candidate who labels scenario 3 as "easy" because the regulation chain is shorter is missing that the social-pressure dimension is its own difficulty axis.
- **Picking the favorite scenario to rehearse.** A learner who rehearses only the night-IFR scenario because it's the canonical one will deliver scenario 1 cleanly and stumble on scenarios 2-4. Rotate. The order across rehearsal sessions matters.
- **Reading the model answer before attempting the scenario cold.** The first delivery should be cold -- you read the prompt and you start talking. The model answer is the *post-rehearsal reference*, not the script. Reading the model answer first compresses your structural map into someone else's words.
- **Skipping the variants.** Each scenario has a Variant Prompts section with 5-7 variants. The DPE is more likely to pull a variant than the canonical prompt. A candidate who rehearses only the canonical prompt is brittle.
- **Confusing "scenario" with "regulation list."** The scenario is *integration mode*, not a regulation list. A candidate who walks the regulation list without integration sounds like they are reading from notes; an examiner notices.

## Drills

The drills for this lesson are reading the four files. Set aside two hours per scenario for the first read. Re-read each scenario at least once before the partner exam. Annotate the model answer in the margins with your own variants and traps; the annotations are the artifact, not the file itself.
