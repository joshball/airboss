# How the capstone works

## What you'll be able to do

- Describe the structure of an oral exam day from the candidate's seat and from the examiner's seat
- Run a partner exam against another learner using the rubric in this lesson
- Switch between scenarios without losing track of which regulatory chain you're walking
- Diagnose your own weak subparts after each oral, in real time

## Why this matters

The CFI initial practical test is, in practice, an all-day oral followed by a flight. Eight hours in a small room with the DPE going through the ACS, looking at your logbook, asking you to walk regulations, asking you variants when you answer too smoothly. The pace is conversational; the load is structural.

A learner who has only practiced one-shot questions ("what's the flight review interval?") arrives unprepared because the oral is *integrated* and *iterative*. The DPE asks, the candidate answers, the DPE pulls a thread, the candidate follows the thread, the DPE pulls a different thread. After eight hours the candidate has spoken about every part of Title 14 and the DPE has a calibrated view of where the structural map is solid and where it is sparse.

This week is the rehearsal. Not for memorized facts -- those have been built up over Weeks 1-9 -- but for the *shape* of the integration. The four sibling capstone orals are the rehearsal scenarios. The partner exam is the rehearsal mode.

## The structure of the capstone day

A typical capstone day has the following shape. Adapt the timing if you are running it solo or with a study partner over multiple sessions.

```text
0830  Arrive. Coffee. Logbook on the table, certificate, medical, IACRA.
      The room is small. There is no clock you can lean on.
0900  Frame: "Walk me through Title 14 from the outside."
      (Week 1 oral. 12-15 minutes. Sets the tone.)
0915  Pivot to Part 61. Walk through it. Answer variants.
      (Week 2 oral. 15-20 minutes.)
0935  Pivot to CFI subpart H. Privileges, limitations, endorsements.
      (Week 3 oral. 15-20 minutes.)
0955  Capstone scenario 1: night IFR with a passenger.
      (Full integration. 25 minutes spoken, more with examiner pulls.)
1030  Break. 15 minutes. The break is a test too -- did you tense up?
1045  Capstone scenario 2: gear-up landing. Post-incident.
      (49 CFR 830, PBR2, the FAA enforcement progression. 25-30 minutes.)
1115  Capstone scenario 3: the friend's flight review.
      (CFI-side authority, [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/59?at=2026)
       refusal, AC 61-65 endorsement. 25 minutes.)
1145  Capstone scenario 4: 250-hour PPL applies for IR.
      (Counting, sub-requirement nesting, simulator substitution.
       25 minutes.)
1215  Lunch. 45 minutes. Real lunch. Eat. The afternoon is
      navigation drills and edge-case questions.
1300  Drills. 50 prompts at 60 seconds each. Mixed bag.
      ("Where is the rule about X?" "Diagnose this pilot." etc.)
1400  Edge cases pulled from the morning's gaps. Examiner picks
      what to push on based on what was rough.
1430  Logbook walk. Examiner goes line by line through your last
      30 days, asking you to defend each entry.
1500  Wrap. Examiner gives a verbal debrief. Then the flight portion
      starts (separate event in the actual practical, simulated here).
```

Six hours of oral, plus drills, plus a logbook walk. The work is *the load*, not the questions. A learner who can deliver clean answers to the first two scenarios can usually deliver clean answers to the second two; the differentiator is whether the candidate's pace and tone hold up after lunch.

## The rubric

Partners grade each other against the four-axis rubric below. Each axis is scored 1-5 per scenario.

| Axis                       | What's being measured                                                  | 1                                                          | 5                                                                                       |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Section recall             | Did the candidate cite by section number, not by name?                  | "There's a rule about night ..."                           | "[@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)(b)"                                   |
| Structural framing         | Did the candidate route the question via subject (pilot/flight/op)?     | Random walk through regulations                            | "Pilot first, flight second, operation third -- that's why I check 61.57 before 91.205" |
| Trap awareness             | Did the candidate name the trap without being prompted?                 | Misses the standard pitfalls                               | "The trap people fall into here is conflating the alternate filing minimum with the alternate approach minimum" |
| Recovery                   | When the partner pulls a thread, can the candidate stay coherent?       | Loses the structural map under pressure                    | Picks up the new thread and ties it back to the original chain                          |

A candidate scoring 4-5 across the four axes on all four scenarios is at oral-test-ready level. A candidate scoring 3 on a single axis is on the edge -- one bad question from collapse. A candidate scoring 2 anywhere is not ready and the partner says so.

The rubric exists to make the partner's feedback actionable. "You did okay" is unhelpful; "axis 2 (structural framing) was a 3 because you skipped the framing on scenarios 2 and 4 -- when the partner pulled a thread you could not anchor back" is actionable.

## Partner-exam mechanics

Two learners pair up. One plays examiner, one plays candidate. The examiner has the four sibling capstone files open; the candidate has nothing.

```text
EXAMINER                                CANDIDATE
--------                                ---------
Reads the prompt verbatim.              Listens. Does not start until done.
Sets a 25-minute timer.                 Begins answering.
After 60s of answer, asks a variant.    Pivots; ties new thread to old.
Pulls one trap mid-answer.              Names the trap explicitly.
Stops the candidate at 25 min.          Stops mid-sentence if necessary.
Scores all four axes.                   Sits silent until scored.
Reads the score with two sentences      Listens. Asks at most one
of feedback per axis.                   clarifying question.
Switch roles.                           Switch roles.
```

Roles switch each scenario. After four scenarios each learner has played examiner twice and candidate twice. After the partner exam is complete, both learners write a one-paragraph note on the *axis they want to improve* and the *scenario where it was weakest*. That note becomes the next week's practice target.

Partner-exam pairs that work well: same study cohort, similar progress, comfortable with mutual feedback. Partner-exam pairs that work poorly: large skill mismatch (the more advanced partner becomes a tutor, not an examiner), close personal relationships that resist honest scoring, or asynchronous schedules that turn the exam into a chat.

A solo learner can run a partner exam against a recorded delivery: deliver the answer to camera, watch back, score yourself against the rubric. The self-score is less calibrated than a partner score but better than no score.

## How scenarios cross weeks

Each capstone scenario is a *cross-week integration*. The skill being tested is not "do you know the regulation" but "can you assemble regulations from different weeks into one chain without losing the chain."

| Capstone scenario               | Weeks contributing the regulations                                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Night IFR with a passenger      | Week 2 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)) + Week 4 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/103?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/167?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/169?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/211?at=2026)) + Week 5 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/205?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/213?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/409?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/411?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/413?at=2026)) + Week 8 (AIM, ACs) |
| Gear-up landing                 | Week 4 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/3?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/13?at=2026)) + Week 5 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/405?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/407?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/417?at=2026)) + Week 9 (49 CFR 830, PBR2, FAA Order 8000.373) + Week 3 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/193?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026)) |
| Friend's flight review          | Week 2 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/56?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)) + Week 3 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/193?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/195?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/197?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/189?at=2026)) + Week 8 (AC 61-65, AC 61-98) + Week 9 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/59?at=2026)) |
| 250-hour PPL applies for IR     | Week 2 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/65?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/51?at=2026), [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026)) + Week 3 ([@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/189?at=2026)) + Week 7 (Part 141 Appendix C) + Week 8 (AC 61-65 A.31 / A.32) |

A learner who can recall this graph on demand can deliver any of the four scenarios cleanly. A learner who has only memorized each week in isolation will know the parts but stall when the parts have to assemble.

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating each scenario as a fresh question.** The four scenarios share regulations. Once you've cited [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/57?at=2026) in scenario 1, you do not re-explain it in scenario 4 -- you reference it and move on. Examiners listen for this; re-explaining wastes the candidate's time and signals shallow integration.
- **Reciting the model answer verbatim.** The model answers in [orals/](../orals/) are *one defensible path*. They are not the only path. A candidate who recites the model answer verbatim sounds memorized; an examiner detects this and pushes on the variants to break the recitation.
- **Stopping when the regulation is recalled.** A capstone-grade answer cites the section, *and* names the trap, *and* anticipates the variant. Stopping at the recall is a 3 on the rubric, not a 5.
- **Going silent under examiner pressure.** When the examiner pulls a thread, the candidate is expected to say "I'm following that thread to [section] -- [name the chain]." Silence reads as a stall. Even an imperfect answer that names where the candidate is reaching is better than silence.
- **Scoring the partner's exam with sympathy.** A partner who scores everything as 4 because the candidate is a friend is destroying the exercise. Score the rubric, not the friendship.
- **Skipping the post-exam notes.** The one-paragraph note about the weak axis is the artifact that drives next week's practice. Without the note the partner exam is just a conversation.

## Drills

The drills for this lesson are the partner exam itself. There is no separate drill set; drills.md in this directory is the timed-50-prompt navigation drill that runs in parallel with the orals.

A solo learner can drill on the rubric by recording one delivery per scenario and scoring it back the next morning. Six hours of recording, six hours of scoring, one full pass per week.
