# Self-assessment and reflection

## What you'll be able to do

- Describe your own map of Title 14 in your head and identify the regions that are dense vs. sparse
- Use the airboss weakness lens to drive the next training cycle
- Write a reflection note after each partner exam that the next session can act on
- Recognize when you are using "currency" thinking in a context that requires "proficiency" thinking

## Why this matters

The capstone is not the end of regulatory study. It is a marker that says "you have a structural map; now go maintain it." Regulatory knowledge decays the moment training stops. A CFI three years out from initial who has not opened the FARs since the practical test will have lost the structural map -- not the high-yield sections, but the connective tissue between them.

This lesson teaches the reflective practice that makes the structural map durable. The reflection happens *during* training, not after. A learner who can audit their own gaps in real time during a partner exam is a learner who can audit their own gaps during a real training event five years later.

The skill being learned is meta-cognitive: thinking about thinking. The artifact is a written reflection note. The mechanism for follow-up is the airboss weakness lens.

## What does your map of Title 14 look like?

Most pilots, asked to describe Title 14 from memory, can sketch the high-yield zones cleanly and the low-yield zones not at all. That is appropriate. The high-yield zones get cited every flight. The low-yield zones get cited rarely, and a pilot who pretends to know them in detail is a pilot who is going to be wrong when those zones are actually consulted.

Try the exercise. On a blank sheet, sketch your map. The instructions:

1. Draw the boxes for the parts you know exist (61, 67, 68, 91, 119, 121, 125, 135, 141, 142, 145).
2. Inside each box, list the subparts you can name from memory.
3. Star the sections you can recite by section number from memory.
4. Mark with a dot the sections you know exist but cannot cite by section number.
5. Mark with a question mark the regions where you do not know what is there.

Now compare against the live source ([Part 91](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91?at=2026), [Part 61](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61?at=2026), and the rest of Title 14). Where the actual regulation has content and your map has a question mark, that is a gap. Some gaps are appropriate ([Part 91](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91?at=2026) subparts F-N for non-turbine GA pilots) and some are not ([Part 91](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91?at=2026) subpart E maintenance for any pilot who is also an aircraft owner).

The most common gaps in CFI candidates' maps:

| Gap                                                                          | Why it matters                                                                  |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/213?at=2026) -- the no-MEL pathway        | Triggered any time equipment is broken; gap means the candidate fakes the answer |
| [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/171?at=2026) -- VOR check                 | Procedural; easy to forget; comes up in any IFR-using-VOR conversation         |
| [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/189?at=2026) -- CFI instructor records    | 5-year retention; gap means the CFI's own paperwork is sloppy                  |
| [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/117?at=2026) -- speed restriction below 10,000 | Frequently cited in airspace conversations; easy to misstate                    |
| 49 CFR 830 -- accident vs. incident definitions                              | Wrong agency, wrong title; structural confusion                                 |
| AC 61-65 endorsement IDs (A.1 vs A.4 vs A.31 vs A.32 etc.)                   | Working CFI's daily reference; gap means freelancing endorsement language      |
| [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/119?at=2026) -- minimum safe altitudes    | Routinely tested; pilot tendency to confuse with [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/117?at=2026) |
| FAA Order 8000.373 -- compliance program                                     | Affects post-incident strategy; gap means missed leverage in enforcement       |

A learner with five or more of these gaps is not ready to deliver the capstone orals cleanly. The gaps are not abstract -- they manifest as silences in the partner exam.

## Which subparts do you find slowest?

Slowness is the diagnostic. A subpart you walk smoothly is a subpart you have integrated. A subpart you walk slowly is a subpart you are still translating from a list you have memorized into a structural understanding.

Try the second exercise. For each part, time yourself walking the subparts in order, with one sentence per subpart. Two minutes maximum per part:

| Part      | Subparts                                                                                   | Target time |
| --------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------- |
| Part 61   | A general / B aircraft ratings / C student / D recreational / E private / F commercial / G ATP / H CFI / I sport / J ground instructor / K light-sport CFI | 2 min |
| Part 91   | A general / B flight rules / C equipment / D special ops / E maintenance / F-N specialized | 90 sec      |
| Part 141  | A school certification / B personnel and aircraft / C training course outline / D examining authority / Appendices A-N (per cert level) | 90 sec |
| Part 119  | (very short) general / certification / certificate authorization                             | 60 sec      |
| Part 135  | A general / B flight ops / C aircraft / D-G operational specifications and crew                | 90 sec      |

A learner who blanks on a single subpart label is not slow -- they have a hole. A learner who can recite the labels but cannot describe what each subpart actually does is on the edge -- the structural skeleton is there but the content is sparse. A learner who walks every subpart smoothly with a one-sentence description is at oral-test-ready level for that part.

The slow subparts are the targets for the next training cycle. Not "review more"; specifically: open the subpart, walk every section in order with the front-to-back commentary from Weeks 1-9, and re-attempt the timed walk at the end.

## Currency vs. proficiency, applied to your own knowledge

The course has emphasized [reg-currency-vs-proficiency](../../knowledge/regulations/currency-vs-proficiency/node.md) as a regulatory concept: currency is the legal floor; proficiency is the actual capability. The same concept applies to *your own knowledge of the regulations*.

You are *current* on the regulations if you can pass the CFI initial knowledge test. The written test is a fixed corpus, predictable, with public study guides. Currency on the test is the floor. You are *proficient* on the regulations if you can deliver the four capstone scenarios cold, in 25 minutes each, with variants. Proficiency is the ceiling. The two are different.

A candidate who is current but not proficient passes the written and stalls on the practical. The DPE is testing proficiency. The compliance program, the ramp check, the post-incident enforcement: all test proficiency. The flight review you give next year tests your proficiency in front of a real pilot whose currency you are signing for.

The reflective practice in this lesson is *checking your own proficiency, not your own currency*. The check is the timed delivery, not the multiple-choice question.

## Using the airboss weakness lens

The airboss study app surfaces a "weakness lens" view that assembles the user's review history into a list of topics where the spaced-repetition model has measured low recall confidence. Conceptually, the weakness lens lives at `/lens/weakness` in the study app and is updated continuously as the user works through cards.

After each partner exam, take the reflection note from [01-how-the-capstone-works.md](01-how-the-capstone-works.md) and translate the *axis* and *scenario* into specific topics:

| Reflection note                                                                       | Translates to weakness-lens query                                  |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| "Axis 1 (section recall) was a 3 on scenario 1 -- I forgot [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/91/213?at=2026) again" | weakness-lens topic: 91.213 inoperative equipment              |
| "Axis 3 (trap awareness) was a 2 on scenario 4 -- I did not catch the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026) instrument-XC definition trap" | weakness-lens topic: 61.1 cross-country definitions          |
| "Axis 2 (structural framing) was a 3 across all four -- I forgot to route via pilot/flight/operation" | weakness-lens topic: Title 14 structural framing                 |
| "Axis 4 (recovery) was a 2 on scenario 3 -- I went silent when the partner pulled the medical-lapse twist" | weakness-lens topic: Hicks letter / CFI-as-PIC during instruction |

The weakness lens then drives the next training cycle. Cards on the weak topics surface more frequently; the candidate runs the partner exam again with the weak topics already reinforced; the rubric scores improve.

This loop -- exam, reflection note, weakness lens, retraining, exam -- is the maintenance loop for regulatory knowledge. It applies during initial training. It applies during recurrent training. It applies when a working CFI feels rusty after a slow month.

## How to write a reflection note

The reflection note is a one-paragraph artifact written within an hour of the partner exam. Format:

```text
Date: 2026-04-29
Scenario: ppl-applies-for-ir
Examiner: <partner name or self>
Score: axis 1 (section recall) = 4
       axis 2 (structural framing) = 3
       axis 3 (trap awareness) = 2
       axis 4 (recovery) = 4

Weakest axis: trap awareness, on scenario 4.
Specifically: I missed the [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026)
instrument-XC definition trap. I counted touch-and-go XC time toward the
50-hour bucket without catching that the instrument rating definition
requires a landing more than 50 nm from departure. The partner had to
pull that thread for me to find the trap.

Next-cycle target: re-read [@cite](airboss-ref:regs/cfr-14/61/1?at=2026)
end-to-end, then run the scenario again on Saturday with a fresh partner.
The trap is a single line in the dictionary section; rehearsing the
scenario without rehearsing the dictionary is not enough.
```

The note is for *you*, not for the partner. Keep them in a folder, dated, scenario-named. Re-read the prior month's notes at the start of each new cycle. The notes form a longitudinal record of where your map has been weak; that record is more valuable than any single test score.

## Common misreadings

- **"Reflection is optional, the practice is what matters."** The practice without the reflection is rote rehearsal. A candidate who runs the four scenarios ten times without reflection has rehearsed the *form* of the answer; the structural integration only deepens when the candidate notices the gap and acts on it. The reflection is the deepening mechanism.
- **"My map of Title 14 should look like the actual eCFR table of contents."** No. The map should look like *your* working understanding -- dense where you operate, sparse where you do not. A CFI who does not fly Part 135 charters has an appropriately sparse Part 135 map. The map is a mirror of working knowledge, not a transcription.
- **"Currency on the regulations is the goal."** Currency is the floor. The CFI initial practical, the working day with students, and any post-incident interaction with the FAA all test proficiency. Aiming for currency means aiming for the floor; the candidate who aims for the floor lands below it.
- **"Slowness on a subpart means I should drill that subpart in isolation."** Sometimes. More often, slowness on a subpart means the subpart's connective tissue to neighboring subparts is sparse. Drill the subpart, then drill the connection -- run a scenario that pulls from the slow subpart and from a strong subpart and notice whether the integration is smooth or rough.
- **"The weakness lens is a dashboard."** The lens is a queue. It surfaces the topics that need attention; it does not score them or rank their importance. The learner decides which weak topic to prioritize. A CFI candidate two weeks from a practical prioritizes differently than a CFI three years post-practical maintaining recurrent knowledge.

## Drills

This lesson's drill is the reflection itself.

After each partner exam this week, write a reflection note in the format above. Do not skip. The note takes 10-15 minutes to write and is the artifact that drives the next session.

A solo learner runs the partner exam against camera and writes the reflection note based on the recording. Self-scoring is less calibrated than partner-scoring but the reflection is still useful.
