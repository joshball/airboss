### Q-A3-01 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-1, RC-2

A person who appears to be a foreign national walks into your flight school and wants to start flight training today. They have a valid passport and visa but no TSA approval. What should you do?

A) Begin ground instruction immediately since TSA approval is only required for flight training
B) Do not provide any flight training until the individual has been vetted and approved through the TSA's AFSP (Alien Flight Student Program)
C) Accept the student and begin training as long as you photocopy their passport and visa
D) Refer them to a Part 141 school since Part 61 schools are exempt from TSA requirements

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Under 49 CFR Part 1552, flight schools and independent instructors may not provide flight training to non-U.S. citizens or nationals until TSA approval is received through AFSP. "Flight training" includes both ground and flight instruction per TSA's definition. Beginning any training without clearance exposes both the instructor and student to serious federal penalties. Option A is incorrect -- TSA's definition of flight training includes ground training. Option C does not satisfy the AFSP vetting requirement. Option D is wrong -- both Part 61 and Part 141 must comply.

### Q-A3-02 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-1, RC-2

A student who was previously approved by TSA at another flight school transfers to your school. They present their prior TSA approval documentation. Can you begin training?

A) Yes, TSA approval transfers between schools
B) No, a new AFSP application must be submitted for your school before training can begin
C) Yes, but only if the previous school provides a written release
D) Yes, as long as the TSA approval is less than one year old

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** TSA AFSP approval is school-specific. When a student transfers, the receiving school must submit a new application through AFSP and receive approval before beginning training. The prior approval does not transfer. This is a common compliance failure point that CFIs must understand. Options A, C, and D all incorrectly assume transferability of TSA approval.

### Q-A3-03 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-02)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RC-1, RC-2

Under TSA regulations, which of the following individuals are exempt from AFSP (Alien Flight Student Program) requirements? (Select all that apply)

A) U.S. citizens
B) U.S. nationals
C) Lawful permanent residents (green card holders)
D) Canadian citizens seeking a private pilot certificate
E) Holders of a current foreign ATP certificate validated by the FAA

**Correct:** A, B, C
**Explanation:** U.S. citizens, U.S. nationals, and lawful permanent residents are exempt from AFSP requirements (49 CFR 1552.1). Canadian citizens are NOT exempt -- they must go through AFSP like all other non-U.S. persons. Holders of foreign certificates are also not exempt from AFSP. The CFI must verify citizenship or permanent residency status before beginning training with any student.

### Q-A3-04 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-2

How long must a flight school retain TSA-related records, including citizenship verification and AFSP documentation?

A) 12 months after the student completes or discontinues training
B) 24 months, consistent with FAA FIRC record retention
C) 5 years, per TSA security record retention requirements under 49 CFR 1552
D) Indefinitely

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** TSA requires flight training security records to be retained for 5 years (49 CFR 1552). This is longer than the FAA's 24-month general record retention requirement. CFIs and flight schools must maintain these records for the full 5-year period. Failure to maintain records is itself a violation. Options A and B cite incorrect retention periods. Option D is not required by regulation.

### Q-A3-05 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-03)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-1

During a lesson, your student (a U.S. citizen) mentions that a friend who is a foreign national wants to start flight training. The friend has been living in the U.S. on a student visa for 3 years. Your student asks, "Since he's been here so long, does he still need TSA approval?" What do you tell your student?

A) Length of U.S. residency does not affect the AFSP requirement -- non-citizens must be TSA-vetted regardless of how long they have lived in the country
B) After 5 years of continuous U.S. residency, foreign nationals are exempt from AFSP
C) Student visa holders are exempt from AFSP because they have already been vetted by the State Department
D) The friend can begin ground school while the AFSP application is processed, since TSA only requires approval for flight training in the aircraft

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** TSA AFSP requirements apply to all non-U.S. citizens and non-U.S. nationals regardless of length of residency, visa type, or other vetting they may have undergone from other agencies. Only U.S. citizens, U.S. nationals, and lawful permanent residents are exempt. Option B invents a residency exemption that does not exist. Option C confuses State Department visa vetting with TSA flight training security vetting. Option D is incorrect -- TSA's definition of flight training includes ground instruction.

### Q-A3-06 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-04)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-2, AV-1

Under 49 CFR 1552, flight schools are required to designate a security coordinator. Which of the following best describes the security coordinator's responsibilities?

A) Filing annual security reports with TSA headquarters
B) Serving as the primary contact for TSA, ensuring compliance with security procedures, and maintaining familiarity with TSA security requirements
C) Conducting background checks on all students and staff
D) Performing monthly security audits that are submitted to both the TSA and FAA

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The security coordinator serves as the flight school's primary point of contact with TSA and is responsible for ensuring the school's compliance with applicable security procedures under 49 CFR 1552. This includes maintaining familiarity with TSA requirements and ensuring that all personnel are aware of their obligations. Option A is incorrect -- there is no annual report filing requirement to TSA headquarters. Option C is incorrect -- background checks on students are handled through AFSP, not by the coordinator directly. Option D fabricates an audit and dual-reporting requirement that does not exist.

### Q-A3-07 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-04)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-1, CJ-2

You are an independent CFI operating under Part 61. A colleague tells you that TSA security awareness training is only required for Part 141 flight schools and their employees. Is this correct?

A) Yes, Part 61 independent instructors are exempt from TSA security awareness training
B) No, all flight school employees and independent instructors who provide flight training must complete TSA security awareness training
C) Yes, but only if the Part 61 instructor trains fewer than five students per year
D) No, but Part 61 instructors may substitute an FAA safety seminar for TSA security awareness training

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** TSA security awareness training requirements under 49 CFR 1552 apply to all providers of flight training, including independent Part 61 instructors. There is no exemption based on the type of certificate held by the training provider or the volume of students trained. AC 61-83K emphasizes that flight instructors should be fully aware of TSA requirements. Option A incorrectly claims a Part 61 exemption. Option C invents a student-volume threshold. Option D fabricates a substitution provision that does not exist.

### Q-A3-08 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-05)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-1, CJ-1

A new student arrives at your flight school claiming to be a U.S. citizen. What documentation should you examine before beginning flight training?

A) No documentation is needed -- you can take the student at their word
B) A valid U.S. passport, U.S. birth certificate, or certificate of naturalization to verify citizenship
C) A state-issued driver's license, since only licensed drivers can be U.S. citizens
D) A signed self-declaration form stating they are a U.S. citizen

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per 49 CFR 1552, flight training providers must verify citizenship or immigration status before providing training. Acceptable documents include a valid U.S. passport, an original or certified copy of a birth certificate issued by a state or local government, or a certificate of naturalization or citizenship. Option A is incorrect -- verbal claims are insufficient; documentation must be examined and records retained. Option C is wrong because a driver's license does not prove citizenship. Option D is incorrect -- a self-declaration form is not an acceptable substitute for verifying actual citizenship documentation.

### Q-A3-09 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-05)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RC-2, AV-2

Which of the following are potential consequences for a flight instructor who provides flight training to a foreign national without obtaining TSA AFSP approval? (Select all that apply)

A) Civil penalties up to $250,000 per violation
B) Criminal prosecution under federal law
C) Suspension or revocation of the instructor's flight certificates
D) Mandatory enrollment in a FIRC within 30 days
E) Permanent ban from the AFSP system

**Correct:** A, B, C
**Explanation:** Violations of 49 CFR 1552 can result in severe consequences. TSA may impose civil penalties up to $250,000 per violation. Criminal penalties including fines and imprisonment are possible under federal law. The FAA may also take certificate action against the instructor, including suspension or revocation. AC 61-83K warns that not following TSA guidelines properly can have serious consequences for both the student and the instructor. Option D is fabricated -- there is no mandatory FIRC enrollment penalty. Option E is also fabricated -- there is no permanent AFSP ban provision.

### Q-A3-10 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-06)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-1

You are a CFI at a Part 141 school. A student who holds a sport pilot certificate wants to add an instrument rating. She presents a Canadian passport and a valid U.S. work visa. She says another instructor at the school already started training her last month without any TSA paperwork. What is your most appropriate course of action?

A) Continue training since another instructor already started -- TSA approval is only needed at the beginning of the first lesson
B) Stop all training immediately, notify the school's designated security coordinator, and ensure an AFSP application is submitted before any further instruction
C) Continue training but submit the AFSP application yourself so it runs concurrently with instruction
D) Have the student sign a waiver acknowledging the TSA requirement has not been met, then continue training

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A Canadian passport holder is not exempt from AFSP requirements regardless of visa status. The fact that another instructor began training without TSA approval means a violation has already occurred. The correct action is to stop training immediately and escalate to the school's security coordinator, who is responsible for TSA compliance. An AFSP application must be submitted and approved before any further training. Option A is wrong -- the prior violation does not create a grandfathered approval. Option C compounds the existing violation. Option D has no legal basis and does not satisfy TSA requirements. Per AC 61-83K, CFIs must be aware that non-compliance has serious consequences.

B) Only certificates that require flight time beyond a recreational pilot certificate

D) Only the ATP certificate due to its connection to airline operations

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** The TSA is interested in any flight training that involves actual operation of an aircraft. AC 61-83K states that flight instructors should know which airman certificates the TSA is interested in. AFSP applies whenever a non-U.S. person seeks flight training, which encompasses training toward any certificate or rating involving aircraft operation. Option A is too narrow -- training for recreational and sport certificates also falls under AFSP. Option B invents a threshold tied to recreational pilot minimums. Option D is far too narrow and ignores the broad scope of TSA's interest in all levels of flight training.

**Competencies:** CJ-1, RM-2

A U.S. citizen student presents a state-issued driver's license as proof of citizenship. You notice the license has a "Federal Limits Apply" marking (indicating it is not REAL ID-compliant). Should you accept this as citizenship verification?

A) Yes, any government-issued photo ID is sufficient for TSA citizenship verification

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** A driver's license does not prove U.S. citizenship. Non-citizens, including lawful permanent residents and visa holders, can obtain state driver's licenses. Option A is incorrect because photo ID does not prove citizenship. Option B confuses residency with citizenship. ### Q-A3-14 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-08)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-1, RC-1

During your security awareness training recurrent review, you learn about indicators of potential security threats at a flight school. A student you have been training for several weeks makes an unusual request to practice approaches to a nuclear power plant at low altitude, claiming it's "just for fun." What is the most appropriate response?

A) Accommodate the request since the student has already been vetted through normal enrollment procedures
B) Decline the specific request, document the incident, and report it to your school's security coordinator or directly to the TSA if you are an independent instructor
C) Decline the request but take no further action since the student is probably joking
D) Immediately contact local law enforcement and have the student detained

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Security awareness training emphasizes that flight instructors should be alert to suspicious behavior and requests. The appropriate response is to decline the request, document the incident, and report it through proper channels -- either the school's designated security coordinator or, for independent instructors, directly to TSA's reporting mechanisms. AC 61-83K emphasizes that FIRC providers should deliver training to ensure instructors are aware of security responsibilities. Option A ignores a clear security indicator. Option C fails the reporting obligation. Option D escalates beyond the instructor's role -- reporting to the security coordinator or TSA is the proper channel, and they will involve law enforcement if warranted.

### Q-A3-15 (Module 4, Pool A3-M4-08)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RC-1

You are a CFI who has just endorsed a new student for solo flight. You verified his U.S. citizenship using his passport when he started training three months ago, but you did not make a copy of the passport or record the document details. A TSA inspector requests your compliance records. What is your situation?

A) You are in compliance because you visually verified the passport at the time of enrollment
B) You are not in compliance -- 49 CFR 1552 requires that you retain copies or records of the citizenship verification documents for 5 years
C) You are in compliance as long as the student can bring the passport to the next lesson for the inspector to see
D) You are in compliance because TSA only audits Part 141 schools, not individual CFIs

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Under 49 CFR 1552, flight training providers must not only verify citizenship or immigration status but also retain records of that verification for 5 years. Simply looking at a document without retaining a copy or recording the relevant details (document type, number, expiration) does not satisfy the recordkeeping requirement. Option A is incorrect because visual verification alone, without documentation, fails the retention requirement. Option C is incorrect -- the obligation is on the training provider to maintain records, not on the student to produce documents on demand during an audit. Option D is wrong -- TSA compliance requirements apply to all flight training providers, including independent Part 61 CFIs.
