### Q-A6-01 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1

Your student reads back a taxi clearance: "Taxi to runway 27 via Alpha, Bravo." ATC issued: "Taxi to runway 27 via Alpha, hold short of runway 9 at Bravo." The student begins taxiing. What is the most critical action?

A) Let the student continue since they will likely see the hold short markings
B) Immediately correct the student's readback error and have them re-read the clearance, emphasizing that the hold short instruction was omitted
C) Make a note to discuss readback errors during the debrief
D) Contact ATC to confirm the clearance yourself

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The student's readback omitted "hold short of runway 9" -- this is a safety-critical error that, if not caught, leads directly to a runway incursion. GA pilots are responsible for over 80% of pilot deviations, and readback errors at taxiway/runway intersections are a leading cause of surface deviations. The CFI must correct this immediately, not during debrief, because the consequence of the error could occur in the next 30 seconds. Option A relies on markings the student has already shown they're not processing carefully. Option C is too late. Option D bypasses the student's learning.

### Q-A6-02 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2

During a VFR cross-country, you realize your student has been drifting north and is now within 1 nm of Class B airspace. The student hasn't noticed. What is the best CFI action?

A) Wait to see if the student recognizes the airspace boundary
B) Prompt the student to check their position relative to the sectional chart and identify any nearby airspace boundaries
C) Take the controls and immediately turn away from the Class B airspace
D) Contact ATC for a Class B clearance

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** With 1 nm of buffer, there is time for a teaching intervention -- but not time for passive waiting. Prompting the student to identify the problem themselves is the most effective instructional approach when time permits. If the student doesn't recognize the issue quickly, the CFI must escalate. Option A risks an actual violation. Option C is over-intervention when time permits a teaching approach. Option D may not yield an immediate clearance and doesn't address the student's SA failure.

### Q-A6-03 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-01)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-1, OD-2

Which of the following are effective strategies a CFI should teach students to prevent pilot deviations? (Select all that apply)

A) Use progressive taxi instructions at unfamiliar airports
B) Always have the airport diagram available and visible during taxi
C) Read back all ATC instructions, especially hold short instructions
D) Trust memory for complex taxi routes to build confidence
E) Brief and review the expected taxi route before calling for taxi clearance

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Preventing pilot deviations requires systematic habits. Progressive taxi (A) reduces memory load at unfamiliar airports. Having the diagram visible (B) provides continuous position reference. Full readbacks (C) catch communication errors before they become deviations. Pre-briefing the taxi route (E) creates expectations that help catch discrepancies. Option D is dangerous -- relying on memory for complex taxi routes is a known contributor to surface deviations.

### Q-A6-04 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1

At a towered airport at night, your student is cleared to cross runway 9 at taxiway Charlie. The student stops at the hold short line but then says, "I can't tell if that's runway 9 or runway 27 from here." What should you teach?

A) Cross the runway -- the clearance was issued, so it must be correct
B) If there is any doubt about position, stop, hold position, and confirm with ATC before proceeding
C) Look at the runway numbers painted on the pavement
D) Use the GPS moving map to confirm position

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** When a pilot is uncertain of their position relative to a runway, the only safe action is to stop and verify. Crossing the wrong runway -- or crossing when there's any doubt about which runway it is -- risks an incursion. ATC expects pilots to query when uncertain. Option A is dangerous -- ATC clearances don't guarantee the pilot is at the correct location. Option C may not be visible at night or from the hold short position. Option D should not be the primary navigation tool on the surface.

### Q-A6-05 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, CJ-2

Your student receives "descend and maintain 4,000" from ATC during a VFR flight following. The student begins descending but overshoots, leveling at 3,700 feet. The student doesn't notice. What is the teaching priority?

A) The 300-foot deviation is within normal tolerance and isn't significant
B) Correct the altitude immediately, then debrief the importance of altitude awareness and the fact that even VFR flight following carries ATC-assigned altitude compliance expectations
C) Report the deviation to ATC and file a NASA ASRS report
D) Take the controls to climb back to 4,000 feet

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A 300-foot altitude deviation from an ATC-assigned altitude is a pilot deviation, even on VFR flight following. The immediate priority is correcting the altitude. The debrief should cover why altitude discipline matters, how to use the altimeter effectively, and the consequences of altitude deviations. Option A incorrectly dismisses the severity. Option C is premature before correcting the situation. Option D takes the learning opportunity from the student.

### Q-A6-08 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-03)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, RM-1

Which of the following are classified as surface deviations under AC 61-83K? (Select all that apply)

A) Runway incursions by GA pilots
B) Altitude deviations during cruise flight
C) Unauthorized entry onto an active runway
D) Course deviations in controlled airspace
E) Crossing a hold short line without clearance

**Correct:** A, C, E
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K broadly classifies pilot deviations as airborne or surface. Surface deviations principally concern runway incursions -- unauthorized entry onto runways, crossing hold short lines without clearance. Altitude deviations (B) and course deviations (D) are airborne deviations, not surface deviations.

### Q-A6-09 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-03)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-2, OD-1

A CFI is reviewing the AELS (Aviation Event Log System) and notices a pattern: three students from the same flight school received pilot deviations for airspace incursions near a local Class C airport in the past year. What is the most appropriate CFI response?

A) File NASA ASRS reports for each incident and move on
B) Recommend the flight school update its ground training to include specific briefings on the local Class C boundaries, common entry/exit routes, and frequency monitoring procedures
C) Advise students to avoid flying near Class C airspace
D) Suggest the flight school only fly in uncontrolled airspace until the pattern stops

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A pattern of airspace incursions from the same school suggests a systemic training gap, not individual student failures. The CFI's role is to identify the root cause and improve instruction. AC 61-83K emphasizes airspace incursions as a principal airborne deviation concern. Updating ground training with specific local knowledge directly addresses the pattern. Options C and D avoid the problem rather than solving it. Option A is reactive, not preventive.
