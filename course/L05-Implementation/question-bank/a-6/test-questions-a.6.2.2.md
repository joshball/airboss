### Q-A6-46 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-05)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, RM-1

AC 61-83K states pilots should plan each flight before it takes place, even when flying to familiar airports. A student asks: "Why would I plan for my home airport? I fly there every week." What is the best CFI response?

A) Agree -- planning for familiar airports is unnecessary
B) Explain that conditions change even at familiar airports: NOTAMs may close taxiways, runway configurations shift with wind, construction may alter taxi routes, and TFRs can appear -- planning ensures the pilot is current, not just familiar
C) Suggest the student check weather only and skip the rest
D) Tell the student to plan only when flying to unfamiliar airports

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K specifically states pilots should plan "even when flying to familiar airports." Familiarity can create dangerous complacency -- a pilot who "knows" the airport may miss a closed taxiway, a changed runway configuration, or a new NOTAM. Planning before every flight ensures the pilot's mental model matches current reality. Options A and D contradict the AC. Option C is incomplete.

### Q-A6-47 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-05)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, OD-1

During preflight planning for a cross-country, your student reviews the weather, files a flight plan, and checks fuel requirements but skips the airport diagram for the destination. Why is this a gap per AC 61-83K?

A) The airport diagram is optional for VFR flights
B) Without reviewing the airport diagram, the student cannot anticipate the taxi route, identify potential conflict points, or prepare for ATC taxi instructions -- this directly increases the risk of a surface deviation at the destination
C) Airport diagrams are only needed for IFR approaches
D) The student can ask ATC for progressive taxi instructions instead

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K emphasizes preflight planning for every flight. The airport diagram is a critical planning element because it allows the pilot to anticipate the taxi environment before arrival. Without it, the pilot is navigating blind on the ground -- a direct contributor to the "lack of airport familiarity" incursion cause. Option D describes a mitigation strategy but doesn't replace planning. Options A and C incorrectly limit when diagrams are needed.

### Q-A6-48 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-06)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, AC-1

Which of the following preflight planning elements specifically reduce runway incursion risk per AC 61-83K's planning guidance? (Select all that apply)

A) Reviewing the current airport diagram for the departure and destination airports
B) Checking NOTAMs for closed taxiways, runway closures, or construction
C) Identifying the expected runway configuration based on current winds
D) Calculating fuel burn for the cruise segment
E) Briefing the expected taxi route from parking to the active runway

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Elements A, B, C, and E directly reduce runway incursion risk by building the pilot's mental model of the surface environment before they begin taxiing. Knowing the airport layout (A), current changes (B), likely runway (C), and expected taxi route (E) means the pilot can compare ATC instructions against expectations and recognize errors. Fuel calculation (D) is important for flight safety but doesn't specifically address surface deviation risk.

### Q-A6-49 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-06)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, OD-2

Your student completed thorough preflight planning including the airport diagram and expected taxi route. After landing, ATC issues a taxi clearance using a different route than expected. The student freezes, unsure what to do. What does this reveal about the student's planning training?

A) The planning was wasted since ATC used a different route
B) The planning was valuable because the student recognized the discrepancy, but the student needs additional training on adapting to real-time changes while using the plan as a reference rather than a rigid script
C) The student should have planned multiple taxi routes
D) This proves planning is counterproductive -- it creates rigid expectations

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Good planning creates a baseline expectation, not a rigid commitment. The student's ability to recognize that ATC's instruction differed from the expected route demonstrates planning's value -- without the plan, the discrepancy would have gone unnoticed. The training gap is in adaptability: the student needs to learn to use the plan as a reference while adjusting to real-time ATC instructions. Option D misunderstands planning's purpose. Option A ignores that recognizing the discrepancy was itself valuable.

### Q-A6-50 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-07)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, ES-2

A CFI is teaching a pre-solo student about preflight planning. The student says: "I just use ForeFlight -- it has everything." How should the CFI address this per AC 61-83K?

A) Agree -- modern EFBs eliminate the need for additional planning
B) Explain that an EFB is a tool, not a substitute for actively reviewing and briefing the airport environment -- the pilot must mentally process the diagram, identify hot spots, anticipate the taxi route, and brief the expected sequence, not just have the information available on a screen
C) Require the student to use paper charts only
D) Tell the student EFBs are unreliable and shouldn't be trusted

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K's planning guidance is about the cognitive process of preparing for the flight, not just having information available. Having an airport diagram on a screen is not the same as studying it, identifying potential confusion points, and briefing the expected taxi route. The CFI should teach the student that planning is an active mental process that uses tools like EFBs, not a passive reliance on them. Options C and D are unnecessarily restrictive.

### Q-A6-51 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-07)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, OD-2

A private pilot diverts to an unfamiliar airport due to deteriorating weather. They have no airport diagram and have never been to this field. Per AC 61-83K's planning emphasis, what is the best approach to mitigate the lack of planning?

A) Taxi slowly and follow other aircraft
B) Request progressive taxi instructions from ground control, explaining unfamiliarity with the airport, and have a passenger look up the airport diagram on a mobile device while taxiing
C) Request progressive taxi instructions from ground control, hold position after landing until a passenger or EFB can provide the airport diagram, then proceed with an active mental model of the taxi route
D) Taxi to the nearest ramp and shut down immediately

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** When planning wasn't possible before arrival, the pilot must create a plan before taxiing. Holding position to review the airport diagram replaces the preflight planning step that was missed. Progressive taxi instructions provide real-time guidance. The key distinction from option B is that reviewing the diagram while taxiing splits attention during a critical phase -- it's better to stop, build the mental model, then proceed. Option A provides no actual navigation strategy. Option D may not be practical.

### Q-A6-52 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-08)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, CJ-2

Two CFIs debate the value of planning for familiar airports. CFI A says: "Planning for my home airport is a waste of time -- nothing changes." CFI B says: "I check NOTAMs and the airport diagram before every flight, even from my home base." Which position does AC 61-83K support?

A) CFI A -- experienced pilots at familiar airports don't need to plan
B) CFI B -- AC 61-83K specifically states pilots should plan each flight, even to familiar airports
C) Both are valid -- it depends on the pilot's experience level
D) Neither -- the AC only addresses planning for cross-country flights

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K explicitly states that pilots should plan each flight before it takes place, "even when flying to familiar airports." CFI B's practice directly aligns with this guidance. The AC recognizes that conditions change -- NOTAMs, construction, runway closures, procedural changes -- and that familiarity without current information creates a false sense of security. CFI A's approach contradicts the AC and models poor practice for students.

### Q-A6-53 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-08)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-1, AC-2

A CFI is developing a preflight planning checklist specifically aimed at runway incursion prevention. Which items should be on this checklist? (Select all that apply)

A) Review current airport diagram (departure and destination)
B) Check NOTAMs for taxiway/runway closures or construction
C) Identify expected runway and taxi route
D) Review airport hot spots if published
E) Check current ATIS/AWOS for active runway information

**Correct:** A, B, C, D, E
**Explanation:** All five items directly support runway incursion prevention through planning. Airport diagrams (A) provide spatial awareness. NOTAMs (B) reveal changes to the expected environment. Expected runway and route (C) create a baseline for comparison with ATC instructions. Hot spots (D) identify known areas of confusion or risk. ATIS/AWOS (E) confirms the active runway before engine start. Together, these build the comprehensive mental model that AC 61-83K's planning guidance is designed to create.

### Q-A6-54 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-09)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, OD-2

Your student plans a flight to an airport they visited three months ago. They say: "I remember the layout -- taxiway Alpha to runway 32, simple." You check the NOTAMs and find taxiway Alpha is closed for construction. What teaching moment does this create?

A) Tell the student to choose a different airport
B) Use this as a concrete demonstration of why AC 61-83K requires planning even for familiar airports -- the student's three-month-old memory doesn't reflect current conditions, and relying on it would have caused confusion or a deviation on the ground
C) Let the student discover the closure during taxi as a learning experience
D) Note it for the debrief but don't interrupt the student's planning process

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This is a perfect real-world teaching moment. The student's assumption that familiarity equals current knowledge is exactly the mindset AC 61-83K warns against. The closed taxiway proves the point concretely: without checking NOTAMs, the student would have expected to use a taxiway that doesn't exist in the current environment. Option C risks an actual safety event. Option D misses the immediate learning opportunity.

### Q-A6-55 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-09)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, ES-1

A flight school wants to improve its planning culture to reduce surface deviations. Which systemic change best aligns with AC 61-83K's planning guidance?

A) Require all students to arrive 30 minutes early
B) Integrate airport diagram review and taxi route briefing into the standard preflight checklist for every flight, making it a non-negotiable step regardless of airport familiarity
C) Post airport diagrams on the flight school walls
D) Add a planning question to the stage check oral exam

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K's guidance that planning should occur for every flight, even to familiar airports, is best implemented as a procedural standard, not a suggestion. Making airport diagram review and taxi route briefing part of the standard preflight checklist normalizes the behavior and prevents the "I know this airport" shortcut. Option A addresses time but not content. Option C provides a resource but not a process. Option D tests knowledge in isolation from practice.
