### Q-A6-66 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-15)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, CJ-1

AC 61-83K identifies failure to follow ATC taxi instructions as the number one cause of runway incursions. A student who just received a complex taxi clearance turns to ask the CFI a question about the destination airport's restaurant. What should the CFI do?

A) Answer the question briefly since it won't take long
B) Immediately redirect the student: "Hold that thought -- we're in a sterile cockpit phase. Focus on the taxi clearance. We can discuss that after we're parked."
C) Answer while pointing at the airport diagram so the student stays oriented
D) Ignore the question and let the student figure out it was inappropriate

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K emphasizes sterile cockpit -- avoiding all conversations and actions not related to flight during critical phases including taxi. The student's question about a restaurant is exactly the type of non-essential conversation that diverts attention from processing and following ATC taxi instructions. Since ATC non-compliance is the #1 incursion cause, the CFI must enforce sterile cockpit discipline immediately and explicitly. Option D misses the teaching opportunity.

### Q-A6-67 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-15)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, RM-2

According to AC 61-83K, which phases of flight require sterile cockpit discipline to prevent runway incursions?

A) Only during takeoff and landing
B) During all critical phases including taxi, takeoff, and landing -- any time the aircraft is on or near an active runway or taxiway
C) Only when ATC is talking to you
D) Sterile cockpit only applies to Part 121 operations, not GA

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K applies sterile cockpit to all critical phases of flight, explicitly including taxi operations. Runway incursions occur on the ground, so taxi is a critical phase where sterile cockpit discipline directly prevents the #1 incursion cause (failure to follow ATC taxi instructions). Option A omits taxi, which is where most surface deviations begin. Option C is too narrow. Option D incorrectly limits sterile cockpit to airline operations -- the AC applies this concept to all GA pilots.

### Q-A6-68 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-16)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, AC-1

Which of the following activities violate sterile cockpit discipline during taxi per AC 61-83K? (Select all that apply)

A) Programming the GPS for the next flight segment
B) Discussing weekend plans with the passenger
C) Monitoring ground frequency for taxi instructions
D) Adjusting the seat or reaching for items in the back seat
E) Reading back a taxi clearance to ATC

**Correct:** A, B, D
**Explanation:** Sterile cockpit means avoiding all conversations and actions not related to the current phase of flight. Programming GPS (A) diverts attention from taxi navigation. Social conversation (B) is non-essential. Reaching for items (D) removes the pilot's eyes and hands from the controls. Monitoring ground frequency (C) and reading back clearances (E) are essential taxi operations and do not violate sterile cockpit. The distinction is between flight-essential and non-essential activities.

### Q-A6-69 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-16)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, OD-1

Your student is taxiing toward runway 27 and receives: "Hold short of runway 27 at Charlie." A passenger in the back seat asks a question and the student turns to respond. The student then continues taxiing past the hold short line without stopping. What AC 61-83K concept does this directly illustrate?

A) Lack of airport familiarity
B) The direct link between sterile cockpit violations and the #1 runway incursion cause -- the passenger's question distracted the student from following ATC's hold short instruction
C) Poor radio equipment
D) ATC issued the instruction too early

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This scenario directly demonstrates why AC 61-83K emphasizes sterile cockpit: the passenger's non-essential question diverted the student's attention at the exact moment they needed to comply with a hold short instruction. Failure to follow ATC taxi instructions is the #1 cause of runway incursions, and sterile cockpit violations are a primary mechanism by which that failure occurs. The distraction created the compliance failure, which created the incursion.

### Q-A6-70 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-17)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, ES-2

A CFI is teaching a private pilot student about sterile cockpit. The student says: "That's an airline rule -- I'm just flying a Cessna." How should the CFI respond?

A) Agree that sterile cockpit is primarily for airline operations
B) Explain that AC 61-83K applies sterile cockpit to all GA pilots because GA pilots cause over 80% of pilot deviations, and distraction during critical phases is a leading contributor -- the Cessna cockpit is actually more vulnerable because there's no copilot to catch errors
C) Tell the student it's required by regulation for all aircraft
D) Skip the topic since the student isn't receptive

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K explicitly includes sterile cockpit as a teaching strategy for GA pilots. The student's misconception is common but dangerous. Single-pilot GA operations are actually more vulnerable to distraction because there's no second crew member to maintain SA when the pilot is distracted. The CFI must correct this misconception firmly. Option A reinforces the error. Option C overstates the regulatory basis. Option D abandons a critical safety topic.

### Q-A6-71 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-17)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, OD-2

During a flight review, you observe that a pilot routinely briefs passengers before engine start but never establishes sterile cockpit expectations. The pilot's passengers chat freely during taxi and approach. Per AC 61-83K, what should you address?

A) This is acceptable -- passengers shouldn't be expected to follow cockpit rules
B) The pilot should establish clear sterile cockpit briefing for passengers before every flight: during taxi, takeoff, and landing, all conversation must be limited to safety-relevant communication only
C) The pilot should ask passengers to whisper instead of talking normally
D) Sterile cockpit only applies when flying with other pilots, not passengers

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K's sterile cockpit guidance applies to avoiding all non-essential conversation during critical phases. This includes passenger conversation. The pilot's passenger briefing should explicitly cover sterile cockpit expectations -- when to limit conversation and why. Passengers can be a significant source of distraction during taxi, and establishing expectations before the flight prevents the awkward situation of shushing someone while processing a taxi clearance.

### Q-A6-72 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-18)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, OD-1

A student is holding short of runway 9 waiting for takeoff clearance. The student picks up their phone to check a text message. What is the CFI's appropriate response?

A) Wait to see if the student puts the phone down before the clearance comes
B) Immediately instruct the student to put the phone away and explain that this is a sterile cockpit phase -- electronic device distraction during critical phases is one of the most common modern sterile cockpit violations and directly increases the risk of missing or misunderstanding ATC instructions
C) Make a note for the debrief
D) Take the phone from the student

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Holding short awaiting takeoff clearance is a critical phase requiring full attention. A phone distraction at this moment means the student may miss the takeoff clearance, misidentify their callsign, or fail to hear a revised instruction. AC 61-83K emphasizes sterile cockpit during all critical phases. Electronic devices are a modern distraction source the AC's sterile cockpit guidance directly addresses. Option A risks a missed clearance. Option C is too late. Option D is confrontational.

### Q-A6-73 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-18)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, CJ-2

A CFI wants to teach the connection between sterile cockpit and the #1 runway incursion cause. Which teaching approaches effectively demonstrate this link? (Select all that apply)

A) Present case studies where cockpit distractions led to missed ATC instructions that caused runway incursions
B) Have students practice receiving and reading back taxi clearances while the CFI introduces deliberate distractions, then debrief the errors
C) Show statistics on pilot deviations caused by distraction
D) Tell students to "just pay attention" without specific techniques
E) Role-play scenarios where the student must enforce sterile cockpit with a simulated distracting passenger

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Effective teaching connects the concept (sterile cockpit) to the consequence (missed ATC instructions leading to incursions). Case studies (A) provide real-world evidence. Distraction practice (B) lets students experience the effect firsthand. Statistics (C) establish the scope of the problem. Role-play (E) builds the skill of managing cockpit distractions. Option D provides no actionable technique -- "just pay attention" is a platitude, not a teaching strategy.

### Q-A6-74 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-19)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, AC-2

A CFI is flying with a student who has established good sterile cockpit discipline during taxi and takeoff. However, during the taxi back to parking after landing, the student relaxes and begins chatting casually while still on active taxiways. Should the CFI intervene?

A) No -- the flight is essentially over and the stress of sterile cockpit isn't necessary
B) Yes -- sterile cockpit applies to all taxi operations, not just taxi-out, because runway incursions can occur at any point while the aircraft is on the movement area, including taxi to parking
C) No -- only intervene if the student is approaching a runway
D) Let it go this time and mention it in the debrief

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Runway incursions don't only happen during taxi-out. A pilot taxiing to parking may still cross active runways, navigate complex intersections, and need to comply with ATC instructions. AC 61-83K's sterile cockpit guidance applies to all critical phases, and taxi operations remain critical until the aircraft is clear of the movement area. Many incursions occur during taxi-in when pilots relax after landing. The CFI should reinforce consistent discipline.

### Q-A6-75 (Module 4, Pool A6-M4-19)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-1, OD-2

A flight school is trying to reduce runway incursions. Data shows most incidents involve student pilots during taxi. The chief instructor proposes adding a sterile cockpit policy to the school's standard operating procedures. What should this policy include?

A) A general statement that pilots should minimize distractions
B) Specific guidance defining sterile cockpit phases (taxi, takeoff, landing), prohibited activities during those phases (non-essential conversation, phone use, avionics programming), passenger briefing requirements, and CFI enforcement expectations
C) A poster in the briefing room about staying focused
D) A rule that students must fly solo to avoid passenger distractions

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** An effective sterile cockpit policy must be specific and actionable. It should define when sterile cockpit applies, what activities are prohibited, how passengers should be briefed, and how CFIs should enforce the standard. AC 61-83K's emphasis on procedures and sterile cockpit supports a structured, systematic approach rather than vague guidance. Option A is too general to change behavior. Option C is awareness, not policy. Option D eliminates dual instruction.
