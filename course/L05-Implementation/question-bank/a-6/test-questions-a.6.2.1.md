# A.6.2.1 -- Proficiency

---

### Q-A6-36 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-16)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, ES-1

AC 61-83K states that training and continuing education lead to proficiency. A private pilot who completed training five years ago and flies 20 hours per year asks a CFI: "I fly regularly -- why would I need recurrent training?" Which response best reflects the AC's guidance?

A) Agree -- 20 hours per year is sufficient to maintain proficiency
B) Explain that proficiency requires both regular flying and structured continuing education, because repetition alone can reinforce bad habits without external feedback
C) Suggest the pilot take a checkride to prove their proficiency
D) Recommend the pilot increase their annual hours to 50

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K links proficiency to both training and continuing education -- not just flight time. A pilot who flies regularly but never receives instruction may be practicing errors consistently. Recurrent training with a qualified CFI provides the external feedback loop that self-practice cannot. Option A conflates activity with proficiency. Options C and D address quantity, not quality.

### Q-A6-37 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-16)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, OD-1

A CFI is planning recurrent training for a pilot who primarily flies out of a non-towered airport. AC 61-83K specifically recommends recurrent training at towered airports. Why?

A) Towered airports have better training facilities
B) Towered airport operations involve ATC communication, complex taxi routes, hold short instructions, and multiple runways -- the specific environment where the majority of runway incursions occur
C) Non-towered airport skills don't transfer to towered operations
D) The FAA requires annual towered airport training for all pilots

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K recommends recurrent training at towered airports because the towered environment introduces the specific factors that lead to runway incursions: ATC instructions to process, complex taxi routes to navigate, hold short lines to respect, and multiple active runways to track. A pilot who only flies at non-towered airports lacks practiced proficiency in these areas. Option A is irrelevant. Option C overstates the gap. Option D is not a regulatory requirement.

### Q-A6-38 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-17)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, RM-1

A CFI is designing a recurrent training session at a towered airport for a pilot who hasn't operated at a towered field in 18 months. Which elements should be included to build proficiency per AC 61-83K? (Select all that apply)

A) Review of current airport diagrams and taxi procedures
B) Practice requesting and following progressive taxi instructions
C) Real-time ATC communication with readback practice
D) A written test on towered airport regulations only
E) Practice identifying and stopping at hold short lines

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Proficiency is built through practice, not testing alone. Reviewing diagrams (A) refreshes airport familiarity. Progressive taxi practice (B) builds the skill of managing unfamiliar routes. Real-time ATC communication (C) develops the ability to process and comply with instructions under workload. Hold short practice (E) reinforces the most safety-critical surface procedure. A written test alone (D) verifies knowledge but doesn't build the skill application that defines proficiency.

### Q-A6-39 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-17)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-2, OD-1

AC 61-83K recommends that recurrent training at towered airports be conducted with an experienced CFI. Why is the "experienced" qualifier important?

A) Less experienced CFIs are not legally allowed to provide recurrent training
B) An experienced CFI can anticipate common errors, provide real-time coaching during actual towered operations, and draw on their own exposure to varied airport environments to enrich the training
C) Experienced CFIs charge more, which motivates the student to pay attention
D) Only CFIs with instrument ratings can operate at towered airports

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The AC specifies an experienced CFI because proficiency training in a towered environment requires more than basic instruction. An experienced CFI has seen the common failure patterns, can coach in real-time without creating additional cockpit workload, and can contextualize the training with examples from diverse airport environments. This experience directly improves the quality of the proficiency training.

### Q-A6-40 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-18)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, CJ-1

A pilot completes a biennial flight review with a CFI at their home non-towered airport. The CFI signs them off as proficient. Two weeks later, the pilot receives a pilot deviation for a runway incursion at a Class C airport they had never visited. What does this suggest about the CFI's proficiency assessment?

A) The CFI's assessment was correct -- the deviation was unforeseeable
B) The proficiency assessment should have included towered airport operations or identified the gap and recommended additional training before the pilot operated in that environment
C) The pilot should have declined the flight to the Class C airport
D) The biennial flight review doesn't cover surface operations

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K emphasizes that proficiency includes towered airport operations. A flight review that doesn't assess or address towered airport skills leaves a known gap. The CFI should have either included towered operations in the review or explicitly identified the pilot's limited towered experience and recommended recurrent training before operating in complex airport environments. Option A ignores predictable risk. Option C puts the burden solely on the pilot. Option D is incorrect -- the BFR should cover all areas where the pilot will operate.

### Q-A6-41 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-18)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, AC-2

A student pilot has completed 15 hours of dual instruction, all at a non-towered airport. The CFI is planning the student's first towered airport visit. Per AC 61-83K's proficiency guidance, what should the CFI do before the flight?

A) Simply fly to the towered airport -- the student will learn by exposure
B) Conduct thorough ground preparation covering towered airport procedures, ATC communication expectations, taxi protocols, and hold short requirements, then fly to the towered airport with active coaching
C) Wait until the student has 40 hours before introducing towered airports
D) Have the student listen to LiveATC recordings for a week, then fly to the towered airport

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Building proficiency requires structured preparation followed by supervised practice. The CFI should ensure the student has the knowledge foundation (ground prep) before entering the towered environment, then provide real-time coaching during the actual experience. Option A skips the preparation phase. Option C delays exposure unnecessarily. Option D provides passive learning without the structured ground instruction or supervised practice that builds proficiency.

### Q-A6-42 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-19)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-2

A Part 61 flight school has CFIs who provide recurrent training but don't specifically include towered airport visits. The chief instructor argues: "Most of our students fly VFR from non-towered fields. Towered airport training isn't relevant." How should this be addressed per AC 61-83K?

A) The chief instructor is correct -- training should match the student's typical operating environment
B) The AC specifically recommends recurrent training at towered airports because GA pilots cause the majority of runway incursions, and pilots who lack towered airport proficiency are at higher risk when they do encounter towered environments
C) Towered airport training should be optional and offered at extra cost
D) The FAA will cite the school if they don't provide towered airport training

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K specifically recommends recurrent training at towered airports with an experienced CFI. The chief instructor's argument assumes pilots will never operate at towered airports, which is unrealistic. When a pilot trained exclusively at non-towered fields encounters a towered environment -- diversion, cross-country, airspace changes -- their lack of proficiency becomes a safety risk. The AC's recommendation anticipates exactly this gap.

### Q-A6-43 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-19)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, ES-1

Which of the following are valid indicators that a pilot's taxi proficiency may be degraded and warrants recurrent training? (Select all that apply)

A) The pilot has not operated at a towered airport in over 12 months
B) The pilot reports feeling uncertain about hold short procedures
C) The pilot recently changed aircraft types with different taxi characteristics
D) The pilot received a NASA ASRS filing acceptance related to a taxi event
E) The pilot consistently flies the same route to the same airports

**Correct:** A, B, C, D, E
**Explanation:** All five indicators suggest potential proficiency gaps. Extended absence from towered airports (A), self-reported uncertainty (B), aircraft type change (C), and prior incidents (D) are direct indicators. Even consistently flying the same routes (E) can degrade proficiency because the pilot doesn't practice adapting to unfamiliar environments. AC 61-83K's emphasis on continuing education applies to all these scenarios -- proficiency is not static and must be actively maintained.

### Q-A6-44 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-20)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, CJ-1

A CFI is providing a flight review for an experienced commercial pilot with 2,000 hours. The pilot says: "I don't need to practice taxiing -- I've been doing it for 15 years." How should the CFI approach this?

A) Agree and skip taxi operations to focus on airwork
B) Explain that proficiency is not permanent -- experienced pilots can develop complacent habits over time, and recurrent training provides an opportunity to identify and correct habits that may have drifted from safe practices
C) Require the pilot to complete a written test on taxi procedures before the flight
D) Cancel the flight review until the pilot has a better attitude

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Experience does not guarantee proficiency. AC 61-83K emphasizes continuing education precisely because habits drift over time. An experienced pilot may have developed shortcuts, incomplete readbacks, or other practices that feel normal but increase incursion risk. The CFI's role is to provide the external perspective that the pilot cannot get from solo practice. Option A enables complacency. Option C addresses knowledge, not skill. Option D is unnecessarily adversarial.

### Q-A6-45 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-20)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-2, OD-2

A flight school wants to implement AC 61-83K's proficiency recommendations systematically. Which approach best achieves this?

A) Add a towered airport visit to every student's training before solo
B) Create a recurring proficiency program that includes initial towered airport training during primary instruction, periodic towered airport visits during continuing education, and structured assessment of taxi operations during every flight review
C) Purchase a simulator with towered airport scenarios
D) Post the AC 61-83K proficiency section on the bulletin board

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K links proficiency to both training and continuing education. A systematic approach addresses proficiency at every stage: initial training introduces towered operations, continuing education maintains those skills, and flight reviews assess current proficiency. Option A covers initial training but not recurrency. Option C provides a supplemental tool but not the structured program. Option D is passive awareness, not active training.
