# A.6.2.5 -- Situational Awareness

---

### Q-A6-76 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-21)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, CJ-1

AC 61-83K states that situational awareness begins with preflight planning. A student asks: "I thought SA was about paying attention during the flight -- what does planning have to do with it?" Which CFI response best reflects the AC's guidance?

A) Agree -- SA is only about real-time awareness during flight
B) Explain that planning creates the mental model against which the pilot compares what they see and hear in real time -- without a plan, the pilot has no baseline to recognize when something is wrong, making true SA impossible
C) SA and planning are separate topics that happen to be in the same section of the AC
D) Planning only matters for IFR flights, not VFR SA

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K explicitly states that SA begins with preflight planning. Planning creates expectations -- which runway to expect, which taxi route is likely, where the hot spots are. During operations, the pilot continuously compares reality against these expectations. A discrepancy triggers recognition that something needs attention. Without the planning baseline, the pilot is reacting to events rather than anticipating them -- which is not SA, it's just response.

### Q-A6-77 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-21)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, AC-2

AC 61-83K emphasizes reframing SA to cover all phases of flight, not just airborne operations. Why is this reframing important for runway incursion prevention?

A) SA during taxi is less important than SA during cruise
B) Most runway incursions happen on the ground, so extending SA to taxi operations, runway crossings, and ground movement ensures pilots are actively monitoring their position and ATC compliance during the phases where incursions actually occur
C) The AC is simply restating what pilots already know
D) SA on the ground is the same skill as SA in the air -- no reframing is needed

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Traditional SA training often focuses on airborne operations -- collision avoidance, weather awareness, navigation. AC 61-83K reframes SA to include surface operations because that's where runway incursions happen. A pilot with excellent airborne SA but no ground SA habit may not actively track their position relative to runways, hold short lines, and other aircraft on the surface. The reframing ensures CFIs teach SA as a continuous discipline across all phases.

### Q-A6-78 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-22)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, RM-1

AC 61-83K emphasizes maintaining a heads-up attitude while taxiing. Which of the following demonstrate proper heads-up SA during taxi? (Select all that apply)

A) Visually scanning for other aircraft, vehicles, and personnel on the taxiway
B) Looking out the window to verify taxiway signs match the expected route
C) Keeping eyes inside the cockpit to monitor engine instruments during taxi
D) Checking for conflicting traffic before crossing any intersection
E) Scanning for runway hold short markings and signs ahead of reaching them

**Correct:** A, B, D, E
**Explanation:** Heads-up attitude means eyes outside the cockpit, actively scanning the airport environment. Scanning for traffic (A), verifying signs (B), checking intersections (D), and anticipating hold short markings (E) all demonstrate active visual SA during taxi. Keeping eyes inside on engine instruments (C) during taxi is heads-down behavior -- engine instruments should be checked briefly, not monitored continuously during taxi, because the primary threat during ground operations is outside the cockpit.

### Q-A6-79 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-22)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, OD-1

Your student is taxiing at a busy Class D airport with their head down, programming the GPS for the next leg. They are approaching a runway intersection. What is the immediate CFI action?

A) Let the student continue -- they'll look up when they hear ATC
B) Direct the student to stop programming, look up, and verify their position relative to the runway intersection before proceeding -- then debrief the heads-down SA failure
C) Take the controls and taxi past the intersection
D) Make a note for the debrief

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K emphasizes heads-up attitude while taxiing. A student with their head down near a runway intersection has completely lost SA -- they don't know their position relative to the runway, can't see conflicting traffic, and may miss a hold short line. The CFI must intervene immediately because the safety risk is imminent. This is a concrete teaching moment: SA requires eyes outside, especially near runways. Options A and D risk an incursion. Option C skips the teaching.

### Q-A6-80 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-23)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, ES-1

A CFI wants to teach SA as a skill that covers all phases of flight, as AC 61-83K recommends. Which approach best achieves this?

A) Teach SA as a separate ground school topic and test it on a written exam
B) Integrate SA training into every phase: during preflight planning (building the mental model), during taxi (heads-up scanning, position tracking), during flight (traffic, airspace, weather), and during taxi-in (maintaining vigilance post-landing)
C) Only teach SA during simulated emergency exercises
D) Tell students to "always stay aware" without specific techniques

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K reframes SA to cover all phases of flight. The most effective teaching approach mirrors this by integrating SA training into every operational phase rather than treating it as a standalone topic. This means teaching planning as the foundation of SA, taxi as a phase requiring active SA, flight as continuous SA, and post-landing taxi as requiring the same SA standard as taxi-out. Option D provides no actionable technique. Options A and C are too narrow.

### Q-A6-81 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-23)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, OD-1

During a night taxi at an unfamiliar airport, your student becomes disoriented and says: "I'm not sure which taxiway we're on." What SA recovery procedure should the CFI teach?

A) Keep taxiing slowly and look for the next sign
B) Stop the aircraft immediately, turn on all available lights, reference the airport diagram to re-establish position, and if still uncertain, contact ground control to confirm position before moving
C) Follow the aircraft ahead -- they probably know where they're going
D) Return to the runway and take off

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Loss of position awareness is a critical SA failure that must be addressed immediately. AC 61-83K emphasizes that SA begins with planning and is maintained through heads-up awareness. When SA is lost, the first step is to stop -- continuing to move while disoriented increases incursion risk. Then re-establish position using the diagram and visual cues. If still uncertain, ATC is the definitive source. Option A continues movement without SA. Options C and D are unsafe or impractical.

### Q-A6-82 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-24)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-2, AC-1

How does preflight planning specifically build the SA foundation needed to prevent runway incursions? (Select all that apply)

A) Reviewing the airport diagram creates a mental map of the taxi environment
B) Checking NOTAMs reveals changes that might not match the pilot's prior experience
C) Identifying expected runways and routes creates expectations that help detect discrepancies with ATC instructions
D) Reviewing hot spots alerts the pilot to areas requiring extra vigilance
E) Planning the fuel load for the return flight

**Correct:** A, B, C, D
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K states SA begins with preflight planning. Each planning element builds a specific SA capability: the mental map (A) enables position tracking during taxi, NOTAMs (B) update the mental model to match current conditions, expected routes (C) create a comparison baseline for ATC instructions, and hot spots (D) prime the pilot for areas requiring heightened attention. Fuel planning (E) is important for flight safety but doesn't build surface SA.

### Q-A6-83 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-24)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, OD-2

A student is taxiing correctly on taxiway Bravo toward runway 27. Without prompting, the student says: "We're on Bravo, approaching the intersection with Charlie. I see the runway 27 hold short line ahead. I expect we'll hold here until cleared for takeoff." What does this demonstrate?

A) The student is talking too much and violating sterile cockpit
B) The student is demonstrating excellent situational awareness -- verbalizing position, upcoming features, and expected actions, which shows active mental tracking of the taxi environment
C) The student memorized the taxi route and is reciting it
D) The student should wait for the CFI to ask questions

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This student is demonstrating exactly the SA that AC 61-83K describes -- active awareness of position, upcoming features, and expected actions. Verbalizing this information externally confirms the student's internal mental model is accurate and current. This is not sterile cockpit violation because the verbalization is flight-relevant -- it's position reporting and anticipation. This is the behavior CFIs should develop in students: continuous, active, verbalized SA during taxi.

### Q-A6-84 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-25)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-1, ES-2

A flight school's incident data shows that most taxi-related deviations occur during taxi-in after landing, not during taxi-out. What does this suggest about how SA is being taught?

A) SA training is working well -- taxi-out incidents are low
B) SA training likely emphasizes taxi-out (when pilots are fresh and focused) but doesn't adequately address maintaining SA during taxi-in, when pilots are fatigued, relieved the flight is over, and more likely to drop their guard
C) Taxi-in deviations are less serious and can be ignored
D) The airport layout is only confusing in one direction

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K reframes SA to cover all phases of flight, including post-landing taxi. A pattern of taxi-in deviations suggests pilots are dropping SA after landing -- a natural tendency when the "hard part" feels over. The CFI must teach that SA is required continuously until the aircraft is parked and secured. The post-landing phase is particularly vulnerable because fatigue, relief, and rushing to park all degrade vigilance. This is exactly the kind of phase-specific SA gap the AC's reframing is designed to address.

### Q-A6-85 (Module 3, Pool A6-M3-25)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, OD-2

A CFI is teaching a student about maintaining SA during taxi. The student asks: "What's the most important thing to look for while taxiing?" Which answer best reflects AC 61-83K's SA guidance?

A) Other aircraft on the taxiway
B) There is no single most important thing -- heads-up SA during taxi requires scanning for runway hold short markings, taxiway signs, other aircraft and vehicles, your position on the airport diagram, and ATC instructions, all continuously
C) The centerline of the taxiway
D) The airspeed indicator to make sure you're not taxiing too fast

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K's heads-up attitude guidance is about comprehensive scanning, not fixation on a single element. SA during taxi requires monitoring multiple information sources simultaneously: visual scanning for markings, signs, and traffic; position tracking on the diagram; and auditory monitoring of ATC. Fixating on any single element (other aircraft, centerline, airspeed) creates a blind spot for the others. The CFI should teach a systematic scan that covers all surface SA elements.
