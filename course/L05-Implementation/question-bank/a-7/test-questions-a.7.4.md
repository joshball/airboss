# A.7.4 -- WINGS Subjects and Delivery

Questions covering accident-data-derived emphasis areas, seminars/webinars, rolling phases, online recordkeeping, and flight review substitution.

---

### Q-A7-47 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-24)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, PS-2

A pilot asks how the FAA decides which topics to include in WINGS activities. According to AC 61-83K, what drives the selection of WINGS subjects?

A) WINGS topics are chosen randomly each year by FAA headquarters
B) WINGS subjects target specific emphasis areas derived from accident data
C) Topics are selected based on pilot surveys conducted at annual airshows
D) Each FSDO independently selects topics based on local weather patterns

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, "WINGS subjects target specific emphasis areas derived from accident data." This data-driven approach ensures that the training addresses the actual causes of accidents rather than arbitrary or anecdotal topics. Options A, C, and D describe selection methods not supported by the AC.

### Q-A7-48 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-24)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-2

You are selecting a WINGS knowledge activity for a private pilot client. You notice that the available topics focus on loss of control, controlled flight into terrain, and runway excursions. Why do these specific topics appear in the WINGS curriculum?

A) They are the most popular topics requested by WINGS participants
B) They are emphasis areas derived from accident data -- the FAA targets WINGS subjects at the actual causes of accidents
C) They are the only topics the FAA has developed training materials for
D) They were selected because they are the easiest topics to teach in a seminar format

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, WINGS subjects "target specific emphasis areas derived from accident data." Loss of control, CFIT, and runway excursions are persistent accident categories identified through NTSB and FAA data analysis. The WINGS program deliberately focuses on these areas to address real-world safety risks, not popularity (A), material availability (C), or ease of instruction (D).

### Q-A7-49 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-25)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** PS-2, AV-1

According to AC 61-83K A.7.4, through which methods are WINGS knowledge activities delivered? (Select all that apply)

A) Seminars
B) Webinars
C) Mandatory flight school attendance
D) University-accredited college courses exclusively
E) Online through faasafety.gov

**Correct:** A, B, E
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, WINGS knowledge activities are "delivered through seminars/webinars," and A.7.2 establishes faasafety.gov as the prime education tool with online courses. WINGS participation is voluntary, not mandatory through flight schools (C). While some activities may have educational institution connections, the program is not limited to university courses (D).

### Q-A7-50 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-25)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-1, PS-2

A pilot who completed a WINGS phase last year asks whether they need to start over from the beginning. According to AC 61-83K, how does WINGS phase progression work?

A) Pilots must restart from Basic level every 24 calendar months
B) Pilots attain and maintain phases on a rolling basis -- progression is continuous, not reset periodically
C) Each phase expires after exactly 12 months and must be repeated
D) Once a pilot completes all three levels, the program is finished permanently

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, "Pilots attain and maintain phases on a rolling basis." This means WINGS is designed for continuous engagement rather than periodic resets (A, C) or one-time completion (D). The rolling structure encourages ongoing proficiency development as part of a pilot's regular flying activities.

### Q-A7-51 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-26)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-2, OD-1

A pilot completing WINGS activities wants to know how their progress is tracked. According to AC 61-83K, what recordkeeping system does WINGS use?

A) Paper logbook entries verified by the local FSDO
B) Online recordkeeping through the FAASTeam website
C) Handwritten forms mailed to FAA headquarters in Oklahoma City
D) No formal recordkeeping -- pilots self-report their completions verbally

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, WINGS uses "online recordkeeping." This is managed through faasafety.gov, where both knowledge and flight activities are documented electronically. Paper forms (A, C) and informal verbal reporting (D) are not the designated recordkeeping methods for the WINGS program.

### Q-A7-52 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-26)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** PS-2, AV-2

Which of the following are features of the WINGS program's structure and delivery as described in AC 61-83K A.7.4? (Select all that apply)

A) Subjects target emphasis areas derived from accident data
B) Knowledge activities are delivered through seminars and webinars
C) Pilots progress through phases on a rolling basis
D) All recordkeeping is done online
E) WINGS phases must be completed in strict calendar-year windows

**Correct:** A, B, C, D
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, WINGS subjects target accident-data-derived emphasis areas (A), are delivered through seminars/webinars (B), progress on a rolling basis (C), and use online recordkeeping (D). Option E is incorrect -- the rolling basis specifically means there are no strict calendar-year deadlines. Pilots work through phases at their own pace.

### Q-A7-53 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-27)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-3, PS-2

A certificated pilot who has been putting off their flight review says they'll "just do a WINGS phase instead since it's easier." Is their assumption about WINGS and flight reviews correct?

A) No -- WINGS is more rigorous than a flight review, not easier, because it requires both knowledge and flight activities targeting specific proficiency areas
B) Yes -- WINGS phases are a simplified version of the flight review
C) No -- WINGS cannot substitute for a flight review under any circumstances
D) Yes -- WINGS only requires ground activities, making it faster than a flight review

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, "Achievement of any WINGS level substitutes for flight review," but WINGS is a structured proficiency program requiring both knowledge and flight activities -- it is not a shortcut. Option B mischaracterizes the program's rigor. Option C is directly contradicted by the AC. Option D is incorrect because WINGS requires both knowledge and flight components.

### Q-A7-54 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-27)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-2, CJ-1

Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, which statement about WINGS and the flight review requirement is accurate?

A) Only the Master level of WINGS satisfies the flight review requirement
B) Achievement of any WINGS level -- Basic, Advanced, or Master -- substitutes for the flight review required by 14 CFR 61.56
C) WINGS substitutes for the ground portion of a flight review but not the flight portion
D) WINGS phases only count toward flight review if completed within 90 days of the review due date

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, "Achievement of any WINGS level substitutes for flight review." This applies to all three levels -- Basic, Advanced, and Master -- not just Master (A). The substitution covers the entire flight review requirement, not just ground (C). There is no 90-day proximity requirement (D). This benefit is one of the strongest incentives for pilot participation in WINGS.

### Q-A7-55 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-28)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, CJ-2

You notice that current WINGS emphasis areas focus heavily on maneuvering flight accidents. A pilot asks why WINGS doesn't cover more "fun" topics like formation flying or aerobatic competition. What is the best explanation?

A) The FAA prohibits WINGS from covering any advanced flying topics
B) WINGS subjects are specifically derived from accident data, so emphasis areas reflect the actual causes of accidents -- maneuvering flight is targeted because it appears prominently in accident statistics
C) Formation flying and aerobatics are covered but only at the Master level
D) The FAA simply hasn't gotten around to developing those topics yet

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, "WINGS subjects target specific emphasis areas derived from accident data." The program deliberately focuses resources on the flying activities and scenarios that cause the most accidents. If maneuvering flight dominates accident statistics, it receives emphasis. This data-driven approach ensures maximum safety impact rather than catering to entertainment preferences. The FAA does not prohibit advanced topics (A), and the issue is deliberate prioritization, not oversight (D).

### Q-A7-56 (Module 5, Pool A7-M5-28)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** PS-2, ES-1

A CFI wants to promote the WINGS program to their certificated pilot clients. Based on AC 61-83K A.7.4, which benefits can they accurately highlight? (Select all that apply)

A) WINGS activities address real-world safety risks because subjects are derived from accident data
B) Completing any WINGS level satisfies the flight review requirement
C) Progress is tracked through online recordkeeping, making it easy to document completions
D) WINGS phases are attained and maintained on a rolling basis, allowing flexible scheduling
E) WINGS participation exempts pilots from all future FAA medical requirements

**Correct:** A, B, C, D
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.7.4, WINGS subjects target accident-data emphasis areas (A), any level substitutes for flight review (B), recordkeeping is online (C), and phases progress on a rolling basis (D). Option E is entirely fabricated -- WINGS participation has no effect on medical certification requirements, which are governed by 14 CFR Part 67 and BasicMed provisions.
