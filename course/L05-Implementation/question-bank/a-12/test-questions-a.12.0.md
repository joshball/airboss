### Q-A12-01 (Module 1, Pool A12-M1-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-3

What is the fundamental difference between the ACS (Airman Certification Standards) and the PTS (Practical Test Standards) it replaced?

A) The ACS added oral exam questions to the practical test
B) The ACS integrates knowledge, risk management, and skill elements into a single framework, whereas PTS primarily addressed flight skill standards and tolerances
C) The ACS removed numerical tolerances from practical test evaluation
D) The ACS only applies to instrument-rated pilots

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The key innovation of the ACS is integration. PTS focused primarily on flight skill tolerances (altitude +/-100 feet, heading +/-10 degrees). ACS adds explicit knowledge and risk management elements alongside skill elements for each Task, creating a three-dimensional evaluation framework. This aligns testing with how pilots actually operate -- knowledge, judgment, and skill together. Option A mischaracterizes the change. Option C is incorrect -- ACS retains tolerances. Option D is wrong -- ACS applies to all certificates where published.

### Q-A12-02 (Module 1, Pool A12-M1-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-3

A student preparing for a private pilot checkride asks why they need to know about risk management -- "Isn't the checkride just about flying the maneuvers?" How should you explain the ACS approach?

A) Risk management is only tested during the oral portion, not during flight
B) Under the ACS, every Task has knowledge, risk management, and skill elements -- the DPE evaluates how you think about risk alongside how you fly, because judgment and skill are both necessary for safe operation
C) Risk management is a separate section of the ACS that's tested after the flight maneuvers
D) The DPE won't test risk management for a private pilot -- that's for commercial and ATP

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The ACS framework integrates risk management into every Task. A student performing steep turns, for example, may be evaluated on their knowledge of aerodynamics (knowledge element), their recognition of when conditions make steep turns unsafe (risk management element), and their execution to tolerances (skill element). This integration reflects real-world flying where these dimensions are inseparable. Options A, C, and D all incorrectly separate or limit risk management evaluation.

### Q-A12-03 (Module 1, Pool A12-M1-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-3, CJ-3

During a post-flight debrief, you want to use the ACS framework to explain a student's traffic pattern errors. The student consistently flew high and fast on downwind. How would you structure the debrief using ACS dimensions?

A) Focus on the skill dimension: "You were 200 feet high and 15 knots fast. Fix those numbers."
B) Address all three dimensions: knowledge (why the pattern altitude and speed matter), risk management (what the consequences of being high and fast are on the approach), and skill (specific control inputs and sight picture to correct)
C) Focus on the knowledge dimension: quiz the student on pattern altitudes and airspeeds
D) Assign ground study on traffic patterns before the next flight

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The ACS framework is a powerful debrief tool when applied across all three dimensions. A student who is high and fast may have a knowledge gap (doesn't know the correct numbers), a skill gap (knows the numbers but can't achieve them), or both. Adding the risk management dimension -- what happens when you're high and fast on downwind (steep/fast approach, overshoot, go-around pressure) -- connects the error to real consequences. Option A treats only the symptom. Option C addresses only knowledge. Option D delays rather than teaches.

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** ES-3

Under the ACS framework, which of the following are elements that a DPE evaluates for each Task during a practical test? (Select all that apply)

A) Knowledge elements -- can the applicant explain the relevant principles and procedures?
B) Risk management elements -- can the applicant identify and mitigate risks associated with the Task?
C) Skill elements -- can the applicant perform the maneuver to prescribed tolerances?
D) Teaching elements -- can the applicant explain how they would teach this Task to a student?
E) Time elements -- can the applicant complete the Task within a specified time limit?

**Correct:** A, B, C

Time limits (E) are not an ACS element -- tasks are evaluated on quality, not speed.

Option D overstates the rule -- both ACS and PTS documents were incorporated, not just ACS.
