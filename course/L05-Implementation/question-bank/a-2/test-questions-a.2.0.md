### Q-A2-01 (Module 4, Pool A2-M4-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-3

You are planning a VFR cross-country training flight that routes near a Prohibited Area. Your student says "We can fly through it if we stay below 3,000 feet." What should you teach?

A) Prohibited Areas have altitude limits, and flying below them is legal if you remain in Class G airspace
B) Prohibited Areas extend from the surface to their published altitude and may never be entered without permission, regardless of altitude
C) Prohibited Areas are only active during published hours, so check the times first
D) You can contact the controlling agency for permission to transit a Prohibited Area

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Prohibited Areas (14 CFR 73.83) may not be entered without specific authorization. Unlike Restricted Areas, there is no routine procedure for pilots to obtain permission to enter -- they exist because flight within them is prohibited for national security or welfare reasons. The student's assumption about altitude limits reveals a confusion between Prohibited and Restricted airspace. Option A is incorrect -- Prohibited Areas typically extend from the surface. Option C confuses Prohibited with Restricted (which have published active times). Option D describes the procedure for Restricted Areas, not Prohibited.

### Q-A2-02 (Module 4, Pool A2-M4-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-3, RC-4

During cross-country planning, your student's route passes through a TFR that was issued 6 hours ago. The student's preflight briefing was obtained yesterday. The student says "The briefer didn't mention any TFRs on this route." What is the most important teaching point?

A) File a complaint with FSS about the incomplete briefing
B) TFRs can be issued at any time, and the pilot's responsibility to check for current NOTAMs doesn't end with a single briefing
C) TFRs are only issued for presidential movements, so this is unusual
D) VFR pilots are not required to check NOTAMs before flight

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** TFRs are dynamic and can be issued with short notice. 14 CFR 91.103 requires the PIC to become familiar with "all available information" before flight. A preflight briefing from the day before does not satisfy this requirement when NOTAMs may have changed. The teaching moment is about the continuous nature of preflight responsibility. Option A deflects PIC responsibility. Option C is factually wrong -- TFRs cover many events. Option D is flatly incorrect per 14 CFR 91.103.

### Q-A2-03 (Module 4, Pool A2-M4-02)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** OD-3

If intercepted by military aircraft, a pilot should take which of the following actions? (Select all that apply)

A) Follow the intercepting aircraft's visual signals and comply with instructions
B) Squawk 7700 on the transponder
C) Attempt to contact the intercepting aircraft on 121.5 MHz
D) Maintain current heading and altitude until the intercepting aircraft departs
E) Turn off the transponder to avoid being tracked

**Correct:** A, B, C
**Explanation:** Per AIM 5-6-2, a pilot being intercepted should follow the interceptor's signals (A), squawk 7700 to declare the emergency/interception (B), and attempt contact on 121.5 MHz (C). Option D is dangerous -- ignoring intercept signals can escalate the situation to use of force. Option E is the opposite of correct procedure and would increase the perceived threat.

### Q-A2-04 (Module 4, Pool A2-M4-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-2, OD-3

Your student is flying VFR near the Washington DC SFRA. The student asks, "Do we need to do anything special since we're just passing nearby?" What is the correct response?

A) No special procedures are needed as long as you stay outside the SFRA boundary
B) You must have an active flight plan, a discrete transponder code, and be in two-way radio contact with ATC before entering the SFRA, but none of that applies if you stay outside
C) The SFRA requires all aircraft within 60 nm of DCA to follow special procedures regardless of whether they enter the SFRA
D) Only IFR traffic needs to follow SFRA procedures

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The DC SFRA (14 CFR 93.339-93.343) requires specific procedures: a filed flight plan, discrete transponder code, and ATC communication. However, these apply only when operating within the SFRA boundaries. Outside the SFRA, normal airspace rules apply. The CFI should ensure the student understands the SFRA boundaries and entry requirements, and plans the route to either comply or stay clear. Option C overstates the geographic extent. Option D is incorrect -- VFR and IFR traffic both must comply within the SFRA.

### Q-A2-06 (Module 4, Pool A2-M4-04)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-2, AV-3

A student asks you to explain the difference between the Washington DC Flight Restricted Zone (FRZ) and the DC Special Flight Rules Area (SFRA). What is the most accurate distinction?

A) The FRZ is the inner ring (approximately 15 nm radius around DCA) and the SFRA is the outer ring (approximately 30 nm radius); the FRZ has more restrictive entry requirements
B) The FRZ and SFRA are the same airspace -- "FRZ" is just the older name
C) The FRZ is only for IFR traffic while the SFRA handles VFR traffic
D) The FRZ applies only during heightened security alerts while the SFRA is permanent

**Correct:** A
**Explanation:** Per 14 CFR 93.335-93.343, the DC SFRA is approximately 30 nm in radius around DCA, while the FRZ is the much more restrictive inner ring of approximately 15 nm. Entry into the FRZ requires specific authorization (approved gateway airports, background checks, and prior approval) beyond the SFRA requirements. A CFI should teach students that these are nested, distinct airspace areas with different levels of restriction. Option B is wrong -- they are separate designations. Option C is wrong -- both handle VFR and IFR. Option D is wrong -- both are permanent post-9/11 airspace.

### Q-A2-07 (Module 4, Pool A2-M4-04)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-3, RC-3

During an election year, you are briefing a student for a cross-country flight. The student completed route planning two days ago using a sectional chart and checked NOTAMs at the time. What is the most important guidance regarding "floating" TFRs?

A) Election-year TFRs are published in the Federal Register at least 72 hours in advance, so the student's two-day-old briefing is adequate
B) "Floating" TFRs can appear with very short notice and may move with a candidate's itinerary; the student must re-check NOTAMs immediately before departure and consider checking again en route
C) TFRs during election years only affect airports where candidates are landing, so en route segments are not affected
D) The FAA suspends TFR enforcement during election years due to the high volume of temporary restrictions

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K specifically identifies "floating" TFRs during election years as a significant challenge. These TFRs follow candidates and can be issued with minimal notice, making preflight NOTAMs from two days ago unreliable. The CFI must teach that NOTAM checking is a continuous process, especially during election season. Option A is wrong -- TFRs can appear with much shorter notice. Option C is wrong -- TFRs create cylinders of restricted airspace that affect all traffic within their boundaries, not just airport traffic. Option D is completely false -- enforcement is always active.

A student inadvertently enters a TFR. As the CFI, which of the following consequences should you have previously taught the student to expect? (Select all that apply)

B) Certificate suspension or revocation proceedings
C) A verbal warning with no further action, since first-time violations are forgiven
D) Potential involvement of federal law enforcement and national security agencies
E) Civil penalties up to tens of thousands of dollars

**Correct:** A, B, D, E

### Q-A2-11 (Module 4, Pool A2-M4-06)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-3, RC-2

You are teaching a student about the difference between Restricted Areas and Prohibited Areas. Which statement best captures the operational distinction a pilot needs to understand?

A) Restricted Areas are permanently closed; Prohibited Areas have scheduled hours of operation
B) Restricted Areas may be entered with permission from the controlling agency when not active, while Prohibited Areas generally may never be entered without specific authorization
C) Both types allow transit with ATC clearance; the distinction is only relevant to IFR traffic
D) Restricted Areas contain hazardous activities but allow VFR overflight above the published ceiling; Prohibited Areas have no altitude limit

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The key operational distinction is access: Restricted Areas (14 CFR 73.3) have controlling agencies that can grant access, and when they are not "hot" (active), ATC may route traffic through them. Prohibited Areas (14 CFR 73.83) exist for national security or welfare reasons and generally cannot be entered without specific authorization. Option A reverses the definitions. Option C is wrong -- VFR pilots must also comply. Option D is misleading -- while Restricted Areas do have published altitudes, the defining difference is the authorization pathway, not altitude limits.

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** OD-3, RM-2

You are teaching a cross-country planning lesson. Your student's route passes through airspace that was a Restricted Area last month but the student's sectional chart, printed 6 months ago, shows it as active. The student checks NOTAMs and finds the area has been converted to a MOA. What should you teach about relying on chart information for SUA?

A) Sectional charts are the authoritative source for SUA boundaries and status; if the chart shows Restricted, treat it as Restricted
B) SUA status can change between chart publication cycles; pilots must cross-reference NOTAMs, the Chart Supplement, and current advisories to verify the current status of any SUA along their route
C) Once a Restricted Area is converted to a MOA, it no longer appears on any chart and can be ignored
D) SUA conversions only take effect when the next sectional chart edition is published

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** SUA designations can change between the 56-day sectional chart cycle through NOTAMs and regulatory action. A CFI must teach students that charts are a starting point, not the final word -- current NOTAMs, the Chart Supplement (formerly AF/D), and FSS briefings provide the latest information. This connects to AC 61-83K's emphasis on staying current with changing airspace. Option A places too much reliance on potentially outdated charts. Option C is wrong -- the area still appears on charts until republication. Option D is wrong -- changes take effect per their published effective date.
