# A.11.3 -- Departure

Flight instructors should provide training ensuring pilots depart in coordinated flight at VY.

---

### Q-A11-37 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-19)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-2, CJ-1

A student departs runway 27 in a single-engine trainer on a calm day. After liftoff, the student pitches for VX instead of VY and allows the ball to drift one diameter to the left. The aircraft is climbing but slowly. What LOC risk is developing?

A) No risk -- the aircraft is climbing and the ball is only slightly off center
B) The combination of VX (near-stall speed margin), uncoordinated flight, and high angle of attack creates a departure LOC setup. If any disturbance (gust, engine hiccup, control input error) occurs, the aircraft has minimal energy to recover
C) The student should pitch for VX because there are obstacles ahead
D) The ball position doesn't matter during climb

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.3, departure should be in coordinated flight at VY. VX provides a lower speed margin above stall, and uncoordinated flight means one wing is producing more lift than the other. At high angle of attack, any asymmetric condition increases the risk of a departure stall with wing drop. VY provides better speed margin, better engine cooling, and more energy for recovery. Option A dismisses the compounding risk factors. Option C may be valid for obstacle clearance but the scenario specifies calm day with no mention of obstacles. Option D is dangerously wrong.

### Q-A11-38 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-19)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-2, AC-1

Which of the following are reasons a CFI should emphasize VY rather than VX for normal departures when teaching LOC prevention? (Select all that apply)

A) VY provides a greater margin above stall speed than VX
B) VY results in better forward visibility due to a lower nose attitude
C) VY provides better engine cooling during the climb
D) VY is always the maximum speed the aircraft can achieve
E) VY provides more kinetic energy available to convert to altitude or maneuver if needed

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** VY (best rate of climb) provides multiple safety advantages over VX for normal departures. Greater stall margin (A) means more tolerance for gusts or pilot error. Lower nose attitude (B) improves traffic and obstacle visibility. Better engine cooling (C) reduces the risk of power loss during climb. More kinetic energy (E) gives the pilot options if something goes wrong. Option D is incorrect -- VY is the speed for best rate of climb, not maximum speed.

### Q-A11-39 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-20)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, AC-3

During a departure in a high-performance single-engine aircraft, the pilot fails to apply right rudder. The aircraft yaws left, the left wing drops slightly, and the pilot instinctively adds left aileron to level the wings without correcting the yaw. What is happening aerodynamically and what is the LOC risk?

A) The pilot is correctly leveling the wings -- the yaw will self-correct
B) The pilot is creating adverse yaw: the left aileron-up on the right wing increases drag on the right, worsening the yaw left. Combined with the high power, high AOA departure configuration, this cross-controlled condition is the setup for a departure spin
C) The aircraft will simply fly in a gentle left turn with no additional risk
D) Right rudder is only needed in cruise flight

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This scenario illustrates the departure LOC chain: high power without rudder correction causes yaw, pilot applies aileron without rudder (cross-controlled), adverse yaw compounds the problem, and the high-AOA departure configuration means the aircraft is near the stall boundary where a cross-controlled condition can produce a spin entry. Per AC 61-83K A.11.3, coordinated flight at VY is the emphasis because it prevents exactly this chain. CFIs must teach that rudder leads in departure -- aileron alone in a yaw makes it worse.

### Q-A11-40 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-20)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, RM-1

A CFI is teaching a student the departure procedure at a short runway (2,500 feet) surrounded by 50-foot trees. The student asks whether to climb at VX or VY after clearing the trees. What is the correct guidance?

A) Maintain VX until reaching pattern altitude for maximum obstacle clearance
B) Climb at VX until clear of obstacles, then transition to VY for the remainder of the climb -- VY provides better speed margin, engine cooling, and energy management once obstacles are no longer a factor
C) Climb at VY from the start -- obstacle clearance is not the pilot's concern
D) Climb at the maximum speed the aircraft can achieve

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.3, the default departure should be at VY. However, obstacle clearance is the one valid reason to use VX. The correct technique is VX until clear of obstacles, then transition to VY. Prolonged VX climbs unnecessarily maintain the aircraft in the low-speed, high-AOA regime where LOC risk is highest. Transitioning to VY once obstacles are cleared restores speed margin and reduces LOC risk. Option A keeps the aircraft in the high-risk regime unnecessarily. Option C ignores a real obstacle environment.

### Q-A11-41 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-21)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, AC-2

A student takes off and immediately begins a left turn to follow a noise abatement departure procedure. The turn starts at 200 feet AGL with the aircraft still accelerating through VY. What should the CFI emphasize about this maneuver?

A) Noise abatement turns should begin as early as possible to minimize noise over residences
B) Turning at 200 feet AGL during the initial climb introduces bank angle and load factor at the phase of flight with the least margin for error. The aircraft should be established in a stable climb at VY before initiating any turns, and turns below 500 feet AGL should be minimized
C) The turn is fine as long as the bank angle doesn't exceed 45 degrees
D) Departing traffic always has the right of way, so the turn is safe

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The initial climb is the highest-risk phase for departure LOC. Bank angle increases load factor, which increases stall speed. At 200 feet AGL, the pilot has minimal altitude for recovery if a stall develops during the turn. Per AC 61-83K A.11.3, CFIs should ensure pilots depart in coordinated flight at VY. Adding a turn before the climb is established and before reaching a safe altitude compresses the available safety margin. Noise abatement is important but should not override the fundamental principle of establishing a safe climb first.

### Q-A11-42 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-21)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-2, CJ-2

A CFI is preparing a student for a first solo. Which departure-related LOC risk factors should the CFI brief before the student departs? (Select all that apply)

A) The aircraft will climb faster without the CFI's weight, requiring the student to adjust pitch attitude
B) Right rudder requirements may differ with the lighter aircraft weight
C) The student should maintain coordinated flight and target VY
D) The student should practice steep turns during the climb to build confidence
E) If anything feels wrong during the departure, the student should continue climbing straight ahead to pattern altitude before troubleshooting

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** First solo departure briefing must address the changed aircraft performance (A, B), reinforce the VY/coordination fundamentals (C), and establish the critical rule that the initial climb is not the time to troubleshoot -- climb first, then assess (E). The lighter aircraft will perform differently than the student has experienced, and the CFI must prepare them for that. Option D is dangerous -- steep turns during departure climb at low altitude are a LOC setup, not a confidence builder.

### Q-A11-43 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-22)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-3, RM-1

An experienced pilot transitioning to a new aircraft type departs at VY per the POH. However, the POH VY is for gross weight, and the aircraft is lightly loaded. What does the CFI need to teach about this?

A) Always fly POH VY regardless of weight -- the book number is always correct
B) VY decreases with weight. At light weights, the POH gross-weight VY provides extra margin above actual VY. The CFI should explain this relationship so the pilot understands they have additional energy available, and can adjust if needed for specific operations
C) VY increases with lighter weight, so the pilot should fly faster
D) Weight has no effect on VY

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** VY (and VX) decrease with decreasing weight because the aircraft requires less lift at lighter weights, which means a lower AOA and lower speed for optimum climb. Flying the gross-weight VY in a lightly loaded aircraft actually provides additional speed margin above the actual VY, which is a safety benefit. The CFI should teach the pilot to understand this relationship rather than blindly following one number. Option A works safely (conservative) but misses the teaching opportunity. Option C reverses the relationship. Option D is factually wrong.

### Q-A11-44 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-22)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, AC-1

During a departure on a hot day at a high-elevation airport, a student notices the aircraft is climbing very slowly at the normal VY pitch attitude. The student pushes the nose down to gain more airspeed. What is the CFI teaching opportunity here?

A) The student is correct -- more airspeed is always better during climb
B) The student should understand that VY as an indicated airspeed doesn't change with density altitude, but the rate of climb decreases. Pushing the nose down accelerates past VY and actually reduces climb rate further. The correct response to poor climb performance is to maintain VY and accept the reduced rate, or to abort if terrain clearance is in doubt
C) The student should pull back to VX for maximum altitude gain
D) Hot and high departures perform the same as sea level if the engine is running properly

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This is a common misunderstanding that leads to departure LOC. At high density altitude, the aircraft climbs slowly, and an anxious pilot may try to "help" by gaining speed. But VY (indicated) is VY regardless of density altitude -- flying faster than VY reduces climb rate. The CFI should teach that reduced climb performance at high DA is normal, not a sign that the pilot needs to do something different with pitch. The decision point is before departure: can the aircraft climb adequately for the departure environment? If not, reduce weight, wait for cooler temps, or don't go.

### Q-A11-45 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-23)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, AC-2

A flight school operates at an airport with a departure procedure that includes a turn at 400 feet AGL. A CFI notices several students have had difficulty maintaining coordination during this turn. What is the best systemic response?

A) Practice the turn more until students get it right
B) Evaluate whether the departure procedure itself creates unnecessary LOC risk, and if so, propose a modified procedure that delays the turn to a safer altitude or reduces the required bank angle -- fix the system, not just the symptoms
C) Only allow advanced students to use this departure
D) Add a warning sign at the runway

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** When multiple students have difficulty with the same maneuver, the problem may be systemic rather than individual. A required turn at 400 feet AGL during departure is exactly the kind of low-altitude maneuvering that AC 61-83K A.11.3 warns against. The CFI should evaluate whether the procedure can be modified to provide more margin -- a higher turn altitude, a shallower bank, or a different routing. Option A addresses the symptom through repetition. Option C restricts the problem rather than solving it.

### Q-A11-46 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-23)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** CJ-1, AC-3

A CFI is teaching departure procedures to a new private pilot student. Which of the following elements should be included in every departure briefing to mitigate LOC risk? (Select all that apply)

A) Target climb speed (VY for normal departure, VX only if required for obstacle clearance)
B) Rudder inputs required to maintain coordination during the high-power climb
C) The planned initial heading and the altitude at which any turns will be initiated
D) The radio frequency for departure control
E) Emergency plan if the engine fails during the initial climb (straight ahead, or limited turn options based on altitude)

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** Every departure briefing should address the LOC-critical elements: climb speed (A) to maintain adequate margin, coordination inputs (B) to prevent yaw-induced stalls, turn planning (C) to avoid maneuvering at unsafe altitudes, and the engine failure plan (E) to prevent panic-driven decisions at the worst possible time. Option D is an operational detail that doesn't directly address LOC risk during departure. The briefing should establish the mental model for a safe departure before the aircraft leaves the ground.
