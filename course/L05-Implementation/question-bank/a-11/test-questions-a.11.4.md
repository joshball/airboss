# A.11.4 -- Return to Field / Engine Failure on Takeoff

Flight instructors should demonstrate and train the "impossible turn" decision-making.

---

### Q-A11-47 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-24)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-2

A student experiences an engine failure at 400 feet AGL after takeoff. The student immediately begins a turn back to the runway. What is the primary risk of this decision?

A) The student will arrive back at the runway too high
B) At 400 feet AGL, the aircraft likely does not have sufficient altitude and energy to complete a 180-degree turn plus alignment with the runway. The turn requires bank angle that increases stall speed, and the descending turn at low altitude leaves no margin for a stall/spin if the pilot misjudges
C) The runway may be occupied by other traffic
D) The engine may restart during the turn

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.4, the "impossible turn" is called that because at low altitudes it often is impossible. The turn back requires a descending 180+ degree turn (often more, accounting for wind). Bank angle increases load factor and stall speed. The pilot is already in a high-stress, low-energy state. If the pilot pulls too hard in the turn or allows the bank to steepen, the result is a low-altitude stall/spin with no altitude to recover. The pre-briefed plan for engine failure below a determined altitude should be to land approximately straight ahead.

### Q-A11-48 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-24)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AC-2, RM-1

Which of the following factors determine whether a return-to-field turnback is feasible after an engine failure on departure? (Select all that apply)

A) The altitude at which the engine fails -- higher means more energy and time available
B) The wind direction and speed -- a headwind on departure means a tailwind on return, increasing groundspeed and the distance needed
C) The aircraft's glide ratio and current airspeed at the time of failure
D) The pilot's emotional state at the time of the failure
E) The bank angle the pilot can maintain while keeping airspeed above stall

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** The feasibility of the turnback is a physics problem: altitude available for the turn (A), wind effects on ground track and groundspeed (B), glide performance (C), and the ability to maintain a coordinated turn above stall speed (E). These are the factors that should be analyzed in advance and briefed as part of the departure plan. Option D -- emotional state -- is real but is addressed by having a pre-briefed plan, not by making it a go/no-go factor in the moment. The point of the pre-briefed plan is to remove decision-making from the high-stress moment.

### Q-A11-49 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-25)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, CJ-1

A CFI is teaching the "impossible turn" to a private pilot student. The CFI says: "We're going to practice this at altitude first. I'll pull the power at 3,000 AGL, and you'll execute the turnback maneuver. We'll measure the altitude lost in the turn to establish your personal minimum altitude for attempting a turnback at this airport." What is this CFI doing correctly?

A) Nothing -- the impossible turn should never be practiced
B) Establishing an evidence-based personal minimum by measuring actual altitude loss in a simulated turnback, at a safe altitude, to replace guesswork with data. This allows the student to make a pre-briefed decision rather than an improvised one
C) The CFI should practice this in the pattern, not at altitude
D) The altitude loss measured at 3,000 AGL won't match the altitude loss at pattern altitude

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Per AC 61-83K A.11.4, CFIs should demonstrate and train the impossible turn decision-making. Practicing at altitude establishes the real altitude loss for a given pilot in a given aircraft, which becomes the basis for the pre-briefed minimum altitude for attempting a turnback. This converts the turnback from an emotional, in-the-moment decision to a data-driven, pre-briefed plan: "If the engine fails below X feet, I land ahead. Above X feet, I may turn back." Option A misses that understanding the maneuver is critical to making good decisions. Option D has a kernel of truth (stress and wind affect results) but the altitude practice is still the correct training approach.

### Q-A11-50 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-25)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, AC-3

A pilot departs runway 9 with a 10-knot wind from 090. The engine fails at 800 feet AGL. The pilot has previously determined that their turnback altitude in this aircraft is 600 feet (altitude loss in the turn). Should the pilot attempt the turnback?

A) Yes -- 800 feet is above the 600-foot minimum, so the turnback is feasible
B) The raw altitude number suggests feasibility, but the 10-knot headwind on departure becomes a 10-knot tailwind on return, increasing groundspeed and the distance needed to reach the runway. The pilot should add a wind margin to their minimum and recognize that 800 feet in a 10-knot headwind departure is not the same as 800 feet in calm conditions
C) No -- the impossible turn should never be attempted
D) The wind has no effect on the turnback decision

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Wind is a critical and often underestimated factor in the turnback decision. The headwind that helped the departure becomes a tailwind on the return leg, meaning the aircraft covers more ground for the same altitude lost, and the pilot needs more distance to reach the runway. A turnback altitude determined in calm conditions should be increased when departing into a headwind. The pre-briefed plan must account for wind, not just raw altitude. Option A applies the number without adjusting for conditions. Option D ignores basic aerodynamics.

### Q-A11-51 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-26)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, AC-2

A CFI demonstrates the turnback maneuver at altitude. The student asks why the CFI used a 45-degree bank in the turn rather than a standard-rate turn. What is the correct explanation?

A) The CFI used 45 degrees because it looks more dramatic for the demonstration
B) A steeper bank angle completes the turn in less time and therefore less altitude loss, but it increases load factor (and thus stall speed) significantly. The CFI chose 45 degrees as the optimal balance between minimizing turn time and maintaining adequate stall margin. Shallower turns lose more altitude; steeper turns risk a stall
C) Standard-rate turns are always preferred in all situations
D) The bank angle doesn't matter -- the altitude loss is the same regardless

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The turnback is an energy management problem. A shallower bank takes longer to complete the turn, losing more altitude. A steeper bank completes faster but increases load factor (1.41G at 45 degrees), raising stall speed by about 19%. The CFI must teach the student to understand this tradeoff and find the bank angle that minimizes total altitude loss while maintaining adequate stall margin. This is why practice at altitude is essential -- the pilot needs to find their own optimum for their skill level and aircraft.

### Q-A11-52 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-26)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-1, CJ-1

A CFI is developing a pre-departure briefing template that includes the engine failure plan. Which elements should be in the briefing? (Select all that apply)

A) The specific altitude below which the pilot will land approximately straight ahead (no turnback)
B) The landing options available straight ahead and within a limited turn (30 degrees either side)
C) The specific altitude above which a turnback may be considered, adjusted for wind
D) A statement that the pilot will "figure it out if it happens"
E) The best glide speed for the aircraft

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** The pre-briefed plan must include the no-turnback altitude floor (A), the straight-ahead landing options (B), the turnback consideration altitude adjusted for wind (C), and the best glide speed (E). The entire point of AC 61-83K A.11.4's emphasis on demonstration and training is to replace improvised decision-making (D) with a pre-planned response. A pilot who has briefed the plan before departure has already made the critical decision -- they just execute when the moment comes.

### Q-A11-53 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-27)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AC-1, RM-2

A pilot departs a runway with open fields on both sides and dense forest straight ahead. The engine fails at 300 feet AGL. The pilot has briefed that their turnback minimum is 600 feet. What should the pilot do?

A) Attempt the turnback anyway because the runway is the safest landing surface
B) Land in one of the open fields to either side. The pilot is below their turnback minimum, and the fields provide survivable landing options. Attempting the turnback at 300 feet AGL risks a stall/spin into the forest or short of the runway
C) Attempt to restart the engine while maintaining altitude
D) Climb to 600 feet before deciding

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The pre-briefed plan exists for exactly this moment. At 300 feet AGL with a 600-foot turnback minimum, the turnback is off the table. The pilot's job now is to find the best available landing option within the remaining energy. Open fields to either side are survivable. The forest straight ahead is not. A slight turn toward a field is a far better outcome than a desperate turnback attempt that ends in a stall/spin. Option A abandons the briefed plan under pressure. Option C is unrealistic at 300 AGL. Option D is physically impossible -- the engine has failed.

### Q-A11-54 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-27)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, AC-3

During the turnback discussion, a student asks: "Why can't I just make a shallow turn and fly a wide pattern back to the runway? That seems safer than a steep turn." How should the CFI respond?

A) "That's a great idea -- a shallow turn is always safer."
B) "A shallow turn feels safer because the bank angle is less aggressive, but it takes longer to reverse direction. In a glide, time equals altitude. A standard-rate turn at 90 knots takes roughly 60 seconds for 180 degrees -- that's approximately 1,000 feet of altitude loss in the turn alone, before you add the distance back to the runway. At low altitude, you run out of altitude before completing the turn. The steeper bank completes faster with less total altitude loss, as long as you maintain stall margin."
C) "You should always use the steepest possible bank angle."
D) "The bank angle doesn't affect altitude loss in a glide."

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This is a critical teaching moment. Students intuitively feel that shallow = safe, but in a power-off turnback, the math doesn't support it. The CFI must explain the time-altitude-bank relationship: shallower banks take longer, and in a descending glide, longer means more altitude consumed in the turn. The practice at altitude demonstrates this concretely. Option A validates a dangerous misconception. Option C goes too far in the other direction. Option D is factually wrong.

### Q-A11-55 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-28)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, AC-2

A CFI is discussing the "impossible turn" with a student who has seen YouTube videos of pilots successfully executing turnbacks from very low altitudes. The student says, "If those guys can do it at 200 feet, why do we need a 600-foot minimum?" What is the best CFI response?

A) "Those pilots are more skilled than you are right now."
B) "Survivorship bias -- you see the videos of pilots who made it. You don't see the ones who stalled and spun in. For every successful low-altitude turnback on video, there are NTSB reports of pilots who attempted the same thing and didn't survive. Your pre-briefed minimum is based on measured performance in your aircraft with a safety margin. That's not negotiable."
C) "You're right -- 200 feet should be fine with practice."
D) "Those videos are fake."

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Survivorship bias is one of the most dangerous cognitive traps in aviation decision-making. The student only sees the successes because the failures don't get posted (or the pilot didn't survive to post). The CFI must counter this with data: the pre-briefed minimum is based on actual measured altitude loss plus a safety margin, not anecdotes. AC 61-83K A.11.4's emphasis on demonstration and training is specifically to replace anecdotal confidence with evidence-based decision-making.

### Q-A11-56 (Module 3, Pool A11-M3-28)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-1

A CFI has completed the turnback altitude determination exercise with a student at 3,000 feet AGL. The student consistently lost 500 feet in the turn. Which factors should the CFI explain will increase the actual altitude loss in a real engine failure below 3,000 feet? (Select all that apply)

A) Reaction time -- in practice the student expected the power reduction; in a real failure there will be a startle response and delay before initiating the turn
B) Wind -- the practiced turns were in calm conditions; wind on departure day will affect the geometry
C) Stress -- elevated heart rate and adrenaline degrade motor control and decision-making
D) The aircraft always performs better at lower altitudes due to denser air
E) Configuration -- the aircraft may not be in the same flap/gear configuration as during practice

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** The altitude determined in practice is a best-case number. Real-world factors will increase the altitude loss: startle/reaction time (A) adds seconds of uncommanded descent, wind (B) changes the ground track geometry, stress (C) degrades performance, and configuration differences (E) change glide performance. This is why the pre-briefed minimum should include a margin above the measured altitude loss. Option D is wrong -- denser air at lower altitude would slightly improve glide performance, not degrade it, and is not a significant factor in this decision.
