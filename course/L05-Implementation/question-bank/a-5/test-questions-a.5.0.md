### Q-A5-01 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, PS-2

According to GAJSC (General Aviation Joint Steering Committee) data, what is the leading cause of fatal GA accidents?

A) Engine failure
B) Controlled flight into terrain (CFIT)
C) Loss of control in flight (LOC-I)
D) Weather-related accidents

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** The GAJSC has consistently identified loss of control in flight (LOC-I) as the number one cause of fatal GA accidents from 2012 through 2021. AC 61-83K specifically states this and emphasizes the CFI's role in reducing LOC through targeted training. While CFIT, engine failure, and weather are significant factors, LOC-I remains the leading cause by a substantial margin. CFIs must understand this to prioritize training emphasis.

### Q-A5-02 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-01)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-1, RM-2

Which of the following are among the top causal factors in GA accidents as identified by NTSB and GAJSC data? (Select all that apply)

A) Low-altitude maneuvering flight
B) Failure to maintain airspeed during approach
C) Inadequate aircraft maintenance
D) Improper go-around execution
E) Runway incursions by ground vehicles

**Correct:** A, B, D
**Explanation:** GAJSC data shows that seven of the ten top causal factors in GA accidents involve low-altitude maneuvering and loss of control. Low-altitude maneuvering (A), failure to maintain airspeed -- especially in the pattern (B), and improper go-around execution (D) are all significant causal factors. While maintenance (C) and ground vehicle incursions (E) contribute to incidents, they are not among the top pilot-related causal factors that CFIs should emphasize in training.

### Q-A5-03 (Module 3, Pool A5-M3-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, RM-3

Your student is planning a flight to an unfamiliar airport with a 3,200-foot runway at 5,500 feet density altitude. The student's experience is limited to sea-level airports with 5,000+ foot runways. The student has computed performance data and says "The numbers work." As the CFI, what should concern you most?

A) The performance data is probably wrong
B) Computed performance data assumes standard pilot technique -- a student at an unfamiliar high-altitude airport with a short runway is stacking risks that performance charts don't account for
C) Students should not fly to airports above 5,000 feet density altitude
D) The student needs to complete a high-altitude endorsement before this flight

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Performance data is based on standard conditions and standard pilot technique. A student with no experience at high-altitude, short-field airports introduces variables the charts cannot capture: unfamiliarity with the sight picture, potential for delayed go-around decisions, reduced climb performance margins, and the compounding effect of multiple new elements. This is risk stacking (RM-1). The CFI should identify these additive risks and either modify the plan or ensure adequate margins. Option A assumes error without evidence. Option C is an arbitrary restriction. Option D conflates high-altitude operations with an endorsement that doesn't exist for this situation.

### Q-A5-04 (Module 3, Pool A5-M3-01)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, CJ-2

During a dual flight, weather begins deteriorating faster than forecast. Your student is on a VFR cross-country with 30 nm remaining. Visibility has dropped from 10 miles to 5 miles and is trending down. Ceilings are lowering. The student wants to continue. What is the safest teaching approach?

A) Allow the student to continue since VFR minimums are still met and this is a learning experience
B) Take the controls and divert immediately
C) Present the risk assessment to the student: current conditions are legal but trending toward marginal, the remaining distance means continued exposure to deteriorating conditions, and the conservative decision is to divert now while options are good
D) Contact ATC and ask them whether you should continue

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** This is a dynamic risk reassessment scenario (RM-2). Conditions are currently legal but the trend is negative. The CFI should model the decision-making process: current state, trend, remaining exposure, and available options. Teaching the student to divert while conditions are still acceptable -- rather than waiting until they're not -- is the core ADM skill. Option A normalizes complacency about deteriorating conditions. Option B resolves the situation but misses the teaching opportunity. Option D abdicates PIC authority.

### Q-A5-05 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, PS-2

A flight school you instruct at has a culture of "getting it done" -- flights rarely cancel for weather, and students are encouraged to "build experience in all conditions." As a newly hired CFI, you are concerned about a scheduled VFR flight with marginal conditions. The chief pilot says "It'll be fine, just stay in the pattern." What is the most professional response?

A) Follow the chief pilot's guidance since they have more experience and authority
B) Refuse to fly and file a safety complaint with the FSDO
C) Express your concern about the conditions, explain your specific risk assessment, and decline the flight if you believe it is unsafe for the student's skill level
D) Fly the lesson but stay in the pattern as suggested

**Correct:** C
**Explanation:** The PIC (which includes the CFI during dual instruction) has final authority over the operation. External pressure from school culture or authority figures does not override the CFI's judgment about safety. The professional response is to articulate the specific concern -- not just say "I don't feel comfortable" but explain why the conditions, combined with this student's experience level, create an unacceptable risk. Option A surrenders PIC authority. Option B escalates without first attempting professional communication. Option D accepts a flight the CFI has judged unsafe.

### Q-A5-06 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-02)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-1, RM-3

Which risk management model best describes the teaching approach where a CFI helps a student identify multiple individually manageable risk factors that, combined, create an unacceptable cumulative risk?

A) The Swiss cheese model
B) Risk stacking assessment
C) The IMSAFE checklist
D) The PAVE model

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Risk stacking is the concept that multiple individually acceptable risks can combine to create an unacceptable cumulative risk level. A student who is slightly fatigued, at an unfamiliar airport, in gusty winds, with a new passenger is facing four manageable individual risks that together may exceed their capability. The Swiss cheese model (A) describes how defense layers fail. IMSAFE (C) is a personal fitness checklist. PAVE (D) categorizes risk areas but doesn't specifically address the compounding effect. The CFI's role is to teach students to recognize when risks are stacking.

### Q-A5-07 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-03)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, CJ-1

A private pilot you are providing a flight review to mentions that he avoids steep turns because "they feel dangerous." His logbook shows 120 total hours, all at the same airport. When you fly together, his turns are shallow and uncoordinated above 20 degrees of bank. How does this relate to GA safety trend data?

A) His instinct is correct -- steep turns are a leading cause of GA accidents and should be avoided
B) His avoidance of steep turns has created a skill gap in the exact area where GA accident data shows the highest fatality rates -- loss of control
C) Steep turn proficiency is only relevant for commercial pilots
D) His hours are sufficient to demonstrate competency without practicing steep turns

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K identifies loss of control (LOC) as the number one cause of fatal GA accidents, with seven of the top ten causal factors involving low-altitude maneuvering and LOC. A pilot who avoids bank angles above 20 degrees has never developed the skills needed to recover from an upset or maintain control in turbulence. This avoidance pattern directly contributes to the LOC accident statistics because the pilot has no practiced response when the airplane exceeds their narrow comfort zone. The CFI's role is to identify and close exactly this type of skill gap.

### Q-A5-08 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-03)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** RM-1, AV-2

Which of the following accurately describe the relationship between ADM/RM processes and GA safety trends? (Select all that apply)

A) Risk management systematically identifies hazards, assesses degree of risk, and determines the best course of action
B) ADM processes are only applicable during in-flight emergencies
C) Risk intervention strategies are a component of aeronautical decision-making
D) Hazard identification is the first step in an effective risk management process
E) ADM replaces the need for stick-and-rudder proficiency

**Correct:** A, C, D
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K defines risk management and risk intervention strategies as ADM processes designed to systematically identify hazards, assess the degree of risk, and determine the best course of action. Hazard identification is foundational -- you cannot mitigate a risk you haven't identified. ADM applies to all phases of flight and preflight planning, not just emergencies (B). ADM complements rather than replaces stick-and-rudder skills (E) -- both are necessary to address the LOC accidents that dominate GA fatality statistics.

You are teaching a student pilot pattern work at a non-towered airport. On downwind, the student banks aggressively to avoid traffic she spotted late on base. The avoidance is successful, but her bank angle reached approximately 50 degrees at pattern altitude. What is the most important teaching point after the flight?

A) She should have called the traffic on the radio before maneuvering
B) The late traffic detection forced a high-bank, low-altitude maneuver -- exactly the combination that GA accident data identifies as the most lethal scenario. The debrief should focus on earlier scan techniques to avoid being forced into aggressive maneuvering

The root cause was not the bank angle itself but the late detection that forced it. Teaching the student to maintain an effective scan prevents the situation from developing in the first place. Option A addresses communication but misses the safety trend connection. Option C creates an arbitrary limit without addressing root cause. Option D is an unnecessary restriction.

### Q-A5-11 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-04)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, CJ-2

A certificated pilot you are giving a flight review to plans a low-altitude scenic flight through a river canyon. He has GPS track logs showing he has done this route "dozens of times." His risk assessment consists of: "I know the route, the weather is good, and the airplane performs fine." What is the most significant gap in his risk assessment?

A) He should file a flight plan for canyon flying
B) He has normalized the risk through repetition and has not accounted for the variables that change on each flight -- wind, density altitude, other traffic, his own physiological state -- in an environment where LOC is unrecoverable
C) Canyon flying requires a specific endorsement from a CFI
D) GPS track logs are not an acceptable substitute for flight planning

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** This pilot exhibits classic risk normalization -- repeated successful exposure has lowered his perceived risk without changing the actual risk. Low-altitude maneuvering in a canyon environment has zero margin for LOC recovery, and the variables he hasn't assessed (wind shear, density altitude changes, traffic, fatigue) are exactly the factors GA accident data identifies as lethal in combination. His familiarity with the route does not mitigate these dynamic risks. The CFI's role in a flight review is to identify this kind of blind spot and recalibrate the pilot's risk perception.

### Q-A5-13 (Module 3, Pool A5-M3-03)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, RM-3

During a lesson on power-off stalls, your student consistently drops a wing at the break. You demonstrate proper coordination and recovery twice, but the student continues to apply opposite aileron at the stall break rather than rudder. The student says "I know what to do, my hands just do it automatically." What does this scenario illustrate about GA safety trends?

A) The student needs more ground instruction before practicing stalls
B) The student's incorrect muscle memory under stress is the exact mechanism that produces the stall/spin accidents identified as a top GA fatal accident category -- the CFI must develop correct automaticity through deliberate, repeated practice until the proper response overrides the instinct
C) Stall training should be discontinued until the student is more experienced
D) The student should practice in a simulator first to build correct habits

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** Stall/spin accidents are explicitly cited in AC 61-83K as one of the top causal factors in fatal GA accidents. The mechanism is exactly what this student demonstrates -- under the stress of a stall, the untrained instinct is to use aileron to level the wings, which deepens the stall on the dropping wing and can initiate a spin. The CFI must build correct automaticity (rudder to counter yaw, reduce angle of attack) through deliberate repetition until it overrides the incorrect instinct. More ground instruction (A) won't change muscle memory. Discontinuing training (C) avoids the problem. Simulator work (D) may supplement but doesn't replace the feel of actual stall recovery.

**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-2

A student pilot preparing for his first solo cross-country has planned a route that passes through a valley at 1,500 feet AGL. The forecast calls for light turbulence and gusty crosswinds at the destination. The student completed his pre-solo cross-country dual requirements last week in calm conditions. Using the PAVE checklist to frame the risk assessment, what should the CFI emphasize?

A) The student meets the regulatory requirements and the weather is within limits, so the flight should proceed
B) The Environment element has changed significantly from training conditions, and the Pilot element includes minimal cross-country experience -- these combine to create a risk profile that warrants either delaying the flight or modifying the route to reduce low-altitude exposure
C) Turbulence and crosswinds are normal weather that all students must learn to handle
D) The student should fly higher to avoid the turbulence

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** PAVE (Pilot, Aircraft, enVironment, External pressures) provides a framework for systematic hazard identification. The Environment has changed significantly from training conditions, and the Pilot's limited experience means his capacity to handle the new challenges is untested. Flying at 1,500 AGL through a valley in turbulence creates a low-altitude maneuvering scenario -- one of the top causal factors in GA accidents. The CFI should model the risk assessment process rather than simply approving or denying the flight, teaching the student to recognize when conditions exceed their demonstrated capability.
