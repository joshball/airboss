### Q-A5-17 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-06)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-2, OD-1

What specific numerical goal did the FAA establish for reducing the GA fatal accident rate, as referenced in AC 61-83K?

A) Eliminate all GA fatal accidents by 2025
B) Reduce the GA fatal accident rate by 10 percent over a 10-year period (2009-2018) and beyond
C) Reduce the GA fatal accident rate by 50 percent over 20 years
D) Reduce the GA fatal accident rate to the level of Part 121 operations

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K states the FAA's goal is to reduce the GA fatal accident rate by 10 percent over a 10-year period (2009-2018) and beyond. This is a measured, data-driven goal that acknowledges the difficulty of achieving safety improvements in a diverse fleet of 220,000+ aircraft operated by pilots with widely varying experience levels. CFIs should understand this goal because their training directly influences whether it is achieved.

### Q-A5-18 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-06)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, PS-1

According to AC 61-83K, what role do flight instructors play in relation to GA safety?

A) Flight instructors are primarily responsible for regulatory compliance
B) Flight instructors play a leading role in promoting GA safety
C) Flight instructors have a supporting role behind the FAA and NTSB
D) Flight instructors contribute equally with all other aviation professionals

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K explicitly states that flight instructors play a leading role in promoting GA safety. This is not a peripheral responsibility -- it is central to the CFI certificate. Every flight lesson, flight review, and instrument proficiency check is an opportunity to identify and correct the habits, skill gaps, and decision-making patterns that contribute to the GA accident rate. The FAA recognizes CFIs as the primary point of contact for influencing pilot behavior throughout a pilot's career.

### Q-A5-19 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-07)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, CJ-2

A CFI at a busy flight school notices that three students in the past month have demonstrated poor go-around execution -- delayed decisions, improper power application, and failure to establish a positive climb. No incidents have occurred. How should the CFI respond in the context of the FAA's GA safety goals?

A) Since no incidents occurred, document the observations and continue monitoring
B) Recognize this as a systemic training gap in one of the top GA fatal accident categories, bring it to the chief instructor's attention, and propose a targeted training emphasis on go-around procedures across all students
C) Add extra go-around practice only for the three students who demonstrated the weakness
D) Wait until the next safety meeting to raise the concern

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The FAA's 10 percent reduction goal depends on proactive identification of safety trends before they become accidents. Three students with the same deficiency suggests a systemic issue, not three individual problems. Go-arounds fall within the takeoff/landing category -- one of the top causal factors in GA fatal accidents. A CFI who plays a leading role in safety (as AC 61-83K states) addresses the root cause across the training program rather than treating symptoms. Waiting (A, D) allows the gap to persist. Targeting only the three students (C) misses others who may have the same weakness.

### Q-A5-20 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-07)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** PS-2, RM-1

Which of the following actions represent ways a CFI can directly contribute to the FAA's goal of reducing GA fatal accidents? (Select all that apply)

A) Emphasizing loss-of-control prevention during every applicable lesson
B) Teaching students to recognize and mitigate risk stacking before flight
C) Ensuring students meet only the minimum hour requirements for certification
D) Conducting thorough flight reviews that identify and address skill degradation
E) Recommending additional training when a pilot demonstrates proficiency gaps during a flight review

**Correct:** A, B, D, E
**Explanation:** CFIs play a leading role in GA safety by targeting the specific accident causes identified in the data. Emphasizing LOC prevention (A) addresses the number one fatal accident cause. Teaching risk assessment (B) builds ADM skills. Thorough flight reviews (D) catch skill degradation before it leads to accidents. Recommending additional training (E) intervenes when a pilot's proficiency has declined. Meeting only minimum requirements (C) does not contribute to safety improvement -- the minimums are thresholds, not goals. A CFI focused on safety pushes beyond minimums to build genuine competence.

### Q-A5-21 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-08)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, OD-2

A certificated private pilot comes to you for a flight review. He obtained his certificate eight years ago and has flown 15 hours in the past two years, all in the traffic pattern. He tells you he is "good to go" and just needs the sign-off. As a CFI contributing to GA safety, what is your primary obligation?

A) Review the regulatory requirements for a flight review and if he meets them, sign him off
B) Evaluate his actual proficiency against the skills needed for safe operations, recognizing that 15 hours of pattern work over two years likely means significant degradation in cross-country, weather decision-making, and emergency skills -- areas directly tied to GA fatal accident causes
C) Require him to complete a minimum number of flight hours before the review
D) Refer him to a different instructor who specializes in rusty pilots

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The CFI's leading role in GA safety means a flight review is not a paperwork exercise. A pilot with minimal recent experience concentrated in one type of flying has likely lost proficiency in the exact skill areas where GA fatal accidents occur -- navigation decisions, weather assessment, emergency response, and unfamiliar airport operations. The CFI must honestly evaluate actual proficiency and address gaps. Simply meeting regulatory minimums (A) misses the safety purpose. There is no hour requirement before a review (C). Referral (D) abdicates the CFI's responsibility.

### Q-A5-22 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-08)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-3, PS-1

The FAA's 10 percent reduction goal for GA fatal accidents specifically targeted the period 2009-2018. What does the phrase "and beyond" in AC 61-83K indicate about the FAA's expectation?

A) The goal was abandoned after 2018
B) The reduction effort is ongoing and the goal extends indefinitely past the initial target period
C) A new, more aggressive goal replaced the original after 2018
D) The phrase has no regulatory significance

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The "and beyond" language in AC 61-83K signals that the 10 percent reduction goal is not a one-time target but an ongoing commitment. GA safety improvement is a continuous process, not a project with an end date. CFIs should understand that their contribution to safety is perpetual -- every student they train, every flight review they conduct, and every proficiency check they perform is part of this sustained effort. The goal's continuation reinforces that the CFI's leading role in safety is a permanent aspect of the certificate, not a temporary initiative.

### Q-A5-23 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-09)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, RM-2

You are a CFI at a Part 61 school. A student asks why you spend so much time on risk management and ADM when "the checkride is mostly maneuvers." How do you connect your teaching emphasis to GA safety data?

A) Explain that risk management is on the ACS and will be tested on the checkride
B) Explain that the GA fatal accident data shows that the majority of fatal accidents are caused by decision-making failures, not aircraft handling failures -- pilots who crash usually could fly the airplane but chose to fly into situations they shouldn't have
C) Acknowledge that maneuvers are more important and adjust the curriculum
D) Tell the student to trust the training program

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** GA accident data consistently shows that pilot decision-making -- not stick-and-rudder skill -- is the primary factor in fatal accidents. Pilots who encounter LOC situations typically arrived there through a chain of poor decisions: flying in deteriorating weather, continuing into conditions beyond their skill, or failing to recognize compounding risks. A CFI who plays a leading role in safety teaches students that ADM/RM skills determine whether they ever face the emergencies that maneuver proficiency is designed to handle. Referencing the checkride (A) motivates compliance, not understanding.

### Q-A5-24 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-09)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** PS-2, OD-1

Which of the following describe characteristics of the U.S. GA community as stated in AC 61-83K that are relevant to understanding why reducing fatal accidents is challenging? (Select all that apply)

A) It is the largest GA community in the world
B) It is the most diverse GA community in the world
C) It includes more than 220,000 aircraft
D) It is primarily composed of airline transport pilots
E) All GA operations are conducted under Part 91

**Correct:** A, B, C
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K describes the U.S. GA community as the largest and most diverse in the world, with more than 220,000 aircraft. This scale and diversity is directly relevant to safety challenges -- it means the pilot population ranges from student pilots to experienced aviators, aircraft range from basic trainers to complex turboprops, and operations span everything from local pattern work to extended cross-countries. This diversity makes a single safety approach insufficient and underscores why CFIs, as the individuals who interact with pilots across the entire spectrum, play such a leading role. Option D is incorrect, and not all GA is Part 91 (E).

### Q-A5-25 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-10)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-1

A newly certificated private pilot who trained at a towered airport in flat terrain is planning to fly to a mountain airport for the first time. He completed his checkride two weeks ago and has 55 total hours. He asks you, his former CFI, for advice. Considering the FAA's emphasis on CFIs promoting GA safety, what is the most appropriate response?

A) Congratulate him and remind him to check NOTAMs
B) Offer to fly the trip with him as dual instruction, using it as an opportunity to teach mountain flying fundamentals, density altitude management, and canyon avoidance -- skills that directly address the low-altitude maneuvering risks identified in GA safety data
C) Tell him to wait until he has at least 100 hours before flying to mountains
D) Suggest he watch some YouTube videos about mountain flying

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A CFI's role in promoting safety extends beyond the checkride. A newly certificated pilot with no mountain experience is about to encounter density altitude, terrain-induced turbulence, and one-way runways for the first time -- conditions directly related to the low-altitude maneuvering and LOC factors that dominate GA fatal accident data. Offering to fly the trip as instruction addresses the specific skill gap while building the pilot's capability for future independent flights. An arbitrary hour requirement (C) doesn't address the actual skill gap. NOTAMs (A) and videos (D) are inadequate preparation for a completely new operational environment.

### Q-A5-26 (Module 2, Pool A5-M2-10)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RM-2, PS-2

What is the significance of the FAA setting a specific, measurable safety goal (10 percent reduction in fatal accidents) rather than a general aspiration to "improve safety"?

A) It satisfies congressional reporting requirements
B) A specific, measurable goal allows progress to be tracked with data, enables targeted interventions on the specific accident categories that must improve to meet the goal, and holds all participants -- including CFIs -- accountable to a defined standard
C) It has no practical significance for flight instructors
D) It allows the FAA to allocate budget to safety programs

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** A measurable goal transforms safety from an aspiration into a program. When the target is specific, the contributing factors can be analyzed, the interventions can be targeted (e.g., focusing on LOC since it is the largest contributor), and progress can be objectively measured. For CFIs, this means their training emphasis should align with the data -- if LOC is the biggest contributor to fatal accidents, then LOC prevention should receive proportional training emphasis. A vague goal produces vague effort. A specific goal produces specific, accountable action.
