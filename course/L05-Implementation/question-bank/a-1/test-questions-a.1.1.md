# A.1.1 -- Technically Advanced Airplane (TAA)

Questions covering TAA definition per 14 CFR 61.1 and 61.129(j), FITS, PFD/MFD/autopilot requirements, TAA training, and transition training.

---

### Q-A1-17 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-09)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-3, RC-2

A student asks you whether a Cessna 172 equipped with a Garmin GPS navigator with moving map but no autopilot qualifies as a Technically Advanced Airplane (TAA). Based on the regulatory definition in 14 CFR 61.1, what is the correct answer?

A) Yes -- any aircraft with a GPS and moving map is a TAA
B) No -- the regulatory definition of TAA under 14 CFR 61.1 requires specific equipment beyond just a GPS with a moving map, and the aircraft must meet all qualifying criteria
C) It depends on whether the aircraft has a glass cockpit PFD
D) Only aircraft manufactured after 2010 can qualify as TAA

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K notes that while the original FITS description defined TAA broadly as an aircraft with a "GPS navigator with a moving map, plus any additional systems," the FAA formally defined "technically advanced airplane" in 14 CFR 61.1 in 2018 with specific qualifying requirements under 61.129(j). A GPS with moving map alone does not automatically make an aircraft a TAA under the current regulation. Option A uses the outdated FITS description rather than the regulatory definition. Option C identifies one possible component but is not the complete standard. Option D introduces a fabricated manufacturing date criterion.

### Q-A1-18 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-09)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-2, PS-2

A commercial pilot applicant tells you they want to use a TAA to satisfy the requirements under 14 CFR 61.129(j). They plan to use a Piper Archer with an aftermarket Garmin G500 TXi PFD/MFD and a basic wing-leveler autopilot. How should you advise this student?

A) Any aircraft with a glass panel and autopilot qualifies under 61.129(j)
B) Verify that the specific installed equipment meets all the TAA qualifying requirements defined in 14 CFR 61.1 and 61.129(j) before logging the time
C) The aircraft must be factory-equipped with a glass cockpit to qualify
D) Only Cirrus and Diamond aircraft are recognized TAA for 61.129(j) purposes

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** The regulatory definition of TAA under 14 CFR 61.1 sets specific equipment requirements. A CFI must verify that the actual installed avionics meet every element of the definition -- not assume that having "a glass panel and an autopilot" is sufficient. AC 61-83K emphasizes that instructors need to "fully understand the definition of TAA." Option A oversimplifies the requirement. Option C is incorrect -- aftermarket installations can qualify if they meet the regulatory criteria. Option D is fabricated and excludes many qualifying aircraft.

### Q-A1-19 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-10)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AV-3, RC-2

According to AC 61-83K, what was the significance of the FAA's 2018 regulatory action regarding TAA? (Select all that apply)

A) It replaced the informal FITS description of TAA with a formal regulatory definition in 14 CFR 61.1
B) It established qualifying requirements for TAA under 14 CFR 61.129(j) for commercial pilot certification
C) It mandated that all flight training must be conducted in TAA-equipped aircraft
D) It required all CFIs to obtain a separate TAA endorsement before providing TAA instruction

**Correct:** A, B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K explains that "FITS initially described a TAA" informally, but "in the summer of 2018, the FAA specifically defined the term technically advanced airplane in the federal aviation regulations under 61.1 and defined the qualifying requirements for TAA under 61.129(j)." This was significant because it moved TAA from an industry concept to a regulatory standard. Option C is false -- no mandate requires TAA-only training. Option D is fabricated -- no separate TAA endorsement exists for CFIs.

### Q-A1-20 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-10)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** AV-2, CJ-2

The original FITS definition described a TAA as "an aircraft that contains GPS navigator with a moving map, plus any additional systems." How does the current regulatory definition differ in practical terms for a CFI?

A) There is no practical difference -- the FITS definition and the regulatory definition are identical
B) The regulatory definition is narrower, meaning some aircraft that met the FITS description may not qualify as TAA under the current rule, and CFIs must verify compliance with 14 CFR 61.1
C) The regulatory definition is broader, meaning all glass cockpit aircraft automatically qualify
D) The FITS definition is still the official standard; the regulatory definition only applies to Part 141 schools

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K makes clear that the FITS description and the regulatory definition are different. The FITS description was broad and informal -- "GPS navigator with a moving map, plus any additional systems (e.g., an autopilot combined with a GPS navigator)." The 2018 regulatory definition in 14 CFR 61.1 established specific criteria. CFIs must understand the distinction because it affects whether a student can use TAA time to satisfy 61.129(j) requirements. Option A ignores the regulatory change. Option C is incorrect. Option D is fabricated -- the regulation applies to all operations under Part 61.

You are a FIRC provider designing curriculum for Topic A.1. A fellow instructor suggests skipping the TAA definition section because "everyone knows what a glass cockpit is." How should you respond?

The regulatory definition in 14 CFR 61.1 is specific and may differ from what many pilots casually consider a "glass cockpit" aircraft. Skipping this creates a gap in CFI knowledge that could affect how they advise students on 61.129(j) compliance. Option A violates the AC's guidance. Option C provides insufficient coverage. Option D replaces regulatory knowledge with a different skill.

The equipment list matters -- not the aircraft make/model or whether the avionics are factory or aftermarket. Option A is unnecessary if both aircraft qualify. Option C is fabricated. Option D is incorrect -- aftermarket installations can meet the regulatory requirements.

### Q-A1-23 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-12)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, PS-3

A private pilot who has only flown Cessna 172s with steam gauges purchases a Cirrus SR22 with Garmin Perspective+. They ask you to provide transition training. According to AC 61-83K, what is the most critical element of this training?

A) Ensuring the pilot can pass a practical test in the new aircraft within 30 days
B) Providing specialized instruction covering the specific aircraft's systems, limitations, automation management, and manufacturer's recommended procedures from a CFI with experience in that make and model
C) Having the pilot complete a Cirrus-approved online ground school before any flight training
D) Focusing primarily on the Cirrus Airframe Parachute System (CAPS) since it is unique to the aircraft

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K states that "pilots transitioning to TAA, or any unfamiliar aircraft, should receive specialized transition training from a qualified flight instructor with experience in the specific aircraft's make, model, and equipment." The AC further states that pilots "should learn their airplane's limitation, systems limitations, and all manufacturer's recommended procedures for its use before a pilot can fly the aircraft safely." Option A introduces a fabricated timeline. Option C may be helpful but is not the AC's guidance. Option D focuses on one system rather than comprehensive transition training.

### Q-A1-24 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-12)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** AV-3, ES-2

A pilot transitioning from a steam gauge aircraft to a TAA should receive training on which of the following, according to AC 61-83K guidance? (Select all that apply)

A) The specific FMS and autopilot modes, including mode annunciations, transitions, and reversionary modes
B) Recognition and correction of automation-induced risky behaviors such as excessive heads-down time and automation fixation
C) Manual flying proficiency in the new aircraft, including hand-flying with the autopilot disengaged
D) A minimum of 50 hours of supervised flight before solo operations in the TAA
E) Understanding that no single level of automation is appropriate for all flight situations

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K covers all of these elements for TAA transition training. Pilots must learn "their airplane's limitation, systems limitations, and all manufacturer's recommended procedures" (A). Instructors must recognize "automation-based risky behavior in their students" including "excessive heads-down time, automation fixation, automation dependency" (B). The FAA "strongly recommends" maintaining "proficiency in manual aircraft control" (C). The AC states "no one level of automation is appropriate for all flight situations" (E). Option D is fabricated -- no 50-hour supervised minimum exists in the regulations or the AC.

### Q-A1-25 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-13)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-3, RM-2

You are providing transition training to an experienced IFR pilot in a new TAA. The pilot says, "I've been flying IFR for 20 years -- I just need to learn the buttons." How should you approach this student?

A) Respect their experience and focus training exclusively on avionics button-pushing and menu navigation
B) Acknowledge their experience but explain that TAA transition involves more than panel operation -- it requires new scan patterns, automation management strategies, and understanding how the integrated systems change cockpit workload and decision-making
C) Require them to start over with basic private pilot maneuvers before touching the avionics
D) Have them read the pilot's operating handbook and then sign them off after a brief checkout flight

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K makes clear that TAA training goes beyond learning the interface. The AC discusses automation hazards (fixation, dependency, heads-down time), the need for appropriate automation level selection, and maintaining manual proficiency. An experienced IFR pilot may have excellent airmanship but still be vulnerable to glass cockpit-specific hazards they have never encountered. Option A addresses only surface-level skills. Option C is demeaning and unnecessary. Option D provides insufficient transition training for a fundamentally different cockpit environment.

### Q-A1-26 (Module 1, Pool A1-M1-13)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** PS-2, RC-2

A CFI at your flight school has been providing TAA transition training in a Cessna 182 with a Garmin G1000. A student asks that CFI to also provide transition training in a Cirrus SR22 with Garmin Perspective+. The CFI has never flown a Cirrus. What is the appropriate guidance?

A) The avionics are similar enough that the CFI's G1000 experience is sufficient for Cirrus instruction
B) The CFI should decline until they have gained experience in the Cirrus SR22's specific make, model, and equipment, as AC 61-83K recommends transition training from a qualified instructor with that specific experience
C) Any CFI can provide transition training in any aircraft
D) The CFI should complete a factory training course, which automatically qualifies them regardless of flight experience in type

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K specifically states that transition training should come from "a qualified flight instructor with experience in the specific aircraft's make, model, and equipment." While G1000 and Perspective+ share Garmin roots, the Cirrus SR22 has distinct systems (CAPS, sidestick, airframe-specific procedures) that require specific experience. Option A conflates avionics familiarity with aircraft-specific knowledge. Option C ignores the AC's guidance entirely. Option D overstates the value of ground-based factory training without actual flight experience in type.
