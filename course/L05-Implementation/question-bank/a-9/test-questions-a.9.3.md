# A.9.3 -- IPC and Flight Review Considerations

Questions covering thorough understanding of ACS/PTS, assessing individual pilot needs, currency action plans, AELS, and the difference between a meaningful evaluation and a pointless exercise.

---

### Q-A9-37 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-19)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-2, ES-1

According to AC 61-83K, what is the difference between a "pointless exercise that only meets the regulatory requirements" and a "truly effective IPC or flight review"?

A) A truly effective evaluation costs more and takes longer
B) In a truly effective evaluation, the pilot comes away knowing something they did not know before, possessing a degree of skill they did not have before, and meeting proficiency standards required for safe flight
C) The difference is only in the paperwork -- the logbook entry for an effective review is more detailed
D) A truly effective evaluation includes a written test component

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.3 states instructors should "know how to instantly formulate an effective plan of action that will make the difference between a pointless exercise that only meets the regulatory requirements, and a truly effective IPC or flight review. The pilot under review/evaluation should come away knowing something they did not know before, possess a degree of skill they did not have before, and meet proficiency standards required to conduct a safe flight." Option A conflates time/cost with quality. Option C reduces the difference to documentation. Option D adds a requirement not in the regulation.

### Q-A9-38 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-19)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, ES-2

A CFI conducts a flight review by running through the same standardized checklist they use for every pilot -- same maneuvers, same order, same altitude, same duration. They tell you this approach ensures consistency. What is the primary concern with this method?

A) There is no concern -- standardization ensures fairness
B) A standardized checklist approach fails to assess the individual pilot's specific needs, operating environment, and proficiency gaps -- AC 61-83K requires the instructor to assess individual pilot needs and tailor the evaluation accordingly
C) The concern is only that the checklist should be longer
D) Standardized checklists are fine as long as they include all Part 91 topics

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.3 emphasizes "assessing individual pilot needs" as a key instructor competency. A one-size-fits-all checklist may miss a commercial seaplane pilot's unique needs or waste time testing a 5,000-hour ATP on basic private pilot maneuvers. The evaluation must be responsive to who the pilot is and how they fly. Option A confuses standardization with effectiveness. Option C suggests the problem is quantity rather than approach. Option D limits the concern to ground content.

### Q-A9-39 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-20)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** ES-1, CJ-1

According to AC 61-83K, flight instructors conducting IPCs and flight reviews should possess thorough understanding of which of the following? (Select all that apply)

A) The applicable ACS or PTS standards
B) Techniques for assessing individual pilot needs
C) How to develop currency action plans for pilots
D) The minimum billing rates for flight reviews in their area
E) How FAA AELS requirements affect flight evaluations

**Correct:** A, B, C, E
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.3 states instructors should "possess a thorough understanding of the requirements and process for conducting an IPC or flight review" including understanding of "applicable ACS/PTS" (A), "assessing individual pilot needs" (B), and "currency action plans" (C). AELS requirements are also addressed (E) -- instructors must identify pilots who do not demonstrate AELS. Option D -- billing rates -- is a business consideration not addressed by the AC.

### Q-A9-40 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-20)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, RM-1

A pilot completes a flight review but you identify several areas where their proficiency is marginal. You provide a logbook endorsement for the flight review. What additional step does AC 61-83K suggest you should take?

A) Nothing further -- the endorsement completes your obligation
B) Develop a currency action plan that identifies specific areas for improvement and provides the pilot with a roadmap for maintaining and improving their proficiency before the next review
C) Report the marginal areas to the FSDO
D) Require the pilot to schedule a follow-up checkride within 90 days

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.3 identifies "currency action plans" as a key competency for instructors conducting flight reviews. A currency action plan gives the pilot specific, actionable guidance for maintaining proficiency between reviews -- not just a signoff and a handshake. Option A misses the opportunity to improve long-term safety. Option C is unnecessary for marginal (not unsafe) performance. Option D imposes a requirement that doesn't exist in regulation.

### Q-A9-41 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-21)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** ES-2, RC-2

During a flight review, you observe that a pilot cannot effectively communicate with ATC and struggles to understand clearances given in standard aviation English. You suspect they may not meet FAA Aviation English Language Standards (AELS). What is the correct course of action?

A) Ignore the issue since English proficiency is not part of a flight review
B) Provide a logbook entry for training received, advise the pilot of your finding regarding the AELS deficiency, and notify the appropriate FSDO for further action
C) Refuse to endorse the flight review and tell the pilot to take English classes
D) Complete the flight review normally and suggest they practice radio communications

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.3 states that instructors "should know that regulations require pilots to meet and maintain FAA AELS for all grades of pilot certificates issued under part 61. When a flight instructor conducts a flight evaluation and/or flight training event, the instructor should identify any pilot that does not demonstrate FAA AELS. In cases like this, the instructor should provide a logbook entry for training received, advise the pilot of the instructor's finding, and notify their appropriate FSDO for further action." Option A ignores a safety and regulatory issue. Option C is incomplete (no FSDO notification). Option D defers a serious concern.

### Q-A9-42 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-21)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** RC-2, AV-2

Which AC does AC 61-83K reference as providing guidance specifically on currency requirements and the flight review and instrument proficiency check?

A) AC 61-65 -- Certification: Pilots and Flight and Ground Instructors
B) AC 61-98 -- Currency Requirements and Guidance for the Flight Review and Instrument Proficiency Check
C) AC 60-28 -- FAA English Language Standard
D) AC 61-91 -- WINGS -- Pilot Proficiency Program

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.3 specifically references "the 2018 revision of AC 61-98, Currency Requirements and Guidance for the Flight Review and Instrument Proficiency Check" as including "a new section on the FAA AELS." This is the primary companion AC for IPCs and flight reviews. Option A covers certification broadly. Option C covers English language standards. Option D covers the WINGS program.

### Q-A9-43 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-22)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-1, ES-2

You are a FIRC presenter teaching the A.9.3 module on IPC and flight review considerations. An attendee asks: "What resources should I use to prepare for conducting a flight review for a helicopter pilot when I normally only instruct in airplanes?" What is the best guidance?

A) Decline to conduct the review since you are primarily an airplane instructor
B) Study the applicable ACS or PTS for the rotorcraft category, review the pilot's specific operating environment and aircraft, and develop a plan of action tailored to their individual needs -- the same principles of thorough preparation apply regardless of category
C) Use your airplane flight review checklist and adapt the maneuver names
D) Refer the pilot to a helicopter-specific CFI since airplane CFIs cannot conduct helicopter flight reviews

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.3 emphasizes that instructors must "possess a thorough understanding of applicable ACS/PTS" and be skilled at "assessing individual pilot needs." The same preparation principles apply across categories -- know the standard, know the pilot, tailor the evaluation. Option A may be appropriate in some cases but the question asks about preparation, not refusal. Option C applies an inappropriate standard. Option D is incorrect if the CFI holds the appropriate rotorcraft instructor certificate.

### Q-A9-44 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-22)

**Type:** multiple-select
**Competencies:** ES-1, CJ-2

According to AC 61-83K, the FAA requires FIRC providers to emphasize the importance of the IPC and flight review throughout the entire FIRC program. Which of the following reflect this emphasis? (Select all that apply)

A) The IPC and flight review should be presented as a standalone core topic
B) The instructor's role in reducing GA accidents through effective IPCs and flight reviews should be woven throughout the entire FIRC
C) The IPC and flight review module should only be covered if time permits
D) Providers should emphasize the difference between a pointless exercise and a truly effective evaluation

**Correct:** A, B, D
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.3 states that "while the FAA requires providers to present the IPC and flight review as a core topic, they should emphasize the importance of the IPC, flight review, and instructor's role in reducing GA accidents throughout the entire FIRC program." It also distinguishes between a "pointless exercise" and a "truly effective IPC or flight review." Option C contradicts the requirement to present this as a core topic.

B) AC 61-98 and the Airplane Flying Handbook, which include sections on LOC

D) NTSB accident reports are the only recommended LOC reference

**Correct:** B

### Q-A9-46 (Module 5, Pool A9-M5-23)

**Type:** multiple-choice
**Competencies:** CJ-2, ES-1

A CFI tells you they sign off flight reviews by asking the pilot to fly three takeoffs and landings, demonstrate slow flight, and do a couple of steep turns. If those go well, they endorse the review. They have never used the ACS or PTS as a reference when planning a flight review. What fundamental gap does this reveal?

A) The gap is that they should add stalls to the maneuver list
B) The CFI lacks a thorough understanding of the applicable ACS/PTS standards and is not assessing individual pilot needs -- they are applying an arbitrary personal standard rather than an informed, tailored evaluation based on published proficiency standards
C) There is no gap -- the CFI has discretion over flight review content
D) The gap is only that they should spend more time on each maneuver

**Correct:** B
**Explanation:** AC 61-83K A.9.3 identifies "thorough understanding of applicable ACS/PTS" and "assessing individual pilot needs" as essential instructor competencies. A CFI who has never referenced the ACS/PTS for flight reviews is operating without the foundational knowledge the AC requires. While the CFI does have discretion (Option C is partially true), that discretion must be informed by the published standards, not exercised in ignorance of them. Option A adds one item without addressing the systematic gap. Option D focuses on duration rather than approach.
