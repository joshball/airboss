# Course Research Rewrite Standards

Date: 2026-04-09
Scope: Shared standards used to rewrite the FIRC course-research topic overviews.

## Purpose

This note captures the standards that should guide any future rewrite of the topic research docs in this directory.

The short version:

> Start with the AC, pull exact regulations when the AC points to them, separate required content from supporting explanation, and build the topic around the instructor judgment or teaching failure the FAA is trying to prevent.

## Core Principles

### 1. Start with the AC, not with memory

For each topic:

- identify the non-negotiable teaching points in the AC first;
- identify whether the AC requires a full lesson, a comprehensive overview, or only an overview;
- preserve the AC's level of force in the rewrite.

### 2. Use exact regulatory anchoring when the topic depends on regulation

If the AC names a CFR section, use the actual rule text or a verified repo extract before summarizing it.

Do not:

- paraphrase from memory;
- blur two different regulatory sections into one concept;
- cite a specific section number you have not verified.

### 3. Separate three layers of content

Each overview should distinguish:

- what the FAA requires;
- what supporting FAA sources help explain it;
- what internal teaching ideas or future additions would make it better.

### 4. Treat dated AC statistics as context, not as evergreen knowledge

When the AC uses dated statistics or historical implementation milestones:

- preserve them when useful for historical context;
- do not make them the centerpiece of the topic;
- shift the durable teaching point to the underlying safety pattern or instructional responsibility.

### 5. Match modal language to the source

Use:

- `must` only when the source clearly imposes a hard requirement;
- `should` when the AC is advisory or recommends a practice;
- `may` or `can` for optional or contextual practices.

### 6. End every overview with one prioritized improvement

Do not leave a generic notes stub.

Each topic should end with:

- one clearly named highest-priority gap; or
- one concrete artifact or scenario that would improve the topic the most.

### 7. Keep living topics living

Some topics are inherently dynamic, especially:

- TSA requirements
- regulatory updates
- current safety trends
- current ACS implementation detail

For those topics:

- avoid overcommitting to stale specifics unless verified;
- direct future maintainers toward current FAA or TSA sources;
- make the maintenance process itself part of the topic.

## Recommended Overview Structure

Use this structure unless the topic clearly needs less:

## What the FAA Wants Taught

- Tight paraphrase of the AC
- Non-negotiable teaching points

## What This Topic Is Really About

- Plain-language statement of the safety or instructional problem

## Teaching Emphasis for CFIs

- What instructors should know
- What they should teach
- What they should notice or correct

## Primary / Regulatory Anchors

- AC section
- Verified CFR / AC / handbook anchors

## Current Internal Coverage

- Existing scenarios
- Existing question-bank or lesson coverage

## Highest-Priority Gap

- One concrete next improvement

## Rewrite Checklist

Before considering an overview complete, verify:

- the AC's central teaching intent is explicit near the top;
- stale or dated statistics are framed correctly;
- every cited regulation or advisory reference is either verified or described more cautiously;
- required content is clearly separated from supporting context;
- the topic's instructor-facing value is obvious;
- the file ends with a concrete next-step gap, not a placeholder.
