# Review -- A.1 GPS, Automation & TAA

Date: 2026-04-09
Scope: Review of the research docs in this directory against `AC 61-83K` and the local CFR extracts in `docs/faa-docs/part-61.md` and `docs/faa-docs/part-91.md`.

## Overall Assessment

This research set was already strong on the FAA's central safety message:

- modern avionics improve capability;
- they also introduce new hazards;
- CFIs must teach disciplined use of automation without allowing basic airmanship to erode.

The main weaknesses were not conceptual. They were mostly about precision:

- some regulatory language needed to be anchored more directly to the actual CFR text;
- some "should" language in the AC had drifted toward sounding like a hard requirement;
- a few supporting claims were stronger than their sourcing;
- one of the TAA scenario references was more acronym-adjacent than directly aligned with the FAA's regulatory-definition teaching objective.

## Findings

### High

#### 1. The TAA section needed exact CFR anchoring

The earlier draft referenced `§ 61.1` and `§ 61.129(j)` correctly in general, but it left the exact component list as unfinished work.

That mattered because A.1.1 is one of the AC's explicit required subtopics. For this topic, a finished research doc should already contain:

- the general definition from `§ 61.1`; and
- the installed-component criteria from `§ 61.129(j)`.

This has now been corrected in the rewritten `A.1.1_TAA-Definition.md`.

#### 2. The A.1.1 scenario alignment was weak

The existing scenario reference under A.1.1 focused on `TAA = Terminal Arrival Area`, not `Technically Advanced Airplane`.

That is a useful supporting acronym lesson, but it does not replace the core instructional task the FAA actually requires here:

- determine whether a specific airplane qualifies as a TAA under the regulation.

The rewritten doc now calls this out explicitly as the highest-priority gap.

### Medium

#### 3. Transition-training wording needed to match the AC's level of force

The AC says pilots transitioning to TAA or unfamiliar aircraft **should receive** specialized transition training. Some of the earlier wording read closer to **required**.

That distinction matters. For research docs that will later influence lesson language, the modal verb should remain faithful to the source unless a regulation creates a harder rule.

This has now been normalized across the rewritten docs.

#### 4. ADS-B airspace coverage summary was incomplete

The earlier ADS-B summary captured most of `§ 91.225`, but it omitted one operationally important item:

- airspace above the ceiling and within the lateral boundaries of Class B or Class C airspace up to 10,000 feet MSL.

It also underplayed the value of briefly teaching:

- limited exceptions;
- ATC deviation requests;
- and the transmit-mode expectation once equipped.

The rewritten ADS-B doc now includes the full teaching-useful list.

#### 5. The top-level overview underweighted one of the AC's clearest lines

One of the strongest sentences in the AC is that pilots should learn:

- airplane limitations;
- system limitations;
- and manufacturer-recommended procedures

before relying on the equipment.

That idea was present in the long verbatim excerpt, but it was not prominent enough in the summary guidance. The rewrite now makes that one of the first teaching points.

### Low

#### 6. Some details were useful but not yet ready as finished teaching anchors

Examples included:

- latency and weather-product detail that needed tighter primary-source grounding;
- anecdotal or future case-study ideas that were still sitting close to finalized lesson bullets;
- stale-sensitive specifics such as exact product versions or implementation-status notes.

These are fine as research ideas, but they should be visibly marked as supporting or future work, not allowed to blend into the required core.

## What the FAA Wants Instructors to Know and Teach About A.1

This is the clearest plain-language summary of the FAA's intent in A.1:

- GPS and automation are now normal parts of GA flying.
- Those tools improve capability, but they also create new accident pathways.
- CFIs must teach students what the systems can do, what they cannot do, and how to use them without becoming heads-down, fixated, or dependent.
- Students must retain manual-control skill and traditional navigation ability.
- Students must understand that no one automation level fits every phase of flight.
- Students must be able to keep flying safely if the automation fails in whole or in part.
- CFIs must also understand the regulatory definition of TAA and have overview-level familiarity with ADS-B and NextGen.

If compressed into one sentence:

> The FAA wants instructors to teach modern avionics competently, skeptically, and reversibly, without sacrificing basic aircraft control and navigation skill.

## Strongest Parts of the Original Set

- Good instinct for the real safety issue: automation dependency rather than mere avionics unfamiliarity
- Strong scenario thinking around mode confusion, fixation, database issues, and FIS-B misuse
- Good question-bank alignment overall
- Good recognition that A.1.1, A.1.2, and A.1.3 should all roll up into the larger modern-cockpit lesson

## Most Important Improvements Applied in the Rewrite

- Added exact TAA regulatory anchoring using `§ 61.1` and `§ 61.129(j)`
- Clarified that A.1.1 needs a true aircraft-qualification scenario, not just acronym overlap
- Tightened modal language so `should` stays `should`
- Completed the operational ADS-B airspace list from `§ 91.225`
- Elevated limitations, system limits, and manufacturer procedures as a central teaching point
- Trimmed or reframed speculative, stale-sensitive, or partially sourced supporting detail
- Kept NextGen at the overview level the AC actually requires

## Recommendation for Remaining Topic Research

Use A.1 as the model for what to do next:

- start from the AC text;
- extract the non-negotiable teaching points first;
- pull exact CFR text whenever the AC cites a regulation;
- separate required content from supporting explanation;
- and make every scenario proposal prove the exact skill or judgment the FAA is trying to build.

The companion guidance document in this directory captures that workflow in a reusable form for the remaining topics.
