# A.2 -- SUA & Airspace Security

FAA Core Topic: Security-Related Special Use Airspace (SUA): What's Going on Where; How to Stay Clear

**Internal name:** SUA & Airspace Security

**Minimum time:** 45 minutes
**Our module:** Module 4 -- Airspace, Security, and Compliance

---

## FAA Description (verbatim from AC 61-83K Appendix A)

### A.2 Main Section

Several significant airspace changes have resulted from the events of 9/11. Pilots in training depend on their instructors to guide them through the intricacies of the new and changing airspace regulations and to make them aware of the consequences of violating those airspaces. "Floating" temporary flight restrictions (TFR), particularly common during election years, are a significant challenge for pilots. Regulations regarding powerplants and stadiums are now in place. Instructors should have thorough knowledge about concepts entirely new to many GA pilots, such as the Washington, DC Flight Restricted Zone (FRZ) and Special Flight Rules Area (SFRA), formally the Air Defense Identification Zone (ADIZ). In addition, all pilots should be made fully aware of intercept procedures. The consequences of violating airspaces have become much more severe and often allow little flexibility with respect to enforcement.

---

## What the FAA Wants Taught

This topic is not a generic airspace lesson. The AC's emphasis is on the security-driven airspace environment that CFIs and their students must actively avoid violating.

The non-negotiable teaching points are:

- significant post-9/11 airspace changes remain operationally important;
- instructors must guide students through changing security-related airspace rules;
- floating TFRs are a recurring challenge;
- powerplant and stadium restrictions are part of the modern operating environment;
- CFIs should understand the Washington, DC FRZ and SFRA;
- all pilots should be made fully aware of intercept procedures;
- the consequences of violating these airspaces are severe and enforcement flexibility may be limited.

## What This Topic Is Really About

The FAA is trying to prevent pilots from stumbling into security-related airspace problems because they treated them like ordinary chart details instead of high-consequence preflight and inflight decision items.

For CFIs, the core instructional task is:

- teach a repeatable habit of checking, understanding, and respecting security-related airspace before every flight;
- not merely mention that TFRs and special procedures exist.

## Teaching Emphasis for CFIs

CFIs should be able to teach:

- how to treat security-related airspace as a live, changing planning item;
- why floating TFRs and event-driven restrictions can catch otherwise careful pilots;
- the difference between ordinary airspace complexity and security-sensitive airspace with sharp enforcement consequences;
- where DC SFRA / FRZ knowledge becomes essential;
- how to recognize and respond correctly if intercept procedures ever become relevant.

This topic should create behavior, not just awareness:

- check security-related airspace every flight;
- brief consequences honestly;
- never assume yesterday's airspace picture is today's.
