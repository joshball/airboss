# Upper Air Wind

The frictional drag of the ground normally decreases with height and becomes insignificant above the
lowest few thousand feet. However, this may vary somewhat, since both strong winds and rough terrain
extend the friction layer to higher altitudes.
10.4 Upper Air Wind
In the atmosphere above the friction layer (lowest few thousand feet), only PGF and Coriolis force affect
the horizontal motion of air. Remember that the PGF drives the wind and is oriented perpendicular to height
contours. When a PGF is first established, wind begins to blow from higher to lower heights directly across
the height contours. However, the instant air begins moving, Coriolis force deflects it to the right. Soon the
wind is deflected a full 90° and is parallel to the height contours. At this time, Coriolis force exactly balances
PGF, as shown in Figure 10-8 on a 500 mb constant-pressure chart (see Section 25.3 for information on
constant-pressure charts). With the forces in balance, wind will remain parallel to height contours as shown
in Figure 10-9. This is called the geostrophic wind.
